Garden Cities of Tomorrow: 
From the Global North to the 
Global South and Back Again
The garden city can be looked upon as a reaction to 
the failure of the 19th-century industrial city, justly parodied as ‘Coketown’ by Charles Dickens in Hard Times, 
to address its inherent social and environmental problems. Industrial cities were exploding and urban infrastructures were often unable to meet the new demands 
being made on them, leading to inadequate housing 
stock, water supplies and sewage disposal. As a result, epidemics like cholera affected many European 
and North American cities from the 1840s to the 1860s.
The reasons for this explosive growth of cities were 
multiple. During the 19th century, industrialization 
spread first to northwestern Europe and the eastern 
states of the United States, and then further afield. It 
was paired with an enormous increase in world trade 
(which trebled between 1870 and 1914) and in mass 
manufacturing. Europe and the United States developed their new industries in cities. In addition, technological changes in agriculture, particularly in Europe, led 
to a contraction in the demand for labour in rural areas 
and were paired with a migration towards cities.
Another reason for the rapid expansion and densification of cities was population growth. Birth and death 
rates were affected by increasing supply of food. Agricultural Revolution in 18th-century Europe had led to 
the use of more efficient farming techniques, which in 
turn had increased food production. This reduced the 
number of people dying from malnutrition, and improved people‘s overall health, causing them to live longer. 
Expansion of the international economy and improvements in transport also contributed to improved food 
supplies by enabling large quantities of cheap food to 
be transported from North America and elsewhere to 
Europe. A third reason for the expansion of cities were 
medical advances, which made childbirth less dangerous. The increasing use of vaccination helped prevent 
major epidemics. 
The late 19th century was also an era of unprecedented intercontinetal migration, as a consequence of the 
rise in population. Driven by the poverty at home and 
attracted by availability of work in the country of destination many people migrated, for instance, from Europe to the United States as well as to Latin America, 
Canada, Australasia, South Africa and Siberia. They 
were facilitated by the revolution in transport, which 
substantially reduced the cost of transatlantic travel 
and attracted by the investment of capital overseas, 
which created opportunities for economic development. Many Chinese migrants turned to Southeast 
Asia, Australia and the United States, to work in mines and plantations or to build railways. With the pressure on resources in Japan, many citizens emigrate to 
Manchuria and the Americas.
Ebenezer Howard defined the garden city as the alternative to unrestrained urban development and the 
profound depression in rural areas. Howard’s scheme 
was based upon a rational plan-based integration of all 
aspects of modern urban life and the marriage of town 
and country – together leading to a ‘new civilisation’. 
Each garden city would be limited to 32,000 residents 
and as near as possible self-sufficient. Ultimately six 
garden cities might form a regional ‘social city’ cluster 
round a larger central city, population 58,000, giving 
a total of 250,000, linked by radial roads and railways, 
but prevented from coalescence by agricultural belts. 
The garden city was Howard’s ‘big idea’. It would be 
realized and refined by Parker and Unwin in the UK 
and gained worldwide acclaim.

The Spanish urban planner Arturo Soria y Mata proposed the concept of the Linear City in 1882, which 
replaced the traditional idea of the city as a centre and 
a periphery. His urban model is composed of linear 
sections of infrastructure - roads, railways, gas, water- 
along a main transport infrastructure, which forms the 
basis for all other components of the city. In comparison of the concentric diagrams of Ebenezer Howard 
Soria y Mata‘s linear city creates the infrastructure for a 
controlled process of expansion that joins one growing 
city to the next in a rational way, instead of letting them 
both sprawl.
In the Soviet Union, the supporters of disurbanism 
conceptualized the dispersion of dwelling across the 
rual territory by proposing modular buildings distributed along infrastructural lines that also determined 
the location of productive facilities. Their urban model 
was strongly related to the electrification of the country 
identified by Lenin as a pillar of the soviet revolution. 
The disurbanism concept was initially propagated by 
the sociologist Mikhail Okhitovitch, and Moisei Ginzburg. It was only in 1930 that Ginzburg and Mikhail 
Barsch presented their radical “Green City” project for 
Moscow, proposing an immediate halt to new construction in the city, the relocation of public enterprises 
into the territory and the transformation of the vacant 
urban spaces into parks, as well as the dispersion of 
the citizens of Moscow along the traffic arteries that 
connected the city with rural areas.
