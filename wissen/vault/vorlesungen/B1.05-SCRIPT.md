Between Ideal and Laboratory: 
Of European Renaissance 
Principles and Laws of the 
Indies 
The notion of “Rinascimento”, or re-birth, was introduced by the Italian architect Vasari to describe the renewed cultural interest in Antiquity that had its origin in Italy 
of the 14th century, flowered during the subsequent two 
centuries and spread to the rest of Europe. It involved 
thinkers, writers, painters, architects with renown names 
such as Dante (1265-1321), Petrarch (1304-1374) and 
Boccacio (1313-1376). This new interest did not emerge 
in isolation. It was paired with new practices and theories 
of the ‚autonomous state‘ with its public servants and 
politics of ‚reason‘, the growth of international trade and 
the introduction of printing.
Renaissance Urban Design emerged in Italy, but rapidly 
spread to France in the 16th century, Britain in the 17th 
century and America in the late 17th and 18th centuries. 
During this era, it also became a feature of cities in European colonies in the Americas, Africa, India, the Far East 
and Australia. In contradistinction to medieval urban development which gradually added subsidiary parts, the 
Renaissance City was conceived as a centralized unit. 
Three main principles characterize Renaissance urban 
design. First, its spatial unity which is based on rational 
design and design order. Second, the finite nature of 
space. The urban spaces that were designed by 15th 
century architects were precisely calculated and visually 
controlled, giving the observer a clear idea of structure 
and an awareness of the spatial rhythm around it. The 
spaces of the Renaissance city are enclosed and at rest. 
Third, the urban spaces are geometric, measured and 
proportioned. The Renaissance city complies to a measured order.
With the perfection of gunpowder and the cannon (invented early in the 14th Century) the defense of cities 
became a new concern in urban design. Canons were 
responsible for the fall of the great city of Constantinople, 
in 1453, marking the beginning of a new age in the history of military fortifications. In the Renaissance period the 
fortified city would be brought to a new level of sophistication. Vertical urban walls were left and new systems 
were invented that could enlarge the horizontal distance between the city perimeter and the attacking artillery. 
Star-shaped or stellar fortifications with intricate successions of interdependent bastions would become a main 
occurrence in many European cities. 
City and State were often interrelated in Renaissance 
thinking, even interchangeable concepts. The city was 
seen as an expression of the ‚state‘ and literary utopias 
accompanied these conceptions of the ‚Ideal City‘. These 
literary utopias were intellectual experiments that focused 
on social revolution. Also Renaissance architects such as 
Alberti and Filarete contributed to the thinking of the ‚citta 
ideale‘. Partially based on the rediscovery of the writings 
of the classical Roman architect Vitruvius, they sparked 
an era of ideal city preoccupation in urban design.
In the Renaissance period urban design was not only 
a matter of the European continent, but part and parcel of colonial ventures. In 1573 the Spanish King Phillip 
II signed the Laws of the Indies. Heavily influenced by 
Vitruvius‘ Ten Books of Architecture and Leon Battista 
Alberti‘s treatises on architecture, this legal document 
would become the main guide to plan and regularize the 
establishment of presidios (military towns), missions, and 
pueblos (civilian towns) in Spanish colonies. It consisted 
of 148 ordinances that would aid colonists in locating, 
building, and populating settlements. It codified the city 
planning process and represented a general approach to 
the urban design of the colonial city, which would include gridiron urban plan, broad squares, as well as stately 
colonnades and straight thoroughfares. The same laws 
of urban design were applied in every part of the Indies 
during the 16th century, the crucial period for the laying 
out of towns in the Spanish domains of the New World.
