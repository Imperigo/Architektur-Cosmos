What roles for ruins? Meaning and narrative of 
industrial ruins in contemporary parks
Abstract
This article is concerned with the notion that parks containing industrial 
ruins possess an emotional attraction that can depoliticize historical narratives. How can we design parks containing industrial ruins that reveal 
the multiplicities of history? In order to pursue this question, the article 
examines the literature to outline the relationships between the historical 
meanings of traditional ruins in landscape design and the contemporary 
emotional appeal of industrial ruins. The article also examines the relationship between cultural perceptions of industry, its ruins, and parks containing industrial ruins. The findings show that each of these three conditions 
(industry, industrial ruin, and park containing industrial ruins) are perceived differently, but all share the roles of icons, emotional objects, settings, workplaces, environmental agents and characters in cultural life. The 
article concludes with a guide for designers to assist in the creation of more 
complex narratives than are found in the contemporary genre of parks containing industrial ruins.

Introduction
Purpose
Motivated by the cultural implications of the genre of parks containing 
industrial ruins, the purpose of this article is twofold: to examine the factors that lead to the apolitical nature of how industrial ruins are perceived, 
and to examine the attraction of industrial ruins with the aim of developing an operational framework to guide the design of parks within these 
charismatic sites. By ‘apolitical’ , I mean that industrial ruins in parks 
provide a neutral history. Parks containing industrial ruins are aesthetically attractive and nostalgic. The draw is less about who worked in the 
industry, what was manufactured, mined or transported on that site, and 
more about the emotional response to the ruins. The ruins are not typically used in ways that project the environmental consequences, production 
practices, economics, or legacies of the industry. Instead, it is my view that 
parks containing industrial ruins are designed and built because people 
enjoy ruins, especially in parks. Industrial ruins in parks are enjoyed for 
particular and complex cultural reasons. By unpacking the sublime qualities of industrial ruins – those qualities linked to the scale, material, function, and power of industry – we can better understand their fundamental attraction. Understanding the attraction of ruins can help to support 
the development of a new framework for designs that can better capture 
the competing narratives of industrial landscapes. Finding ways to make 
cultural landscapes from post-industrial sites is a complex challenge, and 
the challenge of their meaning has been the subject of many recent papers 
(Langhorst 2004, Edensor 2005, Armstrong 2006, Huyssen 2006, Loures et al. 2006). 
Background, knowledge and viewpoints that frame visitors’ readings of 
parks containing industrial ruins are out ofbeyond designers’ control, but 
the design of this type of park can be guided toward more nuanced and 
politically engaged portrayals of history. 
Many designed landscapes rely on myths and imagination to create meaning. Culture and memory are also brought to bear on a park visitor’s phenomenological experience. It is this author’s assertion that the mythmaking power of both ruins and industry has a significant impact on the 
meaning and narrative of industrial ruins in parks. Without question, the 
set of relationships between myth, meaning and narratives of industrial 
ruins in contemporary parks is complex and variable. However, an open 
discussion of the problems of these parks, as well as the differences and 
relationships between industry, industrial ruins and parks containing industrial ruins, has the potential to inform future designs of parks built 
among industrial ruins. 
There are three primary problems of contemporary industrial ruin parks. 
Each of these problems is linked with the passive pleasure-seeking role of 
park visitors (a phenomenon over which designers have little control, but 
with which they must be familiar).
1. Ruins of industry and manufacturing work almost like follies in 
these parks, much as ruins functioned in traditional eighteenth-century English gardens. Structures left behind on post-industrial sites tend 
to be grand and impressive, but usually ambiguous in their purpose. In 
parks, both in Europe and the USA, industrial ruins typically act as an attraction and backdrop to recreational activity. This is a problem because it 
oversimplifies the complex history of such places. The best-known example of a contemporary industrial ruin park is Landscape Park Duisburg-
North in Germany. The structures of the former Thyssen Steelworks serve 
as climbing walls and swim tanks for scuba practice (Fig. 1). What the edifices’ functions were in the past is veiled by their contemporary use or 
state. This is a problem in most contemporary industrial ruin parks.

Significance
The rise of industrial society is arguably the most profound ecological and 
social event in contemporary history. Industrialization has allowed human society to alter landscapes on continental scales and affect the earth’s 
climate system. The rise of capitalist industrialization began a process of 
economic and social transformations that continues to define societal and 
geopolitical dynamics. We are among the first generation to deal with the 
monumental artifacts of this global upheaval. A basic assumption of this 
investigation is that parks containing industrial ruins inherently offer 
cultural narratives about history and our portrayal of history. In our efforts to reclaim industrial sites, we implicitly, if not explicitly, rely on or 
assign industrial ruins to serve as unbiased and authentic records. Because they can appear as neutral historical objects that provide an unfiltered or uninterpreted record, ruins often hold a particularly powerful 
sway over the historical context of place.
In our most basic consideration of ruins, they inform us about the 
past. The pyramids reveal the rituals and social structure of Ancient Egypt, 
Mayan ruins offer clues to a complex culture and its mysterious demise, 
and ancient Roman aqueducts and roads are evidence of imperial conquest 
and ingenuity. But as with all things historical, thinking of ruins as simply neutral remnants allows them to be interpreted imprecisely. Ever paradoxical, industrial ruins offer lessons in history but they can also distort 
the past. They warn of future demise while also commemorating a culture’s accomplishments. The ruins of industry exist in these conflicting 
contexts as artistic objects, enduring mementoes of the past that guide 
how we view present and future landscapes.

This tension between interpretation and the multiplicities of history increases with the growing popularity of industrial ruins as settings 
for parks. Post-industrial sites are some of the most available vacant large 
spaces in today’s cities worldwide. In many instances, the vacant industrial landscapes reflect their roles in fueling both the initial growth of nineteenth-century cities and the environmental legacies that have prevented 
their redevelopment post-abandonment. Consequently many post-industrial sites lie within or near the urban core and on waterways that once 
accessed industrial ports. Following environmental cleanup procedures 
that can require top soil capping, post-industrial sites are key candidates 
for large urban park spaces in Europe and North America. 
In landscape design literature there is a welcome focus on post-industrial landscape reclamation. Much of the research to date has centered on 
an environmental engineering approach to reclaiming former industrial sites, something which has allowed the landscape professions to make 
important progress on remediation techniques and restoration ecology. 
Unfortunately, the design discussion has been regarded with less urgency than the engineering imperative. As more industrial ruin parks are designed and built, this is an important time to examine some of the problems and opportunities that they provide. 
No industrial ruin park treats ruins exactly like the next; there is a 
spectrum of narrative treatments. American architect and author Hugh 
Hardy writes about this, describing one end of the spectrum, in which ruins sit as grand structures but with no contextual explanation: “… there 
are no visual traces of what brought these buildings into being” (2005: 
6). At the other end are examples of keeping the factory intact, even with 
some artifacts of production maintained (Hardy 2005: 6). However, given the scale and complexity of industry it is important to ask if its ruins, 
treated as they are along this continuum, can serve as ‘honest’ and contextually complex historical artifacts. 
Roles of ruins
Ruins as used in Classical landscape design were part of the Picturesque 
aesthetic in which mystery, melancholy and the sublime were intrinsic. 
We see the same emotionally attractive aesthetic devices being used today, 
intentionally or not, in industrial ruin parks.

Melancholy
Ruins, including industrial ruins, symbolize the cycles of life and death 
and the inevitable demise of all life and societies. Christopher Woodward’s 
In Ruins (2001) describes how ruins serve as an example of the fragility of 
life and the relentlessness of time. In his own words: “When we contemplate ruins, we contemplate our own future. To statesmen, ruins predict 
the fall of Empires, and to philosophers the futility of mortal man’s aspirations. To a poet, the decay of a monument represents the dissolution of 
the individual ego in the flow of Time; to a painter or architect, the fragments of a stupendous antiquity call into question the purpose of their 
art. Why struggle with a brush or chisel to create the beauty of wholeness 
when far greater works have been destroyed by Time?” (2) 
This idea of mortality is linked with the notion of hauntedness. The 
past is evident in the ruins, but the story remains incomplete. Perhaps, 
one could speculate, the act of engaging with ruins entails an interaction with ‘ghosts’, with elements of the past that are absent but evident 
as traces. With our imaginations, we the viewers fill in the blanks, gleaning stories from whatever artifacts are left behind. This makes almost 
any ruin seem melancholy. Ruins are about a life destroyed. Hugh Hardy 
writes, “The hypnotic attraction of places like Bethlehem Steel in Pennsylvania, or the former Thyssen Steelworks in Duisburg, Germany, stems 
from the romance of their abandonment—the saga of what was once so 
mighty brought low” (2005: 32).

The attraction of ruins, whether ancient, recent, monumental, vernacular or industrial, reflects complex cultural aesthetics. This is demonstrated in the deep history of ruins used in landscape and garden design. 
Most noteworthy and popular was their use in eighteenth-century English gardens. Indeed, ruins played such important roles that fakes were 
even manufactured for Classical gardens. In the style of the Picturesque, 
these gardens employed ruins along with follies to tell stories about the 
decline of empires or to evoke popular myths, but also to create suggestions of past ages and antiquity. Ruins in this Picturesque context were 
used to create emotionally appealing attractions as well as intellectually 
interesting narratives. In today’s industrial ruin parks, one could argue 
that ruins are being used as follies in much the same way they were used 
in eigh-teenth- and nineteenth-century Europe. Like the ruins at the English estate, Stowe, or the French garden Le Désert de Retz, rusting manufacturing structures built in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries stand in some parks as aesthetic attractions and icons among rolling 
lawns, soft-ened by garden plantings. 
Industrial ruins
How has the emotional appeal of Classical ruins as described above influenced the way designers perceive industrial ruins? Just as ancient ruins were enjoyed in the Classical gardens, industrial ruins are enjoyed for 
their mysterious and sublime qualities, as well as their melancholic (what 
many also call ‘gritty’) qualities. Granted, what these places mean to people is difficult to discern, influence or even understand. To be sure, one 
person’s mystery is another’s gloom; one person’s melancholy is another’s creepiness; and one person’s sublime prospect could be another’s eyesore. It is fair to assume that every individual might have a different perception of industrial ruins based on their prior experience, interests, age, 
and countless other influences. In what follows, perceptions of industrial ruins are derived from articles on and critiques of built or designed industrial ruin parks.

Industrial ruin parks
The meanings of industrial ruin parks are not influenced only by the perception of industry; also and more generally, their meanings are influenced by the ways in which the past is memorialized in Western culture. 
J.B. Jackson (1980) explains how, especially in American culture, ruins of 
vernacular structures and landscapes act more as a reflection of contemporary values than as any kind of monument to an accurate history. They 
represent idealized notions of day-to-day life in the past. He explains that 
traditional monuments are large structures that commemorate an event 
or a person. They mark a social contract between the past and the future, 
guiding actions and obligations with history. Distinct from traditional 
monuments, the ruins of manufacturing do not memorialize a specific 
event, act or person. Instead, ruins like this (in J.B. Jackson’s words), “[are] 
celebrating a different past … a vernacular past, a golden age where there 
are no dates or names, simply a sense of the way it used to be, history as 
the chronicle of everyday existence” (Jackson 1980: 94-95). From this perspective, the ruins of manufacturing used as a backdrop in park design 
are monuments – more symbolic of our culture’s reverence for technology, 
power and scale rather than of any desire to remember particular people, 
acts or consequences of the industrial age. The power of American perceptions of industry and the ways Americans commemorate the past are such 
that post-industrial parks may not be able to represent anything more 
than an idealized past, at least in the United States of America. In another 
study it would be interesting to examine the parallel perceptions of Europeans. How do differences in modes of commemoration affect the ways 
we design sites in an historical context?
Hardy also finds and describes the sublime in industrial ruins: “Often, 
their gigantic size and startling juxtapositions also give them a sinister 
beauty that speaks of prodigious human effort and material transformation wrought by great physical forces. Even silent, they resound with 
power.” (2005: 32). 
Perceptions of industry, industrial ruins, and industrial ruins 
in contemporary parks
Given the similarities between ancient and industrial ruins, there are also 
important differences that distinguish them. The perception of a ruin, it 
is fair to assume, is not only aesthetic but also has to do with the meaning 
and purpose of the structure before it went into ruin. 
Industry 
David Nye, in American Technological Sublime (1994) describes the American 
perceptions of industry being visceral and emotional, where one is transfixed by the engagement of such power. The emotional reactions of individuals also make up a collective experience, which is what he and others 
call the industrial sublime. In this case, Nye argues, the industrial sublime leads to a certain social cohesion. People can be unified by their sense 
of awe as they respond to the machines and structures of manufacturing. 
Beyond making a visceral impact with its powerful physical presence, industry has also always been symbolic of progress and economic force. The 
industrial sublime is a useful concept to understand how the powerful attraction of industry and its ruins is partly a social construct, having more 
to do with the “politics of perception” than reality or the multiplicities of 
history (Nye 1994: xxvi). 
Industrial ruins
In an interesting examination of the potential and meaning of industrial ruins, professor and author Tim Edensor reveals that there can be multiple readings of industrial ruins (Edensor 2005). The conventional notion 
of industrial ruins is that of dereliction, social decline and waste. In Industrial Ruins (2005), Edensor also sheds light on alternative meanings of 
these ruins as repositories of memory, generators of creativity, and places 
of habitation and performance. 
Many would call the abandoned structures of industry wastelands and 
voids. Historically, they have been seen as negative, blighted areas, ripe 
for improvement and reclamation. However, there are others like Edensor 
who see post-industrial sites as more ambiguous. Post-industrial wastelands are seen by some as having a different kind of potential – not so 
much as clean slates, but as places of inspiration and unforeseen potential. Instead of waste, one might see industrial ruins as a natural and necessary condition for a healthy city.

Neutral ruins 
What are the effects of monumentalizing industrial ruins as simple relics 
representing a general ideal of bygone days? If industrial ruin parks represent the broad notion of a past era, then they are historically neutral. If industrial ruin parks are attractive for their myths and character, then the 
historical authenticity remains secondary to the pleasure ground principle. In this view, landscape architects are missing an opportunity to portray more complex accounts of industrial history. Industrial ruin parks 
rely more on the romance of ruins and offer an oversimplified account of 
history. It is here that we can begin to approach the question posed in the 
introduction. When something is viewed as emotionally attractive, that is, 
sublime, melancholy, and haunted, but also symbolizing a general sense 
of the old days, what is lost, and what are the consequences?

Synthesis
Through understanding the roots and appeal of industrial ruins we may 
change design approaches and considerations for parks containing industrial ruins. When we compare industry, its ruins, and parks containing 
industrial ruins (Table 1), the three industrial conditions have different 
meanings relative to their state-of-being and relative to the ways they 
operate in their environments. However, each of these industrial statesof-being (industry, industrial ruin, or park) operate (or used to operate) 
as icons, emotional objects, settings, workplaces, environmental agents, 
characters and predictors of fate. Table 1 illustrates how they serve similar functions but how each state-of-being is also distinct from the others. 
This synthesis is based upon this author’s reading of the claims and insights of Nye, Edensor and Jackson. 
Nye argues that the elements of industry (acting as icons) created a 
sense of social cohesion through the progress and ingenuity they symbolized. Edensor and others show how the industrial ruin can symbolize the 
certainty of one’s own demise. The industrial ruin park may also cultivate 
a certain social cohesion – or at least a perceived cohesion through a broad 
sense of nostalgia and pride in a community’s history and melancholy at 
its decline. The pulsating machines of industry, as a setting, have represented a workplace (and prosperity), and correspondingly the ruins as a 
setting assume the role of representing economic decline. Subsequently, 
the industrial ruin park is a pleasure ground, a place where citizens go to 
play, relax and enjoy. For workers, the machines of industry have represented a certain measure of job security. Industrial ruins, with their incomplete traces, graffiti, broken furniture and rusting time clocks evoke 
ghosts of former workers and the social life of the factories. The industrial 
ruin park, in the spirit of traditional democratic parks, is intended to be 
a great equalizing space for all, and in some cases, one that honors workers (as a collective) and their memory. Industry has had various impacts 
on the environment. The ruins of industry make marginalized environments apparent with their disorder, evidence of decay and traces of waste 
left behind. In contrast, the park containing ruins appears cleaned and 
in a state of healing. Partly because of their size, industrial sites project a 
strong character into the surrounding landscape. The character of an industrial site while in production is vibrant, teeming with human activity, and energetic. The machines of industry seem alive. The machine in a

“A Tour of the Monuments of Passaic, New Jersey” is much more than an 
observant walk through an industrial town. We can see that it is both an 
ironic gaze at a crumbling industrial spectacle and a rendering of the influence of the beholding eye. It is the unevenness of interpretation that 
defines the paradox inherent in ruins. While attracting visitors and setting a stage that rests on history, Smithson shows that representing ruins in specific ways can depoliticize history. The aesthetic pleasure of ruins overwhelms the authenticity of historical accounts. 
J.B. Jackson (1980) suggests that in American culture we have three 
stages that make a place historical. First is the reflection on the ‘good old 
days’, then there is a period of decay, and ultimately it is the present culture that shapes history according to contemporary values and interests. 
We tend to think of ruins as a potential means to carry history through 
to the present. But when history is presented in the form of a tourist attraction it is at risk of being “loaded in favor of the present which is not 
shown as an extension of the past, but as a replacement for it” (MacCannell 
1976: 88). Representations of the past act more as a mirror of present culture and the ways we consume landscapes than as depictions of complex 
histories, or even acknowledgements of the unknown or forgotten. 
The gap between the real and the ideal comes into question when nostalgia is the attraction. Does the Forbidden City teach a lesson about the 
problems of corruption in a dynastic form of government, or is it more a 
spectacle of wealth and mystery? Whose story is told at Landscape Park 
Duisburg-North? The park’s industrial remnants, now serving as climbing walls, fishponds, gardens, theaters and playgrounds, are intended to 
remind visitors of history, and they undoubtedly do. But the questions remain: Whose history is on display? Where are the gaps and what is embellished? Emotional appeal can transcend cognitive understanding of history, and nostalgia obscures social and environmental truths, unknowns 
or misconceptions. 
Authenticity or accuracy in communication is a relevant contemporary issue. The American version of reality is more flexible than it used to 
be. Today we accept tailored reality. We watch reality television knowing 
it is not real but produced and edited. We allow Las Vegas’s follies of Piazza San Marco as an acceptable (and maybe even more exciting) version of 
reality. Theme parks provide new myths and revised versions of old myths. 
Like the replica of the Eiffel Tower at Las Vegas or the miniaturized Pantheon at Stourhead, the structures of dead industries also act as mythmakers and representations of the past. 
This is not to say that Richard Haag in his creation of Gas Works Park, 
or Peter Latz in his design of Landscape Park created spaces that are intentionally misleading, made only to attract visitors. Instead, in this contemporary condition of expansive views of reality we can assume that 
mythologizing the past is probably a contingency of capturing and communicating history, especially when attraction is part of the goal. However, we need to understand that, not unlike the constructed ‘ruins’ at Stowe, 
the physical remains of manufacturing tell stories. The silent smokestacks 
and polished fossils of grain elevators, with their sublime nature and romantic associations, attract and act as scenery in a fabled industrial history. Like any staged or themed place, these ruins can become caricatures of 
themselves. As with Smithson’s characterization of his walk through Passaic, the phenomenological experience of interacting with the industrial 
ruin environment combined with contemporary notions of industry activates the imagination.
The physical and the social are linked. Space makes a statement about 
the past and about the people who built and worked in these places. Professor of architecture, urbanism and American studies Dolores Hayden 
has discussed how there is an important story embedded in every industrial site, and that it is the social history that can turn an abandoned 
rail yard, steel mill or landfill into “a potential resource for projects concerned with larger public meaning in the urban landscape” (Hayden 1995: 
22). This is where the problem of meaning resides. The contemporary genre does not allow the structures to be ambiguous ruins but instead, partly 
(and ironically) out of designers’ efforts to make them more legible, even 
the most complex industrial ruin parks tend to operate as themed follies, 
defining social histories in ways that make them almost rigid in their 
meaning or in their potential for interpretation. 
New roles for ruins
“The debris of industrial culture teaches us not the necessity of submitting 
to historical catastrophe, but the fragility of the social order that tells us 
this catastrophe is necessary. The crumbling of the monuments that were 
built to signify the immortality of civilization becomes proof, rather, of 
its transiency.” (Buck-Morss 1989: 170)

In my research I found that Peter Latz’s work, especially in Landscape 
Park Duisburg-North, presents good examples of places that begin to find 
a middle ground between theme park industrial ruins and totally unembellished (and unusable yet narratively complex) industrial ruins. At 
Landscape Park Duisburg-North the ruins are left standing in a somewhat 
crumbling yet preserved state, while the planting design and program 
help to highlight the complex meanings and history of the steelworks the 
park replaced. The difference between Latz’s work at Duisburg-North and 
other well-known industrial ruin parks is his approach, the ‘syntactical 
planning approach’, a design process that layers information with ‘timeless’ aspects of a place. It is a design approach that requires extensive and 
deep knowledge about a place. Latz views “landscape as a complex structure of meaning and information strata, requiring a complex approach to 
the design” (Weilacher 2008: 109). For Landscape Park Duisburg-North he 
examined the site and designed it in layers, with a special focus on historical information. This was not an historical preservation strategy per se 
but a design based upon a deep analysis and integration of four separate 
concepts, with an overall strategy of using minimal intervention. History is revealed through information and subtle juxtapositions. In my view, 
the park struggles with contradictions between recreation and memory, 
and is still politically neutral on the history of steel manufacturing, but 
nonetheless Latz’s approach seems promising for future industrial ruin 
parks that can be both attractive and informative. 
Latz’s syntactical approach is a very useful reference for designers who 
want to work with such complex sites, and yet the perfect solution might 
not exist for parks containing industrial ruins. One primary goal of my research has been to develop a set of design guidelines for industrial ruins 
parks. Given the broad range of contexts and site conditions, the recommendations must be a combination of a summary of the perceptions of industrial ruins and suggestions for designers based upon these perceptions 
and what I see as promising approaches by Latz.

I hope that by now the reader appreciates how perceptions of industrial 
ruins are powerful instruments in shaping the narratives around industrial history. The aim here has been to explore the meanings and narratives of parks containing industrial ruins, and to link those meanings to 
perceptions of both ruins and industry, in order to shed light on ways to 
improve the design of these parks.

The guide above is meant to provide a framework for designing industrial ruin parks. It is a first attempt draft toward my goal to support 
a movement forward in the ways we design post-industrial landscapes, 
away from spaces of commodification toward spaces of imagination and 
inspiration. Sites needing reclamation are arguably the most abundant 
and ripe places for park design. To support and strengthen this guide, a 
case study of contemporary industrial ruin parks – examining them for 
their timelessness, their narratives and the design strategies used on them

move ahead toward future industrial ruin parks that not only engage the 
multiplicities of history but also integrate into present day needs and perceptions. Everything must be forgotten someday, but I believe the responsibilities of contemporary landscape designers include remembering, not 
ameliorating, industrial pasts.

In my view, after considering the current discussion on industrial ruins, 
there are six primary actions that can be taken by landscape architects to 
reveal and activate the historical narrative of post-industrial sites:
1. In designing parks on industrial ruin sites, landscape architects 
must treat the ruins primarily as historical evidence and secondarily as 
aesthetic devices.
2. Landscape architects must be aware that industrial ruins reflect 
present-day values and the perceptions listed above more than historical facts, and treat this as a critical point of departure. Consider carefully 
the physical and perceptual consequences of designing a park among historical evidence. Take care not to over-design for today’s audience, but instead consider the future audience and aim for a design in which the complex narrative will last. 
3. The historical context of industrial ruins is critical. Allow history to 
act as the primary contextual and conceptual layer in parks that contain 
industrial ruins. Program, aesthetics, and function can follow the historical context. If the historical narrative is used as an afterthought or as a 
secondary concept it will be more difficult to retrieve in the future. As in 
the case of archaeological sites, once the evidence is destroyed or removed 
critical aspects of the story are irretrievable. Whenever historical facts can 
be conveyed through the design, they should be considered carefully.
