HS VL 01: The History and Theory of the City as a Project 
 
What is “the city” that we live and work in? 
- 
The city is not a self-organized landscape/organsim (so Avermaete) 
- 
A man-made material and spatial project, so actions and design made by men. The 
city is the result of a combination of willful acts that can be uncovered, mapped, 
understood, and explained. Cities are no incidental occurrences; they are the 
material results of intentional decisions and processes. 
- 
Politicians, investors and citizens, architects and urban designers all play a crucial role 
in the development of a city. 
 
Forma Ubis Romae and the Logic of the City of Rome 
The Forma Ubis Romae is a massive marble map of the city of Rome created between 203 
and 211 AC. It consists of 180 stone tables, which together describe and understand the 
logics of the city of Rome. A very detailed description of private and public parts of Rome, 
help us understand the social groups, economic situations, etc. 
 
Vitruvius and the Ten Books of Architecture 
Vitruvius talks about the different components of the city and how to construct them. He 
also shows certain typologies of houses and water systems, as well as defense of the city. 
Cities are always a combination of: 
• Building and construction 
• Ideas and concepts, as to say a projection of reasoning and theory 
o Fabrica and ratiocinatio 
Architects have been trying to understand the logics of Architecture since the early days. 
Architecture is made up of different disciplines and various skills. Architecture exists through 
fabrica and ratiocinatio. Fabrica is the continuous and routine practice made out of matter 
by hand. The outcome is what was intended to be. Ratiocinatio is what shows how and to 
what degree things have been made with skill and calculation. 
 
Alberti’s Idea of The City 
- Alberti suggests that the city can be seen as a bigger set 
of ideas and that every part of the city makes a whole. He 
compares the construction of a house with a miniature 
city. He says that almost everything relevant to the 
establishment of a city must be considered when building 
a house; it should easily and conveniently create a 
peaceful, tranquil, and refined life. 
- He describes the city in a circular description made of 
silhouettes (walls) and all kinds of dots which were the 
bigger monuments in the city. 
- 
He considers architecture as that which does not yet exist and must be conceived 
through a series of rules and standards. 
- 
He defines architecture as a project of building the city, as establishing a relationship 
between the repetition of that which already exists and the production of the new 
 
Alberti’s plan was used a century later but in comparison, Pope Sixtus V focuses on the 
religious monuments of the city, creating big axes between the main religious monuments.

Urban Design as Engagement 
Urban design is about engaging with certain characteristics and issues. It is about engaging 
with a certain condition in a particular situation and not about creating an ideal. It is a 
“reflective conversation with the situation”. There are 11 characteristics that need to be 
considered: 
 
• The ground: topography plays an important role in urban design, as it influences the 
form of the city and the streets. à Examples 
o Constantinople à it’s form and organization is a reaction to its hilly site, with 
the waters of the Bosporus to the South and the harbor of the Golden Horn to 
the East, determined by its ground = topography and the river surrounding it 
o Urban layout as well as their building typologies, also depend upon the 
characteristics of the soil, as the foundations of Amsterdam, the modest 
profile of Berlin and the high-rise character of Manhattan illustrate. Time and 
again, the height of the water table and the bearing capacity of the ground 
determine the architecture of the city. 
o Italian cities are situated in higher topographical points in order to properly 
defend from sieges. Sometimes the topography is easy like Las Vegas, 
sometimes it is more complex. In order to understand cities we have to 
understand the topographical conditions like in Scollay Square. 
 
Also, the soil plays an important role. 
o Amsterdam: houses built on wooden poles because of the bad soil 
o Berlin: sandy ground, thus not too high 
o New York: different building heights due to different grounds 
o Lisbon: design changed after earthquake à urban blocks and grid 
o Tokyo: after earthquake, houses don’t touch and there are no parting walls, 
although built densely, they adapted to circumstances and develop new 
principles 
 
• Materials: urban design is a way of engaging with materials that are locally sourced 
and produced. Local materials are important for cities and provide a city image. à 
Examples: 
o Baked bricks in Colombia, which determine the urban tissue à lots of selfmade houses 
o England: limestone is used in Bath, materiality of the historical city “Royal 
Crescent” binds to the new city. 
 
• Climate: urban design is how one can move in the city considering the climate. 
(Vitruvius and Hippocrates) Before it was thought that laying the direction of streets 
and alleys on the lines of division between 2 winds would be better for the climate of 
the city. Layout of the city is influenced by the climate. à Example 
o Montreal has all kinds of urban functions underground, so one can move from 
one place to the other underground because it is very hot in summer and very 
cold in winter à underground path-system 
o Bologna there are various Portici, which are galleries that provide cover from 
rain and the sun.

The Project of History and Theory 
The city is not only a project for architects and urban designers, but also for historians and 
theoreticians. Historians and theoreticians have adopted particular methodological and 
conceptual vantage points to approach what we call the city. The history of modern urban 
planning has the following 3 pedigrees: 
 
• The city as experience: people write about the city basing on their own experiences 
of image, sounds and smells. The city does not appear as a back- drop, but as a new 
product of civilization; as a künstlicher lebensraum, which touches and influences the 
mentality of the people. 
 
• The city as organism: rather than the form, the functioning of the city is central. The 
built and non-built, etc. all function together. The city is one big organism, one big 
ecology, where buildings, nature, etc. live together. 
This model became popular in French and Scottish circles in the 18th century, 
especially in the work of the biologist and planner Patrick Geddes. He introduced a 
whole new vocabulary to talk about the city in terms of heart, lungs, veins and tissue. 
This paradigm approaches the city as a spatial and social body, with such a complex 
structure and organization, and such a capacity for growth and adaptation, that it 
resembles a biological environment. 
The organic paradigm opens up the possibility for discourses on the illness of the 
body, as well as on its cure and healing. 
 
• The city as political archive: Historians looked at the city as a political archive of laws, 
rights, and privileges, which together form the legitimation for the political ideas that 
develop the city. In this perspective, written sources prevail over buildings and 
objects. 
 
An Architectural History 
Camillo Sitte looked at cities and drew plans of them to try to understand what made them 
so qualitative. He looked at the relationship between monuments, squares, etc., and he 
looked at the aesthetic of the city. 
Werner Hegemann & Elbert Peets looked at the composing elements of the city, such as 
squares throughout history, streets, passages, galleries, etc. 
Saverion Muratori looked at different examples like Venice very closely. He watched them 
evolve, as the architectural history of a city is very important to be able to understand the 
transformation of cities. 
Aldo Rossi focuses on building templates that change the history of the city and tries to 
understand how bigger entities build the city.

HS VL 02: Of Rituals, Water and Mud: The Urban Revolution in 
Mesopotamia and the Indus Valley 
 
- 
Geographer Gordon Childe says that the urban revolution took place as from the 9th 
millennia BC in 6 regions simultaneously and independently; Mesoamerica (middle 
America), Ades, Egypt, Mesopotamia; Indus and China. 
 
- 
Fast changes happened, which had to do with new social groupings and settlement 
forms based on ideas of economical and agricultural exchange but also defense. With 
these new social groups came what we could call a city. 
 
- 
Through a combination of migration and trade this new “modus vivendi“ expanded 
and developed into large complex social and cultural systems characterized by 
o Political organization 
o Class inequalities 
o The emergence of a new artefact in the territory: the city. 
 
- 
The emergence of these new settlements has to do with a Neolithic (R)evolution: 
o which represents technological changes, such as technological irrigation, the 
invention of new tools related to special materials like obsidian, leading to 
new concept of time (cyclical) linked to the growth of harvest (faster 
harvesting). 
o These technological innovations lead to the domestication of wild plants, 
animals, and agriculture, which can now be cultivated near one’s house and 
don’t have to be gathered and hunted anymore, leading to a more sedentary 
way of life. 
 
All these aspects led to an urban revolution respectively to different consequences such as: 
• Resource surplus: more harvest than needed. Resources can now be kept and stored 
for a group of people in the long run (=invested in settlements). 
• Social surplus: because people now live in groups in small settlements, they can 
create safety for one another as well as reciprocity. 
• Cultural surplus: through the exchange of cultural knowledge and skills, new 
artefacts, new crafts, new settlements etc. emerge. 
 
The settlements of Göbekli Tepe and Çatal Hüyük in nowadays Turkey and iran cannot be 
qualified as full-fledged cities, but they illustrate how innovative proto-urban setttlemts 
emerged that were organized according to new principles. 
 
Göbekli Tepe, Turkey 9000 BC – (first urban settlement) 
Exemplifies how the new sedentary way of life brought a new conception of time (cyclical), 
which was expressed in the prominence of religious buildings in the organization of the city.

HS VL 03: The Idea of the Polis: Rome, Greece and Beyond 
 
Greek Aegean cities or city states emerged out of architectural development, so 
development of settlements, already in the Bronze Age. They were mainly autocratic 
settlements. In the Mycenaean Period, there were many settlements and most of them 
didn’t become big cities except for Athens. 
 
The colonial empires of Greece and Rome were essential to propel the development of 
urban design: 
- 
They initiated a fundamental exchange of ideas across regions and cultures 
- 
They raised the issue of how to govern territories and people. 
 
à Urban design was in both cases a ‘civilizing mission’ that was as much about bringing a 
specific way of life to distant places, as about conforming people to the norms and forms of 
the empire. 
 
In Greece: 
- 
The polis or city-state expressed a specific way of life and was strongly related to the 
question of politics. (Trade routes as a connection about knowledge in greek world) 
 
In the Roman Empire: 
- 
The city was conceived as the main unit of administration as well as the prime site of 
contact between the ruler and ruled. 
 
The form of the urban layout was in early times not intensively discussed by the Greeks and 
Romans. When philosophers as Plato and Aristotle discuss the ideal city, their main concern 
were Social and political issues. 
 
While the material and spatial characteristics of the city are taken as self-evident. It is only in 
Hellenistic times (336 BC - 30 BC) that technical literature on urban design starts to emerge. 
This is complemented in Roman times by more cultured discussions of authors like Pliny and 
Frontinus. 
 
An important impetus for the cities of Archaic Greece (1000 BC) was the colonial contact 
with the ideas and realities of the ancient East. The idea of the Greek polis became 
materialized through the design of several public institutions and figures. 
à Agora is the central figure of the Greek polis. 
- 
A figure used by greek designer for building new citys as a center (religious, political 
and cultural function) 
- 
Originally no more than a large open space the urban figure of the agora would soon 
turn into a cluster of buildings and open spaces including the meeting place for the 
city council (bouleuterion), the offices for the magristrates, temples and altars, law 
courts and covered halls or stoa. 
- 
Two important elements: Temple for religion // Center of Politics aka auditorium was 
the bouleuterion. 
- 
Stoa as a sort of connecting element between religious and political entities in one 
urban formation, where the discussion could go on. Stoa richly decorated or limited, 
and big (looks like an arcade)

The definition of the Middle Ages as “Dark Ages” (as Petrarch termed them), bridging the two enlightened 
periods of classical antiquity and the Renaissance, has long been outmoded. This historical period, be- ginning 
with the fall of the Roman Empire as a result of raids by northern European tribes of “Vikings” in the late 
antiquity of the fifth and sixth centuries and continuing until the advent of the so-called Italian and English 
renaissances, or rebirths of classical learning, in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, spans an enormous, long 
amount of time. It not only affected the classical inheritance in the west, but also included eastern Europe, the 
Middle East, Asia, as well as North and Central America. 
The Middle Ages saw the birth of Muhammad (c. 570–632) and his foundation of Islam in the seventh century 
which led to the imperial conflict between East and West in the eleventh and twelfth centuries. In Western 
Europe the foundations for modern nationalism and modern law were laid. The Middle Ages were also the time 
of the Crusades which triggered the development of the concept of chivalry and the knight, but also cultural 
encounter, exchange and conflict. The need for defense would become an important foundational logic for 
medieval towns, and the castle emerged as a military outpost, whether in northern Africa, during the Crusades, 
or in Wales, in the eleventh century. 
By the twelfth century, with the centralization of the one (Catholic) Church in Europe, came a new architecture 
for the cathedral—the Gothic—to replace the older Romanesque architecture and thereby to manifest the 
Church’s role in the community in a mate- rial way as well as in spiritual and political ways. Out of the cathedral 
and the convent emerged the medieval institution of the university, but also new public institutions such as 
hospitals, orphanages and elder houses. 
Around the same time, cities rose up in eastern and western Europe as a consequence of trade. New 
commercial patterns, such as the Hanseatic League propelled the necessity for new economic centers 
to accompany the development of a bourgeoisie, or middle class. These cities required new infrastructures that 
were adequate for trade and shipping. During the Middle Ages most cities were unique in plan, function, and in 
social structure. One of the most defining features was their degree of autonomy. This ranged from the citystate, which was in most respects sovereign and independent, to the very restricted independence enjoyed by 
most small towns.

The notion of “Rinascimento”, or re-birth, was introduced by the Italian architect Vasari to describe the renewed cultural interest in 
Antiquity that had its origin in Italy of the 14th century, flowered during the subsequent two centuries and spread to the rest of Europe. It 
involved thinkers, writers, painters, architects with renown names such as Dante (1265-1321), Petrarch (1304-1374) and Boccacio (1313-
1376). This new interest did not emerge in isolation. It was paired with new practices and theories of the ‚autonomous state‘ with its public 
servants and politics of ‚reason‘, the growth of international trade and the introduction of printing. 
Renaissance Urban Design emerged in Italy, but rapidly spread to France in the 16th century, Britain in the 17th century and America in the 
late 17th and 18th centuries. During this era, it also became a feature of cities in European colonies in the Americas, Africa, India, the Far 
East and Australia. In contradistinction to medieval urban development which gradually added subsidiary parts, the Renaissance City was 
conceived as a centralized unit. Three main principles characterize Renaissance urban design. First, its spatial unity which is based on 
rational design and design order. Second, the finite nature of space. The urban spaces that were designed by 15th century architects were 
precisely calculated and visually controlled, giving the observer a clear idea of structure and an awareness of the spatial rhythm around it. 
The spaces of the Renaissance city are enclosed and at rest. Third, the urban spaces are geometric, measured and proportioned. The 
Renaissance city complies to a measured order. 
With the perfection of gunpowder and the cannon (invented early in the 14th Century) the defense of cities became a new concern in 
urban design. Canons were responsible for the fall of the great city of Constantinople, in 1453, marking the beginning of a new age in the 
history of military fortifications. In the Renaissance period the fortified city would be brought to a new level of sophistication. Vertical 
urban walls were left and new systems were invented that could enlarge the horizontal distance between the city perimeter and the 
attacking artillery. Star-shaped or stellar fortifications with intricate successions of interdependent bastions would become a main 
occurrence in many European cities. 
City and State were often interrelated in Renaissance thinking, even interchangeable concepts. The city was seen as an expression of the 
‚state‘ and literary utopias accompanied these conceptions of the ‚Ideal City‘. These literary utopias were intellectual experiments that 
focused on social revolution. Also Renaissance architects such as Alberti and Filarete contributed to the thinking of the ‚citta ideale‘. 
Partially based on the rediscovery of the writings of the classical Roman architect Vitruvius, they sparked an era of ideal city preoccupation 
in urban design.

In his 1845 publication The Condition of the Working Class in England (Die Lage der arbeitenden Klasse in 
England) Friedrich Engels depicted a new reality that had emerged in the second half of the 18th and the first 
part of the 19th century. Following the invention of the steam engine and its implementation in industry and 
transport, the so-called ‘Industrial Revolution’ started. This not only forged the progress of faster transport of 
people and goods between cities and countries, but also drastically changed the way that work was organized 
and propelled a new expansion of cities. Engels, however, illustrated that the Industrial Revolution was not only 
a harbinger of progress, but also worsened the condition of workers. He illustrated that in large industrial cities 
such as Manchester and Liverpool, mortality from disease was four times that in the surrounding countryside, 
and mortality from convulsions was ten times as high. These aggravating conditions of the laboring class would 
provoke reactions with thinkers and designers, who would start to reflect from various angles upon the norms 
and forms of ‘the city of labor’. Their approaches can generally be divided in three categories. A first group of 
architects and thinkers can be la- belled as ‘radical utopians’, because they maintained that there was no way 
out of the crisis and that human civilization had to be built from the very beginning somewhere out of the 
industrial society. Thinkers like Charles Fourier and Robert Owen preferred to conceive a radically new 
foundation for ideal social and environmental conditions. 
A second group of thinkers and designers believed in the possible improvement of social and environmental 
conditions through legislation and large-scale public works. The French Georges-Eugène Haussmann is an 
example of this approach. As from 1853 he launched a large project of public works in Paris, which aimed 
to the end of absolute liberalism and „laissez faire” in order to control development, as a common interest. 
Hausmmann attempted to create a new model for the city and to demonstrate, that the city was not 
condemned to death – as an opposition to the anti- urban ideology of the social utopias. Haussmann’s 
approach would mark the beginning of an urban tradition of transformation of existing cities. 
A third reaction to the industrial revolution and its effects on the architecture of the city came from thinkers 
like William Morris and John Ruskin. They located the ground of the bad condition of the laboring classes in the 
alienation of the worker from the result of his work and maintained that engines and mass production should 
play only a subordinated role. The ethics and aesthetics of handicraft represented in their view the remedy 
from the misery of the industrial society and the mass production. Their model of the city of labor was based 
on the believe in the continuity of tradition and the predominance of handicrafts.
