Finding Meaning in the 
Postmodern City
From the middle of the 1960s, a profound transformation 
took place in urban design culture. A broad-based reaction 
to modern urbanism emerged, which not only attacked the 
theories of the Charter of Athens (1933), but also responded 
to the proposals by groups like Team 10 and the Japanese 
Metabolists. Detractors were critical of the tenets of the modern movement, and especially of the architectural and urban 
products of these tenets. They criticized modern neighborhoods and cities for failing to fulfil the promise of providing 
qualitative and emancipatory dwelling environments. 
In the US, the activist and critic Jane Jacobs launched an 
influential campaign condemning the paternalistic and distanced character of top-down planning, as well as the anonymity of the modern city, with her book, The Death and 
Life of Great American Cities: The Failure of Town Planning 
(1961). An intense critique of the ‘placeless’ character and 
the disconnection between buildings and public spaces in 
modern urbanism characterized the book Townscape (1961) 
by the British architect Gordon Cullen. In Germany, the photographer Elisabeth Niggemeyer and journalist Wolf Jobst 
Siedler published a book, Die gemordete Stadt (‘The Murdered City’), in 1964, in which they lamented the banality and 
lifelessness of the modern Berlin housing estates. As a result 
of this intense criticism, a general and widely shared view of 
modern urban design as a negative and regressive ideology 
emerged. Architects and urban designers no longer believed 
in the modern project for the city, and started to explore radically different approaches.
The City in Context
Against the background of the ongoing criticism, in 1966 the 
Italian architect Vittorio Gregotti published Il Territorio Dell’ 
Architettura, which made a plea to no longer think about new 
architecture in correspondence with the historical city, but 
rather in relation to the natural or rural territory. For Gregotti, 
the main question of urban design was how it could qualitatively locate new buildings and neighborhoods in the context of the territory. This would require that urban designers 
and architects explore ‘the form of the territory’—the topographical, cultural, and infrastructural particularities of the 
landscape—as a base for the development of new buildings, 
neighborhoods, and cities. From this perspective, urban design is understood as an act of recognizing the qualities of 
a landscape, measuring them, and revealing them through 
an architectural or urban intervention. In his project for the 
University of Calabria (1973), Gregotti illustrates how a finegrained analysis of the landscape can lead to a new form of 
urban design, in which architecture depends upon the territory and reveals its essence.
History and Typology
In Italy, another loose group of architects, which came to be 
known as neo-rationalists, started to focus on the history of 
the city as a complex relation between architectural typology 
and urban morphology. As early as 1959, the Italian architect Saverio Muratori introduced this new way of approaching the city in his Studi per una operante storia urbana di 
Venezia. Muratori described the city of Venice as an artefact 
that, throughout history, was reinterpreted and adapted by its 
architects and inhabitants. Muratori explores the rationality 
of the city through the reconstruction of the roots and transformation of architectural and urban form. He introduces the 
urban concepts of ‘type’, ‘urban tissue’ or morphology, and 
‘organism’. According to Muratori, a certain building type 
could not be understood beyond its particular application 
in an urban morphology, just as the urban morphology was 
intricately related to the wider urban organism. Muratori concluded that new urban designs had to be expressed in terms 
of types and morphologies, in relation to the urban organism. 
One of the most influential contributions came from the Italian architect Aldo Rossi. In his book L’architettura della città 
(The Architecture of the City,1966), Rossi criticized the urban design approach of the modern movement in Europe 
for ignoring the complexity of the city and its longue durée 
as an artefact. Instead, the Italian architect gives ‘urban 
artefacts’ a central place in his vision of the city and urban 
design. For Rossi, urban artefacts were not only the expression of specific social, economic, and cultural realties, they 
also had a certain formal and material ‘permanence’. Rossi 
looked upon urban artefacts as primary elements of urban 
design, because their existence contributed to the cultural 
and morphological evolution of the city. The artefact was 
both an event and a form. Aldo Rossi also conceived of the 
city as a ‘locus of collective memory’, understood as the relationship of the collective to its place—expressed in existing buildings, images, and paintings. He maintained that this 
collective memory participates in the actual transformation of 
urban space, a transformation that is always conditioned by 
whatever material realities oppose it. As a result, for Rossi, 
urban design should always be ‘analogous’, relying on past 
city forms (morphology) and building forms (typology). In his 
project for the Teatro del Mondo (1979–80), Rossi illustrated 
how a single floating building could condense the collective 
memory of Venice, and thus self-evidently complement the 
city. That analogous design offered an interesting point of 
departure to complete an urban block was illustrated in the 
housing project for IBA Berlin in 1987.
In 1966, the German architect Oswald Mathias Ungers published his book Grossformen im Wohnungsbau (Grossformen 
in Housing), in which he reconsidered the relation between

architecture and the city through his concept of Grossform. 
Ungers maintained that the city could no longer be thought of 
in its entirety, as some architects and urban designers of the 
modern movement had claimed. Instead, he propagated an 
understanding of urban design which operates through the 
conception of a Grossform, understood as an intermediate 
scale of design between a building and a city. Ungers looked 
upon the Grossform as a counter measure to the logics of 
rapid urbanization. He maintained that the formal coherence 
of Grossform could act as an alternative to the unplannable 
processes of the contemporary city, acting as a stable marker of identity. In his “Study for Roosevelt Island” (1979), Ungers illustrates that the Grossform has the capacity to incorporate the genetic code of a possible future city, articulating 
the relation between typology and morphology, as well as 
between public and private space. This capacity is further 
explored in the project for “Housing on Lützowplatz” (1979), 
which experiments with a new balance between the individual and collective realms, creating a new urban presence in 
the city of Berlin.
Another architect who turned to history as a means of remedying the alleged anonymity and lifelessness of the modern city was Léon Krier. Krier, a Luxemburgish architect, 
objected particularly to the functional zoning of cities. This 
approach, he believed, had resulted in the creation of ‘anti-cities’ that were incapable of producing any sense of community. Instead, he advocated greater attention to the human 
scale, walkability, and communal activities in urban design. 
In the 1970s, he developed a “Charter for the Reconstruction 
of the European City”, which promoted the study of historic 
urban precedents to determine new urban forms, and which 
proposed to introduce a polycentric city model, whereby the 
city would be composed of a collection of smaller autonomous neighborhoods which would be up to 33 hectares in 
size, and which would house all urban functions, including 
work and culture. As such, these neighborhoods would be 
walkable environments that could be traversed on foot in ten 
minutes or less, and could comfortably house 10,000–15,000 
people, thus leading to more tight-knit urban communities. 
Krier developed his ideas about how to ‘cure’ or reconstruct 
the city through various paper projects. One such project was 
a masterplan for the redevelopment of Luxembourg, which 
proposed to divide the city into twenty-three neighborhoods 
(or quartiers). Two of these neighborhoods—the ‘quartiers de 
l’Europe’—were new, and Krier proposed an urban layout for 
them which illustrated his ideas about how to create a clear 
hierarchy and greater legibility in the urban environment. 
Krier’s scheme for the redevelopment of Luxembourg was 
never executed. It is, however, an example of a ‘counter-project’. These are paper projects that were drawn up during 
the 1970s and 1980s, which sought to formulate alternatives 
to modernist urban designs. These counter-projects were 
founded on the memory of pre-industrial European Cities, 
and accordingly proposed to ‘repair’ or ‘reconstruct’ the 
European city. Counter-projects were designed not only by 
Krier, but by several architects across Europe, who joined 
this ‘Movement for the Reconstruction of the European City’. 
In 1978, a group of these architects signed the Déclaration 
de Bruxelles, which was published as a book in 1980. The 
Déclaration de Bruxelles was intended as a foil to the 1933 
Athens Charter, which was based on Le Corbusier’s ‘Ville 
Radieuse’ ideas, and which promoted ideas of functional 
zoning. The Declaration de Bruxelles was diametrically opposed to the Athens Charter, and was informed by the desire 
to reconstruct the European City through the study of historic 
urban morphologies and types.
Krier’s ideas were realized in Poundbury, an urban extension 
of Dorchester (UK), for which he developed a masterplan 
in the late 1980s. Krier reconceptualized Dorchester along 
with its planned urban extension as a group of five autonomous neighborhoods, each with its own center. All buildings in the urban extension were to adhere to a predefined 
building code that set out regulations about, for instance, the 
materials that were to be used in external building walls, the 
form and materiality of the roofs of houses, etc. This building 
code was intended to create cohesion in the urban fabric 
and guarantee that all the buildings would be of high quality.
Krier’s ideas became very popular in the United States, 
where they sparked the emergence of ‘New Urbanism’. The 
birthplace of ‘New Urbanism’ in America is the town of Seaside in Florida. In the early 1980s, the developer Robert Davis 
commissioned Andres Duany and Elizabeth Plater-Zyberk to 
develop a new, autonomous ideal town on a privately-owned 
plot of land of approximately 32 hectares. This marked the 
birth of Seaside. The street layout of Seaside was inspired 
by historical urban morphologies, and the town not only offered dwellings, but also commercial and civic amenities, 
including a school, an amphitheater, a large park, etc. To 
achieve harmony in its urban form, Duany and Plater-Zyberk 
formulated an urban code, similar to Krier’s ‘building code’ 
in Poundbury. The urban code at Seaside set out guidelines 
for the design of yards, porches, balconies, building height, 
etc. Once this ‘urban code’ was in place, the design of the 
individual buildings at Seaside was left to other architects to 
encourage variety in the urban design.
Learning from Popular Culture
In parallel to the Neo-Rationalists and the New Urbanists, another group of architects and urban designers stepped into 
the limelight during the 1970s. They too were concerned with

the disintegrating social fabric of cities and, like Krier and 
his fellow members of the ‘Movement for the Reconstruction 
of the European City’, they too promoted greater legibility 
of urban environments. However, contrary to Krier and his 
colleagues, these urban designers did not seek inspiration 
in historical urban forms, but in everyday, ‘popular’ environments. Key proponents of this movement were Robert Venturi and Denise Scott-Brown who believed that (in their own 
words): “Learning from the existing landscape is a way of 
being revolutionary for an architect. Not the obvious way, 
which is to tear down Paris and begin again, as Le Corbusier 
suggested in the 1920s, but another more tolerant way; that 
is, to question how we look at things […] We look backward 
at history and tradition to go forward; we can also look downward to go upward. And withholding judgement may be used 
as a tool to make later judgement more sensitive. This is a 
way of learning from everything”. In line with this belief—that 
one could ‘learn from everything’—Venturi and Scott Brown, 
together with Steven Izenour (a graduate student at Yale), 
organized a research studio in Las Vegas in the fall of 1968. 
They took a group of thirteen students from the Yale School of 
Art and Architecture (where they were working at the time) to 
Las Vegas to study its urban form, and published their findings in the 1972 book Learning from Las Vegas. This book 
contained detailed analysis of the urban morphology of the 
Las Vegas strip, which they treated with the same level of 
seriousness as the proponents of the ‘Movement for the Reconstruction of the European City’ treated the historical morphologies and types of the European city.
Building on their Las Vegas research, Venturi and Scott 
Brown installed the ‘Signs of Life’ exhibition in the Renwick 
Gallery in Washington in 1976. This exhibition emphasized 
the rich persuasion of signs and symbols in the contemporary environment (albeit environments that were generally not 
held in very high regard by modern architects), and demonstrated their pervasiveness throughout society. The work of 
Venturi and Scott Brown heralded a period in architecture 
and urban design which was highly influenced by studies in 
linguistics and, particularly, by semiotics—the study of signs 
and symbols.
The Metropolis on Steroids
In the mid-1970s, another manifesto for the future development of the city emerged, when Rem Koolhaas founded the 
Office for Metropolitan Architecture (OMA) together with Elia 
and Zoe Zenghelis, Madelon Vriesendorp, and Zaha Hadid, 
and published Delirious New York: A Retroactive Manifesto 
for Manhattan (1978). OMA, and Koolhaas in particular, held 
up Manhattan as a model for future urbanization, and advocated ‘super-urbanism’ as a foil to ‘sub-urbanism’. Koolhaas 
believed that the development of the skyscraper had transformed the urban block into a potential city within the city, 
a world in itself. What occurs in the skyscraper, Koolhaas 
contends, is a vertical schism, which means that the activities on each floor of the skyscraper can be separated and 
isolated from one another. The brilliance of the skyscraper, 
according to Koolhaas, is that it makes it possible to reproduce a given floor space ad infinitum. As such, it presents “‘a 
utopian formula for the unlimited creation of virgin sites on a 
single urban location”. Thanks to the skyscraper, each block 
in the urban grid becomes a potential city within the city: a 
world unto itself, which gives great freedom. The skyscraper, 
Koolhaas thus suggests, enables a form of ‘interior urbanism’. One of the most famous examples of such an ‘interior 
urbanism’ is the Bonaventure Hotel in Los Angeles by John 
Portman (1977). Consisting of five circular towers, placed 
on a large concrete plinth (that takes up an entire block in 
downtown LA), the Bonaventure was the largest hotel in the 
city when it opened, and offered everything imaginable—a 
micro-city—within its confines.
Collage City
In 1978, the same year that Koolhaas published Delirious 
New York, Colin Rowe and Fred Koetter published Collage 
City. This book presented a strategy for integrating disparate cultural impulses and hybridizing fragments of history 
into the urban fabric. It sealed the demise of grand modern 
urban utopias, and heralded the rise of the ‘bricolage’ city: a 
city composed of disparate urban fragments, which develop 
independently of each other. Such a disjointed approach to 
city-building was made possible by the new, neoliberal socio-political context. In this context, urban planning was no 
longer controlled by governments who would plan and carry 
out grand (overall) urban schemes. Instead, the urban fabric 
was sold off in small pieces to private developers, who autonomously decided what to do on their site.
