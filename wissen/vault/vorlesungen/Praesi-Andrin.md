# Präsi Andrin

## S. 1

Introduction 
Till now we encountered four Keywords for describing the way of Working 
of Cai (Pressure / Layering / Traces / Proportion/Dimensions) 
 
Where do we see our Potential? 
Cai creates Traces in every Step and Layer in his Artworks. 
Whether it is not visible on the Artwork, such as the Idea behind the Art or physically visible Traces like the Soot on 
the Canvas in the End. 
 
There is one moment in his creation Cycle where his performance is momentarily and only visible and feelable in 
this exact moment. 
 
This moment is the moment of Igniting and Exploding the Artwork with Black powder. An initial Blow => 
Heat and sound waves, Smoke and Particles and a strong smell. 
 
We see the potential of our Room in this moment of time. Trying to extend this moment into a ﬁxated structure and 
Atmosphere. 
 
We need a Material which reacts to all “Moments of creation”. (Heat, Pressure, Particles and Smoke) 
 
In our Model you can see now especially how the Smoke and Particles affect the Glas. Melting and putting 
pressure, we need to experiment more that it works, but we think its possible. Maybe using Foils or Coatings. We 
are also using a transparent Material to create a Layer effect and control the light with it. 
 
The room itself needs therefore an airtight outer shell (not deﬁned yet, needs to be transparent aswell) 
 
By burning varying amounts of black powder in an airtight space we emulate the blast of an explosion  
 
How do we do this in Real Life – For this dimension we would need big explosions. So we scale it down, create 
Boxes of Glas Panels in different dimensions, we explode and cut them and scatter them in the room 
 
Different imprints implying different distances to the combustion (blast radius)  
By layering those in space we create a progression towards the artwork, bringing in more on more different traces. 
 
 
You Enter the Room 
First, your sense of smell kicks in. 
A strong, pervasive oduor of burnt materials and sulfur oxide lingers thickly in the air. You experience a mixture of 
contradictory feelings: curiosity and dread, order and chaos, joy and sorrow, peace and restlessness. 
Your sense of sight draws you back to the room you’re about to explore. The artwork White Tone by Cai Guo-
Qiang is barely visible through the layers of cracked and fogged glass. On it, you see traces of soot in the form of 
whirls that intensify the deeper you peer into the structure. 
 
You step forward, leaving footprints behind. Like snow, particles of smoke have settled on the ﬂoor, covering the 
wooden walkway with a layer of sulfur dust. 
 
As you move, you lose yourself in layers of fractured glass. The outside world blurs behind suspended panes, their 
surfaces webbed with cracks radiating outward, as if from a sudden, powerful force. A ﬁne layer of soot clings to 
the glass, gathering more heavily with each layer, like a faint memory of smoke. Signs of intense heat appear—
bubbles and molten patches that warp the glass, further distorting your surroundings. Each fractured pane, marked 
by the force that shattered it, seems to guide you toward a single focal point: White Tone. 
 
The light begins to fade to a bright, colorless tone, refracted countless times and reﬂecting an even balance of all 
visible light frequencies. 
 
Standing on the ﬁnal platform, whose materiality now feels closer to charcoal than wood, you gaze at Cai’s artwork. 
The room feels boundless, with no clear end or scale. You feel as if you’re ﬂoating in cosmic dust. As you’re looking 
at Cai’s artwork unseen forces seem to heighten your ambiguous feelings of curiosity and dread, order and chaos, 
joy and sorrow, peace and restlessness, creation and destruction, life and death.
