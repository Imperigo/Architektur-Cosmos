Introduction
Territorial Scope of the Megaproject
The Millennium City Center project is located on the Pest side of the Da­
nube River in Budapest, Hungary. It occupies one of the last large unde­
veloped riverfront areas, strategically positioned near transport hubs, 
such as a regional train terminal and a road to the airport.
 
A new proposal for a Millennium City Center near the Rákosrendező rail­
way station spans 130 hectares and aims to extend the city’s develop­
ment further into the 13th and 14th districts .
Timeframe
The original project was initiated in the early 1990s and remains incom­
plete today, with major milestones like the construction of the Palace 
of Arts (MuPa) and National Theater completed by 2005. Meanwhile, the 
new Millennium City Center project is planned to start in 2024, with com­
pletion expected by the end of the decade.
Reasons for Initiating the Megaproject
 
• 
Previous Land Use: 
 
 
The original site was a derelict industrial zone, while the new site
 
 
is an illegal landfill. Both projects aim to transform underutilized
 
 
land into vibrant urban hubs.
 
• 
Main Spatial Conflict: 
 
 
In both cases, balancing large-scale urban development with 
 
 
local needs and environmental considerations
 
 
has been a significant challenge.

2.1 Position of Professional Planners
Role and Challenges
The fragmented nature of Hungary’s planning system heavily influenced the 
role of professional planners in the Millennium City Center project. Hunga­
ry’s two-tier local government structure divides responsibilities between 
the municipal government (citywide issues) and district governments (lo­
cal matters like building permits). This often leads to conflicting priorities 
and inefficiencies. For example, while the municipality handled zoning, dis­
tricts could veto or delay projects by refusing permits. The lack of centrali­
zed, binding planning guidelines further weakened planners’ authority, as 
seen in the failure to organize a planning competition for the site, which led 
to ad hoc decisions .
Connection to Hungary’s Planning System
The challenges of the Millennium City Center reflect systemic issues in 
Hungary’s urban planning framework. Decentralized responsibilities and 
the absence of an independent coordinating body hinder effective largescale urban projects. Weak public engagement and transparency exacer­
bated these problems, leaving planners marginalized. Similar risks may 
arise in the new project unless stronger collaboration and clearer regula­
tions are implemented.
Marginalization
In the earlier project, political interference further marginalized profes­
sional planners. Decisions like relocating the National Theater without 
proper consultation exemplify how ad hoc political motives overruled pro­
fessional planning inputs. In the new project, the government’s proacti­
ve negotiations with global developers suggest a potential shift toward a 
more structured approach, but whether planners will have a stronger role 
remains uncertain.

2.2 Developers’ Approach and Values
Approach
Both projects emphasize public-private partnerships (PPP). In the original 
development, the private developer TriGranit played a dominant role, parti­
cularly in funding and designing key cultural institutions like MuPa and the 
National Theater. However, the prioritization of profitability often oversha­
dowed community needs, leaving parts of the area without vibrant public 
spaces.
The new project reflects an evolved approach, with an explicit commitment 
to green urbanism. The proposed park will span 250,000–350,000 square 
meters, making it the largest in Budapest. This focus on environmental 
quality represents a significant shift toward sustainable development.
Values
Economic growth remains central to both projects. The original Millen­
nium City Center sought to create a multifunctional district blending cul­
ture, residential spaces, and offices. Similarly, the new development plans 
600,000–650,000 square meters of offices, apartments, and commercial 
areas to drive investment. However, the inclusion of substantial green 
spaces in the new project suggests a stronger emphasis on public wellbeing and sustainability compared to its predecessor.

2.3 Local Community Strategies
Minimal resistance
The project limited community resistance, as the site was previously in­
dustrial and uninhabited. However, critiques emerged over the lack of ac­
cessible public spaces and inadequate transport adjustments, which made 
the area less inviting for residents and visitors.
The new project might avoid these pitfalls by integrating transport infras­
tructure upgrades, including the extension of a metro line and the creation 
of few public transport hubs.
Architectural Community
Economic growth remains central to both projects. The original Millennium 
City Center sought to create a multifunctional district blending culture, re­
sidential spaces, and offices. Similarly, the new development plans around 
650 m2 of offices, apartments, and commercial areas to drive investment. 
However, the inclusion of substantial green spaces in the new project sug­
gests a stronger emphasis on public wellbeing and sustainability compa­
red to its predecessor.

2.4 Public Officials’ Role
Towards Investors
Public officials prioritized attracting private investment, offering conces­
sions to developers to ensure the project’s progress. This included facili­
tating negotiations with foreign investors and allocating public funds for 
infrastructure improvements, such as the M1 metro extension and trans­
port hubs.
Towards the Public
The project has faced criticism for its lack of public engagement in earlier 
phases. However, recent additions, like the planned park and improved pu­
blic transportation, indicate a shift toward addressing public needs.

3 Discussion
Positive Aspects of the Megaproject
1. Cultural Contributions:
The Palace of Arts (MuPa) has become a respected cultural landmark in Bu­
dapest, hosting international performances and enhancing the city’s cultu­
ral identity.
2. Economic Potential:
The integration of offices, residential spaces, and commercial areas aims 
to drive investment and establish the Millennium City Center as a vibrant 
urban hub.
3. Infrastructure and Green Spaces:
The planned transport upgrades and the addition of a significant public park 
reflect a focus on sustainability and improved urban living.
Negative Aspects of the Megaproject
1. Incomplete Implementation:
The project has seen delays and remains unfinished, with some key com­
ponents, such as the congress center, yet to be realized.
2. Public Accountability and Engagement:
Early phases of the project lacked transparency and meaningful public con­
sultation, limiting its integration into the broader community.
3. Political Interference:
Ad hoc political decisions, such as those surrounding the National Theater, 
undermined professional planning inputs and contributed to inefficiencies .

Political Context:
The project reflects the challenges of operating in a fragmented governan­
ce system, where political conflicts and changing priorities often hinder 
coherent planning and execution.
Economic Context:
The reliance on private investment underscores the broader market-dri­
ven development approach in Budapest. While this has attracted significant 
funding, it raises concerns about prioritizing private over public interests.
 Cultural and Social Context:
The inclusion of cultural landmarks and new green spaces highlights ef­
forts to enhance Budapest’s identity, but earlier shortcomings in public en­
gagement remain a concern.

Andrin Baumann | European Aspects of Spatial Development | ETHZ | 30.11.2024
3 Discussion
Critical Assesement:
The Millennium City Center demonstrates both the potential and the pitfalls 
of urban megaprojects. While its cultural achievements and infrastructu­
re plans are commendable, the project has been hindered by governance 
issues, political interference, and a lack of transparency. To realize its full 
potential, future phases must prioritize collaboration among stakeholders, 
strengthen planning frameworks, and ensure public needs are at the foref­
ront.
