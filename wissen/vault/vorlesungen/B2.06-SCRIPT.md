New Capitals for 
New Democracies, 
New Institutions for 
Old Democracies 
The experience of war, and the desire for a fresh 
start—for a more democratic and egalitarian postwar society—continued to play a role during the 
1950s and 1960s. 1947, the year in which India 
became independent, announced the start of a 
second wave of decolonization in Asia and Africa. 
The dissatisfaction of the indigenous administrative 
elite, often educated by a colonial power, caused 
fierce reactions in several countries. In addition, 
European powers were completely exhausted by 
mutual struggles in two World Wars. The two most 
important post-war powers, the United States and 
the Soviet Union, were both opponents (at least in 
name) of European imperialism. In the meantime, 
they were eager to build up their own spheres of 
influence in the newly independent territories. Simultaneously, new independent nation states were 
planning and constructing new urban centers. 
After the independence of Pakistan and India, 
the president of India, Jawaharlal Nehru, decided 
in 1947 that the state of Punjab required a new 
capital. A team led by the architects Le Corbusier, 
Pierre Jeanneret, Maxwell Fry, and Jane Drew was 
commissioned to design a city that would represent 
an indigenous modernity. They developed a system 
of sectors—large urban districts measuring 800 by 
1200 meters—in which various housing typologies 
and a series of public and collective spaces could 
be positioned. In these sectors, various social 
classes were mixed; a ground-breaking urban 
design proposal in a country like India, where the 
caste system was prevalent. “The General Master 
Plan of Chandigarh” is an extendable grid of sectors which is superimposed on a green landscape 
(Leisure Valley, green open spaces, and sector-greens) and punctuated by the Capitol Complex 
(Sector 1), the City Center (Sector 17), and several 
cultural and educational institutions. A diversified 
circulation system, the network of roads called ‘the 
7Vs’, completes the grid, and precisely situates different forms of traffic (from automotive to pedestrian) in the city. Le Corbusier and his team believed 
firmly in the precision of architecture, as articulated 
in the meticulous use of proportions and choice 
of materials. However, they were also aware that 
in a rapidly modernizing society, living and dwelling patterns would not remain stable, and thus 
required transformability. In order to obtain this 
capacity for transformation, they introduced socalled ‘aesthetic controls’: specific elements in the 
urban environment which would remain stable and 
accommodate change.
The government of Pakistan also decided to respond to decolonization by creating the new capital of Islamabad. In February 1959, the President 
of Pakistan commissioned the Greek urban planner Constantinos Doxiadis to design Islamabad as 
the nation’s new capital. The proposal by Doxiadis 
and his team sub-divided the Islamabad metropolitan area into sectors, called Communities, in 
which housing and public functions were attuned 
to specific income groups. These sectors ensured 
that a unified scale of urban development could be 
maintained for the whole metropolitan area. Simultaneously, it accommodated road traffic, as well 
as the organization of the various land-use zones, 
into Communities. The overall plan is based on 
the ‘dynametropolis or ‘dynapolis’ concept, giving 
the possibility of continuous expansion of the city 
with the smallest-possible adverse effects on the 
functioning of the metropolis and its transportation 
systems. As well as the central cores, the residential areas of Islamabad were designed in such a 
way that they could expand dynamically.
In Brazil, President Juscelino Kubitschek opted 
in 1957 for the planning of an entirely new city. 
Brasília was to serve as the new, neutral capital of 
the country, replacing Rio de Janeiro. The new federal capital of Brasília was planned by Lucio Costa 
in the country’s remote interior. Amid the wilderness of nature, the first lines of a new city arose: a 
monumental east–west axis which would be punctuated by a sequence of public buildings, and a

north–south motorway along which housing areas 
were planned. This division between civic space 
and residential areas enabled the rapid completion 
of the most prominent civic structures, designed by 
Oscar Niemeyer, to create an emblematic image of 
the nation’s new capital. Along the arched north–
south freeway, Lucio Costa designed large residential blocks or superquadras of 300 by 300 meters, 
which are enclosed by a frame of trees and green. 
One superquadra can house approximately 3,500 
people in eleven six-story buildings and around 
1,500 apartments. Giving priority to everyday civic 
life, the superquadras are designed as continuous 
and shady green spaces, amongst others by the 
landscape architect Roberto Burle-Marx. They are 
punctuated with schools, gyms, pools, markets, 
and churches. Although Brasília is designed for the 
automobile, all necessary services are within walking distance. With his superquadras, Lucio Costa 
intended to change the traditional social hierarchy 
of Brazil, so that white collar workers and laborers 
would share the same neighborhood. 
At Fort Lamy (Chad, 1962), the urban design 
proposal by Candilis-Josic-Woods derived from a 
detailed investigation of the existing urban condition. The city of Fort-Lamy had been the capital 
of colonial Chad since 1920. After the installation 
of the independent Republic of Chad in 1960, it 
was decided that it would remain so. However, the 
urban form of Fort-Lamy was the sheer material 
articulation of the historical colonial power relations. 
The city was clearly divided into an African quarter, 
comprised of courtyard dwellings within a dense 
urban tissue, and a European quarter, planned 
during the French colonial period and consisting of 
detached buildings along wide avenues. Both were 
separated by a large zone sanitaire; an empty zone 
whose main function was to distance colonizer and 
colonized. 
This empty zone became the main focus of the 
project by Candilis-Josic-Woods. The firm aimed to 
reconnect the two city parts materially and symbolically, as well as to propose a way of planning that 
differed from the prescriptive and limiting methods 
of urban design used by the colonizer. In order to 
achieve these goals, the architects studied existing patterns of urbanization and appropriation. As 
a result, they introduced the planning instrument 
of the ‘Stem’; a continuous figure of public street 
space defined simply by a tracé and adjacent 
collective buildings. To this first structuring axis, a 
second figure of multi-level buildings was added, 
which continued the public domain of the Stem as 
‘streets in the air’. In its turn, the public domain of 
the ‘streets in the air’ was prolonged in a fine weave 
of small streets or alleys that structured low-rise 
patches of urban tissue. The result is a continuous 
public realm—with varying degrees of privacy and 
collectivity—that connects new and existing urban 
neighborhoods. 
Candilis-Josic-Woods’ project for Fort-Lamy is 
a remarkable effort to engage simultaneously 
with traditional typologies of dwelling and public space, and with new high-rise buildings and 
internal streets. Moreover, it is an attempt to alter 
the segregating character of the colonial city as 
well as the prescriptive planning instruments of the 
French colonizer. In short, the project is a testimony 
to a multifaceted architectural venture to inscribe 
the expectations of a postcolonial present onto the 
reality of a colonial past. 
In parallel to the construction of these new capital 
cities for newly established democracies, several ‘new’ building types were introduced into the 
urban fabric of cities in more established democracies—particularly in Europe—some of which were 
still undergoing reconstruction after the Second 
World War. At this time, the modern welfare state, 
which is an ideological concept of government, 
matured. The welfare state concept is based on 
the principles of equality of opportunity, equitable 
distribution of wealth, and public responsibility for

those unable to avail themselves of the minimal 
provisions for a good life. In countries that adopted 
this welfare state concept—including many European countries—the state assumed a key role in 
the protection and promotion of the economic and 
social well-being of citizens. This resulted in the 
construction of new government planning institutions that were designed to facilitate the redistribution of wealth, knowledge, and political power. 
Such institutions outlined a set of policies designed 
to mitigate social antagonisms, which targeted not 
only housing, social security, and health care, but 
also democratized the right to leisure, culture, and 
education. As a result, many European countries 
not only saw the rise of heavily subsidized housing 
estates (many of these were built in New Towns), 
but also the rise of a new set of ‘institutions’ for 
culture, community, and education, which solidified into built form in numerous ways and through 
various building types.
A new building type that emerged in Europe at 
this time was the cultural center. This type was 
designed to create spaces where everyone, regardless of social status, could get a taste of high 
‘culture’ (e.g. theatre performances, art exhibitions, 
concerts, etc.), while simultaneously providing 
room to accommodate bottom-up initiatives (e.g. 
art classes for children, meeting areas for local 
clubs, rehearsal spaces for amateur dramatics, 
etc.). This infrastructure was thus attributed both 
an educational function by promoting ‘high culture’ 
through its own program, as well as a supportive 
function by offering spaces for the organization of 
‘grassroots’ events. One of the most famous cultural centers to be built in Europe is the Kulturhuset in 
Stockholm by Peter Celsing, which was designed 
and constructed between 1966 and 1974. Celsing thought of the Stockholm Kulturhuset (House 
of Culture) as the “shop window of the welfare 
system”. It was proposed as a new type of environment where boundaries between ‘official’ art 
and ‘amateur’ creativity, as well as the boundaries 
between performer and public, would dissolve 
in one large continuous happening. The realization of the Kulturhuset played an important role in 
the redevelopment of the area of Sergels Torg in 
inner-city Stockholm. The complex is both a part of 
the city, and a city in its own right. The building is 
arranged in such a way as to generate two public 
squares (Sergels Torg and Brunkebergs Torg), and 
form a barrier between both, which stand in conceptual contrast to each other, by virtue not only of 
their situation, but also of their façades. While the 
Kulturhuset is closed off to the exterior, through its 
large display window it allows those on the outside 
to observe and feel involved in the activities taking 
place inside.
In France, in 1966, the Minister of Youth and 
Sports, Francois Missoffe, launched one the largest community programs developed by the French 
government for its citizens during the post-war 
period: the Mille Clubs program of youth clubs. The 
main official intention of the program was to create 
a socio-cultural infrastructure for the leisure time of 
the youth, and initially consisted of 1,000 so-called 
‘youth clubs’ that would be distributed over the 
entire French territory, in both urban and rural settings. These youth clubs were meant to be places 
for meeting and conversation—for encountering 
others—and their designs intended to articulate the 
social and spatial characteristics of mass leisure 
for youths. Several architects designed such youth 
clubs for the government, including Gerard Grandval (with the construction firm SERA, 1966), Jean 
Prouve (in collaboration with the construction firm 
CIMT, 1966), and Daniel Bertrand (with the construction firm SCAC, 1972).
Welfare state governments in Europe were not only 
interested in providing the population access to 
cultural and community facilities, but also wanted 
to provide universal access to education. It was the 
belief of welfare state governments that everyone 
should have good access to education. After the

war, as a result of post-war optimism and growing 
prosperity, the population in Europe increased 
exponentially—the so-called ‘baby boom’. This, 
paired with the fact that, in many places across 
Europe, the age for compulsory education increased progressively throughout the twentieth 
century, necessitated the construction of many 
new schools for primary and secondary education. At this time, a new concept of the school was 
popularized in Switzerland: the school as an ‘open 
house’. In a similar way to the cultural center, which 
was to combine various activities and cater to a 
varied audience, these new ‘open schools’ were 
conceived as centers not just for the school community (i.e., children), but for the entire population 
of a village, town, or new housing settlement. They 
should not just cater to school-children, but to all 
age groups—they were to become community centers for continuing education. A beautiful example 
of such a school that was destined to become an 
‘open house’ is the Riedhof in Höngg, which was 
designed by Alfred Roth and constructed between 
1961 and 1963.
Another important component of post-war welfare 
state thinking with regards to education was the 
belief that tertiary education needed to be democratized. Up until the war, access to tertiary education (university) was largely restricted to the upper 
classes. However, as part of the democratizing 
zeal of welfare states, tertiary education was now 
to become accessible to everyone. To cater to this 
growing number of students, many new, ‘democratic’ universities needed to be realized. In the UK, 
seven new universities were founded between 1961 
and 1965, which were mostly built during the second half of the 1960s. These universities, which all 
had a very modern architecture, were called ‘plate 
glass universities’, on account of their extensive 
use of the material. This terminology is a reference 
to the more established English universities that 
had been founded in the nineteenth century, such 
as those of Manchester and Liverpool, which were 
called the ‘red-brick’ universities. All these new 
plate glass universities were established at the 
edges of existing towns and cities. They thus did 
not contribute to the urban development of cities, 
but signified forms of urbanization in their own right. 
One of the most extraordinary examples of a plate 
glass university is the University of East Anglia 
(1962–68), designed by Denys Lasdun & Partners.
In parallel to these initiatives by the state, the 
private, for-profit sector (i.e., developers and 
businesses) also set up several initiatives from 
the 1950s to the 1970s—the heyday of the European welfare state—that affected civic urban life. 
An important new building type that this private, 
for-profit sector introduced in Europe in this period was the shopping center. One of Europe’s 
pioneering shopping centers was built in Sweden 
in 1955. Designed by the British émigré architect 
Ralph Erskine, ‘Shopping’ in Luleå was to create an 
enjoyable environment for everyone all year round, 
irrespective of current weather conditions, which, 
given its location in the northernmost part of Sweden, were very harsh at times. It offered not only 
bountiful merchandise and goods, but also entertainment and social spaces, making the shopping 
center a natural meeting point for everyone in the 
city—a city within a city.
Another important actor in the European welfare 
states was the third sector: i.e., voluntary, non-profit 
organizations. Religious associations constituted a 
large portion of this third sector in post-war Europe. 
However, after the Second World War, it became 
increasingly clear that Christian belief systems 
were insufficiently adapted to the modern world 
and its secular institutions. As a result of initiatives 
by the state and the market (the private, for-profit 
sector), who, for instance, built cultural centers 
and shopping centers, the number of churchgoers 
decreased between the 1950s and the 1970s. In 
response, religious leaders embarked on a fundamental rethinking of the role of faith in modern 
society. They were joined by architects and urban 
planners, who enthusiastically took up their cause, 
and sought to express religious renewal in physical form. At this time, a different kind of church 
emerged. Instead of grand representational church 
buildings, small-scale, community-oriented parish 
centers were realized that were well-embedded in 
their surrounding urban fabric. A beautiful example 
is the Maria Krönung parochial center in Witikon by 
Justus Dahinden (1965).
Each of the ‘new’ institutions that were built for ‘old 
democracies’ adopted a range of different urban 
design strategies and developed different relationships with the surrounding city fabric. Urban design 
became a matter of dispersed interventions that 
charged the physical and social fabric of the city. 
The common denominator was that they all strove 
to shape a new, more open, and more egalitarian 
post-war society.
