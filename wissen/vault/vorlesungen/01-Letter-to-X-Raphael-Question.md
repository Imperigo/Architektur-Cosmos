# 01 Letter to X Raphael Question

## S. 1

Question 
In Raphaels Letter to Leo X, Raphael identiﬁes three “manners” of building in Rome, 
each marking a di=erent historical period and altering the city’s appearance over time. 
He writes “Of all the arts, it was architecture that was the last to be lost,” with it, praising 
architecture for its exceptional capacity to endure and to carry meaning across 
centuries. In addition, he says, “building manners became reduced to a style that 
matched their wretchedness,” referring to the period of domination by the East 
Germanic “Goths.” He characterizes their work as “without skill, measure, or any grace 
whatsoever,” implying a pragmatic and almost barbaric approach to construction. 
In this, Raphael points to the mutual transformation of architecture and society: 
Architecture shapes civilization, and civilization in turn reshapes architecture to reﬂect 
its own values or its decline. This leads to my question: 
How would one declare/deﬁne an architecture style as preservable and 
respectable (like Raphael is trying to do), in a world of constant change? 
Does the surrounding space ﬁrst need to change for us to recognize what is being lost? 
 
Opinion 
From the Renaissance perspective, Raphael’s letter seems to argue for a return to a 
“purer” and more ordered civilization, guided by norms and classical form. The 
humanists clearly believed that a better, more virtuous society had once existed and 
that the recovery of its architectural principles could help restore cultural excellence. 
For me, this is compelling: change is inevitable, and architecture necessarily evolves 
with society. When we look at Rome today, some buildings still seem to fulﬁll Raphael’s 
ideal of being “alive forever,” but their meaning is no longer static. it depends on how 
people engage with them. If architecture does not adapt to changing needs, it risks 
becoming a relic admired visually but detached from everyday life. 
In this sense, change is necessary not just to create new architecture but also to 
sharpen our awareness of what is worth preserving. When human behaviour, values, 
and ways of living shift, architecture must act both as a usable framework for the 
present and as a reminder of the past. Architecture deserves preservation when it can 
continue to perform this double role, as an active participant in contemporary life and as 
a critical witness to what came before.
