# Presentation Mid

## S. 1

Presentation Mid-critics
1. Quickly explain that model failed in many ways but also we learned a lot whiel 
doing it. 
2. You enter the room. (1min 30s)
3. Expanding Traces
a. Cai creates Traces in every Step and Layer in his Artworks.
b. Whether it is not visible on the Artwork, such as the Idea behind the Art 
or physically visible Traces like the Soot on the Canvas in the End.
c. There is one moment in his creation Cycle where his performance is 
momentarily and only visible and feelable in this exact moment. This 
moment is the moment of Igniting and Exploding the Artwork with Black 
powder. An initial Blow => Heat and sound waves, Smoke and Particles 
and a strong smell.
d. We see the potential of our Room in this moment of time. Trying to 
extend this moment into a fixated structure and Atmosphere.
4. Pressure & Layering / Traces & Proportion
a. Like Cai we experimented with Pressure and Layers. Forming the room
b. We observed the Behavior of the Explosions in differently Proportioned 
airtight Spaces.
5. Material Glass
a. We need a Material which reacts to all “Moments of creation”. (Heat, 
Pressure, Dust)
b. By burning varying amounts of black powder in an airtight space we 
emulate the blast of an explosion 
c. Different imprints implying different distances to the combustion (blast 
radius) 
d. By layering those in space we create a progression towards the 
artwork, bringing in more on more different traces.

## S. 2

You Enter the Room
First, your sense of smell kicks in.
A strong, pervasive oduor of burnt materials and sulfur oxide lingers thickly in the air. 
You experience a mixture of contradictory feelings: curiosity and dread, order and 
chaos, joy and sorrow, peace and restlessness.
Your sense of sight draws you back to the room you’re about to explore. The artwork 
White Tone by Cai Guo-Qiang is barely visible through the layers of cracked and 
fogged glass. On it, you see traces of soot in the form of whirls that intensify the 
deeper you peer into the structure.
You step forward, leaving footprints behind. Like snow, particles of smoke have 
settled on the floor, covering the wooden walkway with a layer of sulfur dust.
As you move, you lose yourself in layers of fractured glass. The outside world blurs 
behind suspended panes, their surfaces webbed with cracks radiating outward, as if 
from a sudden, powerful force. A fine layer of soot clings to the glass, gathering more 
heavily with each layer, like a faint memory of smoke. Signs of intense heat appear—
bubbles and molten patches that warp the glass, further distorting your surroundings. 
Each fractured pane, marked by the force that shattered it, seems to guide you 
toward a single focal point: White Tone.
The light begins to fade to a bright, colorless tone, refracted countless times and 
reflecting an even balance of all visible light frequencies.
Standing on the final platform, whose materiality now feels closer to charcoal than 
wood, you gaze at Cai’s artwork. The room feels boundless, with no clear end or 
scale. You feel as if you’re floating in cosmic dust. As you’re looking at Cai’s artwork 
unseen forces seem to heighten your ambiguous feelings of curiosity and dread, 
order and chaos, joy and sorrow, peace and restlessness, creation and destruction, 
life and death.
