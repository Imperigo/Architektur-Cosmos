# Unseen Cosmic Forces

## S. 1

Cai Guo-Qiang | White Tone 
Unseen Cosmic Forces 
 
From 2D Artwork to 3D Architecture 
Cai Guo-Qiang is a Chinese artist known for creating paintings through gunpowder explosions and for 
his spectacular ﬁreworks performances. He often performs his art in a Workshop-like Hall, usually 
with his team and rarely in front of a small audience. His technique involves layering on a 2D surface 
to create the drawings. The base (typically canvas, sometimes glass, or rarely 3D forms) is covered 
with gunpowder, often applied with stencils for precision. For larger surfaces, wooden beams are used 
as bridges to avoid disturbing the gunpowder spread below. The gunpowder is in direct contact with 
the base material, rice paper, and a covering layer (usually cardboard). Once the setup is complete, 
the gunpowder is ignited, resulting in an explosion that leaves traces on the surface. This surface is 
then displayed. 
 
Stepping into the Impact 
Volume Model (18m x 5.4m x 12m) Based on Artwork Dimensions (18m x 4m): 
The traces of the explosion in the volume reveal how Soot appears on the walls, showing diUerent 
levels of gradation. These wall patterns serve as the blueprint for our transparent walls in the space. 
Each wall is unique, reﬂecting the individual impact of the explosion. 
 
Material Gesture: 
Glass, one of Cai’s foundational materials, becomes central to our project as well. However, unlike 
his layering technique, we reverse the process. To “photograph” the moments of explosion capturing 
smoke, particles, and scent released into the air—our foundational material is suspended above the 
explosion. This allows it to collect these ephemeral elements. 
The distance between the glass and the gunpowder is critical. It determines the transparency of the 
glass and the patterns it captures. The topography where the gunpowder is spread can also be 
manipulated, enabling us to replicate the gradation levels of the volumetric box. Sand is used as a 
manipulable and reusable base material. 
To avoid competing visually with the artwork, the resulting wall piece is fragmented, rearranged into a 
new pattern. This process recreates the atmosphere of an explosion in the space without a direct 
visual connection to the artwork itself. Now, we step into the Impact. 
 
 
The Production Process 
 
1. Steel Structure: 
 
A steel framework for four walls and a ceiling is constructed on the ground. 
The structure is divided into segments and includes spacers for the glass panels. 
 
2. Topography Setup: 
Spacers deﬁne the topography. Sand is ﬁlled in and leveled according to the plan. 
Gunpowder is evenly distributed across the surface and covered with paper. This ensures a 
controlled explosion. 
 
3. Surface Preparation: 
The ground becomes a “lava ﬁeld.” Glass panels are laid step-by-step, doubling as bridges 
over the “lava” during preparation. 
 
4. Explosion Process: 
One wall is exploded at a time, creating a “family” of panels. After the explosion, panels are 
classiﬁed into three gradation levels: Level 1: Completely dim. Level 2: Moderately dim. 
Level 3: Almost milky translucent. The process is repeated for all walls and the ceiling. 
 
5. Assembly: 
The ceiling and walls are erected into a stable structure. Glass panels are installed according 
to the planned pattern.
