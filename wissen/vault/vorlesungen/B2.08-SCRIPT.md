Countercultural Experiments
with Urbanity
By the mid-1960s, the welfare state, consumer culture, 
and the Cold War had become dominant logics that 
strongly influenced the conception and realization of 
the architecture of the city. Several European welfare 
states conceived of their role as the main providers 
(l’état providence) of new built environments. Politicians 
and developers collaboratively constructed the city, 
from housing to infrastructure. At the same time mass 
consumer culture gained importance, turning the 
dwelling space into an accumulation of disposable 
consumer products. At a geo-political level, Cold War 
politics influenced how urban designers and architects 
thought about the built environment. Though both East 
and West defended the idea of modernization in the built 
environment, the methods and tools that they deployed 
to achieve it differed radically. These dominant logics of 
welfare state, consumer culture, and the Cold War elicited 
strong reactions: they provoked a counter culture. Artists, 
writers, cultural critics, and activists made attempts to 
define an alternative culture with values, mores, and 
practices that ran counter to those of the established 
society. Informed by these countercultural movements, 
during the 1960s and 1970s architects and urban 
designers sought to devise alternative urban solutions 
to contemporary building and dwelling practices.
Imagining an Alternative Urban Frame for 
Growth and Change
In The Netherlands, the artist Constant Nieuwenhuys 
reacted strongly to the overdetermined logics of the 
market and state that had entered the urban environment 
with his ‘New Babylon’ project, which he initiated in 
1956 and continued to work on until 1969. Constant’s 
hypothesis is that ongoing industrialization will free man 
from his labor duties, and offer citizens the possibility to 
engage differently with architecture and the city. Inspired 
by the Dutch historian Johan Huizinga, the inhabitant of 
New Babylon is a homo ludens, a citizen that is liberated 
from work and that invents and reinvents the modes and 
forms of dwelling in the city every day. In order to allow 
for this continuous reinvention, Constant conceives of 
the city as a labyrinthine space. In a series of paintings, 
sketches, and architectural models, he describes 
the shape of a post-revolutionary city composed of 
transformable structures, including ladders, stairs, 
and platforms. 
The Hungarian-French architect Yona Friedman 
worked in a similar vein for his ‘Ville Spatiale’ (1958–62), 
which he considered an alternative to the inflexibility 
of the existing architectural stock in cities, and the 
incapacity of architects and urban designers to serve 
the needs of individual citizens. Instead, Friedman 
aimed to provide a ‘mobile architecture’: a system of 
construction that allowed each citizen to determine 
the form, orientation, and character of their dwelling, 
and to change these elements at any time. In ‘La Ville 
Spatiale’, Friedman proposes the use of massive, 
multi-level space-frame structures hovering several 
stories above existing urban and natural territories. 
These space frames are supported by enormous 
columns, which would provide access to the elevated 
area and be the conduit for access and delivery. The 
framework would be filled in with prefabricated and 
small-scale units, whose positioning, density, and use 
could be adjusted. This system would allow citizens 
to construct and reconstruct their habitat endlessly. 
For Friedman, the citizen was the designer, while the 
role of the urban designer or architect was mainly 
technical and informational. In ‘La Ville Spatiale’, the 
urban designer creates no final product, but rather 
initiates a process of continuous construction through 
the conception of the specific elements of the spaceframe. 
Consumer Culture Logics Subverting 
Urban Design
The English architects of Archigram adopted a 
radically different position. In order to design an 
alternative city, they fully embraced the ephemerality 
of consumer culture. Their proposal for an ‘Instant City’ 
(1968–70) uses the imagery and fast temporalities of 
consumer culture for the design of a new urban logic. 
‘Instant City’ is conceived as a ‘traveling metropolis’ 
which, for a short time, superimposes new spaces for 
leisure, culture, communication, and education onto

an existing city. It is brought to the towns by a fleet 
of all-terrain vehicles, air ships, and helicopters. It 
infiltrates existing cities with a new audio-visual 
environment (of words and images projected 
onto suspended screens) and mobile forms of 
dwelling (tents, pods, and mobile homes) that are 
supported by a lightweight technology (cranes and 
scaffolding). ‘Instant City’ arrives on a site, creates 
an event, and then disappears, thereby signifying 
that urban design does not have to aim for immediate 
and permanent construction, but can be simply an 
event, an action in the present which redefines the 
future. 
The Italian architects of the Archizoom group 
critically reflected upon how modernization in the 
1960s, with its new technologies and infrastructures, 
had quickly turned the entire world in a fully manmade and inhabitable territory. Their project for 
the ‘No Stop City’ (1969–71) explores the idea of 
the disappearance of a more conventional form of 
architecture within such a condition. The organization 
of this ‘endless city’ was inspired by the factory and 
supermarket: it has a neutral, equal, and continuous 
structure, punctuated by a repetitive pattern of 
technological hubs which provide the citizen 
connections to the power and communication grid. 
‘No-Stop City’ is a multi-layered construct of generic 
interior spaces, equipped with artificial lighting and 
air conditioning. It enables citizens to set up new 
lightweight dwelling forms which are composed of 
unexpected assemblages of commodities, artefacts, 
and natural elements. With ‘No-Stop City’, Archizoom 
provides a model for a city which is dedicated to the 
continuous flow of information and energy, that not 
only redefines the architecture of dwelling, but also 
the forms of urban association and community.
Emancipating Citizens through 
Self-Build Initiatives
In parallel to these ‘paper projects’, several built 
examples of ‘counter projects’ also emerged during 
the 1960s and 1970s, which sought to devise 
alternative solutions to contemporary building 
and dwelling practices. In London, the Germanborn British architect Walter Segal, for instance, 
developed 
a 
self-build 
method—the 
‘Segal 
Method’—that enabled ordinary people to design 
and build their own houses quickly and cheaply 
using readily available (off-the-shelf) materials. Segal 
insisted that his method was “an attitude of mind 
rather than a system of construction”, as it strove 
for a more humane architecture that gave citizens 
greater freedom, autonomy, and self-determination 
in the construction of their homes. In this respect, 
the ‘Segal Method’ responded to the spirit of its time. 
In the mid-1970s, the ‘Segal Method’ was adopted 
by Lewisham Council—Lewisham is a borough of 
London—for an experimental self-build housing 
project. Impressed by Segal’s ideas, the Council 
offered those on the waiting list for council housing 
the opportunity to build their own house using the 
‘Segal Method’ on a designated plot in the borough. 
In all, twenty-seven houses were completed as 
part of this experimental project, and, in memory 
of Segal, the two streets in Lewisham where these 
houses are located were named after him: Segal 
Close and Walters Way.
In America, the countercultural movement became 
very strong: the Vietnam War, the Cold War, the 
sexual revolution, the Civil Rights Movement, etc. 
all fueled a myriad of countercultural initiatives, 
only a few of which ‘solidified’ into built form. Many 
of these projects were also inspired by the ‘backto-the-land’ movement, as they aimed to offer 
inhabitants a greater degree of self-sufficiency, 
autonomy, and sense of community than could be 
found in the dominant way of life, which was mostly 
taking place in America’s sprawling suburbs. The 
most emblematic, and best-known, example of a 
countercultural ‘city’ to emerge in the US was Drop 
City outside of Trinidad in Colorado. Founded in 1965 
by four art students from the University of Kansas 
(Clark Richert, Richard Kallweit, Gene Bernofsky, 
and JoAnn Bernofsky), Drop City was a place where

creative people could share meals and ideas. The 
design of the buildings at Drop City was inspired 
by the geodesic dome that Buckminster Fuller had 
designed for the American pavilion at Expo 67. These 
domes were considered joint works of art, as each 
family or group would develop a design for their own 
dome, which was then constructed collectively by 
the Drop City community. This praxis of collective 
construction made Drop City an experiment in 
social transformation. The Drop City dwellings were 
also inspired by vernacular and nomadic forms of 
dwelling (such as tipis and yurts), as its residents 
were fascinated with the impermanence associated 
with such dwelling forms and the limited impact that 
these structures had on the Earth. Drop City was thus 
also founded on a concern for the environment, and 
the residents engaged in experiments with passive 
solar energy. For instance, they constructed a solar 
collector and an air loop rock storage heater on site. 
The desire to find alternative, countercultural ways 
of building and dwelling was popular not only in 
Europe and the United States, but also elsewhere. A 
good example is the ‘Open City’ or Ciudad Abierta, 
founded in Chile by a group of professors from the 
School of Architecture and Design at the Pontificia 
Universidad Católica de Valparaíso in 1970. Like 
Drop City, the Open City was conceived as a place 
free of any institutional constraints, and one of its key 
goals was to encourage encounter and stimulate the 
exchange of ideas. To this end, several agoras were 
built in the Open City, and all the dwellings that were 
constructed on site (often with recycled materials) 
were conceived as hospederias (guest houses) 
rather than homes. The founders of the Open City 
rejected the notion of private property, and so, 
more than offering a living space or a home, the 
hospederias were designed to receive guests and 
thus promote the exchange of ideas.
Against Suburbia
During the 1960s and 1970s, several countercultural 
projects were also designed that sought to develop 
alternatives to ingrained dwelling practices. The 
‘Sea Ranch’ in Sonoma County (California) is one 
such example. Although initiated by a developer, 
Alfred Boeke, this housing estate was devoted 
to finding an alternative way of dwelling that was 
more respectful of the environment, and that would 
present an antidote to American suburbia, which he 
believed to be inauthentic, individualistic, and driven 
by consumerism. In the mid-1960s, Boeke appointed 
the landscape architect Lawrence Halprin to devise 
a site plan for ‘Sea Ranch’. Having lived on a kibbutz 
near Haifa in Israel, Halprin was enamored with the 
idea of a shared open space and communal forms of 
living, and it was these principles that he translated 
into the urban design for ‘Sea Ranch’. For instance, 
all housing units were to be set back 100 feet from 
the coast to keep the coastline open and accessible 
to all, and all housing units constructed on the site 
were to be clustered to keep the meadows open as 
a commons. Halprin was also motived by ecological 
concerns. He wanted to preserve the natural 
beauty of the site, and to this end, he produced 
a planting guide for owners, which, for instance, 
specified that plants should “avoid prettiness”, 
have no “conspicuous blooming performance”, and 
maximize the site’s “rugged character”. One of the 
earliest (and most famous) housing clusters realized 
at ‘Sea Ranch’ was Condominium One, designed by 
MLTW.
Urban Ecology
Countercultural projects that emerged during the 
1960s and 1970s were often informed by concerns 
for the environment. One of the champions of the 
need to build in a more environmentally sensitive 
way was Paolo Soleri, an Italian-born architect 
who spent most of his life and career in the US. 
Following on from earlier architectural experiments 
with the concept of ‘cosmic potentials’—the idea 
that energy should be produced where it is used—
and his experience with building his own dwelling/
working complex from 1955 (called Cosanti), Soleri 
devised the concept of ‘arcology’—a portmanteau

of ‘architecture’ and ‘ecology’. In 1969, he 
published a book, entitled Arcology: The City in 
the Image of Man, which explained the concept 
in greater detail. It not only provided a theoretical 
explanation regarding the moral, philosophical, 
and economic necessity for humanity to transition 
to arcological urban landscapes, but proposed 
thirty possible arcologies; i.e., cities designed using 
Soleri’s arcological principles. One example of 
such a theoretical arcological urban development 
proposed by Soleri was ‘Mesa City’. This design 
proposal expressed several of Soleri’s arcological 
principles: (1) It was to be built in arid highland, 
because the inhospitable conditions would make 
construction difficult and thus generate respect for 
nature; (2) The energy for the city should be derived 
from energy sources in the surrounding nature; 
(3) The city should work against discrimination, 
and was to accommodate a synergy of different 
ethnicities; and (4) The city was to have a very 
high density—it was destined to house two million 
inhabitants on about 100 acres—as Soleri believed 
that higher life forms, such as bees, live in organized 
and dense settlements, while lower life forms, such 
as corals, are more spread out. In 1970, Soleri 
founded an experimental city in the Sonora Desert 
(in Arizona) called ‘Arcosanti’, which was to test 
some of the ‘arcological’ principles that he had been 
developing. The city was intended for 5,000 people, 
and purposely built in an extreme environment to 
show that sustainable living is possible anywhere, 
and that his arcological model could thus be easily 
replicated. But Arcosanti was intended to be more 
than ‘just’ an experiment in building an ecological 
city. Based on the theory that the shape of the 
city could change human behavior, Arcosanti was 
conceived as an urban laboratory for the discovery of 
new ways of human living. At Arcosanti, inhabitants 
were to denounce consumerism, and lead a life of 
frugality. This, Soleri believed, would increase the 
so-called ‘urban effect’, meaning that quality of life 
would improve, and that citizens’ creativity would 
increase.
