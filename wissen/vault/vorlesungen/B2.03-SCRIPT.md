Cities and Ideologies: 
Building for Healthy Minds 
in Healthy Bodies
Housing the Modern Citizen
While in the beginning of the twentieth century the 
idea of better housing was still a matter of isolated 
experiments, the 1920s and 1930s witnessed a generalization of this concern. The socialist concepts for 
collective housing that were initiated by enlightened 
organizations and governments in cities like Vienna 
and Zürich were now also introduced in other parts 
of the world, and paralleled a broader search for 
an ‘existenzminimum’ (Karel Teige, “The Minimum 
Dwelling”, 1932). The urban environment became 
increasingly the subject of reform programs in which 
the improvement of the architecture of the city was 
directly related to specific ideologies and ideas 
about social progress. Newly developed neighborhoods and buildings were believed to create the 
healthy community of which many were dreaming. 
Across Europe, large housing neighborhood programs were initiated that not only provided dwelling 
space, but also relied upon architecture to define 
new, modern communities. 
In Frankfurt, the city architect and urban planner 
Ernst May initiated the Das Neue Frankfurt program, 
which situated the new housing estates (Siedlungen) in a broader cultural ambition of creating new 
communities and new patterns of life. May imagined 
a new Frankfurt, with a new metropolitan culture, in 
which rationality and functionality would be the main 
means of achieving a new social balance. He assumed that everyone had equal dwelling needs and 
that these could be met through large-scale building interventions. As a result, he maintained, society 
would become more social, with the ultimate goal of 
creating a classless society. May’s Siedlungen provide a unique combination of mass housing within 
a larger productive landscape that should secure 
the autarky of the single family, and combined these 
with a large array of community facilities. 
New Siedlung programs were also developed in 
Berlin, where landscape elements and architectural 
color were used to negotiate the tension between 
collectivity and individuality. In 1926, the architect 
Bruno Taut, together with Hugo Häring and Otto Rudolph Salvisberg, designed the Onkel Toms Hütte 
estate. Financed by the GEHAG (Gemeinnützige 
Heimstätten-, Spar- und Bau-Aktiengesellschaft, 
‘Housing Cooperative for Savings and Construction’), Taut envisaged an ideal society, which would 
be classless, and at one with nature. On a 12-hectare site, he conceived 1,100 multi-family homes divided into various types. Designed in a modernist 
and rational idiom, Taut tried to avoid monotony and 
anonymity by proposing a varied and sophisticated 
color scheme and by introducing specific architectural details such as asymmetric windows. In keeping with the architects’ intention to bring inhabitants 
closer to nature, vegetation plays a key role in the 
estate. In this Waldsiedlung (Forest Estate), low-rise 
housing is amalgamated with trees that not only cast 
dramatic shadows, but also incorporate the individual houses in a larger collective landscape.
At the end of the 1920s, Berlin’s city architect Martin Wagner commissioned the design of a new residential estate to six architects: Walter Gropius, Hans 
Scharoun, Otto Bartning, Hugo Häring, Fred Forbát, 
and Paul Rudolf Henning. They were asked to create 
affordable housing for the employees of the nearby Siemens plant—resulting in 1,379 apartments 
with two or two-and-a-half rooms. The main aim of 
Siemensstadt was to provide an alternative to the 
so-called Mietskaserne (rental barracks) with their 
gloomy backyards that predominated in Berlin. The 
buildings were intended to provide all tenants with 
the same conditions: equal in size, with the same 
incidence of light and fresh air. Hans Scharoun was 
responsible for the overall urban design of Siemensstadt. He envisaged a completely new type of estate for Berlin in which he could realize his idea of a 
good neighborhood. Scharoun pursued the idea of 
a city with many open green spaces, in which residents would find a place for recreation, experience 
a sense of community, and feel at home between the 
buildings which framed their world.

In the Dutch city of Rotterdam, the architect Michiel 
Brinkman engaged with this same tension between 
the individual dwelling and the collective in his urban design for the Spangen district. In 1919, he designed 273 dwellings in the Justus van Effen complex in a neighborhood that was characterized by 
dense urban blocks. Brinkman conformed to this 
urban morphology with a large perimeter block of 
147 by 85 meters, encircling a courtyard containing a few smaller housing blocks and a central taller 
service building with a central heating plant, baths, 
and cycle shelter. A significant urban design feature was the use of an ‘upper street’ (bovenstraat), a 
raised walkway along the inner edge of the block at 
third-story level. These ‘streets in the air’ were a typological invention to allow for more social encounters between the different inhabitants, and thus to 
mitigate the anonymity of mass housing. Most housing units are entered from the inner courtyard. The 
maisonettes at the higher floors are reached from 
the ‘upper street’, while the lower units are accessed 
at ground level and have their own garden. 
Locating mass housing in more familiar urban structures was also a concern for the French architect 
Albert Laprade in his project for the new Medina 
in Casablanca, in which traditional urban elements 
were activated to provide the mass housing estates 
with a familiar sense. Laprade, a Beaux-Arts architect, meticulously studied the vernacular elements 
of the traditional Moroccan medina. From this perspective, he deliberately designed an urban space 
that was distinctively Moroccan in a Neo-Moorish architecture, while simultaneously providing new spatial configurations and technologies. Laprade maintained that traditional ornaments and urban forms 
held intrinsic ‘values of ambiance’ that contributed 
to the social character of urban space. Within the 
frame of vernacular urban architecture, he planned 
an intensely modern neighborhood, which combined mass housing with important collective units, 
such as schools, mosques, a cinema, and market 
spaces.
Mass housing was, however, not only a concern 
in Europe and North Africa, but also became one 
of the main focus points in other geographic locales. Among the most prominent examples is the 
Dom-Kommuny housing program that was developed in Russia in the 1920s, following the Russian 
Revolution (1917) and foundation of the Russian 
Soviet Republic—the world’s first constitutionally socialist state with the ideology of Communism. The 
Dom-Kommuny conflated architectural design with 
social programming, allowing architects to view their 
work as the creation of a new way of life. One of the 
best-known examples of the Dom-Kommuny housing program is the Narkomfin compound in Moscow 
by Mosei Ginzburg and Ignaty Milinis (1928–29). 
Designed for the workers of the Ministry of Finance, 
this reinforced concrete building was divided into 
residential and communal sections. It was raised 
on pilotis and striated by ribbon windows. Another prominent, but lesser-known, example is the 
‘Checkist Village’ in Ekaterinburg by Ivan Antonov 
and Veniamin Sokolov (1929–36). In these projects, 
new housing typologies were developed that radically recalibrated the relation between the individual 
dwelling cell and the collective aspects of housing. 
Leisure Infrastructure for Nourishing Mind 
and Body
The emphasis in the new Russian Soviet Republic 
was, however, not only on housing. Apart from experimental housing complexes with ‘minimum dwellings’, new collective facilities were also constructed, 
designed to create healthy citizens, both in mind 
and body, and which—most importantly—were for 
a community of citizens who shared a strong belief 
in the Soviet ideology. Notable examples include the 
Dinamo Sports Complex in Ekaterinburg, as well as 
the building program for the construction of ‘workers’ clubs’ in cities, towns, and villages across the 
Soviet Republic. These were centers for entertainment and political education, and generally included 
a theater hall, classrooms, meeting halls, and sports 
equipment. One of the most prominent examples is 
the Rusakov (workers’) club in Moscow, which was 
designed by Konstantin Melnikov and constructed 
between 1927 and 1930.
In Nazi Germany, the idea to build leisure facilities to 
shape strong citizens (both in mind and body), who 
could support the Nazi ideology and (eventually) fight 
in the War, also took root. A key organization in this respect was Kraft durch Freude (Strength through Joy), 
which was a sub-organization of the German Labor 
Front (DAF). Their Volkswagen project and short- and 
long-distance travel were among the most important 
activities of this organization, and dovetailed neatly 
with Hitler’s Autobahn project, which was initiated 
around the same time that Kraft durch Freude was 
founded (ca. 1933). The comprehensive activities 
of this organization were primarily aimed at integrating the working classes into the Volksgemeinschaft. 
Kraft durch Freude events were intended to provide 
relaxation and regeneration to increase work performance. By offering access to previously bourgeois 
privileges (such as holidaying), Kraft durch Freude 
ultimately served the idea of a classless society in 
the sense of a National Socialist national community.
One of the best-known built initiatives of Kraft durch 
Freude is Prora, a massive beach resort on the island 
of Rügen, built between 1936 and 1939 following the 
designs of Clemens Klotz. This complex consisted of 
eight identical buildings, and stretched for 4.5 kilometers in a sandy arc along the Baltic Sea, parallel to 
the beach. This elongated structure was intended to 
ensure that all rooms had a sea view, while corridors 
faced the land side. Life in the holiday resort was to 
take place in the community, following the totalitar-

Cities and Territories as Forms of Power
From the mid-1930s, the capital cities of Germany 
and Russia were rebuilt (or planned to be rebuilt) to 
express the power of their new leaders, Hitler and 
Stalin, respectively. Hitler, for instance, planned to 
rebuild Berlin as the imperial capital; the center of a 
great Germanic world empire—the Third Reich—and 
called his new capital city ‘Germania’. Determined to 
demonstrate the power of Adolf Hitler’s vision for the 
future of Nazi Germany, the architect Albert Speer 
produced many plans for the monumental redevelopment and reconstruction of Berlin, which include 
a massive Volkshalle. Drawing inspiration from the 
Roman Pantheon, this building was to become the 
architectural centerpiece of Berlin as the world capital, both in its size and symbolism.
A similar tendency towards urban monumentality in 
the name of ideology emerged in Russia in the same 
period. From the 1930s, great emphasis was placed 
on Moscow, which, in 1918, following the Russian 
Revolution, had (once again) become the capital city. By the mid-1930s, the urban population of 
Moscow had almost doubled, and the city had fallen 
into disrepair. Urban infrastructure lagged far behind its growth, and basic services, such as urban 
transport, had improved little since the Revolution. In 
1935, a plan for the ‘Reconstruction’ of Moscow was 
put forward, to transform the city from a decrepit urban center into a global metropolis, able to express 
the power and superiority of the Russian communist 
regime. The key elements of this plan were large urban boulevards, which were set out in a radial and 
concentric pattern (with the Kremlin and Red Square 
as the point of convergence), and the construction 
of new monumental buildings that offered communal 
functions for Soviet citizens. These buildings were 
placed at large squares and intersections, and no 
longer followed the experimental, constructivist design that was commonly used in the construction 
of the Dom-Kommuny complexes, but rather drew 
on classical precedents, resulting in what has been 
called a ‘Socialist Classicism’. One of the most striking projects that was proposed for Moscow in this 
period (though never realized) was Palace of the Soviets by Boris Mikhailovich Iofan (1934), and the accompanying Boulevard of the Palace of the Soviets. 
A symbol and symptom of its epoch, the Palace of 
the Soviets expressed the confluence of authoritarian despotism and utopian dreaming that characterized Stalin’s 1930s. Perpetually in construction, but 
never built, it was to be a soaring monument to Soviet power, and gave utopian form to the centralized, 
hierarchical, and bureaucratic apparatus of Soviet 
socialism.
