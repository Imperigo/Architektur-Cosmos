## S. 2

Senses
You Enter the Room
First, your sense of smell kicks in.
A strong, pervasive oduor of burnt materials 
and sulfur oxide lingers thickly in the air. You 
experience a mixture of contradictory feelings: 
curiosity and dread, order and chaos, joy and 
sorrow, peace and restlessness.
Your sense of sight draws you back to the room 
you’re about to explore. The artwork White Tone by 
Cai Guo-Qiang is barely visible through the layers 
of cracked and fogged glass. On it, you see traces 
of soot in the form of whirls that intensify the 
deeper you peer into the structure.
You step forward, leaving footprints behind. Like 
snow, particles of smoke have settled on the floor, 
covering the wooden walkway with a layer of sulfur 
dust.
As you move, you lose yourself in layers of 
fractured glass. The outside world blurs behind 
suspended panes, their surfaces webbed with 
cracks radiating outward, as if from a sudden, 
powerful force. A fine layer of soot clings to the 
glass, gathering more heavily with each layer, like 
a faint memory of smoke. Signs of intense heat 
appear—bubbles and molten patches that warp the 
glass, further distorting your surroundings. Each 
fractured pane, marked by the force that shattered 
it, seems to guide you toward a single focal point: 
White Tone.
The light begins to fade to a bright, colorless tone, 
refracted countless times and reflecting an even 
balance of all visible light frequencies.
Standing on the final platform, whose materiality 
now feels closer to charcoal than wood, you gaze 
at Cai’s artwork. The room feels boundless, with 
no clear end or scale. You feel as if you’re floating 
in cosmic dust. As you’re looking at Cai’s artwork 
unseen forces seem to heighten your ambiguous 
feelings of curiosity and dread, order and chaos, 
joy and sorrow, peace and restlessness, creation 
and destruction, life and death.
