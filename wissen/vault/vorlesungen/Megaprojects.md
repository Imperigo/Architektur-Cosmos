# Megaprojects

## S. 1

CHAPTER 4
THE PETTY POLITICS OF A
MEGAPROJECT IN BUDAPEST
Judit Bodnar and Judit Veres
This chapter looks at the changing politics of urban redevelopment in a
politically divided democratic regime following the end of state socialism
in 1989. It contrasts the emergence of two cultural institutions of national
importance, the Palace of Arts and the National Theatre, as part of
a megaproject in Budapest. They emerged almost at the same time as
part of the Millennium City Center, a large-scale urban redevelopment
project, but have come to stand for two radically opposed worlds dividing
the nation and pitting against each other – the cosmopolitans and the
nationalists. The research design is that of incorporated comparison; the
two case studies are embedded in the analysis of the larger redevelopment
project. The study mixes primary and secondary sources; draws on
interviews, extensive discussions with architects and planners, as well as
an analysis of planning documents, expert reports, and media coverage. It
describes the dynamics of private–public partnerships in urban politics
pointing to the changing role of the post-socialist state and the new power
relations among the various groups involved in urban development in a
newly democratizing regime. On the one hand, the analysis shows how
local and national-scale political ﬁghts make sense from a larger
political–economic perspective of waterfront regeneration; on the other,
it argues that party politics in politically divided regimes have serious

INTRODUCTION
There are two new iconic buildings in Budapest, the Palace of Arts (MuPa)1
and the National Theater, facing each other on the left bank of the Danube
(Figs. 1 and 2). They emerged almost at the same time as part of the
Millennium City Center, a large-scale urban redevelopment project, but have
come to stand for two radically opposed worlds dividing the nation and
driving yet another rift between the cosmopolitans and the nationalists.
While self-professed cosmopolitan citizens with a reﬁned sense of aesthetic
and political criticism see MuPa as a high-quality truly cosmopolitan art
venue in a forward-looking piece of architecture, the National Theater
immediately evokes associations of petty party politics and feuds. For them
the theater stands as the incarnation of right wing resistance to globalization
and leftist politics encased in an anachronistic architectural design which
aspires to be national. Politically more conservative citizens appreciate the
beauty and grandeur of the National Theater and have no strong sentiments
about the Palace of Arts. Frequent visitors to the Palace of Arts rarely
make trips to the Theater. Yet both audiences are a new addition to this part
of Budapest. No one would have thought that elegantly dressed staunch
followers of high culture would attend concerts and art performances in a
formerly dilapidated industrial part of the city, which was still crisscrossed by
industrial tracks until 1963. Quite a change compared to the former location
of the Ludwig Museum for Contemporary Art, which moved from the
uniquely located and accordingly priced inner-city Castle District to MuPa.
The move itself was a ministerial decision but followed from the broader
history of changes in the urban landscape, the revaluation of urban land,
the shifting emphases in urban development, and the reconﬁguration of
the local and national state amid the turbulence of ending state socialism.

It is the emergence of the Millennium City Center and the shifting
environment of urban development that this chapter excavates from the two
cultural complexes that stand on the Danube embankment today. They
both have convoluted histories but in different ways. It is precisely these
differences displayed as part of the same larger scheme through which some
general features of the politics of megaprojects unfold along with the
variations of the genre. The Millennium City Center is indeed a large-scale
urban development project with an iconic design component that aims to
transform radically the image of a part of the city in the collaboration of
public and private actors (Introduction). It is what we call a megaproject but
it is decidedly an ordinary one. The Millennium City Center is a mediumscale undertaking with moderate ambitions. While transnational capital is
involved, its national component takes priority in the process which is not
perceived as a catalyst for world economic growth. We are witnessing the
emergence of a mixed-use development scheme with a dominant role cast to
culture but with no aspiration to replicate the Guggenheim Museum’s
paradigmatic story in Bilbao. While the terms of the success the Millennium
City Center has pursued are also set by global political–economic exigencies
and aesthetics, local historical legacies, and political considerations have an
even more formative role. The functional and ﬁnancial determinations of the
prominent buildings overrule the restrained iconic design component, which
has not become a ﬂagship of urban renewal anchoring a local growth
machine. The Budapest case demonstrates the difﬁculties of the scalar
politics of megaprojects under democratic regimes that tend to be politically
deeply divided. This ordinary project not only points at variations across
space and time, it also softens the rapture between megaprojects and largescale urban developments of the past, and in general, calls for a closer
historical look at large-scale urban transformation projects.
The analysis follows a research design of incorporated comparisons
and proceeds through three parallel stories that are intertwined: the story of
the megaproject of the Millennium City Center, and those of its two most
visible public function institutions, the Palace of Arts and the National
Theater.

In 1991 the ﬁrst steps were made to reclaim the derelict site when 20 acres
were proposed as the site of the exhibition pavilions. In 1994 both the
district and the municipality accepted the detailed regulation plan for this
area – a plan that was kept up to this day, despite the fact that an entirely
new development took place at the site. As the then chief architect later
confessed in an interview, there were months-long negotiations about the
necessity of a planning competition for the area given the fact that this was a
unique stretch of land in Europe insofar as it lay in the center of the city and
it was virtually empty. In the end, for fear of the municipality reducing
the density of the built-in area no planning competition was organized
(Va´ radi & Pet+ocz, 2005). In essence, the project offered a safe process of
redevelopment as it was originally state-owned and unoccupied. It did not
require the razing of vibrant neighborhoods, no ugly scenes of displacement,
sustained citizen opposition, or ensuing compensation trials were in sight.
Megaprojects are often scaled back or cannot be brought to completion
due to resident groups’ opposition, and for this reason tend to develop
along the lines of least political resistance (Altshuler & Luberoff, 2003).
By all accounts, a redevelopment plan that turns a derelict industrial
area into a thriving mixed-use site and a showcase for post-socialism as
the Expo promised to be must have looked a rational and widely acceptable idea. Why is it then still unﬁnished, has the aura of failure, and
evokes only associations of uncertainties, betrayals, and difﬁculties at a
mega scale?
This area between the two southernmost bridges was for a longtime
regarded as a damned site. In the wake of the decision to prime the land, the
freight depot of the Hungarian State Railways had been cleared and new
infrastructure had been built for billions of forints with the hope that with
the coming world exhibition many developers would ﬁnd the area a good
investment opportunity. Although it is difﬁcult to detect a very clear and
consistent party pattern in support of or against the Expo, a few weeks after
a new government came into power in 1994, the Parliament voted to cancel
the event. By this time only three sites had been sold and the remaining area
went to the State Holding Company, which called for an international
tender for the remaining 12 sites. TriGranit won the competition but after
the socialist–liberal coalition formed a government, the State Holding
Company declared the bid ineffectual and called again for a tender.
TriGranit’s subsidiary won the bid for 3.59 billion forints5 for the second
time in the absence of any other bidders interested in the entire area.
However, the tender again was declared ineffectual. The winning company
ﬁled a complaint to the State Holding Company. After a prolonged silence,

After several allegations concerning the private developer’s partnership
with the state – believed to do a damage to the state – TriGranit, the
development corporation, prepared a material that it made public providing
details of the contracts in relation to the Palace of Arts. Most of the
allegations had to do with the considerably high costs of the project and
consequently the strain on the state budget. The original lease period of
10 years was modiﬁed to 30 years in 2005 to ease the pressure on the budget.
In the report the company insisted that the calculations were not right,7 and
demonstrated that they could have secured a much higher proﬁt had they
simply chosen to keep the money in a bank at an 8% interest rate and at the
end of 30 years walk away with almost 340 billion compared to the 97.7
billion that they would realize according to their contract. The company
concurred that it was clear proof that their philosophy was based on longterm calculations, foregoing short-term windfalls for more lasting gains. As
such, the Millennium City Center, a ‘‘city within a city’’ extensively building
upon cultural and touristic elements was hoped to become a sort of
prototype that the company could sell to other cities. This hope the ﬁrm has
substantiated through its advance in many parts of the world, with its latest
culture-led redevelopment taking shape in China in Fujian province.
In 2006 the State Court of Auditors found that during 2001–2006 the role
of the state in this development was not sufﬁciently thought out, and that
the investment proceeded on the basis of ad hoc goals and that the nominal
value for operating and maintaining the ediﬁce for 30 years amounts to
335.2 billion, a ﬁnancial burden not foreseen at the time of negotiating the
deal (Kultura.hu, 2007). While a public-private partnership (PPP) is a good
construct as it enables the state to engage in infrastructural development
without increasing the budget deﬁcit, Hungarians have not quite learnt
how to master it (Index, 2007). Some of the large PPP projects, such as
MuPa, even though they did not fail, could not deliver the expected results,
and proved unreasonably expensive to the state, concludes the expert
report (ibid.).

had shunned its responsibilities. The minister of culture, for example,

although unique in the Hungarian context at that time, was quite conventional in the West and working quite well, implying that it could work
well in Central Europe too. Several measures were cited as proof that
the state’s interest was safeguarded throughout the process. On the one

The Millennium City Center megaproject, which seemed to offer an easy
ride to planners and developers turned out to be less straightforward,
efﬁcient, or rational than expected, and is more often talked about in terms
of frustration and failure. With parts of it still in the making, it is
understood as a product of ad hoc responses and of murky decisions against
the general backdrop of the post-socialist state in the process of redeﬁning
its scope and function within a capitalist economy. The marginalization of
planning, which is not rare in politically important large-scale redevelopment projects in the neoliberal era (Malone, 1996), was complicated by the
over-politicized nature of the entire project, the political party machinery
at times virtually encroaching upon all aspects of the development. It,
however, did not translate into a state efﬁciently promoting a special project
in a deregulated environment through a public–private development agency,
or a strong local growth machine that saw its chance in a redevelopment
project of national scale. The fragmentation of the state through that of the
institutional setting and party politics brought surprising U-turns and
altering loyalties to the project instead of sustained support.
The Institutional Regulative Context
The post-socialist state, the private developer, the architectural and
planning profession all struggle to redeﬁne the rules of the game and state
their interests in the absence of clear rules or for that matter in the midst of
an overproduction of new regulations and laws in a post-socialist context.
The transition from a state planned economy to a market economy in postsocialist Budapest has created a complex urban planning context where
various spheres of authority and competence are more blurred than before,
and this opens plenty of opportunity for contestation.
Although the municipality of Budapest does not own large tracts of land
any longer and therefore is not involved directly in most urban development
projects, it does play a crucial role in setting the regulatory framework for
these projects. This capacity is, however, signiﬁcantly diminished by the
power of the lower scales.
The post-socialist administrative structure of Budapest’s local government system was established in 1990. Its peculiarity is the two-tier structure
which is made up of the municipal government – owner of public utilities

and responsible for the tasks related to all or large parts of the capital – and
the 23 district governments. Following privatization, the district governments became owners of previously state-owned commercial and retail
units, vacant land, and public housing stock at the same time assuming all
public service functions. Many celebrated the new system of local government as the cornerstone of democracy, but more and more came to see the
disadvantages of the independence of districts, especially in citywide issues
and projects. The two-tier structure often has proved disastrous as the two
scales are in the position to successfully challenge and block each other’s
ideas – the municipality through its zoning authority and the district
government via its right to issue building permits. There is thus a constant
struggle which is not conﬁned to the allocation of the ﬁnancial means of
development, but extends to the allocation of regulatory power. Although a
constant source of frustration for planners, the decentralized system is
difﬁcult to change: the modiﬁcation of the Local Government Act requires
two-third majority in Parliament. In 1994 the law was modiﬁed giving the
municipal government more rights vis-a` -vis strategic planning issues
affecting the whole city (Tosics, 2005). This has not altered the fundamental
setup or the constantly changing allocation of regulatory power on the
ministerial level. Interestingly, it did not change even with the two-third
parliamentary majority secured by the right in the 2010 elections and the
ensuing recentralization drive.
The reconﬁguration of local government took place against the backdrop
of the declining signiﬁcance of the public sector in general, which lost its
previously dominant role in the economy extremely fast. The excellent
geographical location of Budapest, rapid cash privatization, and political
stability all attracted extensive foreign investment (FDI) with more than half
of it coming to the capital (Bodna´ r, 2001). In the mid-1990s local governments were unprepared for investors and in the absence of restrictions large
areas were sold for commercial development (Tosics, 2005). Although
zoning regulations had to be respected, large developers could easily avoid
those regulations by making use of the loopholes in the two-tier local
government system. While in the centralized planning system the state
determined the urban development process from a dominant position, its
present task is to regulate the dominant market processes from a weakened
position. Cuts in central funds and difﬁculties in extracting revenues
from the private sector led to a ﬁnancially strapped public sector, which
nevertheless had to assume increasing responsibilities, such as maintaining
local infrastructure. From 1990 to 2010 Budapest had a liberal, noninterventionist leadership – with the same mayor in power. This leadership

Planning professionals see the ofﬁce of the chief architect, whose position

THE DICTATES OF CULTURAL POLITICS:
THE NATIONAL THEATER
The history of the National Theater has been that of great sentiments and
initiatives. The popular myth holds that its history is the narrative of the
failure to build it. This image of long-dure´ e failure took so strong a hold on
people’s mind regardless of political conviction that it is conveniently
forgotten that Budapest had a National Theater for most of the time. The
idea of erecting such a cultural ediﬁce emerged as part of the nineteenthcentury nation building momentum all over Europe. The National Theater
opened in 1837 yet the idea resurfaced many times following the demolition
of its ﬁrst building in 1913. The National Theater became ‘‘homeless,’’
moved from one transitory arrangement to the next with the promise of
ﬁnally getting a proper location and building. Its importance, however, has
been too great to let the institution settle. Whenever the proposed location
moved again, minor political storms accompanied.
In 1965, following the demolition of the building that housed the National
Theater, it was decided in a competition to erect the new building in the City
Park only to have another competition for a new location in 1988. This time

A SMALL CHRONICLE OF SERENDIPITIES: THE
PALACE OF ARTS (MUPA)
A construction company of the TriGranit development corporation began
the building of the Palace of Arts in March 2002 and ﬁnished it exactly three
years later in 2005. The 700,000-square-foot cultural center houses the
Ludwig Museum of Contemporary Arts, the Be´ la Barto´ k National Concert
Hall with the resident National Philharmonic, and the Festival Theater that
is home to the National Dance Ensemble. It was the ﬁrst public-private
partnership construction ever in the region with a cash-strapped but
culturally aspiring government and a developer who had grown strong and
equally ambitious in shaping the city. The agreement was signed under the
center-right FIDESZ government, construction started and was completed
under the socialists. The complex is owned by the Hungarian state, the costs
of development originally were to be paid back to the developer in 10 years,
which as we recounted was later modiﬁed to 30 years.
The Palace of Arts is not an example of ‘‘starchitecture’’ (Sklair, 2006)
and it did not aspire at becoming one. As its architects confessed it did not

From Petty Politics to a Global Model
The intertwining of public and private is one of the deﬁning characteristics
of megaprojects. In fact, it is not the cooperation of the two but its form
that is new. As it has already been acknowledged with respect to other
megaprojects the distribution of risks and beneﬁts between the public and
private sectors is rather murky in the complicated mixing of the two sectors
(Fainstein, 2008; Orueta & Fainstein, 2008). Moreover, by including a
plurality of functions in an area and the copresence of public and private
that refers not only to the actors but the designation of spaces and functions,
contemporary megaprojects exhibit an unusual urban complexity that is not
easy to coordinate (Bruttomesso, 2001). This is exactly what proved
controversial and mobilized local sentiments in the unfolding of the
Millennium City Center. It is not surprising then that there has been much
criticism around this project, directed against the ambiguous interlocking of
state sources with private capital, against the private developer reaping
much of the proﬁt at the public’s expense and at the weakness of the state to
defend public interests. Similar private–public partnership projects have
stirred comparable sentiments and uproars. The Glass Whale (CET)
purported to become the latest cultural-entertainment venue in Budapest,
and also an iconic piece of architecture inserted into an old rehabbed
warehouse seemed to be left to decay before its opening due to a stalemate
created by the developer, the constructor, the district, and the city
government, and sported banners ‘‘Budapest, Ghost House, Irresponsible
Government, Dying Public Asset’’ for more than 500 days until the state
ﬁnally took it over (Tenczer, 2012; Veres, ms).
Compared to other European countries, urban development in postsocialist Budapest has been more market-oriented, more transnational,
and less regulated by the state (Bodnar & Molnar, 2010). The legalinstitutional framework of projects is characterized by regulation through
contracts and agreements rather than through planning, as the regulative
planning measures are often overridden or circumscribed by momentary
considerations. As usual with bigger megaprojects, the timeline for their
implementation is long (Orueta & Fainstein, 2008). Due to the difﬁculties of
sustaining political will, support, and ﬁnance, megaprojects are risky: prone
to changes in shape, function, and projected costs (Flyvbjerg, Bruzelius, &
Rothengatter, 2003; Introduction to this volume). This development is no
different; it is being completed in several stages, thus the process is
compounded by many reversals, corrections, additions, and mistakes, which
makes any claim to transparency and accountability rather difﬁcult to

maintain in a larger context where public participation is not required in the
approval of major projects. Given that many contracts concern a private
developer, details have been kept secret, secrecy that only feeds into the
widespread feeling that urban development is running amok and that
the entire regulative framework should be put on a new basis. In the absence

The Millennium City Center project is not completed to date and the ﬁnal

CONCLUSIONS
Inspired by the successful and well-publicized waterfront projects of
Baltimore and Boston, waterfronts became the new (or retrieved) frontier
in urban development (Hall, 1991). The mixing of high-density commercial
and residential development with festive leisure activities based on a rise
in disposable incomes is in line with postmodern sensibilities and, more
importantly, with the political economy of late capitalist urban development
(Harvey, 1991). Derelict industrial sites in potentially central but not easily
accessible locations become ripe for redevelopment. The costs of such
brownﬁeld developments are high and even though the expected beneﬁts are
even higher, the scale, complexity, and uncertainty of the project require
that the state lubricate private investment and provide a more secure longterm business environment. The political economy of waterfront regeneration is very simple: if there is no more construction site in the inner city,

it needs to be created, and it is created by the city expanding in the direction
of the water either on reclaimed land or artiﬁcial islands. Building over
existing sites, for example, by covering train tracks and adding another
layer of usable land is driven by similar ambitions. These megaprojects often
start with the advent of an event of national or international importance
that requires relatively large space such as international exhibitions.
Vancouver’s waterfront development came to full gear following Expo ’86
just as Expo ’98 was the original excuse for the redevelopment of the Lisbon
seaside. The Budapest Expo designated two areas across the river as its site,
which was quickly followed by a series of utopian plans to offer a relocation
site to Hong Kong businesses, or at least bring high-rise ofﬁce development
to the island far down south in the river that hosts the now defunct
steel works. A former ﬂood area came to be privatized south of the new
university campus, another industrial site was cleaned up for luxury
residential development far up north, and a small island was completely
redeveloped to accommodate industrial parks, a convention center, and
yuppie entertainment – pushing for the need to reactivate the use of the river
as transportation route.
Since waterfront redevelopment switched gears, the stakes have become
higher, and the new urban frontier is fought not only by economic but also
political means, and the ﬁght has reached the regulatory framework. A
debate concerning high-rises and the ensuing re-regulation of building
height ﬂares up every other year, pitting against each other the hitherto
conservative municipal government and the ambitious developers who aim
to maximize proﬁts by maximizing height and density. The fact that public
access should be provided to the river makes the involvement of the state in
these developments automatic and produces very circumscribed forms of
deal-making between private developers, cash-strapped local governments,
and the no more well-off national state that is nevertheless haunted both by
its former nineteenth-century greatness and its socialist prominence.
What looks from a local point of view a story of serendipities, or a series
of failures and petty political ﬁghts accidentally resulting in a building, from
a comparative perspective reads as a slow coming of a medium-scale
waterfront regeneration project, of which the National Theater and the
Palace of Arts are only a small but highly visible chapter. Regardless of the
political leanings of governing parties waterfront regeneration has been
happening everywhere. The local peculiarity lies in the interpretation of
certain features of megaprojects. The murkiness of the intertwining of public
and private is generally attributed to the 1989 regime change and to what is
perceived as the difﬁculties of a transition from one system to another, in a
