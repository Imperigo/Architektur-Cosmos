Architecture History and Theory I and II
THEME AND APPROACH
The course explores two intertwined stories about Euro­
pean architecture of the period ca. 1500–1850. It tells these 
two stories by discussing buildings and texts. 
The first story is about architects and authors who seek 
to define sound rules and principles for architectural design. 
It is in essence a history of attempts to provide architectural 
design with a sound basis, both in practice and in theory. In 
the period and region that concerns us here, this quest 
unfolds initially as an attempt to design and build architec­
ture in imitation of the Ancients. This story is important, 
because it has defined to an important extent how we here 
today understand what architecture is, what an architect 
should know and do, and how buildings should be designed. 
In other words, the history of the recovery, definition and ap­
plication of the principles of ‘classical’ architecture is, in the 
Western tradition, one of the – if not the – most important 
ways of defining architecture as an art form. In this tradi­
tion, architecture is determined by how it is designed and 
made; that is, all objects generated by the right principles 
are ‘architecture’; architects are those who master these 
principles.
In this story, some key questions and problems keep 
reoccurring: what exactly is ‘ancient architecture’? Where did 
it originate? How can we know it? What are our sources? 
What are its main components? How can we codify, commu­
nicate and teach its principles? What is the value of ancient

examples for the present? How do historical circumstances 
affect how we use classical principles? How does ‘classical’ 
architecture relate to other forms of building and design, like 
the gothic, the vernacular or the non-European? 
The course traces an historical arc with regard to these 
questions, going from the free interpretation of Antiquity in 
the 15th century over the attempts to normalize the principles 
of architecture by means of the five orders and the ensuing 
debates about the authority of Vitruvius and ancient ruins, 
to the increasingly complex understanding of Antiquity itself 
and the development of design systems that distance them­
selves from ancient precedents, or incorporate them in new 
constellations.
Crucial to this story are its limits. These limits can be 
considered internal and external to the problem at hand. 
Looking into the various attempt to define the principles of 
architecture shows how these attempts always ran into 
certain limitations, which essentially revolve around the 
problem of authority: who has the capacity to define, impose 
and verify these principles of architecture? Is it the individu­
al architect, the builder, their patron, society, the individual 
user? These internal limits become an important and explic­
it theme of architectural theory and practice from the second 
half of the 17th century, but they are present in, and intrinsic 
to, all attempts to formulate an architectural position. They 
also point to the external limits of any attempt to establish 
the principles of architecture. If, for instance, architecture is 
defined foremost as a matter of architects, many other

and fundamental figure of thought to express this nexus 
between architecture and the values of society is the origin 
myth. This type of myth presents architecture as an intrinsic 
part of society: no society without architecture, and no 
architecture without society. This double bind legitimizes the 
existence of architecture, and immediately describes its 
function: as the material expression of the very structure of 
society itself. This idea is foundational to the classicist 
notion of architecture explored in our first story, and gives 
rise to notions such as decorum, beauty or character – 
means or instruments to connect buildings to their cultural 
context. These notions hold out the promise that a well-de­
signed building will fulfill its role in society, and be appreci­
ated by all involved.

As we know, things are not that simple; in fact, our 
discussion of for instance character demonstrates how deli­
cate the relationship between architects, their patrons, build­
ings and society is. But there is more to the problem. As our 
discussion of the propagation of neo-classicism as an official 
style for modern government buildings in the US suggests, 
proposing a close bond between architecture and society 
implies specific ideas about what society is, how it should 
function, and who it welcomes. And conversely, as for in­
stance the House of Loreto demonstrates, buildings can 
attract meaning and attention regardless of whether they 
adhere to design principles. Their significance is determined 
by events, stories and myths that engender all kinds of 
behavior and actions in and around buildings that are not in 
the least concerned with beauty, proportion or the five or­
ders. In more general terms, much of what happens in the 
built environment is only marginally concerned with architec­
ture; looking at the history of the built environment through 
the lens of architecture, as we do here, leaves much unseen.
Still, much can be gleaned from architecture about the 
forces and ideas that shape a society. As part of his con­
demnation of the classical tradition, Victor Hugo already 
argued that architecture primarily exists as the expression of 
human thought, and derives its features from how thought 
shapes a society. Even if we don’t have to agree with Hugo’s 
position, his perspective on architecture draws attention to 
its context. Buildings do not express meaning on their own, 
but as elements of an environment, or in dialogue with 
images and texts found in books, plates or, indeed, build­
ings.

14. Versailles: Ceremonial Court and Baroque Palace
INTRODUCTION 
After a lavish construction project carried out from the 
1660s onwards, from 1682 the Palace of Versailles became 
the residence of the French Monarch for more than 100 
years, until 1789. The building was transformed from a hum­
ble hunting lodge into a vast and spectacular palace, made 
of a central U-shaped body flanked by two long wings, 
composed by multiple bodies. The splendor of the Palace of 
Versailles is typical of the French Baroque period, where a 
finely tuned architectural and decorative apparatus coexisted 
with a complex system of social and political protocols and 
rituals, all set around the god-like figure of Louis XIV.

THE GENESIS OF THE PALACE
In 1610, at the age of nine, King Louis XIII of France 
succeeded his murdered father, Henry IV. The actual power 
to rule was assumed by Louis’ mother, Maria de’ Medici, as 
regent, who was later replaced by her advisor, Cardinal 
Richelieu, who in 1624 became Louis XIII’s first minister. 
Two decades later, Louis XIV (1643-1715) became king at 
only five years of age. Similar to his father’s first steps, he 
remained under the guardianship of his mother, Anna of 
Austria, while the government was led by the first minister, 
Cardinal Jules Mazarin. After Cardinal Mazarin’s death in 
1661, a series of intrigues and power struggles unfolded 
around his succession. The general expectation that the 
chief financial director, Nicolas Fouquet, would become 
prime minister was disregarded. On 9 March 1661, the morn­
ing after Mazarin’s death, 18-year old Louis XIV called the 
Council of State and declared he would be his own first 
minister and govern as an absolute monarch.
Louis’ decision was inspired by Richelieu’s last will: ‘If it 
is true that the monarchy more than any other form of 
government imitates the government of God, then one can 
assert that reason dictates that God gives this office to a 
single person who is above all others.’ Louis XIV chose the 
sun as the symbol of his power and declared himself Sun 
King, making his official motto ‘Nec pluribus impar’ (literal­
ly, ‘Not unequal to many’; its actual meaning is obscure), 
which he had printed on the royal medal. The sun metaphor 
was consequently used and reflected in all arts, decoration, 
and architecture related to the king. The palace of Versailles

1. Building Design and Representation
The Baroque palace design, of which Versailles is em­
blematic, evolved in response to two historical developments. 
Firstly, because of a technical revolution in firearms produc­
tion weapons became more destructive while also more avail­
able. As a result, defending a single building became point­
less. Secondly, the consolidation of state sovereignty, or 
absolutism, corresponded to the internal pacification of 
countries through absolutist domination, which in turn 
changed the way in which political centers of power were 
designed.
These two factors led to the separation of the two func­
tions of a palace: defense and representation. At Versailles, 
the task of defense was now taken over by fortresses, built 
around the city or even on the borders of countries, while the 
function of representing the power of the state was moved 
from the fortresses into the palace itself. This was accompa­
nied by deep social transformations, with the nobility turning 
from a warlike feudal elite into a ‘decorative’ court presence. 
In other words, the purpose of a palace changed, and rep­
resentation took the place of defense. This shift in function 
triggered changes in architectural form. 
In the logic of fortification, the most appropriate building 
offers the smallest outer surface to outside attack and the 
smallest radius to internal defense, such as a cubic or cylin­
drical shape. However, the logic of representation demands 
the exact opposite: an extensive front side, a ‘Schaufront’. 
Therefore, the invisible inner courtyard of earlier palaces was 
replaced by a wide court of honor, which was also the main

presenting to the visitor an elongated, windows-rich facade. 
This same tendency of forming facades, outward appearanc­
es, for the purpose of representation found many similarities 
in Baroque fashion, such as the wig.
The ornament of the palace celebrated the military and 
political triumphs of Louis XIV. For example, in the center 
of the eastern facade, which faces the Court of Honor, sculp­
tures of Hercules and Mars flank a clock just above the 
king’s bedroom, in the middle of which the sun appears with 
a halo of rays. Hercules, the founder of the Roman Empire, 
and Mars, the warlord, are allegories of the king who rules 
the world and time, the apparent inheritor of the Roman 
Empire lineage. On the garden side, Louis XIV is celebrated 
as the Prince of Peace and patron of the arts, and Louis in 
the circle of the gods. 
2. Building-Nature Relationship
Another essential characteristic of the Baroque palace is 
its specific relationship to its surroundings, open to the 
grounds. Mostly placed on a flat terrain, Versailles was 
situated at the center of a complex system of avenues, 
gardens, and canals. Oriented along a longitudinal axis, the 
palace and the grounds follow and grow out into nature 
according to this axis.

However, nature is never accepted as it is, but, like the 
palace itself, is the result of a rigorous design process and is 
a part of architecture. Large edge walls extend the interior 
into the open space. Arcades are laid out in complicated 
geometric patterns or even as labyrinths. Bushes and trees 
are trimmed into geometric shapes that decorate the gar­
dens as spheres, cones, pyramids, and columns. Flowery par­
terres with ornamental patterns, also of colored stones, 
gravel, shells, brick, and coal dust, continue the decorative 
floors of the interior out into the exterior. All these shapes 
are artistic products. In a way, they intend to ‘replace’ 
nature, promoting the issue of representativeness and power 
(i.e. who rules nature?). 
The gardens of Versailles are where Apollo, the Sun God 
and a metaphor for the Sun King, created harmony and 
peace. For instance, one of the fountains contains gilded 
sculpture of the handsome Apollo emerging from the water 
to begin his daily trail across the sky. The garden is the 
place where the king takes his rest after his deeds, to in­
dulge in leisure. Therefore, the geometric, axially symmetri­
cal order of buildings and gardens is a symbolic reflection of 
the political and social conditions of the Baroque period. 
The design of the building and garden corresponds to the 
absolutist concept of power: the axially symmetrical arrange­
ment of architecture, and the location of the palace on a 
wide plain in the center of star-shaped routes leading to it, 
is directed toward and reflected in the figure of the absolute 
ruler as the personification of the state.

3. Internal Spaces and Ceremonials
The Baroque palace of Versailles also has new, specific 
features in its internal organization. Especially with regard 
to the ceremonial aspects, two architectural elements are im­
portant.
The Staircase
In the southern wing of the palace of Versailles, the 
queen had her own staircase by which visitors could reach 
the rooms above. In the northern wing was the king’s stair­
case, the so-called Staircase of the Ambassadors. This was 
made of two straight arms running in opposite directions 
and with a common entrance. This staircase was destroyed 
in 1752 on the king’s orders, because of the difficulty of 
maintaining the glass roof and to extend his apartments.
Generally speaking, in the Renaissance the staircase was 
treated more or less as a purely technical problem of con­
necting floors. Staircases usually remained narrow and steep. 
During the Baroque, however, an originally purpose-built 
structure was transformed into an exhibition of abundance 
and a stage for ceremonies.
The Enfilade
The enfilade, a suite of rooms deliberately aligned, played 
an important role in projecting the physical experience of 
Baroque architecture (see chapter 13). In Versailles, the 
Staircase of the Ambassador led to the series of rooms 
comprising the king’s apartment, including the rooms of

Venus, Diana, Mars, and Mercury. This enfilade culminated 
in the Hall of Apollo, where the king received his visitors on 
official occasions on a silver throne 2.6 meters high. Later, 
this throne was moved to the end of the Mirror Gallery, 
constructed for the reception of the Siamese ambassador in 
1686. Finally, with the central relocation of the bedroom in 
1682, the reception was also held in the king’s bedroom.
Indeed, another characteristic of the Baroque palace is 
that there is no functional separation between private and 
official rooms, between the rooms that serve intimate func­
tions and those that serve for festivities and receptions. In 
Versailles, the Royal Apartments were a pivotal component 
for the political function of the building. Designed by Le 
Brun between 1671 and 1681, they were the center for both 
the king’s daily life and his state affairs.
ARCHITECTURE AS RITUAL
Palace as Stage
The palace also functioned as the stage for a sort of 
divine epiphany. The Baroque palace represented the pas­
sages from worldly triumph to a union with the gods, and 
from work to leisure, every day anew. The daily routine of 
the ruler was to be a metaphor of a divine epiphany, like 
sunrise and sunset. 
When the Court and the state administration both moved 
from Paris to Versailles in 1682, the king made the bedroom 
the symbol of his monarchy. Here stood his bed of state,

symbol had disappeared behind the horizon line of the Great 
Canal. In the bedroom, Louis XIV received legations 
through planned rituals. Most days, in the morning and 
evening, the ritual called ‘Lever’ was enacted. At eight in the 
morning the king’s valet, who slept at the foot of the royal 
bed, woke the king up. The curtains were drawn open and 
then the doors opened. One chamber page stood at the door 
and let in the ladies and gentlemen who had earlier been 
granted the privilege of entering. This privilege was strictly 
circumscribed. Five different groups of people were allowed 
to enter, one after the other, in ‘entrées’, or ‘entrances’, from 
those related to the king to his most trusted courtiers and 
members of the nobility. The king’s appearance would 
change at different stages. This ritual of the ‘Lever’ was only 
the first of ceremonies and ritualized activities that tightly 
shaped the day for everyone in the palace: meals, the daily 
mass, meetings with his advisers, even his campaigns, the 
hunt, and his walks in the garden. For instance, the ritual of 
‘Coucher’ took place in a similar way when the kind went to 
bed.
Every action of the King, up to the ‘most inconspicuous 
gesture’, was planned and ritualized in great detail. Addi­

the king, when and where; in what position; whether one or 
two doors were opened when entering; whether the person 
concerned had to sit on a chair or a stool; whether one was 
allowed to keep the hat on, hold it, or leave it outside; how 
one would dress; and so on.

Every aspect of the king’s life was precisely regulated and 
turned into a ritual. While these rituals surprise or amuse us 
today, they should not be dismissed as curiosities. At the 
time, they held a double meaning:
1. A highly symbolic meaning, similar to the one at­
tached to the decorative program of the building 
itself. Louis XIV, as king and absolutist ruler, repre­
sents, performs, and puts on stage a divine order. He 
does so by employing every pictorial or visual method 
available: not only by the display of general splendor, 
pictures, statues, but also by means of actions and 
performances, characteristic of the Baroque.
2. They are used as instruments of power. These various 
actions have no practical purpose. But each act in the 
course of the ceremony conveys precise values of 
prestige, which are communicated to those involved in 
it. These values serve as an indication of the position 
of the individual within the balance of power among 
the courtiers. 
What gave these acts their serious and grave significance 
was their validity: they conveyed actual prestige to those 
involved in them within courtly society, and expressed rela­
tive positions of power, rank, and dignity. In this sense, the 
whole court and courtly life can be described as a constant 
exchange of values. Louis therefore bound the nobility and 
other important persons to Versailles, first spatially, and 
then socially-psychologically, through rituals of etiquette and 
protocol. The king used all of these activities, even his most 
private ones, to establish differences in rank and to award 
favor, mercy, or punishment.

Decorated Space as Performance
The same system of meanings and rituals applies to the 
architecture and furnishings of a Baroque palace. These 
reflected the ruling system of the time, the sovereignty of the 
ruler, whether king or prince, his power of order and his 
divine grace, through the splendor, the size, as well as the 
symmetry of the building, through the absolute identity of 
private and state spaces. These claims—the sovereignty of 
the ruler, his power of order, his divine right—were also 
constituted in the architecture and decoration and in their 
capacity for performativeness.
The particular characteristics of the Baroque palace are 
its strictly systematized hierarchy, not only in decorative 
forms, but also on architectural forms, which could be used 
for ritualistic purposes: the courtyard (Cour d’Honneur, 
Court of Honour); the stairs, with intermediate landings; the

path from bottom to top and vice versa. All these elements 
are integral parts of the performative staging of courtly 
ceremonies. The outer form, the inner structure, and the 
furnishings were thus essentially determined by the function 
of the Baroque palace, the frame, stage, and backdrop for 
representations and daily ceremonies.

The Baroque palace cannot be understood without 
knowledge of courtly ceremonies and festive practice. In its 
complex systems of spaces and physical elements, as well as 
in its intricate performative articulation, in its fashion and 
rituals, Versailles became a model for the Baroque palace. 
These aspects were emulated across Europe, for instance in 
the Viennese Imperial Court in Schönbrunn Palace; at the 
Court of Tsar Peter the Great, in St. Petersburg; at the

A SOLEMN RECEPTION
On 1 September 1686, the ambassadors of the King of 
Siam visited the King of France Louis XIV at Versailles. The 
reception was a significant diplomatic event for the French 
Court and was reproduced in books and illustrations as an 
important piece of political propaganda. An engraved print, 
made in 1687 and attributed to Pierre Paul Sevin, illustrates 
the moment when the three Siamese emissaries bow to the 
French monarch who, seating on his throne, receives their 
gifts as signs of reverence and recognition. The scene is 
crowded with members of the French high Court, including 
children and a priest. The three guests look significantly 
different from the rest of court, both in terms of their ‘exotic’ 
appearance (clothes, skin colour…) and their position vis-à-
vis the king and his entourage. 
At first glance, the image appears to explicitly suggest a 
strong sense of hierarchy and clearly distinguishes the 
French Court from the Siamese diplomats. At the same time 
however, the ambassadors are performing their own political 
protocols, by bowing in front of the king and by offering 
highly symbolic gifts. The image then projects a sense of 
ambiguity, between the expression of specific Siamese cultur­
al habits and a highly politicised French setting. In a way, it 
is as much a celebration of French monarchical power as it 
is a performance of Louis XIV according to the expectations 
of his visitors, in order to earn their respect. 
In such as an ambiguous circumstance, Versailles acts as 
a fundamental architectural frame and a projection of the 
king's own persona and the rituals that surround his life.

Louis XIV (1638-1715) became king at the young age of

Mazarin (1602-1661). In 1661, the day after Mazarin died, 
23-year-old Louis XIV declared himself the sole ruler of

Bellori, although primarily interested in painting and 
sculpture, also wrote about architecture. His ideas on archi­

century, French architecture was dominated by the attempt 
to standardize a concept of beauty in order to distinguish, in 
absolute terms, good buildings from bad. The goal was to 
establish a form of French classicism, something predicated

local and global values: a specific context (France) claims 
to generate a universal and eternal set of principles. These 
tensions are most explicitly articulated in writing.

not an architect, but rather an amateur with an interest in 
architecture. He was related to François Sublet de Noyers, 
the building superintendent of Louis XIII. Over his life,

A CATALYST FOR FRENCH CLASSICISM: BERNINI’S

The program put forward by Fréart de Chambray and

1665 Bernini, the most important artist in the service of the

the eastern wing being the very final stage.

PERRAULT’S TREATISE AND FRENCH IDENTITY
 
The Louvre first appeared as a model of French architec­
ture in a vital publication, Perrault’s translation of Vitruvi­

tion to re-found classicism and the theory of architecture 
and to steer practice and consent. On the frontispiece of the 
book, allegories of the three sister arts, as three women, 
present Vitruvius’s book to France, supported by the allego­
ry of abundance. In the background, a triumphal arch and 
Perrault’s façade for the Louvre celebrate the king, a French 
version of Vitruvius’s dedication to Augustus. Aside from its 
celebratory significance, the book was also a meticulous and 
very high-quality translation of Vitruvius. In addition, Per­
rault annotated the text with an extensive corpus of foot­
notes, his own commentary on Vitruvius. In the footnotes, 
Perrault makes several striking remarks that challenge the 
authority of Vitruvius; the point about the double columns is 
one of these. Perrault confronts the conception of Roman 
architecture as an absolute reference and of Vitruvius as a

THE CONTEXT OF THE QUERELLE DES ANCIENS ET 
DES MODERNES
Perrault’s translation of Vitruvius was predicated on a 
cultural debate known as the querelle des anciens et des 
moderns, a quarrel between the ‘ancients’, such as Vitruvius, 
and the ‘moderns’, in this case late 17th-century French 
culture. The querelle originated in debates about literature 
and the relevance of ancient models for contemporary au­
thors, but it quickly expanded to all forms of human activi­
ty. The querelle was not confined to France, and it became 
part of a battle for cultural hegemony—the question of who 
was seen as the leading nation in the field of the arts. In 
this debate, Claude Perrault takes the side of the moderns, 
together with his brother Charles. In 1683 Charles published 
a long celebratory poem entitled Le Siècle de Louis le Grand, 
in which, like his brother, he argued that the ancients could 
not stand against the magnificence of Louis XIV and the 
achievements of French artists and scientists. In the poem, 
Charles wrote:
Ainsi les humbles toits de nos premiers ayeux,
Couverts negligeament de joncs & de glayeux,
N’euvent rien de pareil en leur Architecture,
A nos Riches Palais d’eternelle structure . . .
According to Charles, the primitive structures of the 
ancients cannot compare to the grand palaces of Louis XIV.

The optimistic celebration of the king was predicated on the 
achievements of modernity, such as printing, artillery, and 
science. The authority of ancient authors thus became the 
object of debate. Claude Perrault similarly embodied a mod­
ern spirit and adopted Vitruvius in order to question him, 
pointing out inconsistencies and lack of relevance in his Ten 
Books. In Perrault’s subsequent Abregé des Dix Livres de 
Vitruve (1674), the suppression of Vitruvian authority be­
comes even more evident. Perrault points out the difference 
between Vitruvius’s general approach to architecture, such 
as in the use of materials, construction methods, and gener­
al design principles (the orders, proportions), and what only 
retained relevance for our understanding of ancient architec­
ture, such as the old building types of baths or amphitheat­
ers. Above all, Perrault’s reading of Vitruvius questions the 
importance of the Roman author’s erudite and historical 
approach to such topics as origin myths or the cosmos. 
According to Perrault, Vitruvius included this sort of subject 
matter to prove his erudition and establish his authority 
about matters of architecture. Vitruvius’s books, Perrault 
says, were not really about architecture but rather about 
establishing credibility, and therefore authority, and there is 
little in the treatise that actually holds direct relevance for 
contemporary architects. In that sense, Perrault’s translation 
and his Abregé further developed the questions already 
raised by Fréart de Chambray: what are relevant ancient 
examples, what is real architectural knowledge, and who has 
the authority to establish rules?

THE ACADÉMIE ROYALE D’ARCHITECTURE
Perrault’s reflection on the authority of Vitruvius was set 
against the first institutionalization of architectural educa­
tion in France. The Académie d’Architecture was founded in 
1671, under royal patronage, to guarantee an education for 
young French architects that at the same time would coun­
ter the role of artisans in the realm of architecture. The 
origins of the Académie can be traced to the Petit Conseil 
for the Louvre, and also to the Petite Académie, a small 
group of literates who had the task of writing the inscrip­
tions on royal buildings. This pedagogical project of course 
raised the question of authority. On which models and on 
what basis were architects meant to operate?
The Académie d’Architecture was intended to establish 
an ‘original’ classicism as a way to control the architectural 
representation of the state. It is around this definition of 
classicism that a heated debated arose in the intellectual 
circles of the Académie, generating new notions of architec­
tural theory and composition. A didactic program was devel­
oped by the Académie’s first director, the architect François 
Blondel, around the critical study of treatises as established 
by Fréart’s Parallèle, giving priority to Italian authors. 
The Académie had two key functions: pedagogy and 
deliberating institutional control over building construction 
in France. The emergence of the Académie ultimately 
demonstrated how the arts could be used as a representative 
of the State. The rejection of the Bernini’s project for the

ANTOINE DESGODETZ, LES EDIFICES ANTIQUES DE 
ROME
In 1674, as part of the Académie’s project to collect and 
interpret new data on ancient architecture, Antoine Desgo­
detz was sent to Rome to measure the ruins using the latest 
survey tools, like the toise. After almost a decade in Rome, 
Desgodetz’s efforts resulted in Les edifices antiques de Rome 
dessinés et mesurés très exactement, published in 1682. 
Desgodetz’s book embodied a divergence in architectural 
authority, namely the word of Vitruvius against the evidence 
of ruins. Les edifices antiques de Rome suggested an urgency 
to adopt a scientific approach to architecture, giving space 
to what a building can tell. For example, the Temple of 
Peace is represented as it is, realistically, precisely measured 
and without attempts to complete it, as had often been the 
case previously (for example by Palladio, examined in chap­
ter 6). However, Desgodetz also displays the same ambigui­
ty as Fréart. On the one hand, his method illustrates the 
belief that ancient architecture is founded on precise princi­
ples and that these can somehow be rediscovered. At the 
same time, his project recognizes that these principles are 
inaccessible, as the evidence we have is scattered and frag­
mentary, and all we can do is gather as much information 
as possible and investigate it.

FRANÇOIS BLONDEL, THE COURS D’ARCHITECTURE
François Blondel’s Cours d’architecture enseigné dans 
l’Académie Royale d’Architecture (1675–83) was a reflection 
of his own teaching at the Académie. The book was again 
dedicated to the king, absolute patron of the arts, and its 
aim was to ‘liberate’ architecture. Blondel’s interpretation of 
architectural principles was given legitimacy through mathe­
matical rules and by following Fréart’s project to define a 
clear and legitimate form of classicism. The key condition 
for this project was an objective, universal, comprehensible, 
and teachable basis, namely mathematics. Authority there­
fore is not Vitruvius, but science.
As a consequence of its mathematical substance, archi­
tecture was conceived through absolute rules directly related 
to the notion of beauty and accompanied by the notion of 
‘beinsénance’ (propriety, decor). This implied a ‘demystifica­
tion’ of architectural proportion, as a way to save it: study­
ing proportions does not reveal any sort of divine mystery, 
as suggested by Desgodetz in the same time period, but 
rather a deeper more substantial and comprehensive order 
that guarantees predictable rules. Blondel writes, ‘La nature 
est toujour la même dans toutes le choses’.
In a way, Blondel positions himself in the middle, be­
tween ‘ancients’ and ‘moderns’. His rejection of the authority 
of Vitruvius is very modern, but his interpretation of archi­
tecture as originating from absolute rules is close to the 
traditionalist position. Unlike Perrault, Blondel is very un­
comfortable with an architecture that can be subjected to 
change.

CLAUDE PERRAULT, THE ORDONNANCE
Blondel’s Cours makes explicit reference to the ‘opinion’ 
that there is no absolute proportional system that guaran­
tees the beauty of architecture; while Blondel acknowledges 
that this is an interesting view, he disagrees with it. This 
view is also held by Claude Perrault, and he develops it at 
length in the preface to his Ordonnance des cinq espèces de 
colonnes selon la méthode des anciens (1683). 
A Critique of Ancient Authority
The book opens with this sentence: 
The ancients rightly believed that the proportional rules 
that make buildings beautiful were taken from the pro­
portions of the human body.
Just like the title of the work (The order of the five kinds 
of columns according to the method of the ancients), this 
statement appears to accept the authority of the ancients, 
and to defend their system of the five orders. But the preface 
proceeds to elaborately refute the idea that the proportions 
devised by the ancients, or any proportional system for that 
matter, are the only necessary condition for the beauty of 
architecture. On the contrary, proportional systems, just like 
the ornament that defines them, are a matter of custom, or 
habit: people like them because they are used to them; they 
are, in Perrault’s words, ‘arbitrary’, not ‘objective’. They 
change over time and depend on the circumstances in which 
they are applied. Perrault therefore claims the authority to

propose his own proportional system. In a typical ironical 
twist, he even claims that his system is probably closest to 
the original system of the ancients, which was lost precisely 
because architects wrongly believed they had to adhere to 
later proportional systems. In the preface, Perrault offers a 
painstaking defense of this position. It is worth exploring it 
in some detail, because (in response to Fréart and Blondel) 
it addresses a number of issues that would remain central to 
the architectural debate for a century to come.
After the opening sentence, Perrault immediately casts 
doubt on the idea that the beauty of buildings depends on 
the degree to which their dimensions adhere to a specific 
system of ratios. The only thing ‘that architecture has well 
established’ is that the orders can be distinguished ‘with 
little exactitude or precision’ on the basis of their ‘propor­
tions’ (the ratios of their dimensions) and ‘characters’ of 
ornamentation. For the ‘precise measurement of their mem­
bers’ there is no absolute rule, just as there is none for ‘the 
exact outline of [the] profiles’ of the ornamentation. In fact, 
among the things determined by proportion, ‘a number of 
architects have approached an equal degree of perfection in 
different ways in the judgment of experts’. This is demon­
strated by the great variety of proportions and ornamenta­
tion that can be found in ancient and modern buildings 
alike.
In the next step of his argument, Perrault refutes two 
traditional arguments in favor of the necessity to use a 
specific proportional system in architecture to achieve beau­
ty: the analogy with music and the analogy with physical 
phenomena. Perrault argues that we perceive sound different­

If these analogies do not explain the beauty of propor­
tional systems, what does? To understand this, Perrault 
states that we need to distinguish two causes of beauty: 
absolute ones and customary ones. Absolute causes will 
always generate beauty, while customary causes depend on 
context, on the social, cultural, or artistic habits or customs 
of a certain time and place. Absolute causes include size, 
beauty of materials, good construction, usefulness, and 
‘symmetrie’, the general composition and alignment of a

What does that mean? Proportional systems change over 
time according to the whims and fancy of humankind. In 
essence, they depend on taste. Despite these changes, how­

For instance, everyone finds the Pantheon a masterpiece. 
This is because it contains many objective causes of beauty, 
such as its size, magnificence, construction, ... But the very 
fact that everyone finds the Pantheon beautiful is itself a 
cause of confusion. We tend to think that every aspect of the 
building contributes to its absolute beauty, including its pro­
portions. Perrault identifies this process as the ‘principle of 
association’, by which certain characteristics are ‘projected’ 
from the realm of custom or taste into the universe of abso­
lute qualities. The Pantheon is a great building not because 
of the proportion of its columns, but because it is well built 
and serves its purpose. We wrongly assume that its specific 
proportions have to do with its beauty.

judgment, people tend to mirror what we would call trendset­

court, with the king at its center. If the king said something 
was beautiful, society would follow. As a consequence, within 
a certain historical context, arbitrary causes seem just as 
fixed as absolute ones: these causes are a code or a set or

The Value of Taste
Perrault’s theory of proportion of the five orders is based 
on ‘average’ measures found in Vitruvius, Desgodetz, and 
other authors. His proportional system was intended to be 
simple: by choosing a small module—one third of a column 
diameter—Perrault expressed the difference in the proportion 
of columns a simple progression of these modules, i.e. each 
step in the order adds three modules to the height of the 
column. This proportional system would have little influence 
on architectural practice. But Perrault’s challenging of the 
idea that proportion in architecture is based on immutable 
laws of nature has marked the history of architectural theo­
ry. Perrault puts forward taste as a fundamental aspect of 
architecture that needs to be taken into account. According 
to Perrault, taste is not relative or individual but is deter­
mined by a larger historical and cultural context, which is 
characterized by centers of power, such as the king; this 
center produces consensus. We, who live in this context, do 
not directly perceive our ‘taste’ as such, but consider it as 
an absolute system. The issue of consensus, which for Fréart 
was determined by a selected group of experts and for Blon­
del was established by universal mathematical rules, for 
Perrault varies over time and space and is determined 
around centers of authority.
Nevertheless, Perrault and Blondel worked in the same 
context, where the ultimate authority was the king. In the 
case of Blondel, the authority of the king becomes manifest 
in the institution of the Académie, which establishes and 
protects the profession of the architect. In the case of Per­
rault, royal authority works in a more subtle way, as a

fashion, and architecture. As a consequence, in a way, Blon­

could obtain, is enough to become a valuable architect. On 
the other hand, Perrault argues that only by accessing 
centers of power can one get in touch with the taste of the 
time, which is a more elitist position.
Summary on Perrault
1. Architecture is not the rational imitation of absolute 
models—of nature, the primitive hut, the human body, 
manufacturing, ...—nor is proportion based on the 
laws of nature. Instead, architecture is the application 
of a simple, rational system or standard, which is 
subjected to the mechanisms of custom. Beauty de­
pends on grace, not proportion, and is recognized and 
achieved by people who have perfected good taste 
through their training.
2. Custom, grace, and taste cannot be defined rationally 
or in absolute terms. The way buildings look is deter­
mined by their historical and cultural context.
3. The criterion for good architecture is beauty. Beauty 
is caused partly by objective, ‘absolute’ qualities 
(material, workmanship, usefulness) and partly by 
‘customary’, arbitrary qualities (proportion and orna­
ment). Nevertheless, we experience the beauty of a 
building as absolute, because it fits the criteria of its 
time.

CONCLUSION
The institution of a public debate over the values of 
classicism had profound repercussions over the history of 
architectural thought. As demonstrated by example of the 
Trump administration at the beginning of this chapter, these 
conversations have not ceased to exist. The foundation of 
the Académie d’Architecture and the heated querelle that de­
veloped around it sparked important disputes over the no­
tion of beauty, the concept of modernity and the agency of 
the architect, which for the first time acquired a crucial 
public and national dimension. Over the following century of 
so, the growth of the Enlightenment as a form of thought 
will further question these important aspects of architecture, 
but in theory and in practice.

devoid of meaning, French architecture, he says, had become 
a perfect blend of judicious and appropriate design with 
magnificence and beauty. This was implicitly a critique of 
Italian splendor, which was considered excessive and irra­

ROCOCO: DEFINITION AND GENERAL RECEPTION
‘Rococo’ is a 19th-century coinage, like ‘Baroque’, and is 
derived from ‘rocaille’, the pebbles and shells often used in 
decoration, particularly in relation to the ‘goût du pitto­

social classes and categories of objects and buildings. Roco­

and was not limited to the court, but also flourished in mon­

‘style’ of the Enlightenment, a culture of conversational 
sensibility, of exchanges, sociability, and cosmopolitanism. 
Women played a key role in this culture, as nodes in interna­

also be understood as a reaction against what has been 
called the ‘monarchy’s hegemonic response to innovation’, a 
function of the querelle des anciens et des modernes (see 
chapter 15).
Crucial to understanding the Rococo is the distribution of 
artistic agency. Rococo was not primarily a matter for archi­
tects, but also for different categories of patrons and of 
artisans, such as stucco decorators and woodcutters. As 
such, it embodies the exact opposite of what Fréart, Blondel, 
and Perrault were advocating a few decades earlier, in favor 
of the architect’s authority. The reception of Rococo was

‘DISTRIBUTION’ AND THE PALAIS BOURBON
One of the most celebrated examples of ‘distribution’ is 
the Palais Bourbon, built for the daughter of Louis XIV 
between 1722 and 1728. The plan is organized in a U-shape, 
with the entrance located on the right side. From an oval 
vestibule, one would move into an antechamber, and from 
there into the ‘salle à manger’ and the ‘salon’. These last 
two rooms would grant access to two parallel sequences of 
rooms. The first, overlooking the garden, was a series of 
reception spaces for guests and visitors, with rooms of differ­

the more private part of the palace, with the apartments of 
the princess (reminiscent of Versailles). While the first se­

ostensibly more private but with a public function centered 
around the ‘chambre a coucher’, the bedroom of the prin­

and ‘sacred’ of all, where royalty would receive diplomats, 
religious emissaries, and other relevant representatives. 
Then, behind the ‘chambre a coucher’ was a second, truly 
private apartment, connected to the reception bedroom with 
a system of cabinets and corridors located ‘behind the

THE HOTEL DE SOUBISE ET DE ROHAN
Already in the late 17th century, Claude Perrault noted

and along centers of authority (see chapter 15). This same 
mechanism, which can be observed in the Palais Bourbon, is 
also present in the Hotel de Soubise, another important

building boom, especially in the most aristocratic parts of 
the city. The stylistic lexicon developed in Versailles then is 
readopted in new courtly palaces, such as the Hotel de 
Soubise. Here again, the court acted as a sort of trailblazer, 
a center of artistic production where decorative languages 
were developed and appropriated in the interest of power 
representation. In a way, accepted taste ‘trickled down’ from 
the highest centers of power into settings of lower status.

GERMAIN BOFFRAND AS ROCOCO ARCHITECT
The author of the antechambers of the Hotel de Soubise 
was Germain Boffrand (1667–1754). Boffrand received a 
humanistic training from his uncle before starting to work 
for Jules Hardouin-Mansart, first architect of king Louis 
XIV, on several royal commissions. Around 1700 he began 
an independent career, building a number of private resi­
dences and hotels in Paris and elsewhere, such as a hunting 
lodge at Bouchefort (Bosvoorde) near Brussels. In 1705 he 
entered the services of Max II von Bayern, governor of the 
Southern Netherlands in Brussels. He also worked for Duke 
Leopold of Lotharingen in Nancy, and with Balthasar Neu­
mann on the residence of the Prince Bishop in Würzburg. 
His portfolio of commissions expanded to include a wide 
range of works, exerting a major influence on 18th-century 
interior architecture, especially around the palaces in Mu­
nich. In 1745 he published his Livre d’Architecture, in both 
French and Latin, with many of his own designs, including 
the Hotel de Soubise. Boffrand belonged to a unique class of 
practitioners, partly still within the milieu of the Académie 
and partly as a newly developed, international architectural 
culture. 
Boffrand is an important exponent of what has been 
called ‘Rococo’. But his theoretical work also gives insight 
into how ‘Rococo’ fits into, or rather relates to, what has 
been called ‘classicism’: less a matter of rejection or a turn 
to Baroque examples as an alternative to classicism, and 
more a shift in focus toward the interior and the expression 
of ‘caractère’ by means of ‘distribution’ and ornament.

BOFFRAND’S LIVRE D’ARCHITECTURE
Boffrand’s Livre d’Architecture was subtitled ‘Dissertation 
sue ce qu’on appelle le bon goût en architecture’. It embod­
ied an important late 17th-century shift in architectural 
thinking, where the primary issue became good taste. Per­
rault (and to some extent Fréart) had already discussed this 
aspect of architecture, but only now did it acquire the di­
mension of an entire treatise. The problem of ‘bon goût’, 
consisting in properly distinguishing the excellent from the 
merely good, was laid out by Boffrand through a number of 
principles. The Livre presented a history of taste from the 
origins in Greece, to Rome, to Gothic, Renaissance, and to 
the present, associating each era with its own taste.

Boffrand adopts a position similar to that of Fréart, 
namely the need for principles to counter the volatilities of 
fashion, which risks distorting taste. However, he takes this 
premise to a different level by emphasizing the notion of 
‘caractère’, meaning architecture as the expression of its 
use. In Boffrand’s rationale, fashion has created results that 
lack good taste, moving interior ornaments to the exterior, 
resulting in a confusion of lines on the facades of buildings. 
Lines in architecture are like sounds in music, expressing 
different emotions. This principle is true for public buildings 
but also for houses, where everything should be proportioned 
and shaped according to its use. The tone of the building is 
set by its master and will express that character, the identity 
of the owner. Unlike Fréart, who adopted the authority of 
the ancients to define taste, or Perrault, who called upon 
centers of authority, Boffrand employs the internal logic of 
the building.
Boffrand defines ‘caractère’ through an analogy with 
poetry (different characters are like different poetic genres, 
such as tragedy or comedy), according to the model of 
Horace’s Ars Poetica. Character should guarantee the inter­
nal coherence and the poetic unity of the work, which is also 
what distinguishes between different genres and at the same 
time instructs and delights. Boffrand emphasizes that the 
means to express this character is not the orders but lines, 
the basic system of architectural design, like notes or words. 
Architects should limit their vocabulary to three types of 
lines. These lines are the basic means of expression in archi­
tecture, not the orders or principles of proportion.

CIRCULATING ARTISTS AND ARTISANS, MODELS AND

The dissemination of Rococo as a style occurred through 
a variety of media and projects. From architectural treatises 
to the scenographic organization of chapels, from ceiling 
frescos, to perspectival theatre scenes, the Rococo spread 
throughout wealthy establishments across Europe. artisans 
circulated throughout Europe, often employed solely for the 
design of interiors. Examples include George Semple’s Head­

through a collection of real as well as non-existing build­

vases, adopting a broken entablature and circular and oval 
architectural shapes. Architecture was now closely associat­

but also of social standing, and was depicted as space of 
leisure and conversation.

CONCLUSION 
The development of Rococo is deeply intertwined with 
debates around classicism and the institution of concepts 
such as ‘caractère’ and ‘distribution’ in French architecture. 
The social dimension of these concepts, especially in the 
context of interior space, was one of the reasons for its 
international dissemination, resulting in a far-reaching 
movement of architects, artisans and languages. Rococo was 
then readapted and relocated in different formats, such as 
books, furniture, artisanal objects and garden design.

THE LANDSCAPE GARDEN
In Richard II (c. 1595), William Shakespeare compared 
the care of the garden to the care of a State. Why bring 
order into gardens, he posited, when the political order in 
the country is shaken by riots? The ideal, ordered garden 
imagined by Shakespeare found realization half a century 
later in the Baroque garden. One of the most emblematic ex­
amples is the garden of Versailles, designed by André Le 
Nôtre and begun in 1661 (see chapter 14). Commissioned by 
Louis XIV, it was part of a majestic redesign planned for 
the Palace of Versailles, which became the royal residence in 
1682. Compared to the already massive size of the building, 
the vastness of the garden, which spreads beyond what the 
eye can see, is a clear image and expression of the political 
plans of Louis XIV.
 
Symmetrically organized, the complicated sequences of 
vegetation and built structures articulated a vast and highly 
complex space. A five-pointed ‘patte d’oie’ (goose foot) 
organizes bosquets, which divide the Parterre de Broderie—
plant beds shaped in symmetrical patterns—from the Palace. 
Controlling nature at this scale also had a religious implica­

order and in the construction of the garden Louis XIV 
expressed his desire to be personified as a god.

new designs, especially in England which, unlike France, was

MARC ANTOINE LAUGIER AND THE ESSAI SUR L’AR­
CHITECTURE
Marc Antoine Laugier (1713–69) was a Jesuit priest, who 
entered the order after completing a three-year novitiate in 
Avignon and four years of training near Lyon. He took his 
vows in Paris in 1745 and soon became court preacher to 
Louis XV. Along his religious duties, Laugier also wrote on 
painting and music. 
After transferring to the provinces, he left the Jesuits to 
enter the order of the Benedictines. He later returned to 
Paris and devoted himself entirely to art criticism and his­
torical research. His Essai sur l’architecture appeared in 
1753. In 1765 he published a second treatise on architecture, 
entitled Observations sur l’architecture. Laugier was not a 
trained architect but rather a writer, and so he approached 
architecture as a ‘free agent’, acting through the popular 
press to disseminate his ideas.
Laugier’s Essai sur l’architecture was written in 1752 and 
first published anonymously the following year, then extend­
ed, in response to his critics, in 1755. The essay is firmly 
rooted in earlier writings on architecture, such as that by 
Amédée-François Frézier and Louis de Cordemoy, but would 
have a much larger impact because of the rhetorical power 
of its central figure: the primitive hut, especially in the way it 
was represented on the cover of the second edition (see 
below). It is safe to say that this primitive hut is one of the 
most well-known images in the history of Western architec­
ture, and it has an enduring appeal.

## S. 135

135
In the Essai, Laugier discusses the origins and principles 
of architecture, arguing that, if there is a universal order to 
architecture, it is based on nature. As such, Laugier’s ap­
proach disregards historical precedents in favor of ‘pure 
theory’. In a sense, he follows Fréart’s lead in trying to 
retrieve the pure source of architectural principles, but he 
takes Fréart’s position one step further. According to Laugi­
er, to demonstrate the truth of the contingent rules of archi­
tecture, one needs to move beyond historical examples or 
the text of Vitruvius, following instead a sort of empirical 
method. In other words, the authority to establish rules does 
not lie with the Ancients or the Moderns, and it is not a 
matter of consensus (as suggested by Perrault), but it 
should instead be rooted in nature, a position typical of the 
Enlightenment. Laugier emphasizes that, as a consequence, 
certain architectural principles can be known and understood 
by all, not just by a professional class of architects.
Laugier’s empirical method consisted in observing build­
ings and reflecting on the effects they had on the beholder. 
To justify this trajectory, and to find an absolute method to 
build his argument, Laugier walks around Paris and at­
tempts to identify the aesthetic pleasure provided by build­
ings. According to Laugier, the results of this experimental 
approach suggest that there are in fact universal notions of 
beauty, principles that can explain why certain buildings are 
more pleasurable than others. Such buildings are independ­
ent of habits or fashion, instead rooted in nature. By ration­
alizing the aesthetic impact of architecture on the observer, 
one can then extract an answer about the causes of beauty 
that is abstract and absolute. As a way to visualize these 
natural, timeless rules of architecture, Laugier uses the

LA PETITE CABANE RUSTIQUE
Like many others before him, Laugier points out that 
humans have an innate instinct to protect themselves from 
weather through building shelter, and this prompts them to 
build a primitive construction, which Laugier calls la petite 
cabane rustique. This hut echoes important passages of 
Vitruvius, such as the origin myth in Book II (see chapter 
1). In Book IV, Vitruvius discusses the ‘wooden’ origins of 
the Doric entablature, and in Book V he suggests that when 
two columns are superposed, the diameter of the upper 
column should be smaller than the lower one, in imitation of 
the tapering of trees trunks. In the Cours d’architecture 
(1675), Jacques-François Blondel had already combined 
these passages into the description of a single building, a 
hut, as a way to describe the origin of the true and universal 
principles for architecture (see chapter 15). For Blondel, the 
primitive hut is a ‘model’ because it is the historical origin 
of all subsequent development.
Laugier picked up Blondel’s invention of the hut, but 
fundamentally transformed the logic of the argument. Laugi­
er’s hut is no longer the historical origin of architecture, but 
an eternal benchmark: architecture should always aspire to 
resemble the primitive hut as closely as possible. For Laugi­

columns, entablature, and pediment. This is why Greek

THE GRECO-ROMAN CONTROVERSY 
The primitive hut became a polemical tool, fitting in a 
larger European dispute known as the Greco-Roman debate. 
As we have seen, Laugier’s idealization of Greek architecture 
simultaneously argued that Roman architecture was corrupt­
ed, as for instance the Romans used arches instead of 
beams, and pilasters instead of columns, and therefore 
moved away from the true principles of architecture. This 
position formed part of a renewed controversy across Europe 
about the old question of authority and legitimacy of ancient 
models, now framed as the question of whether Greek or 
Roman antiquity should be considered the true reference in 
architecture. In this debate, Greek and Roman architecture 
are act like fictions, to which certain values are attached, 
just as the actual artifacts retrieved (mainly) on Greek or 
Italian soil.

THE STAKES IN THE CONTROVERSY
The question underlying the Greco-Roman debate was a 
matter of cultural and historical identity. The debate in 
architecture grew out of disputes concerning literature and 
the visual arts that emerged in the late 17th century, where 
the French challenged the hegemony of Italian examples. In 
architecture, too, at the origin of the French celebration of 
Greek architecture was a competition for primacy against the 
Italian claim for the authority of Rome. This competition 
had its origins in the 17th century. As we have seen, the 
affaire of the Louvre if the 1660s was not just a question of 
architectural composition, but also a political stand against 
the Papacy (see chapter 15). In the 18th century, choosing 
Greek against Roman architecture ultimately had far deeper 
meanings than just architectural. The choice raised the 
question of who the true inventors of Antiquity are, and the 
underlying question of the values and aesthetics of ‘classical 
architecture’, and how these values translate into form: how 
is classical architecture ‘modern’, and what degrees of inven­
tion does it allow? This sort of questions ultimately pertains 
to cultural politics, and challenges the inheritance of the 
greatness of Antiquity: papal Rome or Paris? But also, 
Weimar, London, Edinburgh... The debate over precedence in 
the origins of architecture became increasingly complicated 
towards the end of the 18th century, not in the least because 
of an increasing awareness of the achievements of non-Euro­
pean architecture. With it, came a more substantial ques­
tioning of the theoretical basis of architecture as a whole 
and of the creative use of antiquity.

SAINTE-GENEVIÈVE 
The Greco-Roman debate was ultimately transposed into 
building through the adoption of two different compositional 
models, an architecture of columns and architraves (Greek) 
versus one of vaults, walls, arcades and massive stones 
(Roman). A significant example of the Greek, or rather the 
Greek-gothic position, is the church of Sainte-Geneviève. 
Built as a votive church to the patron saint of Paris on the 
orders of Louis XV, it was designed and built from 1756 
onwards by J G. Soufflot and Jean-Baptiste Rondelet. 
The church seemingly adopted a Greek model, with a 
temple-like front, Corinthian columns and a Greek cross in 
plan. Soufflot tried to produce a building where walls were 
not part of the structure and not part of the spatial system. 
We might read Sainte-Geneviève as an attempt to make 
Laugier’s essay into built form. The whole architectural 
scenography is entirely driven by the relationship between 
the dome and the columns, which run around the interior 
and then come to the outside building at the front. Soufflot 
attempted to make the walls as transparent as possible by 
inserting large windows (these windows were closed off when 
the church was transformed into the Pantheon during the 
French Revolution).
The intentions behind Soufflot’s design can be gleaned 
from a book that was written by Julien-David Le Roy for the 
occasion of the laying of the first stone of the new church: 
Histoire de la disposition et des formes différentes que les 
chrétiens ont données à leurs temples, 1764 (The history of 
the various arrangement and forms which the Christians have

## S. 149

given to their churches). In this book, Le Roy presents a 
genealogy of Christian church buildings (i.e. an account of 
its historical development), to show how the Roman basilica 
(the market building which was used as a model for the 
earliest purpose built Christian churches) was gradually 
enriched with colonnades and domes, and progressively 
evolved from a longitudinal to a centralized plan. The means 
to achieve the integration of vaulting and domes with colon­
nades was a Gothic structure, as illustrated, of course, in 
Sainte-Geneviève, as well as in the church of the Madeleine, 
which was planned at around the same time but never built. 
Importantly, Le Roy does not explain this evolution as the 
consequence of tectonic considerations, or as the imitation 
of a primitive model, but rather as driven by a search for the 
most powerful aesthetic effect. Le Roy’s history of church 
building thus bears witness of the increasing attention to­
wards understanding and conceptualizing perception in 
architecture. Le Roy would later integrate these ideas in the 
second edition of his Les ruines des plus beaux monuments 
de la Grèce. As we have seen, Laugier also started from 
‘measuring’ the effect of buildings on himself to arrive at his 
theory for the Essay. This ‘sensationalism’ (attention to 
sense perception) would culminate in the work of Nicolas 
Camus-de-Mézières and Etienne-Louis Boullée.

18TH CENTURY: RUPTURE WITH THE ACADEMY IN THE 
SCIENTIFIC AGE
During the 17th century, Claude Perrault and Antoine 
Desgodetz had already questioned the whole idea of univer­
sal proportions and universal beauty in architecture (see 
chapter 15). However, during the 18th century the explosion 
of travel and tourism became a major agent for questioning 
the entire architectural system as developed in classical trea­
tises. As discussed in the previous chapter, architects began 
to go back to the original architectural ruins, particularly 
those in Rome, and measure them. It was the height of the 
scientific age, and architects wanted to return to an original 
evidence. And when they did so, they found a great deal of 
discrepancies, suggesting that architecture was in fact rela­
tive and not shaped according to universal proportion. 
In the age of enlightenment, the orders no longer carried 
divine meaning and were instead now wedded to scientific 
rationalism. The belief was that an ideal system could be 
created that would dictate the rules of architecture. But the 
system began to break down. The year 1800 marked a 
period of transition when the mature rationalism of the 
Enlightenment met with the will to find absolute principles 
for architecture. The year 1800 thus embodies the suspen­
sion between the two worlds of relative scientific rationalism 
and a pre-industrial attitude towards tradition.

THE INFLUENCE OF PIRANESI: PROTOPIRO AND DI­
DASCOLO
At the core of this desire to return to the original archae­
ological evidence was the notion that Antiquity represented 
a living force, and that the artist or architect could become 
an agent for its transmission into the modern world. For 
someone like Piranesi, discussed before, this method was 
crucial. In 1765, Piranesi published a written attack on the 
architectural critic Pierre-Jean Mariette, entitled Parere su 
l’architettura (Opinion on architecture). Mariette believed 
Greek architecture was superior to Roman architecture, 
whereas Piranesi argued for the opposite. 
In a short addendum to this text, Piranesi invented a 
conversation between two fictional authors, Protopiro and 
Didascolo. Protopiro represents the side of the Greek revival­
ists, adherents to emerging theories on the austerity and 
simplicity of Greek architecture. Mariette was one such 
revivalist. Didascolo, on the other hand, acts as the mouth­
piece of Piranesi. He criticizes the limitations of an attitude 
of austerity and restraint and favored instead the creative 
license of the designer to invent and create new forms of 
architecture. Both sides agree, however, that the true source 
of architectural wisdom lies in Antiquity. Essentially, one 
side argues for tradition, the other, innovation. At the end of 
the conversation, Didascolo appears to win the argument.

NEW MEANINGS FOR ARCHITECTURE: JEAN-NICO­

At the beginning of the 18th century, Paris-born architect 
Jean-Nicolas-Louis Durand (1760-1834) became an impor­
tant advocate for a transition in architectural discourse. The 
dissatisfaction with the academic system did not begin with 
Durand. Towards the end of the 18th century the views of 
Didascolo, the inventor, were becoming popular among 
influential figures, even as the academies continued to assert 
their dominance over the rule of tradition. The entire debate 
was further complicated by one very important event, the 
French Revolution, which shook the foundation of French 
traditional cultural and political values. Once architecture 
was no longer about universal proportion and the correct­
ness of the orders, a fundamental, new quest was open for 
the search of meaning in architecture.
Durand emerged as an architect at a very critical mo­
ment in the history of architecture, at the turn between the 
18th and 19th centuries. On the one hand, old architectural 
traditions—represented by Scamozzi, Serlio, and others—were 
now being questioned by scientific rationalism. God didn’t 
make architectural proportions, man made them. In a scien­
tifically ordered society, man cannot possibly believe that 
there is some universal order to architecture. The Enlighten­
ment demonstrated that the world is relative. Cosmic order 
doesn’t exist, neither in architecture nor in anything else. At 
the same time, Europe was on the verge of the industrial 
revolution, during countries began developing disparities in 
development. Technology had not yet begun its gigantic 
onslaught, so this was a moment of suspension—between the

## S. 161

161
old world and a new, modern world. For architecture, this 
was a critical moment of redefinition. Durand believed that 
without the standardization of rules, architecture would be 
lost. But if these rules no longer come from God, then man 
must create them. Durand was aware that the correctness of 
proportions had no absolute value anymore. Instead, he 
tried to formalize a new, more practical set of rules that 
were based not on divine order but on on human order and 
scientific rationalism, in keeping with the new age in which 
he was living.

Political and Social Context
At this time, France was engaged in a huge nation-build­
ing project, which included architecture. In keeping with the 
ideals of the Revolution, civic buildings—buildings for the 
people—were now considered more important than religious 
buildings. However, many of these civic building types had 
yet to be invented. What should a prison, or a hospital, or a 
school, or a museum look like? 
As architects were engaged in inventing new typologies to 
house these functions, Durand provided a sort of kit of parts 
that they could use. Both young engineers and later archi­
tects started to adopt and Durand’s rules for designing new 
buildings. Furthermore, his lectures convey a completely 
anti-aristocratic sentiment. The reason was that at the time 
beauty was definitively associated with waste, with royalty, 
with the church, with the past, and therefore with corruption 
and oppression. Instead, Durand was offering a fresh, more 
neutral and secular system of architectural composition, 
directly inspired by the context of post-Revolutionary and 
early-industrial French culture.
Against Architectural Drawing
In his intentions, Durand was willing to break down the 
abstract rules of theory in favor of practicalities. As he 
declared in the opening sentence of his published lectures: 
‘Architecture, it is certain that of all the arts this is the 
most generally practiced and the most expensive.’

Deconstructing Laugier and Vitruvius
Durand’s Précis opens by laying down the objectives of 
architecture, namely the composition and execution of build­
ings both public and private. Durand then proceeds to divide 
these buildings up into a number of types. The architect’s 
job, according to Durand, is to assemble elements into 
different compositions, depending on what is required by 
each building type.
Furthermore, Durand attacks two origin myths about 
architecture: 1. that architecture is based on the primitive 
hut (the first architecture erected by man) and 2. that it is 
based on the proportions of the human body. Durand writes: 
‘And, as the orders of architecture invented by the 
Greeks, imitated by the Romans, and adopted by most 
of the nations of Europe are an imitation of the human 
body and of the hut, it follows that they constitute 
the essence of architecture. It follows that the orna­

matters of decoration no expense is ever to be spared.’ 
The direct objects of his attack are therefore on one 
hand, the origin myths of the primitive hut, as discussed 
extensively by Laugier in his Essai sur l’architecture (see

myths are not always literal, but rather metaphorical. For

art, for Durand, is entirely outside the realm of architecture. 
Architecture has nothing to do with drawing, art, decoration, 
beauty, or ornamentation. Instead, architecture is about only

Like all architects of the time, Durand used history to 
justify his position. His first publication, which appeared

SCHINKEL THE IDEALIST
Schinkel’s attention towards the sublime places him 
within the realm of the German idealists of the early 19th 
century. The pursuit of idealism in architecture was the 
rediscovery of an architectural ideal, rediscovering architec­
ture’s status as an art rather than a science. Schinkel was 
working in Berlin at the turn of the century and was thus 
immersed in the philosophies of German idealism. The best 
summary of the idealist position towards architecture, the 
position that Schinkel shared, came directly from the Hegeli­
an philosopher August Schlegel, a slightly older contempo­
rary of Schinkel. Schlegel thought that it was necessary to 
recover a sense of the unifying central idea of a work of 
architecture. 
This higher principle, Schlegel posited, could no longer 
be nature or God, as it was in the Renaissance. Schlegel 
wrote that this new principle had to be a human idea in 
keeping with the times, a human purpose. The higher princi­
ple in Schinkel’s Altes Museum is art, elevated to a spiritual 
level. This is why Schinkel’s rotunda in the Museum, dis­
cussed in the previous chapter, is not a temple to the gods 
anymore, as it was with the Pantheon, the ultimate rotunda. 
It is now a temple to the glory of art.

THE GROWTH OF BERLIN
To understand what Schinkel meant with the performativ­
ity of a building, it is necessary to place the building in 
relation to the city of Berlin and to see how Schinkel intend­
ed this building to be viewed, both at the intimate scale of 
the rotunda and at the urban scale. In the middle of the 17th 
century, Berlin was a small town on the east side of the river 
Spree. To the west of the river, surrounded on all sides by 
the river and a system of canals, was a second town called 
Kölln. Kölln was the home of the Hohenzollern family, Elec­
tors of Brandenburg, who lived in a palace there. To the 
north of this residence Schinkel would later build the Altes 
Museum. The Electors of Brandenburg would later become 
the Royal Family of Prussia, a great military power in Eu­
rope. 
In the second half of the 17th century the city began to 
expand. After 1674 a new section called the Dorotheenstadt

## S. 186

186
was added to the original part of Kölln. This was surround­
ed by a moat with protective fortifications, which cut the 
palace gardens off at the north end. The site of Schinkel’s 
museum would be just above this new moat. At the end of 
the century the city expanded further to the west, with an 
area called the Friedrichstadt. Running through the middle 
of the Friedrichstadt was the Unter den Linden, the only 
defined link from the palace to the Tiergarten through the 
city. At this time, these expansions were driven without any 
idea of a ‘whole city’ and with no attempt to connect the 
four different towns of Berlin, Kölln, Dorotheenstadt, and 
Friedrichstadt.
In 1701 Frederick I, King of Prussia, made Berlin Prus­
sia’s new capital. On 1 January 1710, Berlin, Kölln, Doroth­
eenstadt, and Friedrichstadt were finally all united as the 
‘Royal Capital and Residence of Berlin’. At this time, of the 
55,000 inhabitants of the city, 5,000 were soldiers. Berlin 
was a garrison town and far from being a capital of culture. 
In 1740, Frederick the Great came to power, with the ambi­
tion to transform Berlin into a center of culture and the arts. 
He embarked upon yet another expansion project. The old 
defensive walls around the moats of the city were removed, 
now being useless for defense. The city was still not physi­
cally unified; waterways and canals still punctuated the 
different parts of the city and divided them. Nonetheless, in 
a little over a hundred years Berlin had gone from a medie­
val village in the middle of a swamp to the capital of Prus­
sia. The city expanded in all directions, Friedrichstadt be­
came larger, and the Unter den Linden occupied the center 
of the city. This was the city where Schinkel grew as an 
architect.

SCHINKEL’S BERLIN
Schinkel was practicing architecture during a decisive 
time for Berlin. In his lifetime, the city doubled in population 
from 200,000 to 400,000 people, making it the fourth larg­
est city in Europe. The industrial revolution began in earnest 
in the 1840s, and by the late 19th century Berlin was a 
metropolis of staggering proportions. In 1805, Schinkel, still 
very young, returned from a two-year Grand Tour through 
Europe to a city under occupation. In 1806 Napoleon’s 
troops swept into the city, exiled the king, and took posses­
sion of the palace, looting its collection. The city remained 
under French occupation until 1815. In this time, architec­
tural patronage came to standstill. Schinkel spent most of 
these years working as a stage designer for the theater and 
as a designer of popular panoramas. During this time, he 
also designed a bedroom for Queen Louise, when she and 
the king were allowed to return to the palace. In Schinkel’s 
project, her bedroom, intended to be used by Napoleon, 
became a form of political resistance, a way of commenting 
on social and public ideals. 
Frederick the Great was particularly sensitive to civic life 
of the 18th century. His idea of a city was a place to provide 
the best possible spaces for his army to engage in parades 
of military power. The city was built for the Prussian army, 
not for the elegances of public life or popular leisure. Schin­
kel was concerned with the lack of a graceful civic life in his 
city, and in 1817 had already drawn one of his first plans for 
Berlin. Though never carried out in its entirety, parts of this 
plan were realized in later years.

The conditions that Schinkel was working with in Berlin 
at the time were economically complicated. Coming out of 
the financially burdensome Napoleonic wars, the king had no 
money to invest in building, and he was not enthusiastic 
about embarking on any sort of grand urban development 
project. Schinkel was well aware of this and decided to focus 
his energies on a few smaller sites. He began by opening up 
streets and cross-axes around those sites, planning a set of 
visual relationships between parts of the city.
THE IDEA OF THE ALTES MUSEUM
The first outline of the Altes Museum appeared in 1819 
in one of Schinkel’s drawings. The sketch demonstrates that 
Schinkel had been nursing this project for some time. Four 
years elapsed between drawing this plan and bringing his 
proposal to the king. During those years, he mentioned the 
project in letters to friends and clearly gave it much thought, 
developing the idea into a way of implementing a much 
wider vision for the city center.
In 1823, the circumstances seemed appropriate for him to 
pitch his project to the king. After Napoleon’s final defeat in 
1815, the terms of Prussia’s treaty with the French included 
the return of royal art collections of Prussia, which had been 
forcibly brought to Paris in 1806 and put on display at the 
Louvre. Upon their repatriation, the collection was exhibited 
to popular success at the Berlin Arsenal, where they became 
synonymous with a patriotic Prussian feeling. Thus, they 
were rapidly transformed in the public imagination from ‘per­
sonal crown property’ into ‘national treasures’. The king

and renovate the Academy and Stable building. All for less 
money than the sum required to renovate the Academy

The Letter to the King 
As part of his plan to persuade the king, Schinkel wrote 
a very convincing letter, where he coolly recounted numbers, 
facts, and figures, wisely removing from the equation any 
hint of subjectivity, as well as any urgent personal or emo­
tive desire to see the project realized. The majority of the 
report is therefore focused not on describing the architecture 
itself, which he confines to a few paragraphs at the very end 
of the document, but on explaining the benefits of the 
scheme within the wider improvement of Berlin’s built envi­
ronment.
Schinkel presents a lengthy description of the cost prob­
lems associated with the old scheme at the Academy of 
Science. He then moves on to the new project, which he 
begins to describe with reference to a set of drawings, which 
he also included. The main goal of the project was to find a 
new typology for the building, not to fill in an existing build­
ing with a new function. The most ingenious part of the 
letter is represented by the cost calculations. He provided 
two costs: first, the cost of continuing with the present work 
on converting the Academy building; second, the cost of his 
new proposal. By showing his new proposal to be significant­
ly cheaper, he made it even more attractive for the king. But 
by far the longest part of the letter, besides the costs, is the 
section on the preparation of the site to receive the museum.

The Conditions of the Site
Schinkel was prepared to work with the difficult condi­
tions of the site to realize his project for the Altes Museum. 
The most glaring one was a deep waterway cutting directly 
across the Spree Island in the precise location that Schinkel 
proposed for the building. The Pomeranzengraben, as this 
canal was known, was constructed in the late 17th century to 
divert river traffic between the Spree and the Kupfergraben. It 
had the awkward effect of cutting the island into two. Why 
then was this site—not the most obvious or easy choice—so 
coveted by Schinkel? 
The historian Hermann Pundt, referring to Schinkel’s 
siting considerations, argued that he was working with what 
he termed ‘visual relationships independent of the imposed 
rational order’. The Altes Museum was developed in a con­
sideration of how it would appear in a perspective view. Its 
orthogonal site plan reveals nothing of a ‘rational order’ or 
logic of its insertion into the city. The building even appears 
rather oddly placed in plan. It does not follow the axes of 
the buildings around it, nor does it sit perpendicular to the 
square. It appears to stand resolutely on its own, with each 
of its four sides potentially visible and able to be traversed. 
However, in perspective the building appears perfectly em­
bedded within the surrounding cityscape. There seems to be 
a strange discrepancy between the revelations of perspective 
and the logics of the plan. In practice, however, this is the 
root of its success.

The Altes Museum as a Place of Power Conflicts
Schinkel knew that the king would not have approved an 
expensive project for rebuilding the city, but that he might 
approve the building of one museum. The museum therefore 
became a means by which to achieve a rearrangement of the 
city. These implicit aims of Schinkel put him into conflict 
with the king on many occasions during the design process. 
Conflicts slowly emerged, not just between the architect and 
his patron, but over the question of whether the museum 
represented monarchical power or the will of the people. In a 
way, the site enacted the antagonism between the older 
aristocratic, militaristic powers of the city against the rising 
bourgeoisie made rich by the industrial revolution.
The building itself became a battle ground for civic 
control over who had the right to control the city and public 
space. One of the major conflicts that arose referred to the 
positioning of the museum. Initially, Schinkel drew the muse­
um in the position he preferred, facing head on to the pal­
ace, in a sort of visual challenge. The space between the two 
institutions, the square, became charged in this scenario, 
with the two facades in competition with one another. How­
ever, the king was not happy with the positioning and Schin­
kel was forced to turn it out of alignment, setting it side­
ways to the palace and allowing the palace to re-establish a 
dominant position. Further changes to the landscaping 
around the museum also occurred, which can be read as the 
king’s attempt to maintain dominance over the palace.

SCHINKEL’S OTHER BERLIN PROJECTS
After the Altes Museum opened in 1830, Schinkel contin­

Schinkel could now implement his other project: the reloca­

the north. The new custom houses, on the north of the 
island behind the Altes Museum, are three individual inter­
ventions, whose impact was again calculated visually. When 
viewed from the palace bridge, the three custom houses 
come together as a procession, the gap between them closes 
up, and they form a visual continuity with the corner of the 
Altes Museum. As the relocation of the custom houses 
meant that all cargo boats now stopped at the north of the 
island, the river to the south was now used only by pleasure 
boats. This completely changed the bank of the river, which 
became a place of leisure and promenading.

CONCLUSION
The Altes Museum is a significant example of Schinkel’s 
architectural and urban design strategy. It effectively encap­
sulates his understanding of architecture as artistic product 
and Zweckmässigkeit, as well as his vision for a new Berlin. 
Most of Schinkel’s Berlin has now disappeared, either 
bombed during the war or demolished. The Altes Musem, 
while nearly destroyed, remains as a reminder of how a 
monument can reorient the direction of a city.

INTRODUCTION 
After the start of the construction of the Altes Museum 
in 1823, Schinkel traveled to France, England, and Scotland. 
He was interested in England because of the British Muse­
um, which he oculd use as a reference for the Altes, but also 
for the technology and landscape architecture of England. In 
addition to the idealized landscapes of the English pictur­
esque (see chapter 17), Schinkel discovered and was im­
pressed by the scale and the dramatic effects of the industri­
al revolution. The scale of the factories had reached the 
dimension of ‘royal palaces’, a scale that had an effect on 
workers as well. The revolution of the social order in Eng­
land brought a revolution in architecture as well. Architec­
ture, work, and production were closely linked through 
changing class relations.
The effects of industrialization in England instigated a 
reactionary search for traditional values and a revival of 
social principles that could be represented in architecture. 
Gothic architecture became the new reference for this quest. 
In contrast to classical tendencies, Gothic architecture was 
seen to embody a purer form of aspiration to God and 
‘truth’. The Arts and Crafts movement brought about a 
critique of industrialization in the form of utopian social 
experiments. At the same time, industrialization was chang­
ing cities. The development of the railway and railway sta­
tions not only shaped new architectural typologies and 
spaces but also heavily influenced the cultural and artistic 
imagery of the time.

DEFINING 19TH-CENTURY GOTHIC
The Gothic refers to a very long period of time, spanning 
between the 12th and 15th century, and its meaning develops 
on two levels. On the one hand, it was a technical revolu­
tion, an optimization of constructive processes, as evidenced 
in the cases of Notre-Dame or Chartres Cathedral. On the 
other hand, it had strong political and social connotations 
beyond religious buildings. The function of Gothic architec­
ture in service of the State cannot be overlooked and its 
wide European extension includes castles and palace com­
plexes, especially in regions like Spain or Poland. 
In 19th-century England, various theoreticians and practi­
tioners studied Gothic architecture not just for its formal 
and aesthetic qualities, but for the moral and ethical ones 
they believed it conveyed, and the associations they provided 
with earlier periods of their national history. The interest in 
Gothic architecture was a reaction against the current mod­
els of the time, derived from the Grand Tour and the Classi­
cal tradition as well as the impersonal and alienating work­
ing conditions of an industrialized building world. The 
Gothic of the Middle Ages represented an ideal epoch in 
which workers freely created high-quality works and could 
express their creativity and where people were believed to live 
simpler, more congenial lives. For instance, monks, whose 
mission was to help their fellow man, thrived in England at 
that time, until they were mostly extinguished in the 16th 
century. 
The moral attributes embedded in Gothic architecture 
were attractive because of their relationship with the past,

view of the Gothic. The perception of Gothic cathedrals as 
the outcome of a cooperative enterprise was primarily fueled

ways the Gothic construction system anticipated the strict 
division of labor, which was formalized during the Renais­

maker and ‘author’ of a building. Brunelleschi, discussed in 
chapter 2, is a very important example of this formalization. 
Ultimately, the neo-Gothic demonstrates how a style depends 
not only on the specificity of its time, but also on the chang­
ing perspective of later epochs on earlier ones, which in art 
history is called ‘reception’, which includes processes of 
appropriations and original development.

PUGIN’S CONTRASTS 
Although the first motivations for the emergence of the 
neo-Gothic can be found during the 18th century, it is in the 
19th century that a moral dimension was applied to the 
formal characteristics of this architecture. One of the most 
important theoreticians of the 19th-century Gothic Revival in 
England was Augustus Welby Northmore Pugin (1812–52). 
A fervent Catholic, Pugin wrote about Gothic England as the 
embodiment of lost ideals that had to be regained and 
played a central role in the propagation of the neo-Gothic 
style. In 1836 he published a book that had far-reaching 
consequences and influence, entitled Contrasts, or a Parallel 
Between the Architecture of the 15th and 19th Centuries. By 
means of illustrations, the book compares images of cities in 
an idyllic and merry past with images of the present, in 
which the city has been overwhelmed by the transformations 
of the industrial revolution.
The effects of industrialization on the environment are 
the central feature of the book: factories along the river, a 
ruined monastery church, a new prison in the foreground, 
chimneys instead of steeples as dominant vertical elements. 
With the motto ‘Catholic England was Merry England’, 
Pugin, who was a converted Catholic, advocated for a return 
to tradition and to Catholic England. The association came 
through the verticality of Gothic architecture as an expres­
sion of aspiration towards God and morality. This compari­
son with the past was not just aesthetic but contained a 
deep social and cultural critique of the present. One of the 
most important of Pugin’s works was the arrangement of the 
Medieval Court at the Great Exhibition of 1851. This ar­

JOHN RUSKIN
John Ruskin (1819–1900) was a writer, draughtsman, 
painter, and collector and one of the most important and 
critical contributors to the Gothic Revival. Ruskin adopted 
Pugin’s Gothic enthusiasm and quasi-religious charge but 
reinterpreted it morally and called for a more contemporary 
Gothic style. He took up the idea that architecture should 
acquire a moral dimension. While Ruskin was not a Catho­
lic, he was influenced by various Christian symbols and 
meaning. His first important work, The Seven Lamps of 
Architecture (1849) was structured around Christian values, 
represented by seven lamps, or seven criteria for good archi­
tecture. The title comes from a line in a psalm, ‘Thy word is 
a lamp unto my feet’ (Psalm 119:105). With The Seven 
Lamps Ruskin transferred Pugin’s Catholic morality into a 
Protestant-English ethic. This work became central to the 
Gothic Revival movement. 
The ‘seven lamps’ of Ruskin’s book are Sacrifice, Truth, 
Power, Beauty, Life, Memory, and Obedience, and are dis­
cussed in relation to architecture. For example, Ruskin 
understood Truth to be the rejection of 1) the simulation of 
false support structures; 2) the painterly imitation of false 
materials (e.g. marbling of wood) and sculptural ornamenta­

tion; and 3) the use of any cast or machine-made element. 
Where previous writers had rejected ornament for various 
reasons (for example Durand), Ruskin embraced it:
‘Ornament […] has two entirely distinct sources of 
agreeableness: one, that of the abstract beauty of its 
forms, which […] we will suppose to be the same wheth­
er they come from the hand or the machine; the oth­
er, the sense of human labor and care spent upon it.’
The decision to return to the Gothic was embedded in a 
social and political view centered on English Protestantism. 
Ruskin defended what he called the ‘northern style’ as built 
by the Goths, free people rather than slaves. He pleaded the 
case for an ‘imperfect’ architecture that is the result of 
truthful and real building, completed by many rather than 
one. 
Between 1851 and 1853 Ruskin published another mile­
stone, The Stones of Venice, an expanded reformulation of 
The Seven Lamps. In it, he examines the Venetian Gothic, 
advancing the idea that it represented a golden age of cul­
tural unity: religious, economic, and political life transformed 
into public expression and visible in architecture. Ruskin 
structures the narrative according to a logic of cultural 
advancement and decline, the first part on the foundations, 
the second on sea-stories, and the third on the fall. Ruskin’s 
argument was clearly opposed to the Renaissance and Pal­
ladian traditions, which he saw as the embodiment of coer­
cive and elitist forms of production, decided by few and 
realized by many without freedom of expression.

WILLIAM MORRIS
William Morris (1834–96) took up the urgency expressed 
by Ruskin and applied it to a comprehensive design strategy 
for the built environment, including not only architecture but 
also wallpaper, fabrics, and book design. As a committed 
socialist, Morris strove for a society where people could work 
for pleasure, a notion already proposed by Ruskin. Morris 
was one of the most successful and original followers of Rus­
kin’s theories, using them to inform a movement called the 
Arts and Crafts. The first showcase of the Arts and Crafts 
Exhibition Society took place in 1888. The Arts and Crafts 
aimed to re-establish the value of craftsmanship against an 
overwhelming industrial production, and against the per­
ceived decline of taste produced by it. Connected to the 
Gothic Revival and to the Pre-Raphaelite movement in visual 
art, the Arts and Crafts was headed by Morris and his 
immediate followers, particularly W. Crane and C. R. Ashbee. 
Morris had considerable influence in all fields of applied art, 
such as wallpaper, furniture, textile, typography and writing, 
as well as architecture. He also founded a printing house, 
called Kelmskott Press, and produced highly decorated 
books following early printing techniques. For example, he 
designed and published Ruskin’s book The Stones of Venice 
in 1892.

THE RAILWAY
The development of the railway was a particularly power­
ful phenomenon of the new industrial society criticized by 
Morris. The English railway system expanded substantially 
during the first half of the 19th century and, although this 
was an uneven development made up of many separate 
private enterprises, by the early 20th century it had trans­

the railway proceeded quickly; by 1845 more than 3,000 kilo­

CONCLUSION 
The reconsideration of craftmanship and applied and 
decorative arts proposed by the Gothic Revival and the Arts 
and Crafts movement was intended to generate what came 
to be known as a ‘total work of art’, where all aspects of a 
product concurred in its artistic character. Architecture soon 
turned into the quintessential embodiment of this idea, a 
combination of all other forms of arts. The moral and artis­
tic importance that architecture played for the Gothic Revival 
and the Arts and Crafts had a strong influence on later 
movement, such as the Garden City, the Art Nouveau and 
20th century modernism, especially the Bauhaus.

NOTRE-DAME DE PARIS 
The French writer, poet, and politician Victor Hugo 
(1802–1885) was one of the grands hommes of 19th-century 
France. Notre-Dame de Paris is one of his earliest novels, 
published in 1831–32. The story takes place in Paris in the 
late 15th century, its Gothic period. The novel is a romantic 
and dramatic story that plays around the church of No­
tre-Dame. It was meant to portray values and ideas of the 
past, as well as to mirror conditions of Hugo’s present. 
Notre-Dame Cathedral in fact appears almost as a character 
in its own right and Hugo injects the novel with details and 
a true engagement towards medieval architecture.
‘This Will Kill That’
The last chapter of the book, entitled ‘Ceci Tuera Cela’ 
(‘This Will Kill That’), is a discourse on architecture that 
expresses Hugo’s programmatic outlook related to the 
notion of the monument. This part was added to the second 
edition of the book. The chapter narrates a meeting between 
Archdeacon Frollo and two mysterious guests who want to 
learn alchemy. Instead of opening up books, Frollo shows 
his guests the Gothic churches of Paris, explaining that they 
are alchemical sources made of very explicit decorations, 
inscriptions and symbols. 
In Frollo’s argument, the true sources of knowledge that 
one should study are buildings. Then at the end of his 
explanation, Frollo picks up an early book and shouts, ‘This 
will kill that!’. With this expression, Hugo is comparing

## S. 227

227
‘this’, the book, to ‘that’, architecture, to warn that the 
former are dangerously taking over the role of the latter: the 
book will kill the building. At the root of Hugo’s theory is 
the assumption that architecture, like printing, is primarily 
an expression of human thought that becomes materialized 
in stone. Printing is also capable of this and therefore has 
the potential to ‘kill’ the function of true architecture.
Proving Hugo’s Argument
Hugo theorizes that in architecture, the medium and the 
message (what Vitruvius called ‘signifier’ and ‘signified’) 
determine each other. Human thought is at the center of 
Hugo’s argument. It is considered an entity that exists and 
seeks to be expressed, commemorated, and recorded. To 
understand how architecture relates to human thought, 
Hugo writes an origin myth. Utterly different from the Vitru­
vius’s myth, according to which people start to build as 
response to the need for shelter, Hugo’s story foregrounds 
the idea that human thought by its very nature seeks ex­
pression and materialization. To this end mankind, almost 
instinctively, chooses stone, the most durable and permanent 
medium at its disposal. And because human thought 
evolves, the expression of human thought in stone evolves as 
well.
Architecture as a Language

tences, so architecture originates from combinations and

The Invention of Printing
The primacy of architecture as the ‘mother of all arts’

the impact of printing on European and global culture. 
Basically, it all roots back to the idea that because of the 
emergence of printing, the transmission of knowledge was 
fundamentally changed. This important idea is already 
present in Hugo’s thought.

ready dead by the late 15th century. During the Renaissance, 
architecture changed, becoming just as the other arts, a 
response to human needs and no longer a fundamental 
expression of human thought. According to Hugo, the role 
of the Renaissance architect was only to copy forms, motifs, 
and geometrical systems borrowed from Greek and Roman 
architecture. For Hugo, the Renaissance, rather than a 
rebirth, was the ‘setting sun which we mistake for the dawn’.
Hugo thus criticizes architecture from the late 15th centu­
ry to his own time. Baroque and Rococo are similarly criti­
cized. His own time, the 19th century architecture, lacked any 
authentic artistic expression. It was an architecture only of 
function. Additionally, the arts became separated, and archi­
tecture therefore lost its collective role as ‘mother of all 
arts’. For Hugo the ‘death of architecture’ came about as a 
result of the appearance of the book and the freedom of 
thought facilitated by printing. From then on, architecture 
as a bearer of meaning was replaced by an architecture of 
imitation, frivolity, social inequality, and dry functionalism. 
Therefore, architecture lost its purpose and meaning.

What is Hugo’s Purpose?
In his writings, Hugo addresses the current problems of 
the 1820s and 1830s. A complete sterility of Neo-Classicism 
had been developing since the mid-18th century, and he 
observed an incapacity to understand the true value of 
historic buildings. 
Hugo’s focus comprises two connected problems. The 
first is understanding what a system of architecture is and 
where it is generated. Hugo’s architecture originates from 
society rather than nature (like in Laugier). This is turn 
produces a defense of medieval past and a harsh criticism of 
Neo-Classicism. The second central problem is the search 
for an appropriate form of representation of architecture fit 
for a specific society. This problem is related to the under­
standing and definition of beauty, but also to ‘caractère’ 
(like in the writings of Boffrand), the reflection of the archi­
tect’s intentions onto the building.

HENRI LABROUSTE
One of the architecture students that Hugo was thinking 
of, and with whom he was writing and exchanging letters 
with in the 1820s, was Henri Labrouste (1801–75). 
Labrouste is one of the architects that most engaged with 
the topics Hugo addressed, such as how to engage with the 
architecture of the past centuries and how to develop a 
meaningful architectural language that would be not as 
sterile as classicism. He is mainly known for two very impor­
tant buildings, the Bibliothèque Sainte-Geneviève and the 
Bibliothèque Nationale in Paris.
Labrouste studied at the Ecole des Beaux Arts, where in 
1824 he won the Prix de Rome, the most important and 
prestigious prize that an architecture student could obtain. 
It allowed him to spend five years in Rome, from 1825 to 
1830. This study program entailed a practice-based curricu­
lum structured around survey drawings of Roman buildings 
and their reconstructions.

THE PARIS OF BARON HAUSSMANN
The Paris Opéra (1861–75) by Charles Garnier (1825-98) 
can be interpreted as an architectural statement, along the 
same lines of Labrouste’s library projects. It follows the 
logic of the Romantic movement, which moved away from 
the classical tradition, while still using some classical ele­
ments, to address the needs of contemporary society. It thus 
can be seen as a truly 19th-century monument. The Opéra 
was realized at a time when the relationship between archi­
tecture and the city was changing, and therefore can only be 
fully understood as part of the urban redevelopment of Paris.
 
The Development of Paris 
During the 19th century, city administrators became 
preoccupied with the urban development of Paris. Other 
European cities, such as London or Berlin, were undergoing 
enormous growth. With industrialization came a dramatic 
influx of people and with it many new problems: hygiene, 
transportation, housing, geographical expansion. The main 
actor in the development of Paris was Georges-Eugène 
Haussmann (1809–91). He was a prefect and was mandated 
under Napoleon III to redesign Paris.

concerning historical monuments. In the Paris of Hauss­
mann—still visible today—long streets (boulevards) were 
intercepted with monuments. It was a very deliberate strate­
gy to treat monuments as isolated objects, in a way relata­
ble to what Nicholas V did in Rome in the 16th century (see 
chapter 11).
Meanwhile, public space became shared by different 
social classes. This sharing of space was also related to 
developments in the housing market, where new residential 
typologies foreshadowed the diverse layering of classes in 
one area. These new typologies affected the interior of hous­
es as well, which underwent a process of increasing speciali­
zation of rooms, where public and private spaces became 
more separated.
THE OPÉRA GARNIER
Garnier, put in charge to design the new Opera House, 
was a man of humble origins, who took drawing lessons as 
a child and was eventually admitted to the École des Beaux-
Arts, where he won of the Prix de Rome. When he returned

## S. 253

253
from his travels, rather than becoming an independent archi­
tect, he entered into different kinds of state services, eventu­
ally becoming the architect of the what became known as 
simply the Paris Opéra.
The Brief 
When the redevelopment process of Paris began, the 
northwestern zone of the center lacked a suitable public 
monument. It needed some sort of a marker within the new 
structure of the city. Thus, it was decided to develop the 
project for an opera house on this still dense medieval urban 
site. Haussmann redesigned the urban structure of the 
boulevard to accommodate the new monument. 
The building was thought of as an integral part of the 
city, visible from the boulevard, embodying the idea of a real 
public building functioning as an isolated monument, stand­
ing as a landmark. An initial proposal by Charles Rohault 
de Fleury was dismissed, and a competition was held in­
stead. The brief was a very short. The new building had to 
consist of three essential parts: public theater; administra­
tion, appropriate for visits from the emperor Napoleon III; 
and a dialogue with the street. 
Garnier composed an ensemble of different parts. The 
main rectangular body was flanked by satellite volumes on 
the sides, and the roofs clearly articulated the different parts 
of the building inside. Garnier planned a building that typi­
cally embodied the design principles of the École des Beaux-
Arts:

1. Marche (rigid succession of spaces, often organized 
around an axial or bi-axial system).
2. Composition (assemblage and combination of vol­
umes).
3. Intersection of simple geometrical forms in plan to 
create an ordered grid of spaces.
4. Coherence and legibility in the exterior as well as the 
interior.
The marche was a leading principle that emerged at the 
École des Beaux-Arts and entailed the capacity to organize 
the spaces in a logical sequence. The other important 
Beaux-Arts principle was the notion of composition. The 
plan should be generated by combining intersections of very 
basic geometrical forms. And finally, a series of axes, prima­
ry and secondary, should keep the whole geometrical compo­
sition together, so as to create a legible, coherent construc­
tion. 
The Staircase and its Users
Garnier, who was involved with the building of the opera 
throughout the whole process, wrote an extensive description 
of his design choices, entitled Le Théatre (1871). In his text, 
the idea of a new monument connecting with the conditions 
of Haussmann’s Paris becomes very clear. Additionally, in 
response to the social reconfigurations of Paris, Garnier’s 
building was designed to function for different groups of 
users. In fact, an important theme in the design was how 
these different groups of users should be separated, meet,

## S. 255

255
look at each other, and experience the opera. Garnier distin­
guished four groups of users of his building: people who 
arrive on foot and have a ticket; people who arrive on foot 
but do not have a ticket; people who arrive in coach or by 
horse and have a ticket; people who arrive at the last minute 
and do not have a ticket. In Le Théatre, Garnier essentially 
describes how each of these groups can use his building. 
All groups were supposed to meet on the monumental 
staircase. This is considered the heart of the building, since 
it is the first space where everyone comes together. It is also 
considered the place where the real pleasure of going to the 
theater in unfolded. The space consists of three boxes that 
interlock: the main perimetral walls of the volume, a second 
frame of balconies, and a third box of rooms behind the first 
two. This layered spatial configuration around the staircase 
is designed to facilitate a socially driven game of watching 
each other, seeing each other, of entering and showing off. 
For Garnier, it was very important to have a variety of 
rooms, as people want to do different things and have differ­
ent kinds of encounters during the breaks in the perfor­
mance. The heart of these social scene was the Grand Foyer. 
Theater as Social Metaphor
The whole idea behind this building is Garnier’s belief 
that the best way to think about society and how society 
works is a theater. According to him, everything that hap­
pens is in fact like a play and the matrix of society is itself 
a theatrical interplay between people. A building like the 
Opéra materializes the very processes that are inherent to

CONCLUSION
The theoretical and discursive articulation of architectur­
al history laid out by Hugo can be associated with 
Labrouste’s and Garnier’s projects for Paris as prototypical 
instances of a transformation in architectural sensibility in 
19th-century France. Within the institutional curriculum of 
the École des Beaux-Arts, Hugo and Labrouste embody an 
active critique of architectural education and the search for 
new linguistic meanings and genealogies suitable for the 
19th-century. In turn, Garnier adopted a classical vocabulary 
to monumentalize the social protocols of Parisian society in 
the Opéra, which also fits with Labrouste’s libraries as 
components in the larger urban reconfiguration of Paris.
