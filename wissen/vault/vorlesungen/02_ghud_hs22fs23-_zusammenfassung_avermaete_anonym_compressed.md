## S. 4

THE HISTORY AND THEORY OF THE CITY AS PROJECT 
01 
THE CITY? 
-
currently we are experiencing ‚planetary urbanization‘ all over the globe 
-
more than 50% of world population is living in urban environments since start of 21st century 
-
questions that we will discuss in this course: 
-
What is this condition called ‚the city‘ that we live + work in? 
-
How did it come into being? 
-
According to which principle was is developed? 
-
How did it evolve into the megalopolis that we know today? 
Dictionary ‚City’ 
-
a large town 
-
British A town created a city by charter Gründungsurkunde + usually containing a cathedral 
-
North American A municipal centre incorporated by state or province 
-
A place or situation characterized by a specified attribute 
-
Middle English: from Old French cite, from Latin civets, from civil ‚citizen‘ related to citizen and the people, ‚befitting a citizen‘ 
-
many field + people have tried to understand of what the city is about 
La revolution urbaine, Henri Lefebvre, 1970 (City defined by social aspects, a way to stay with other people; city provides for new social grouping + division) 
Les Villes et les Institutions Urbaines, Henri Pirenne, 1939 (city defined by economical aspects, in city everything is traded + exchanged) 
-
also architects that tried to define, what city is about 
 
Learning form Las Vegas, Venturi, 1972 
 
Delirious New York, Rem Koolhaas, 1978 
A MANMADE MATERIAL AND SPATIAL PROJECT 
- The city can be understood as a manmade material + spatial project. 
- Hypothesis 
-
city is not self organized artifact or organism but result of a combination of willful acts that can be uncovered, mapped, understood 
+ explained 
-
Cities are no incidental occurrences, they are material results of intentional decisions + processes 
-
Along with other parties such as politicians, investors + citizens, architects + urban designers play a crucial role 
-
a lot of architects from the very beginning were interested in describing city, understanding how it evolved: 
-
Piranesi 
-
Forma Urbis by Piranesi, Map of ancient Rome, 3rd century: an attempt to describe the city 
-
Following a great fire in Rome in 191, Emperor Septimius Severus not only rebuilt famous Temple of Emperor Vespasian, but also commissioned large marble 
map of Rome, which was made between 203 and 211 + attached to the wall of an auditorium of the temple 
-
150 marble slabs with detailed outlines of individual buildings, streets + aqueducts 
-
this gives us a very detailed information about the city 
-
Vitruv 
-
De Architectura Libri Decem by Vitruvius, Roman architect, describing how a city was constructed from scratch 
-
1 beginning with house of correct site + layouting of walls 
-
2 collection of building materials 
-
3-7 construction of different types of building 
-
8 securing future flourishing of city by supplying it with water 
-
9 clocks 
-
10 defense mechanisms 
-
city is always an interplay of 2 important elements: fabrica (=construction, practice of building, making) + ratiocinatio (=ideas, conceptual approach, thinking)

De Re Aedificatoria, we can look at the city as a project + project of building the city

(unlike Vitruvius) he considers architecture as something that doesn’t exist yet + must be conceived through series of rules + standards; something that can

in his 5th book, he’s saying that house is a miniature city

his idea inspired a lot of people like pope Sixtus V

URBAN DESIGN AS ENGAGEMENT 
-
architecture can be understood as a sort of engagement with the city 
-
we will look upon urban design as a ‚reflective conversation with the situation‘ ‚immersed in material culture, and draw upon it 
as the primary source of their thinking‘ - Donald Schön 
-
the main point is that we will try to have a look at the city as a material artifact, city is intentional and not made by itself 
The Ground 
-
architecture is often an engagement with ground/topography 
-
in a very early stage, people tried charting + mapping ground, understanding the topography of a city 
-
a lot of cities are strongly determined by their topography zB Constantinople 
-
what is nearer to the water + lower will become the commercial zone where trading + shipping is going on, on the higher 
topology there will be the main public + religious buildings 
-
measuring the topography as a 1st step in urban design water, mountains, … 
-
sometimes topography went hand in hand with a military logic, location of a city out of a defensive mechanism was high on a hill 
(those places had the best view on the territory + easiest defended) 
-
cities that are shaped due to topography 
-
Las Vegas: a city that is formed along a street 
-
Boston: relation of morphology + geography at Scollay Square, all the streets in Boston were about crossing the slope (without understanding the 
topography, you can’t understand this Scollay Square) 
-
Cairo: formed along the Nil river, even the metro line was following the system of topography 
-
we can also look at the ground as the material quality 
-
Amsterdam: quality of soil played a big role, was built on a very swampy area, water canals, whole city is based on a wooden foundation structure, very 
humid soil required particular way of making foundations, whole city can be understood in relation to condition of this soil 
-
Berlin: built in Delta among some rivers including the Spree, in this Delta there’s a very sandy ground, soil has again not very strong bearing capacities, height 
of city is therefore very consistent (26m) 
-
New York: bedrock is a very very hard stone on which you can built very high, if we look at the map today we can see that these high buildings are exactly 
placed where this stone is, quality of soil is again determining layout of city 
-
earthquakes, sometimes ground is something that you need to battle, to conquer, to react to 
-
Lisbon: in 1755 big earthquake, caused a fire + big demolition, city was rebuilt in new principles, with wider streets + more solid blocks 
-
Tokyo: 1855 city was hit with an earthquake + fires, changing outlook of city as well, it remained small scale but one feature that reminds us of this 
earthquake: all houses don’t have parting walls, openings in-between all houses 
Materials 
-
often engagement with local material available 
-
used + also reused material 
-
Bogota: the soil is full of clay, bricks are easily produced with clay soil, clay bricks are also reused in city, bricks are also used for large projects 
-
Bath, UK: in 18th century a big extension was made, consistency between new part + historical part of city because of same material quality, limestone is a material 
that you can find nearby 
Climate 
-
we believe that relation with climate is enormously important 
-
Hippocrates: „Cities that are exposed to winds between the summer and the winter risings of the sun, and those the opposite to them, have the following 
characters: Those which lie to the rising of the sun are all likely to be more healthy than such as are turned to the North, or those exposed to the hot winds.“ 
-
Vitruvius: „the direction of your streets + alleys be laid down on the lined of division between two winds.“, he believed that we have to locate our city gates inbetween the winds 
-
Montreal: harsh + very cold winter, subterranean city which connects all the buildings in the city without that you need to go out in this harsh winterly climate, we 
find city squares + passages that are in a subterranean way built and often built like a shopping mall 
-
Bologna: very hot climate + humid climate during fall and spring, galleries allow you to walk dry in fall and spring and in shadow in summer, there are 37km portici 
where one can walk through city in shadow 
Culture 
-
Paris: capitalist bourgeois culture was emerging, urban element of ‚passage‘ was introduced in already existing blocks, interior of urban block became part of public 
domain, that meant that one could have had shops there, and passages as scenery for bourgeois life of society, Parisian flaneur 
-
London: Garden Squares in London emerged from big agricultural fields, represent a new idea of living in city, new urban figure with houses around it (they look 
public but they are private) + meant for the people living around it, semi public space fenced 
Philosophy 
-
philosophical texts + treatises have underpinned conception + development of city throughout time 
-
Treatise Politea by Plato, harmonization of whole (the city) with part (the individual), twelve sections + each citizen will have 2 residences (one urban, central + 
one rural, peripheral); geometry of urban layout can regulate relation between citizen + collective; everyone should have equal access + rights in the city 
-
this idea will then inspire greek architect Hippodamus of Miletus he works with double residences in his urban plan of Athens

independently in 6 parts of the world

THE EARLY SETTLEMENTS OF SOUTHWEST ASIA

-
illustrates how proto-urban artifacts emerged that were organized according to new principles 
-
proto-urban artifacts 
-
found ca. 8000 BC, nowadays Turkey 
-
was located at hilly site with a very good soil for agriculture (one of first settlements that produced agriculture) 
-
areas with houses + areas with sort of public buildings, in this case ritual temples 
-
on map: yellow = housing, green = temples 
-
public buildings are connected to new conception of time, rituals were performed there

columns supported a roof 
Settlement of Catal Hüyük: 
-
founded ca. 7400 BC 
-
emerged from trade in most sought after (begehrteste) commodity of Neolithic period: obsidian, material that comes from lava (vulcano) 
-
capacity to be very strong + very sharp 
-
very pressures material because it could be used for harvesting for this agriculture 
-
topography again very important, 2 mountains - it is believed that Catal Hüyük covered entire hill 
-

only houses (vgl. Göbekeli Tepe - no public buildings here), religious dimension happened now inside house (ritual put inside houses, made private) 
-
houses made of clay bricks that could dry with sun, walls were made of clay bricks with a wood structure covered with straw + clay 
-
houses were built next to each other, so that whole structure was more stable, they could use less material, but also to defend settlement: in this way there were 
no roads or paths, access to individual houses happened via roofs (roof circulation movement, houses all had a latter to enter through roof) 
-
society was very balanced + equal, all houses are same or similar 
-
houses consisted of 3 platforms: sleeping/sitting, cooking, ritual zone 
-
pattern of town, dense fabric of rectangular houses without streets + with circulation on roofs, illustrates emergence of new social arrangements
