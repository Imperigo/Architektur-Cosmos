Open-Ended Strategies 
for Imploding Cities
The ‘Projets Urbains’
In the last decades of the twentieth century, the unbridled urbanization in the Global South and North 
was paired with the first wave of deindustrialization. 
This process of the displacement and reduction of 
heavy industry and manufacturing not only brought 
economic and social change to specific regions and 
cities, but also posed new urban challenges—especially in Europe and North America. Vast sites of former harbors, ship yards, factories, and coal mines 
became vacant. Generally located in advantageous 
locations near city centers or along waterfronts, and 
supported by existing infrastructure, these post-industrial landscapes constituted a valuable resource 
that could be repurposed for new urban development.
The transformation of these post-industrial landscapes required the elaboration of new methods 
and tools of urban design. In France, the destruction of the former Parisian wholesale market of Les 
Halles in 1973 resulted in a complex political and 
public debate on the role of urban design. As a result of the fierce urban debates on the future of Les 
Halles, an alternative design approach called a ‘projet urbain’ was developed from the late 1970s. This 
new approach, which soon gained currency across 
Europe, took as its point of departure that it was no 
longer possible to design large encompassing projects for the future city. As an alternative, ‘projets 
urbains’ were conceived as specific architectural 
design projects in which the different programs, but 
also the various scales of the city—ranging from the 
territory to the building—are related to each other in 
a concrete fashion. Such a ‘projet urbain’ was credited with a strategic role in the future development 
of the city.
The initiative for a ‘projet urbain’ was typically taken 
by city administrations. In the case of Les Halles, the 
Paris Urban Agency (APUR) offered a first proposal 
on how to deal with the various programs and infrastructures of the site. This initial project was followed 
by competitions (1975 and 1979) in which renowned 
architects such as Ricardo Bofill, Franco Purini, and 
Aldo Rossi participated. An important characteristic 
of the ‘projets urbain’ is that design is not considered as a final blueprint for the city, but rather as an 
open-ended proposal that should be criticized and 
negotiated by various stakeholders.
 
The competition projects for Les Halles by several 
architects were believed to offer different scenarios 
on the future of the city center. They were understood as statements about future urban forms and 
public spaces, and ranged from proposals that suggested mimicking the historical city to more radical 
plans to introduce a mega-structure in the very heart 
of Paris. The competition drawings and models were 
widely published and publicly exhibited. They would 
become part of a large public and political discussion on the future of the historical city center. On the 
basis of these discussions, a final project for the socalled Forum des Halles was elaborated in 1979 by 
Claude Vasconi and Georges Pencreac. 
One of the most exemplary ‘projets urbains’ was the 
urban design for Euralille (1989) by OMA, on a largely vacant site in between existing rail and road infrastructure in the northern French city of Lille. Based 
on a spatial analysis as well as discussions with 
prominent international architects, the commissioning authorities defined a ‘programmatic diagram’ for 
Euralille which consisted of four main functions: an 
urban service center, a transport facilities center, a 
city park, and a business center. They also determined that Euralille would not only become a new 
neighborhood of Lille, but also a crucial crossroads 
of main rail connections on the European continent, 
especially in relation to new TGV lines. 
As a result, OMA’s ‘projet urbain’ OMA was, first 
and foremost, based on a specific architectural approach to the infrastructural complexity of railroads, 
highways, and roads that firmly related the site to 
its wider context. This intertwinement of different in-

Infrastructural Urbanism
In Barcelona, Manuel de Sola-Morales suggested a 
more infrastructural approach to urban design with 
his renowned project for the Moll de la Fusta (Spain, 
1983). The point of departure was a well-known phenomenon in many cities at the end of the twentieth 
century: an old vacant harbor area and an intensively used road infrastructure next to the historical 
center, which created a barrier between city and 
sea. Starting from the idea that automobility and the 
related infrastructure were integral parts of the twentieth century city, Manuel de Sola-Morales proposed 
an urban design project to mitigate the border of 
the harbor and the road. The urban design project 
consists of an inventive new urban section which recalibrates the relation between city and highway by 
situating them on different levels. 
The public space of the city, through a system of 
bridges and public esplanades, is extended to the 
quays and docks of the old harbor. At the same time, 
and without hiding them from the city, the roads 
and parking spaces are located at a lower urban 
level, which allows them to co-exist with the other 
programs of the city. On top of the new urban landscape, a set of pavilions provides a frame to shops, 
restaurants, and cafés, thus expanding the public 
program of the historical city into the old harbor. In 
a single project of infrastructural urbanism, Manuel 
de Sola-Morales combines and regulates different 
programs, several modes of movement, and various 
urban atmospheres. His new urban section not only 
created a new connection between city and water, 
but also opened the gateway for the reconversion of 
Barcelona’s old harbor into a new part of the contemporary city.
Landscape Urbanism
Around the turn of the twenty-first century, landscape 
emerged as both a model and tool for contemporary 
urbanism, leading to the rise of ‘landscape urbanism’. Proponents of landscape urbanism propose 
a disciplinary realignment in which landscape supplants architecture’s historic role as the basic building block of urban form. They suggest that landscape is useful as a way of thinking through urban 
form in the wake of economic transformations, which 
have led to the shrinking of cities—what can also 
be called the ‘urban implosion’—and the abandonment of countless brownfield sites in the wake of industrial decline. Proponents of landscape urbanism 
claim that landscape is a medium uniquely capable 
of responding to temporal change, transformation, 
and adaptation, and thus well-suited to the contem-

porary urban condition, which is characterized by 
open-endedness and indeterminacy. 
The earliest projects to reveal the potential for landscape to operate as a model/tool for urban processes were proposed by European architects and 
urbanists interested in program and event as surrogates for contemporary urbanism. Bernard Tschumi’s (winning) entry for the Parc de la Villette competition in Paris (France, 1982), which was located 
on the site of a former slaughterhouse, presented a 
conceptual leap in the development of landscape 
urbanism. It pinpointed landscape as the most suitable medium through which to organize programmatic and social change over time. Rather than design a 
final form for the park, Tschumi’s concept proposed 
to set in place certain structural logics, consisting of 
three (interacting) structural elements: (1) a system 
of surfaces, (2) a system of lines, and (3) a system 
of points that were placed on a uniform grid. Each of 
the points on this grid were placed 110 meters apart, 
and assumed the form of a ‘folly’, an element that 
was very popular in eighteenth-and nineteenth-century English landscape architecture. While, in these 
historical precursors, follies were purely ornamental 
and had no function, Tschumi’s follies in the Parc 
de la Villette housed specific pieces of the park’s 
program. The system of lines, then, comprised different ‘types’ of line. Some of these functioned as 
structuring devices—such as the ones traversing 
the site from north to south, and from east to west—
while others were more whimsical (and also more 
freeform in design), such as the promenade cinematique, which meanders across the site. Finally, 
the system of surfaces comprised different types of 
surfaces; apart from paved surfaces and water bodies, there were also several large lawns, of which the 
most prominent are the ‘Prairie du Triangle’ and the 
‘Prairie du Cercle’.
Another paradigmatic example of landscape urbanism is the landscape park Duisburg Nord by Peter 
Latz (Germany, 1990). In 1988, the government of 
North Rhine-Westphalia (in Germany) decided to 
carry out the Internationale Bauausstellung (IBA) 
Emscher Park program to convert the industrial German ‘rust belt’ into a green, modern, and wealthy 
metropolitan area. Within the frame of this exhibition, 
some hundred projects were realized that sought 
to transform the formerly industrial Ruhr region—in 
environmental, economic, and social terms. Peter 
Latz’s landscape park Duisburg Nord was one of 
these projects. In his design, Latz integrated the existing patterns that were formed by the site’s previous industrial use into a new spatial syntax to create 
a new recreational urban landscape. Amongst these 
elements were the existing railway bundles and 
lines, which were converted into promenades, and 
the Emscher wastewater channel that traversed the 
site from east to west. This channel was subjected to 
an intensive purification process, and transformed 
into a water promenade and water park. These different linear elements re-appropriated as structuring 
devices defined the outline of different landscape 
‘chambers’, each with its own character, which was 
further articulated through smaller, site-specific projects.
Another example of early landscape urbanist practice is West 8’s redevelopment plan for Borneo and 
Sporenburg in the Amsterdam harbor (The Netherlands, 1993). The redevelopment of these peninsulas was part of a larger project for the redevelopment 
of Amsterdam’s Eastern Docklands. In the post-war 
years, the port activities that had traditionally taken 
place in this area, which included mostly cargo and 
passenger transportation to the former Dutch colonies, gradually diminished, and by the 1970s had 
moved altogether to the better-equipped Western 
docklands, which were able to cater to the needs 
of the modern shipping industry. With these docks 
vacated, a large urban redevelopment project was 
initiated, conceived as an enormous landscape 
urbanist project, and orchestrated by West 8, into 
which the work of numerous other architects and designers was inserted. The project shows the diver-

sity that exists in landscape urbanist strategies, as 
(contrary to the Parc de la Villette and Latz’s design 
for Duisburg Nord) it does not rely on large (regional 
and urban) gestures, but rather on a small-scale and 
punctual approach. It proposed to insert numerous 
small landscaped courts within the development, 
through a regulation that specified that each of the 
individual houses needed to include internal patios 
of generous dimensions, which comprised between 
30 and 50% of the lot size.
Since its inception in the late twentieth century, 
landscape urbanism has conquered the globe, with 
projects ranging from small-scale urban incisions 
to large territorial interventions. The 10-hectare 
Yuezhong Shipyard Park by the Beijing-based practice Turenscape is another prime example (China, 
2001). In their design, Turenscape reduced, reused, 
and recycled natural and man-made materials to 
create a new public realm on the water, and a tribute 
to the former workers of the shipyard, which closed 
in 1999. The reconversion of the shipyard was one of 
Turenscape’s first major projects, and demonstrates 
the potential of landscape urbanism, not only in social and cultural terms, but also in ecological terms. 
Seeking to counterbalance the disruptive effects 
of ongoing (and rapid) urbanization, Turenscape 
proposed a different type of urban design: one that 
does not work against nature, but that works with 
nature—a type of ecological urbanism.
Finally, one of the best-known landscape urbanism 
projects is undoubtedly the High Line in New York 
(US, 2004). This project was a combined effort by 
the architects Diller Scofidio + Renfro and landscape architect James Corner of Field Operations 
to revitalize an abandoned overhead railway in west 
Manhattan, by cleverly and carefully inserting a recreational use. The High Line emerged as the result 
of community opposition to the demolition of the 
freight line. Its design consists of different type-sections that each have a different proportion of natural 
(vegetated) surfaces vis-à-vis artificial (or paved) 
surfaces, ranging from unplanted in the ‘pit’ to fully 
vegetated in the ‘flyover’ section. Furthermore, different types of vegetation were introduced in these 
different type-sections to create different natural 
landscapes along the High Line’s trajectory: from 
meadow, to wetland, to woodland, etc. This combination of different landscape types also ensures 
that different natural cycles would occur within the 
vegetation, and that the High Line would always be 
animated; some plants would be flowering while others were not, and some areas would require greater 
maintenance at any given point in time than others. 
While the project itself can be described as a work 
of landscape architecture, the urban implications 
of the project are equally evident, as the intervention has catalyzed urban development and brought 
about an intensity of urban activity.
