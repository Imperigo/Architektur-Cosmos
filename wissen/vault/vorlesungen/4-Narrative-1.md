# 4 Narrative 1

## S. 1

Planning cultures, professions 
and education in the EU
Each national or regional context is characterised by particularities of history, by attitudes, beliefs
and values, political and legal traditions, different socio-economic patterns and concepts of justice,
interpretations of planning tasks and responsibilities, and different structures of governance – in
other terms: by its speciﬁc cultural characteristics.
(Knieling and Othengrafen 2009b: xxiv) 
Introduction
The preceding chapters in this book have concentrated on the formal institutions and
instruments of European spatial planning and territorial cooperation. However, many of the
challenges of international working between regions and countries are to do with the unofﬁcial
but taken-for-granted understandings of what spatial planning is and how to do it that are part
of everyday planning practice. When spatial planners from different countries get together in
cooperative activities, very different assumptions about the nature of planning quickly come to
the surface. They reﬂect deeply embedded differences in planning culture.
Working in a context of multiple planning cultures presents challenges but it also offers 
great rewards. Planners who are exposed to different assumptions about the notion of 
spatial planning than is usually the case in their own domestic settings are given a new 
and penetrating perspective on their practice. Indeed, working with colleagues from other
countries exposes the whole notion of alternative planning cultures and how planning education
and professional organisation help to perpetuate them. An understanding of planning culture
can prepare planners for multinational working and also provide fresh insights and knowledge
about one’s own planning approach. A consideration of European spatial planning and
territorial cooperation is thus not complete without a discussion of the concept of planning
culture.
This chapter ﬁrst introduces the concept of planning culture, how planning cultures may be
evolving in Europe in response to international and EU inﬂuences, and how they mediate
international forces for change. The different professional terminologies for spatial planning 
(as explained in Chapter 3) are an expression of the underlying planning cultures. The idea of
a profession is introduced and the particular characteristics of planning professions examined.
Professions take responsibility for guiding the education of new planners. Planning education
is a very topical subject because of the strong inﬂuence of EU harmonisation initiatives on
universities and their study programmes. The chapter concludes with a note on the likely
implications of current changes for the ﬁeld of spatial planning, its cultures, professions and
education.

## S. 2

Planning cultures in Europe
From the early 2000s there has been a renewed interest in academic debate on the idea of
planning culture and how it varies internationally, including across Europe (see, for example,
the edited volumes by Sanyal 2005 and by Knieling and Othengrafen 2009a). This ‘cultural turn’
recognises that the wider societal and cultural context has considerable influence on the
organisation and functioning of a planning system and development process. Institutional
contexts of planning vary widely among nations: differences in history, attitudes, beliefs and
values; divergent legal and governance traditions; and different interpretations of planning tasks
and political priorities. Much of this is evident in the planning culture.
Increasing international cooperation on planning has brought to the fore the signiﬁcant
differences in how planning is organised and understood, and the alternative ways that things
are done (see, for example, Sykes 2008). Problems arising from these differences explain, for
example, why reaching agreement on policy maps in the ESDP and spatial vision processes were
so controversial (Dühr 2007, 2009). Even cultures in neighbouring states can be very unalike,
as between the Netherlands and Belgium where the extensive cross-border cooperation is often
riddled with problems and misunderstandings (De Vries 2008). Over larger territories the mix
of cultures becomes more complex. De Vries and van den Broeck (1997) describe the differences
in planning in the Benelux (Belgium, the Netherlands and Luxembourg), where there is a long
history of cooperation, as a ‘microcosm of planning cultures’.
Experiencing other planning cultures in practice can have great value. The experience shared
by many involved in international planning work is that by investigating foreign planning
approaches and cultures, one learns most about one’s own planning system and planning culture.
Many aspects of planning are taken for granted because they are part of the traditional or
cultural understanding of ‘how things are done’. They are rarely questioned in the domestic
context. Comparisons of planning systems enrich experience and bring a cultural sensitivity to
how planning is practised in other parts of Europe or the world. They also provide insights into
planning practice and culture ‘at home’. And comparisons are ‘the seedbed of theorizing about
planning culture’ with Europe providing ‘an increasingly fertile ground for comparison’ (Faludi
2005b: 287). Moreover, planning in a globalising world cannot afford to be provincial and focus
only on domestic concerns within one cultural setting. Communicating and engaging with
planners from other countries, be it through writing or speaking, requires an ability to empathise
with an international audience. There are many pitfalls in cross-cultural practice and communication, as Chapter 3 explains, not least the tendency to inappropriately borrow solutions
from one place (and its planning culture) and apply them elsewhere without any consideration
for institutional and cultural differences. An appreciation of planning culture can help to avoid
such problems.
Understanding the concept of ‘planning culture’
The concept of planning culture is often rather loosely deﬁned and used broadly to represent
the norms, values and principles that underlie planning practice. It includes aspects of the broader
institutional context (such as assumptions about the role of government) and the wider cultural
context of spatial development (such as the value attached to landscapes). Faludi (2005b: 285–6)
deﬁnes planning culture as ‘the collective ethos and dominant attitudes of planners regarding
the appropriate role of the state, market forces, and civil society in inﬂuencing social outcomes’.
Friedmann (2005: 184) has described planning culture as ‘the ways, both formal and informal,
that spatial planning in a given multi-national region, country or city is conceived, institutionalised and enacted’. A planning culture is ‘characterised by particularities of history, by
attitudes, beliefs and values, political and legal traditions, different socio-economic patterns

Notions such as the ‘decision environment’ or ‘planning environment’ are very closely linked
to the concept of planning culture. The decision environment (Friedmann, quoted in Faludi
1973) refers to inﬂuential factors such as the number and diversity of organised groups and their
relative power, the degree of tolerance for political opposition, the role of characteristics of
private companies involved in planning processes and the availability of information. De Vries
and van den Broeck (1997) use a similar notion when they speak about the ‘planning environment’, which refers to the ‘fragmentation and diversity of interests and the equilibrium between
private enterprises and collective decision making in society’. They add that the ‘methods
government can adopt in implementing planning policy allow for different styles of spatial
planning, ranging from fairly selective to widely comprehensive plans’ (De Vries and van den
Broeck 1997: 58).
What all these deﬁnitions have in common is that they highlight the mental predispositions
that the majority of those involved in a planning culture share. That means planning culture
has much to do with mutual expectations that guide the actors’ behaviour in planning processes,
or to put it differently: ‘planning culture refers to values, attitudes, mind sets and routines shared
by those taking part in planning; it inﬂuences the perceived planning tasks, the behaviour in
groups or communities and the pursuit of particular interests therein’ (Fürst 2009: 26).
Despite its (perhaps understandably) rather fuzzy deﬁnition, planning culture is a notion that
is frequently used to explain differences (and difﬁculties) between planners from different
countries. In cross-national comparative research, however, this fuzziness presents both
conceptual and methodological challenges. As Fürst (2009: 23) explains, ‘planning culture
addresses a diffuse research area and is not bound to a speciﬁc body of theories’. Yet, culture
determines how we see and understand phenomena. Gullestrup (2009: 3) argues that ‘“reality”
will always be perceived and understood through one’s own culture, i.e. through one’s own
cultural glasses’, that is, as ‘reality’ is socially constructed, the way we perceive it is culturally
determined. Knowing this and the difﬁculties it creates is one thing, but separating out the effect
of planning culture from the effect of other inﬂuences such as government structure or language
on practice is difﬁcult (Fürst 2009).
The notion of culture can been broken down into its constituent parts to help with crossnational comparisons. For example, Gullestrup (2009) offers a model that separates the complex
entity of culture into ‘culture segments’ and ‘culture layers’. This model consists of a horizontal
dimension – which is the aspect of culture that is directly ‘perceivable’ and also reﬂected upon,
such as planning processes in law or decision-making arrangements – and a vertical dimension
– which refers to the underlying and more difﬁcult-to-perceive social structures and moral norms.
These are the deep and fundamental basic core values that are largely resistant to change, 
even if the immediately perceivable cultural layers appear to demonstrate considerable change
(Gullestrup 2009). So when we observe changes in planning culture it may be only on the
horizontal layers, whereas for a full understanding we would also need to investigate the core
values.
Planning culture is not ﬁxed; it will evolve in response to social, political, and economic
inﬂuences, both internal and external. Globalisation is a major factor ‘bringing about major
changes in the institutional structure, processes, inﬂuence and scope of planning’ (Friedmann
2005: 184). In Europe there have been quite dramatic changes in political conditions in parallel
with European integration and the demise of the Communist states. To some extent this may
prompt similar changes in different cultures (Waterhout et al. 2009). For example, Keller et al.
(1996) reviewed the evolution of planning cultures of Germany, France, Italy and Switzerland,
and observed similar changes in behaviour patterns towards ‘communicative planning’ and

cultures in Europe from more superﬁcial differences in customary planning practices and ways
of working. In the following sections three ways are reviewed in which the underlying aspects
of planning culture have been investigated that can offer insights into the effect of planning
culture in European spatial planning and territorial cooperation processes. These three
perspectives are: the relationship between planning cultures and social models; the concept of
the ‘spatial consciousness’ of a planning culture; and the concept of planning doctrine.
Planning cultures and social models
It is axiomatic that cultures are embedded in particular socio-economic political contexts. They
are the product of the way that society has evolved through history in particular places. So there
is, arguably, a very clear geography of cultures. This geography tends to persist despite European
integration; indeed, the European Union supports many actions that are intended to reinforce
local cultures in the interests of maintaining diversity. Culture is often aligned to political
geography, though this relationship breaks down in parts of continental Europe because of population and border movements. It may be that race, faith and other factors are more important
determinants of culture for some groups. However, planning cultures are likely to be strongly
linked to political geography because they are embedded in ways of governing society.
The fundamental values and principles underlying the approach to the organisation and
governance of territories have been collectively referred to as ‘a model of society’. Some of those
principles will involve: the relative roles of the state, market and civil society; the application
of democratic means in decision-making; the rights of citizens to participate in government; and
expectations about the distribution of services and beneﬁts to citizens. A particular model of
society suggests that certain principles are shared within a territory and provide mostly unspoken
‘ground rules’ or shared assumptions that guide policy- and decision-making in government. The
idea of a model of society has gained particular currency in the claim that there is a common
model across the EU. In particular, from the outside the similarities or ‘shared values’ across
Europe stand out: democracy, individual rights, free collective bargaining, the market economy,
the equality of opportunity for all, social welfare and solidarity (CEC 1994c). Elsewhere the
European Commission has emphasised that the European model is ‘characterized in particular
by systems that offer a high level of social protection, by the importance of the social dialogue
and by services of general interest covering activities vital for social cohesion’ (European
Council 2000b: 4).
While not disputing the very broad generality of this claim, other authors, looking from the
inside, have pointed to important variations across Europe, especially in relation to the way
countries address inequalities and welfare policy (see, for example, Giddens 2005). A common
starting point for these discussions is the well-known typology of European welfare states by
Esping-Andersen (1990). He investigated variations in the way that states seeks to maintain

The ‘spatial consciousness’ of planning traditions in Europe
The concept of ‘spatial consciousness’, introduced by Healey (2006a), is an important
component of planning culture that has speciﬁc effects on transnational cooperation on spatial
planning. It helps to explain differences in the approach of different planning traditions to
mapping the state and perspectives of territories and to visualising spatial policy options, which
frequently present considerable problems in cooperative planning processes (Dühr 2007).
‘Planners’ from the EU member states who are involved in transnational spatial planning
processes come from different disciplines (such as architecture, law, economy and urban design)
and represent different planning traditions that may use and interpret policy maps differently.
Planners engaged in European spatial planning or territorial cooperation activities are
required to see their territories in a wider geographical context, in the process of what Williams
(1996: 97) has called ‘spatial positioning’ (see Chapters 2 and 5). Williams (1996: 97) explains
that:
most local planners have a clear sense of the location within national space of the place for
which they are responsible, often without thinking consciously about it. The capacity to
conceptualise or think about one’s location or situation within the spatial structure of
Europe as a whole is a skill which often needs to be developed.
Williams (1996: 98) illustrates the idea of spatial positioning with reference to the differences
in spatial thinking in France and Britain over the Channel Tunnel rail link: ‘On the French side,
its spatial relationship to the TGV system was thought through in a way that led to a national
strategy for transport and infrastructure, while the UK was totally preoccupied with the issue
of the rail link to London and exhibited a total lack of thinking at the national spatial scale.’
Healey (2006a) has shown how such differences in spatial consciousness have evolved over
time. Planning cultures in Europe were dominated in the mid-twentieth century by concepts of
urban form and physical structure, though in different ways: in the Netherlands physical
planning concepts were strongly developed and underpinned by geographical and technical

is proving difﬁcult to achieve because it requires another change in planning culture (Nadin
2007). The Belgian regions of Wallonia and Flanders have also been pursuing strategic spatial
planning approaches since the 1990s. Reawakening a spatial consciousness in contexts with a
more project-oriented or sectoral policy-guided approach to planning has, however, not been
easy, and for Flanders at least considerable political efforts were needed to drive this forward
(Albrechts 2001a).
The notion of planning doctrine
The notion of planning doctrine is closely related to the concept of planning culture. Planning
doctrine refers to ‘a body of thought concerning (a) spatial arrangements within an area; (b)
the development of that area; (c) the way both are said to be handled’ (Faludi and van der Valk
1994: 18). It thus refers to a conceptual scheme that gives ‘coherence to planning by means of
conceptualising an area’s shape, development challenges, and ways of handling them’ (Faludi
1999: 333). Planning doctrine is not something that is explicitly set out in a written document
or law, but rather the combination of all norms, rules, concepts, methods and procedures and
ways of ‘understanding things’ that characterise a planning culture. It could be described as the
fundamental underlying substantive principles on which planning policies and practices are built.
The notion of planning doctrine is thus also related to the concept of framing, which is deﬁned
as ‘a way of selecting, organising, interpreting and making sense of a complex reality so as to
provide guideposts for knowing, analysing, persuading and acting’. A frame is ‘a perspective
from which an amorphous, ill-deﬁned problematic situation can be made sense of and acted
upon’ (Rein and Schön 1986: 4).
The concept of doctrine hence refers to durable concepts that provide the frame of reference
for planning processes. This does not mean that ideas embodied in planning doctrine are nonambiguous, however. Rather, because planning doctrine sets the frame for different actors and
interests, the ideas incorporated are often vague and sometimes also contradictory. Spatial
planning doctrine presupposes that there is an agency with responsibility for the plan area. 
It must ‘incorporate a spatial organisation principle expressed by means of an abstract representation of the plan area, together with directions for its development. Successful images rest
on powerful metaphors’ (Faludi 1999: 334). The notion of planning doctrine relates to the view
of planning as communication. It is based on the recognition that planning ideas are the
outcomes of discussions, negotiations and conﬂict resolution.

An example for planning doctrine can be given for the Netherlands. It is expressed in the longstanding spatial concepts of the Randstad (the polycentric ring of towns and cities in the Western
Netherlands) and the ‘Green Heart’ inside. Alongside notions of the role of plans and planning,
Dutch planning doctrine conceptualises the shape of the country and its developmental
challenges, which are for the Netherlands the need to restrict urban sprawl in a densely
populated country by only permitting carefully planned urban extension sites and by protecting
open space.
The concept of planning doctrine has been primarily promoted in the context of spatial
planning in the Netherlands, but it could be transferred to other planning cultures that are built
on strong substantive guiding principles. For example, planning doctrine in Germany could be
summarised as relating to a decentralised settlement pattern and quality of living conditions in
the territory. German planning concepts, such as central places and decentralised concentration,
are expressions of this doctrine. In England, planning doctrine is built on the understanding that
urban development has to be contained and the countryside protected from development (or
even change per se). Planning instruments, such as the ‘green belt’ policy, or the ‘sequential
approach’ that prioritises development in existing urban areas and on brownﬁeld land over
greenﬁeld expansion, are expressions of this doctrine.
Planning professions
Planning culture, doctrines and spatial consciousness are cultivated within professional
communities and infused in new recruits to that profession through the institutions of planning
education. These are the arenas in which the pressures for change are mediated and transformed
into new professional norms and practices. This section brieﬂy reviews the state of planning
professions in the EU.
What makes an occupation a profession is that the latter has some control over an identiﬁable
body of knowledge that is of value to society. Control is exercised in professions such as law
and medicine (and their sub-professions) by: taking a leadership role in the speciﬁcation, consolidation and reproduction of knowledge; limiting entry into the membership of the profession
to those who demonstrate certain qualities; regulating the education and training of members;
and imposing common standards of conduct and ethical practice (Johnson 1972). Professions
are largely autonomous, being responsible for regulating their own activity. A profession’s
responsibility over an area of work may be legitimised by some kind of state recognition so that
it has exclusive rights over its professional title. This arrangement has proved mutually beneﬁcial
to the state – which can rely on certain minimum standards in professional practices (such as
in engineering) – and to the profession, which through this control is able to raise the status
and incomes of its members (Low 1991). The power of professions in Western society has come
under critical scrutiny because in claiming speciﬁc expertise to address societal problems they
have tended to mystify decision-making in which citizens and their representatives should play
a part (Low 1991).
What is recognised as ‘a profession’ and ‘the profession of spatial planning’ varies in each
country and is related to the social model and planning culture. The ‘planning profession’ will
be aligned with other disciplines (architecture, public policy, geography and economics); it may
be weak or strong and more or less autonomous. For example, in Mediterranean countries
planning is more associated with architecture, in Germany there are regional planners with an
economic and public policy orientation as well as architect-planners who specialise in urban
design and physical planning backgrounds. Differences reﬂect the style (or model) and culture
of planning. For example, in the UK, the discretionary nature of planning regulation in which
planners have considerable autonomy in their decision-making is closely interlinked with strong

recognised by the state and where use of the title of ‘spatial or urban planner’ is regulated. The
UK has a relatively strong professional organisation, the Royal Town Planning Institute (RTPI),
which regulates the education of planners and their ongoing training (in collaboration with the
universities), controls entry to its membership by education and qualiﬁcation, and imposes a
code of ethics that is strictly observed. There are about 20,000 subscribing members who are
recognised by the state to call themselves ‘chartered town planners’. Other countries also have
their own associations with often large memberships, such as the Towarzystwo Urbanistów
Polskich (TUP) (Society of Polish Town Planners), the Société Française des Urbanistes (SFU)
(French Society of Urbanists), and the Associação de Urbanistas Portugueses (AUP) (Portuguese
Association of Town Planners).
Twenty-four associations representing professional spatial planners in Europe cooperate 
at the European level through the European Council of Spatial Planners (ECTP) (the name was
originally ‘town planners’). The ECTP was established in 1985 and since then has sought to raise
the proﬁle of the planning profession across Europe. It represents the interests of professional
planners in responses to the major actions on European spatial planning (see, for example,
Vogelij 2008). In 2003 the ECTP adopted a revised version of its Charter of Athens in which
it sets out a vision for European cities in the twenty-ﬁrst century and explains how spatial
planners can address this vision – as scientist, designer, political adviser and urban manager.
The paper neatly embraces the variation in planning models and cultures in Europe.
Planning education
Changes to planning systems in response to international inﬂuences, and increasing territorial
cooperation in Europe, are also interrelated with changes in planning education. There are
obvious links between the planning profession and planning education, and within Europe both
are subject to European inﬂuences that mutually inﬂuence their development.
International exchanges in planning education predate most of the exchanges among practitioners, at least in volume. There is a long history of international exchange or ‘mobility’ of
university students and staff in Europe and it has increased dramatically since the EU’s Erasmus
programme for mobility in higher education began in 1987. By 2009 Erasmus had enabled more
than two million students to experience a period of study in another country. Evaluations
conﬁrm many beneﬁts, especially for students’ attractiveness to employers and the overall quality
of higher education in Europe, but the popularity of Erasmus in many institutions is not high,
partly because of the costs of administration (CHEPS 2008). However, planning schools have
taken many opportunities through Erasmus, and where there has been a long period of
collaboration it has led to a more common understanding of the nature of education in spatial
planning and curriculum changes (Askew and Winter 2004). There is also extensive cooperation

International educational mobility and research collaboration have contributed to a thriving
academic community in Europe concerned with research and education in spatial planning.
There are concerns: that collaboration is not equally spread across the EU (and north-west
Europe dominates the research collaborations); that exchange may be unwittingly contributing
to a loss of a distinctive identity of planning education in some places; and that there are
continuing weak links with professional practice (Fubini 2004). Nevertheless, spatial planning
is well established as both a teaching and a research discipline in its own right at the European
level. One basic but helpful indicator of academic activity is the number of journals in the ﬁeld.
A list of some European-based planning journals is given in Box 24.1. Most of these publish
articles only after a blind refereeing process. There are many other journals published in different
European languages that are not listed here and that are aimed at stimulating exchange among
the practitioner audience. The list also demonstrates how the academic community and the
publications industry in Europe are dominated by the English language. The dangers of the
reliance on English are discussed in Chapter 3.
Cooperation among higher education institutions with an interest in spatial planning has been
facilitated since 1987 by the Association of European Schools of Planning (AESOP). AESOP is
a representative body with more than 150 members (Geppert 2008). Its mission is to take a lead
on debate with key stakeholders on education, research and professional qualiﬁcations. The
steady increase in membership and activities demonstrates the success and popularity of AESOP.
It holds a major annual congress that is periodically shared with the American Collegiate Schools
of Planning (ACSP) and plays a central role in the Global Planning Education Association
Network (GPEAN). There are numerous thematic groups coordinating research and publications, events, and regular meetings of Heads of Schools of Planning, which have been concerned
with the nature of the common curriculum and professional standing as explained below.
AESOP supports a ‘Young Academics’ network and hosts an annual and very popular Ph.D.
workshop. Teachers and researchers in spatial planning are also active in other related networks,
for example the European Urban Research Association (EURA).
As part of its task of leading the debate in education on spatial planning AESOP prepared a
recommended list of core requirements for planning education, which is shown in Box 24.2. This
is no more than an advisory document, but it can play an important role in identifying the core
of ‘planning knowledge’, ensuring more coherence among institutions and thereby raising the
status of the profession. Member schools are encouraged also to ensure a specialism component
in planning education that will be informed by the planning culture and disciplinary interests
of domestic institutions.
Planners in Europe are predominantly educated in the context of their planning traditions
and in response to the needs of the planning discipline. However, during the 2000s planning
schools throughout Europe have had to address the central component of the changing landscape
for planning education: the ‘Bologna Process’. The Bologna Declaration is not an EU initiative
but the result of an intergovernmental process initiated by European countries. The Bologna
Declaration was signed in 1999 and was followed by the Prague Declaration in 2001. They took
forward the objective of creating a ‘European Area of Higher Education’ by 2010 to strengthen
the international competitiveness of European higher education (Davoudi and Ellison 2006).

In recognition of the European dimension of spatial planning, and the need for the new
generation of spatial planners to possess cross-cultural communication skills that are a
prerequisite for territorial cooperation, many planning schools have over recent years launched
International Masters programmes on different aspects of European or international spatial
planning. Overall, there are apparently obvious beneﬁts from the harmonisation of university
degrees, also for planning education. However, Kunzmann (2004: 2) warns that underlying the
laudable aims of the Bologna process is the model of:
the globally successful Anglo-American system of higher education and the related forward
and backward linkages with a diversity of public and private institutions of higher
education, such as professional bodies, publishers or other knowledge industries, all
embedded in a neo-liberal market environment.
Although the Bologna Declaration professes to respect diversity, it has raised concerns that in
reality it is imposing the US-UK system on the rest of the EU (and inevitably Europe).
Kunzmann (2004) points out the consequences of Bologna. The problem is not primarily
harmonisation through a uniform model. Though it may mean the loss of academic traditions
and academic rituals for many countries, harmonisation may assist in the wider European
project. The issue is rather the market orientation and the likely consequences in terms of competition and reform of educational method. Kunzmann foresees a loss of independence of
planning schools, an increasing divide between theory and practice and a loss of more interactive
teaching methods. The operation of the UK system, on which the Bologna system has been
modelled to a considerable extent, can illustrate the possible fate of planning schools across
Europe. Planning schools are now almost wholly part of modular structures that offer choice to
prospective students; undergraduate education is declining in favour of short postgraduate
conversion courses for students from other disciplines; and teaching tends to rely more on the
large group lecture format in order to make it economically viable. The race to implement the
Bologna format may also be damaging mobility as the more intense style of education leaves less
opportunity for exchange – something that stands in stark contrast to the professed objectives
of the Bologna agreement (Heinze and Knill 2008).
In this context of rapid change in the education sector, AESOP completed a study in 2004 that
examined changes in planning research, doctoral courses and the profession that involved more
than 60 planning schools in 20 countries (Fubini 2004). The report provides an insight into the
varying forms of planning education in many European countries, including the relationship with
the profession and practice. The accent was on identifying trends in the changing context of
European integration when ‘the planning community is supposed to redesign its role to ﬁt recent
territorial conditions and demands as well as being able to develop projects aimed at renewing
planning education’ (Fubini 2004: 15). The report notes how EU actions on spatial planning have
inﬂuenced education, in some cases reafﬁrming practices that are closely aligned with principles
proposed in European statements on spatial planning, and in others, providing a model for the
introduction of new approaches, tools and techniques. More general societal trends have also been
inﬂuential, in particular increasing participation of citizens in government decision-making and
the increasing role for private sector actors in the planning process.

A meeting of Heads of Schools of Planning in Europe in Leuven in 2007 considered the value
of creating a ‘common platform’ for recognition of the planning profession at the EU level. This
common platform refers to a set of minimum standards and additional competencies required
for practice in particular countries. When adopted, the common platform would become a
mandatory requirement in all member states (Geppert and Verhage 2008). The EU does not
deﬁne any professional standards but rather seeks to reduce barriers to mobility. The ECTP has
shown that there are barriers in Europe because, for example, some countries require that anyone
wanting to practise as a spatial planner must also be registered as an architect. The meeting
conﬁrmed that the AESOP Executive Committee would cautiously proceed with the research
needed for the process of moving towards a common professional platform, and that it would
do this in collaboration with the ECTP.
Conclusion
The depth of professionalisation and the orientation of education in spatial planning are
intertwined with planning culture. They each demonstrate the great diversity in ideas about
spatial planning across Europe. The practice and study of spatial planning develops within
particular legal, administrative and institutional settings. It is established in the history and
geography of particular places and shaped by the local socio-economic conditions. It is ﬁrmly
rooted in fundamental values and deep-seated beliefs about society, and especially the
organisation of space. The differences are not likely to erode in the short-term, and nor should
they. Diversity is a strength. The notion of spatial planning must be pliable so that it can be
shaped to match local needs and expectations. Yet, there are also common threads in planning
cultures across Europe that provide a basis for strengthening planning practice and education
so that planners in future are better equipped for the task of cooperation with colleagues from
other countries. This is particularly important in those parts of Europe where the status of
planning is weak and can beneﬁt from the support of a European-wide college of planners. The
challenge is to be able to deﬁne and strengthen the coherence of the common elements of a
European planning culture, while recognising and facilitating diverse locally embedded practices.

of papers edited by Knieling and Othengrafen (2009a). Another edited volume is that by Sanyal

recent position regarding common professional standards is given in the AESOP publication
