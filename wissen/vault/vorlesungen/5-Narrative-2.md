# 5 Narrative 2

## S. 1

The European spatial 
planning agenda
The ﬁrst 12 chapters of this book have set the context for European spatial planning and
territorial cooperation by introducing some key concepts and terms, explaining the patterns 
and trends of spatial development at the European scale, and laying out the complex web of
relevant organisations and processes through which policy and decisions are made. Part IV now
concentrates on the core issue of European spatial planning and what it means in theory and
practice.
This part asks the question: what is European spatial planning? It attempts to answer that
deceptively simple question in three ways. First, in Chapter 13 the notion of European spatial
planning is compared to the predominant styles or models of spatial planning that are found in
the member countries of the EU. These are ideas about planning that readers may be more
familiar with. Does European spatial planning correspond to domestic ideas about planning?
Does the European dimension have any distinctive characteristics that set it apart from planning
in the member states?
Second, Chapter 14 provides a historical review of the emergence of European spatial
planning, explaining the incremental and sometimes accidental way that events come together
in important deﬁning moments. European spatial planning is not a static concept; it is alive and
constantly evolving in the multitude of ongoing collaborations and debates. The mix of organisations, statements, attitudes, beliefs and more that make up the notion of European spatial
planning in 2010 cannot be appreciated without some understanding of these historical origins
and how these have interconnected with the development of the European Union itself.
Third, Chapters 15 and 16 provide a catalogue and critical review of the tools of European
spatial planning. European spatial planning is perhaps best deﬁned by what we claim as its
products or outputs. What things (be they activities, documents, programmes or plans) come
under the banner of European spatial planning? Chapter 15 describes the major supranational
and transnational statements that provide the cornerstones of a common European perspective
on spatial planning. They come in the form of spatial perspectives, strategies and visions.
Chapter 16 then considers the tools of transnational cooperation in its many programmes and
activities. Transnational cooperation is a bridge between the grand and rather general ideas at
the European level with the practical concerns and problems faced by planning practitioners in
regional and local planning authorities. Again, this is evidence of the real practice where a
deﬁnition of European spatial planning can be found.
Although the preceding discussion in the ﬁrst three parts of this book may give an overall
impression of the inexactness of the notion of European spatial planning, Part IV should help
to paint a clearer picture and show that it has many tangible outputs.

Introduction
The question mark in the title of this chapter indicates some uncertainty. We use the term
‘model’ here in the sense of providing a simpliﬁed but systematic representation of the main
properties of the (in reality) very complex mix of phenomena and ideas that make up European
spatial planning. But is it possible to describe a model of European spatial planning? And if so,
what is the value of such a model? Is it required? We do not want to give the impression that
there is a universally agreed model of European spatial planning. It may be more appropriate
to talk about the ‘notion’ of spatial planning – the more general and contradictory mix of
opinions and understandings. However, there is in our view sufﬁcient agreement about the
distinctive characteristics of European spatial planning to give an account of its main characteristics. Thus we use the word ‘model’.
In this chapter, the ‘model’ of European spatial planning is examined by contrasting it to
models of planning that predominate at the national and regional levels. So the chapter begins
by explaining how styles or models of spatial planning vary across Europe. The chapter moves
on to consider in more detail some of those distinctive characteristics and raise questions about
the operation of spatial planning that are particularly salient in relation to the European
dimension. It concludes by summarising the main characteristics of ‘European spatial planning’
according to the same criteria that are used to consider spatial planning at the national level.
Models of spatial planning in Europe
The subtle but critical variation in wording here needs to be emphasised. This section is about
models of spatial planning in Europe, that is, the varying forms of planning thought and practice
at the national and regional levels. Take care to distinguish this idea from the European model
of spatial planning, which is considered later. This book is not primarily concerned with ‘spatial
planning in Europe’ (which would involve explanations of how planning is carried out in
different European countries) but about European spatial planning – the cooperative activity on
spatial planning among member states and regions at the supranational and transboundary
levels. However, at this point it is useful to brieﬂy review variation in the theory and practice
of spatial planning in the countries of Europe.

## S. 3

Cross-national comparative studies have created classiﬁcations or ideal-type taxonomies 
that generalise about the differences and similarities in the way planning is thought about and
practised. Such general typologies are useful for identifying trends, particularly evidence of
convergence and the diffusion of ideas (Hantrais 2008). Comparative typologies (sometimes
called families) have been popular in ﬁelds such as comparative law (Zweigert and Kötz 1998),
social policy (Wilensky et al.1985) and welfare provision (Esping Andersen 1990; see Chapter
24). In Europe this type of analysis is encouraged by European integration. EU law and policy
has to be implemented in national contexts so knowledge of differences in policy regimes in
member states is particularly useful.
There has long been an interest in cross-national comparison of aspects of spatial planning (see, for example, Bunbury 1938). European integration and transnational cooperation
have given an impetus for new studies to assist directly with the formulation and application
of supranational planning measures and transnational cooperation (Williams 1996), especially
to support the European Spatial Development Perspective (ESDP) and INTERREG projects. For
example, the COMMIN INTERREG project was designed speciﬁcally to support transnational
and cross-border working in the Baltic Sea region by providing comparative information on
spatial planning systems (COMMIN 2009). Cross-national comparisons have been also
employed to help in the ‘positioning’ of planning practice in one place compared to others, as
a form of benchmarking.
Undertaking cross-national comparison and generalising about similarity and difference is a
considerable methodological challenge and ﬁndings are often limited and partial (Nadin 2008;
Williams 1984). There are a number of reasons for this that should be spelled out to caution
against the reaching of ill-considered judgments. Most studies are small-scale and concentrate
on the analysis of two or three systems (see, for example, Booth 1993; Booth et al. 2007; Dühr
2007; Masser 1984; Spaans and de Wolff 2005; and Thomas et al. 1983, among others). While
such studies may inform the creation of more general taxonomies, the ﬁndings will often be
limited to the countries concerned. Second, most comparative studies have been undertaken 
in, or led from, north-west Europe (for example, Davies et al. 1989) and may have helped to
perpetuate the dominant stream of northern European thinking in European spatial planning
(Janin Rivolin and Faludi 2005). Third, comparative studies have for many years focused on
Western planning systems, although over recent years since the fall of the Iron Curtain there
has been increasing attention given to approaches to planning in the new member states in
central and eastern Europe (see, for example, Adams et al. 2006; Altrock et al. 2006a). Fourth,
many studies are mostly descriptive. While they provide very helpful information about aspects
of spatial planning they give little attention to comparing notions of planning or explaining the
differences (Dal Cin and Lyddon 1996; GMA Planning 1993; Ryser and Franchini 2008;
Schmidt-Eichstaedt 1996). Other studies that have engaged in comparison and explanation tend
to be based on limited empirical evidence (Healey and Williams 1993) or one aspect of planning
(Healey et al. 1997; Seaton et al. 2000; Spaans et al. 1996; Thomas et al. 1983) One useful and
well-used classiﬁcation of planning systems by Newman and Thornley (1996) is based only on
the criterion of the form of the legal system, which is brought forward from Zweigert and Kötz
(1998, originally published 1987).
The last point on criteria is critical. Comparing spatial planning from place to place entails
choosing meaningful characteristics to compare. The choice should be based on some general
proposition about the important characteristics of systems and the particular questions at hand.
In practice much emphasis is placed on formal aspects of systems such as the division of
competences among tiers of government and the legal status of plans. More meaningful criteria,
such as the ‘planning doctrine’ – the dominant ‘spatial organising principles’ that guide intervention
(Faludi and van der Valk 1994) – are more difﬁcult to research and much less often considered.

## S. 4

Nadin and Stead (2008) summarise the major cross-national comparative studies that have
produced typologies of spatial planning. The EU Compendium of Spatial Planning Systems and
Policies (CEC 1997a; Nadin et al. 1995) is by far the largest cross-national comparative
research project on spatial planning in Europe, but shares some of the limitations of other studies
– it is mostly descriptive and it only covers the EU-15, but it does provide an elementary typology
of spatial planning that is based on a wider set of criteria. The Compendium proposed four ideal
types of spatial planning in Europe. An ideal type takes certain meaningful characteristics or
attributes of an object and accentuates them to create a clearly deﬁned type that does not exist
in reality. Ideal types provide a benchmark or measuring rod against which to compare reality,
and as such they prove useful for a discussion about the characteristics of European spatial
planning.
The Compendium used the term ‘tradition’ for its ideal types (CEC 1997a: 33) to emphasise
the way that forms of planning are deeply embedded in the complex historical conditions of
particular places. The form of system of law and six other variables were used to distinguish
types:
•
the scope of the system in terms of policy topics covered over which it has some competence
or seeks inﬂuence (such as social, economic and environmental sectors);
•
the extent of national and regional strategic planning as well as the ubiquitous local
regulation of development;
•
the locus of power or relative competences of levels of government (which is a question
about subsidiarity, explained in Chapter 2);
•
the relative roles of the public and private sectors and the extent to which planning can be
described as plan-led or market-led;
•
the maturity or completeness of the system including the existence in practice of plans and
the level of public acceptance of planning intervention; and
•
the apparent distance between expressed goals for spatial development and outcomes.
On the basis of these criteria four traditions (or styles or models) of spatial planning were
proposed and are summarised in Figure 13.1. All national spatial planning approaches will
exhibit a combination of traditions or models. Some places might exhibit a strong tendency to
one tradition and be much weaker in others, whereas some places may exhibit a more complex
combination. Figure 13.1 is used here to show that the four ideal type models overlap in some
way. They each have dominant characteristics that set them apart, but they also share a common
concern with organising space or territory. The ESPON Project 2.3.2: Governance of Territorial
and Urban Policies from EU to Local Level took forward the Compendium ideal types and
applied them to the new member states and neighbouring countries, 29 countries in all. It also
seeks to ‘give a modest update on the movements that took place’ since the Compendium project
was published (ESPON 2006d: 112).
Box 13.1 gives more explanation of the four models and also gives a very tentative list of
countries under each one. This is not to suggest that national planning systems can be ‘pigeon
holed’ into classes but rather gives an indication of the predominant model or style of planning
in that country. For example, the French system has a very strong vein of regional economic
planning but also reﬂects the urbanism model. There is a particular difﬁculty with the former
communist countries that have undergone, and in some places continue to undergo, radical
change. The ESPON 2.3.2 Governance project (2006d) does cover the new EU member states,
but readers should be aware that this study sometimes gives a quite different understanding 
to the four ideal types than was originally intended. These problems, which are recognised in
many studies, illustrate the vagaries and inconsistencies of cross-national comparative planning.

The comprehensive integrated model is about spatial coordination. It has wide scope and its main
task is to provide horizontal (across sectors), vertical (between levels) and geographical (across

The land use management model is about the regulation of changes of use of land and property.
The operation of planning is geared to managing physical development, mostly at the local level,

wider societal goals such as housing provision and protecting environmental heritage. It makes
use of policy statements and decision rules and there are extensive mechanisms for citizen

development and land value capture.

disparities in the interests of ‘spatial equity’ especially at the regional scale. It has a wide scope
in terms of its desired inﬂuence on other policy sectors. There is a strong public-sector
orientation, and there are mature planning institutions. The national and regional levels of
planning are important, though with vertical integration to local actors. It makes use of strategies, programmes and economic support instruments alongside plans and direct investment
in infrastructure projects.
European countries that show features of this ideal type include: FR, DE, PT, HU, LV,

of methods of intervention through zoning instruments, design codes, building control and
development projects. There is little interest in strategy or planning at the national and regional

physical environmental change and wider societal objectives. The model has a strong bias to
design and professional solutions with only limited mechanisms for citizen involvement and
managing conﬂict.

## S. 7

European spatial planning approach has been strongly inﬂuenced by certain ideas from the
member states. In short, these come predominantly from the regional economic and comprehensive integrated models, as explained below. First, consideration is given to the land use
management and urbanism approaches.
Perhaps it is self-evident that the ‘land use management’ and ‘urbanism’ approaches are less
relevant for the European Union if the principle of subsidiarity is applied, given their concern
with predominantly local and regional planning issues (see Chapter 2). However, this has not
been the view in some member states where there are concerns about how European laws and
other actions impinge on the detailed operation of land use regulation. For example, DG
Environment has been keen to ensure that member states establish and effectively implement
land use regulation in so far as it serves the objective of sustainable development (see Chapter
21). It may be that there is insufﬁcient attention to detailed questions of regulation and
enforcement at the European level given the land use impacts of climate change, urban sprawl
and other relevant problems. However, this is not the realm of the model of European spatial
planning that is discussed here. The detailed operation of land use regulation or urban design
has never been central to the European dimension of spatial planning. Nevertheless, the fear of
such potential ‘interference’ has been one of the reasons for member states to consistently oppose
the granting of an explicit competence to the EU for spatial planning, while continuing to
cooperate on planning initiatives, as explained in Chapter 2.
The origins and rationale for spatial planning at the European level are quite different to those
that led to the strengthening of public regulation and planning of land use in many countries
in the early twentieth century. At national level spatial planning was established as a response
to the damaging effects of rapid and uncoordinated urban development on the social and
economic state of cities and the wellbeing of citizens. (These problems continue for much of
Europe.) The result was the creation of varying systems of regulation through land use zoning
and other means of intervening in state and market development processes. Local regulation was
later supplemented with regional and strategic planning (Hall 2002). The underlying goals are
in part social and economic, but the means and the day-to-day objectives were, and still are,
about controlling physical development. The Council of Europe’s European Regional/Spatial
Planning Charter (Torremolinos Charter) (CEMAT 1983) follows this line:
Regional/spatial planning gives geographical expression to the economic, social, cultural and
ecological policies of society. It is . . . an interdisciplinary and comprehensive approach
directed towards a balanced regional development and the physical organisation of space
according to an overall strategy.
(CEMAT 1983: Section 5, emphasis added)
The findings from the EU Compendium project suggest that the control of physical
development at the local level still lies at the core of the meaning of spatial planning for most
countries, with very few exceptions. In all EU countries there is a sector of government activity
that is concerned with regulating physical land use change. This sector is represented by a
government ministry or department that to a greater or lesser extent also seeks to coordinate
the land use impacts of other sectors of government, such as transport, economic development,
agriculture, environment, health and others. Planning is just one sector of government activity
that rarely has any special inﬂuence over the activities of other departments other than the
competence to regulate physical development. It is often relatively weak (CEC 1997a).
Professional planners in member states have compared many aspects of domestic spatial
planning and made joint statements about their relevance and value so as to raise its status 
and to promote better land use regulation more widely in Europe. This is not ‘European

In the European model suggested here, spatial planning is not positioned as a sector of
government regulating physical development but as a cross-governmental activity intended to
guide and integrate the spatial development impacts of all sectoral policies. It is not about
passively regulating the physical development activities of sectors but about proactively
anticipating and integrating the spatial impacts of sectoral policies. It identiﬁes and seeks to
address the contradictions and opportunities for synergy and complementarity of spatial policy.
It is a method of securing ‘convergence and co-ordination between various sectoral policies’
through a territorial development strategy (CEC 1998a: 7) (see also Bastrup-Birk and Doucet
1997). The object of planning is not land use but spatial organisation, which is concerned with
spatial development in a broader sense including the quality of physical, social and economic
changes and their relationships. It can operate at different scales though it is particularly closely
related to the EU’s Regional or Cohesion Policy. One of the sectors subject to spatial
coordination would be land-use planning.
The distinction made here is by no means deﬁnitive or unqualiﬁed, but rather a starting point
that reﬂects the general tenor of the literature on the nature of European spatial planning. 
For example, both Albrechts (1997) and Faludi (2002a) make the distinction between conventional (or traditional) regulatory forms of planning, which tend to dominate at national levels, 
and the European model of spatial planning. Faludi (2002a: 7) explains how in the 1990s the
European Commission favoured the terms ‘spatial development policy’ because of the ‘restrictive
connotations of spatial planning as a concept’. Thus, spatial development ‘signals a move away
from regulation toward capital programs and the like . . . [and] a willingness to engage in publicprivate partnership to achieve public goals’ (Faludi 2002a: 8). European spatial planning (or
spatial development policy) will not (and cannot) replace the regulation of land use and design
of place, which are necessary for the implementation of cross-sectoral spatial planning. However,
member states have shared in the resurgence of interest in strategic spatial planning (Healey 
et al. 1997), informed in part by their joint activity at the EU level.
The contrast being made may seem rather ﬁne. The models are simpliﬁcations of the complex
mixture of approaches in reality. Also, this is a moving target; practices in member states are
changing. Kunzmann (2006b: 43) argues that ‘in essence, there is not much difference’, but this
underplays the variety of approaches to planning in the member states. Also, there are distinct
emphases about the scope and role of European spatial planning at the core of the principal
supranational and transnational statements such as the ESDP, the Territorial Agenda and the
CEMAT Guiding Principles. These and other statements offer guidance for the way planning is
done at the national level, but they also promote a particular model or style of spatial planning.
European spatial planning has emerged as response to the contradictory impacts of spatial
impacts of EU sectoral policies, which require coordination in all types of territories, and 
as an expression of the political objective of achieving more balanced development across the
Community in order to address disparities. The ‘model’ of European spatial planning as it 
has been pursued to date is thus centrally concerned with conﬂicting spatial policies. Williams
(1996: 7) explains spatial policy as ‘any Community policy that is spatially speciﬁc in practice,
whether or not it is deliberately designed to be’. The impact of spatial policies demands
cooperation and coordination of policy horizontally (between sectors), vertically (between levels
of government) and geographically (across borders). As the ESDP states, ‘cooperation is the key
to an integrated spatial development policy and represents added value over sectoral policies
acting in isolation’ (CSD 1999: 35).

The French and Dutch contributions
The regional economic and integrated comprehensive models of planning have been most
important in the European debate and parallel respective contributions by the French and the
Dutch. The French system of aménagement du territoire is by far the strongest example of
regional economic planning model. It is a proactive approach concerned with ‘interventions of
the national administration to promote development in less favored regions’ (Faludi 2002a: 6).
The term ‘regional planning’ as used in the Torremolinos Charter (CEMAT 1983) has been
interpreted as referring to this model of planning, that is, in the sense of reducing regional
disparities in European regions so as to achieve cohesion of the territory as a whole (Faludi
2002a). Care must be taken not to confuse this with the regional planning that is undertaken
by the regions and seeks to organise land use within sub-national territories. An alternative term
for interregional planning (as described by the Torremolinos Charter) is ‘national-regional
planning’ (Hall 2002). Though some countries now reject regional policy for all but the most
critical situations, it remains at the core of the French model of spatial planning, supplemented
by a regulatory code-based urbanism model. It is associated with the use of indicative plans and
spatial scenarios and is concerned with the whole of the national territory. It is bound up with
the concept of spatial equity in the sense of an ‘obligation on the part of the central state to
guarantee equal living conditions to its citizens’, which ‘echoes the same concern’ as European
Cohesion Policy (Faludi 2009a: 11). Signiﬁcantly, it was the model of planning on the table at
the ﬁrst meeting concerning the ESDP (Faludi 2004a). The French have continued to inject this
model into the European spatial planning debate subsequently, particularly in relation to the
concept of territorial cohesion, as explained below.
Spatial planning in the Netherlands has had a strong regulatory and generally restrictive style,
but it is also an exemplar of the comprehensive integrated model. It has, since the 1970s,
followed a ‘sector facet model’ where ‘planning was considered a multidimensional policy . . .
and therefore had been given a sort of coordinating role’ (Waterhout 2008: 163). It has also
exhibited strong vertical relations between national and local levels (as the centre has sought to
determine outcomes) and successive national spatial planning documents have sought to
coordinate the spatial dimension of other sectors (Nadin and Stead 2008). In relative terms the
Netherlands exhibits a model of integrated spatial planning. As a small and prosperous country,
it has also understood the importance of cross-border transnational impacts, and taken a leading
role in the emergence of European spatial planning ‘arguably to try to promote or export
concepts and processes from the Dutch spatial planning system’ (Nadin and Stead 2008: 43).
Although the Netherlands is much less likely to support a strong regional policy planning
approach than France, the two models are mutually reinforcing in their effect on the European
model. Waterhout (2008: 45) argues that the European spatial planning approach ‘comes 
very close to the comprehensive integrated planning approach that is common practice in, for
example, Germany and the Netherlands, but which is in turn increasingly inspired by French
ideas too’. In different ways they focus attention on spatial planning as spatial policy coordination. Thus, ‘spatial planning adds an extra dimension to spatial policies: one of deliberateness

improve the quality of locations’ (Faludi 2002a: 7). Therefore he sees the regional economic
approach as more amenable to the EU situation where legal competences are missing because
they are not needed to improve spatial policy coordination. However, this may underestimate
the importance of strategic ‘spatial organising’ within an integrated model and the identiﬁcation
of the spatial impacts of sectoral policies. The view taken here is that both models offer ways
to develop spatial strategies that can inﬂuence sectoral policy.
Transnational strategies, collaboration and learning
The nature of a transnational planning instrument or ‘plan’ will differ from the various forms
of strategies and plans that are employed at the national and regional levels. The scale, scope
and range of interests involved at the transnational level are obviously very different to the norm
for plans and strategies within member states. Also, there is no law or guidance that speciﬁes
the characteristics of transnational strategies. Research on the characteristics of transnational
planning instruments is slowly emerging, for example, on spatial visioning (see Nadin 2002;
Zonneveld 2005b, and Chapter 16) and transnational cooperation (see, for example, De Vries
2002). This work builds on well-established concepts to provide some theoretical pointers on
planning instruments at the transnational level. The distinction between ‘strategic plans’ and
‘project plans’ is a useful starting point for explaining how the nature of European and
transnational planning instruments will differ from national practice. Faludi (2000b) (drawing
on Mastop 2000 and Faludi and Mastop 1982) approaches this from the perspective of planning as a decision-making procedure. He explains that a typical project plan is a blueprint type
plan, and its success is evaluated on the extent to which outcomes conform to the plan. This
approach is to be found in the traditional continental European approach to zoning. Faludi
(2000b: 302) quotes the Centre for Environmental Studies (1970: 16) to explain how a strategic
plan differs: it is ‘not so much concerned with producing a plan as with gaining a better understanding of the problems with which we are faced now and in the future, in order that we can
make better decisions now’. European spatial planning is concerned with such strategic plans.
The strategic planning process in this sense is concerned with the learning experience that the
strategy offers and the interaction between planners and other actors involved in spatial development. Faludi (2001a: 664) has suggested that ‘applying any strategic planning document aims
at shaping the minds of actors involved in spatial development’. A strategy is concerned with
‘framing’ the understanding of actors (who may have much stronger powers of implementation)
to particular understandings of problem and possible solutions, not prescribing solutions. It will
draw different sectoral actors together around a shared understanding.
From the perspective of strategies as framing mechanisms, Faludi (2001a) has therefore
argued that it is more appropriate to speak of their ‘application’ than their ‘implementation’.
Implementation assumes a direct and measurable impact of the plan, whereas application refers
to a reinterpretation of policy options in an interactive process. Similarly, in evaluating European
spatial planning we should talk of performance rather than conformance (Faludi 2000b; Janin
Rivolin 2008). European and transnational strategies are not plans whose performance can be

compulsory elements on other actors who have discretion to decide where and when they act
or not. For these reasons attention in strategic planning generally has turned more to its potential
role in inﬂuencing the reference framework of crucial actors.
An example of how actors’ understanding of spatial development or their worldviews can
be inﬂuenced that is particularly important at the transnational scale is ‘spatial positioning’
(Williams 1996: 97). Chapter 5 explains how administrative boundaries deﬁne the geographical
horizon of planning practice (or spatial positioning) and how European and transnational spatial
planning has brought other transboundary spatial relationships into the picture, though generally
only to a limited degree. In this framing and reframing task, spatial concepts and images are
especially useful and powerful tools (Dühr 2007; Faludi 1996). Spatial concepts and
visualisations are part of what Needham et al. (1997) have described as the ‘vision model’ of
strategy-making. This is the primary approach to strategy-making in transnational spatial
planning, where plan-makers try to make a plan ‘so powerful and attractive that all those whose
support is needed willingly range up behind it. The plan challenges and motivates, it convinces’
(Needham et al. 1997: 876) through promotion of a strong vision of how the territory should
develop. In the alternative ‘consultation model’ the plan-makers enter into discussions with those
who can directly shape the physical environment (the target groups) at an early stage of the plan
preparation, with the aim of generating support and commitment for the new plan. With the
multitude of actors and interests involved, the consultation model has limited applicability at
the transnational scale. Instead the ‘vision model’ is a much better approximation, and explains
too why ‘packaging’ and presentation, and spatial concepts and visualisations are so important,
albeit politically difﬁcult to negotiate (Dühr 2007; see Chapter 15).

argumentation’ (p. 261). Strategies are ‘deliberate efforts in collaborative social mobilisation,
the objective of which is to transform policy discourse’. Successful strategies are about changing
the way participants see the world by establishing a new discourse. They innovate by ‘re-framing
issues and enabling new types of actions to be thought about’ (p. 267). The communicative role

Territorial cohesion
From the 2000s the problem of deﬁning a form of European spatial planning has shifted to the
objective of territorial cohesion. Article 2.3 of the Treaty of Lisbon includes the statement that
‘it [the Union] shall promote economic, social and territorial cohesion, and solidarity among
Member States’ (Council 2007c).
There is no clear and politically agreed deﬁnition of the objective of territorial cohesion (see
Davoudi 2005a; Faludi 2005a; Molle 2007), but there is an acknowledgement in the academic
and policy debate that territorial cohesion is a multi-dimensional concept with at least three main
components:
•
territorial quality: the quality of the living and working environment; comparable living
standards across territories; similar levels of access to services of general interest and to
knowledge;
•
territorial efﬁciency: resource efﬁciency with respect to energy, land and natural resources;
competitiveness of the economic fabric and attractiveness of the territory; internal and
external accessibility; capacity for resistance against de-structuring forces related to the
globalisation process; territorial integration and cooperation between regions; and
•
territorial identity: presence of ‘social capital’ and a sense of belonging that translate into
a competitive advantage of each place and territory (Battis and Kersten 2008).
Throughout the 2000s the discussion in European planning circles was dominated by the elaboration of territorial cohesion. Early discussions had made a strong connection between this
concept and the European model of spatial planning. The Third Cohesion Report (CEC 
2004a) and the special Interim Report on Territorial Cohesion (CEC 2004b) deﬁned territorial
cohesion in terms of ‘the balanced distribution of human activities across the Union [and] . . .
fair access to all citizens and economic operators to services of general economic interest
irrespective of the territory to which they belong’. The Committee of the Regions deﬁnes
territorial cohesion in direct relation to spatial planning as ‘reducing development disparities and
physical or economic dislocation within European regions by means of spatial planning and
other public policies with a territorial impact’ (CoR 2003: 1). The Dutch took a similar view,
arguing that territorial cohesion means ‘incorporating a spatial planning perspective into
decisions that are now made primarily on economic and social grounds’ (VROM 2004: 4). Thus
territorial cohesion draws attention to the ‘de facto’ territorial consequences (the spatial
development impacts) of the interaction of disparate EU policies in particular places. The Fourth
Report on Economic and Social Cohesion (CEC 2007b) refers to territorial cohesion in the same
vein, pointing to continuing disparities and calling for a deeper integration of spatial policy –
the territorial impacts of sectoral policies.
Faludi (2007a: 12) argues that the publication of the Territorial Agenda (EU Ministers 2007a)
and the Green Paper on Territorial Cohesion (CEC 2008c) are indications that the ‘ministers
[of the EU member states] have come to ﬁnally accept that the EU – and thus the Commission
– needs to have a territorial cohesion policy’. The member states have accepted a model of spatial
planning that is different and separate from the spatial planning that is undertaken in their

Conclusion
This chapter concludes by applying the criteria used to distinguish national models or styles of
spatial planning, introduced above, to the European model of spatial planning. This will
provide a systematic review of the nature of the model and allow for comment on some
remaining questions.
First, the scope of the model is very wide. The subject is not land use but spatial development
in its widest sense. It embraces the spatial distribution of physical development and human
activities, but also, because of the underlying principle of cohesion, the changing qualities of
economic and social life and fair access to opportunities in employment, health, recreation and
the like. Second, European spatial planning is not concerned with land use regulation but is
about strategic spatial planning, which provides positive direction to mobilise and integrate
coalitions of interests around a common agenda, and shared deﬁnitions of decision situations
(Albrechts 2001b; Faludi 2009a; Healey 2006b). Note that European policies and actions do
have implications for government regimes regulating physical land use change, but this is not
the concern of the European spatial planning model. The spatial focus is on the region at
different scales. These are primarily regions deﬁned by functional relations that cut across
boundaries, either cross-border or transnational.
Third, the locus of power is in principle with the member states and regions that collaborate
supranationally or transnationally on strategic spatial planning. In practice the initiative rests
with a small group of professional experts and there is little involvement of stakeholders.

it is ‘a very closed shop’ (Albrechts 2001b: 300). The emergence of European spatial planning
