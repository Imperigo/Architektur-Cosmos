## S. 5

La derniere fois que je la vis [the Taj Mahal] fut avec un de nos Marchands 
François qui ne pouvoit aussi bien que moy se lasser de le regarder; je n’osois luy 
en dire mon sentiment apprehendant de m’étre corrompu le goust &, me l’étre fait 
à l’Indienne; mais comme il revenoit fraichement de France, je fus bien aise de luy 
entrendre dire qu’il n’avoit jamais rien veu de si auguste ny de si hardy dans 
l’Europe.
The last time I saw it [the Taj Mahal] was in the company of one of our French 
merchants, who, like myself, did not tire of looking at it. I did not dare to express 
my opinion, fearing that my taste might have become corrupted and Indianized; 
but since he had recently come from France, it was quite a relief for me to hear 
him say that he had seen nothing in Europe so grandiose and daring. 
François Bernier, Voyages, 1699
Lucknow artist, View of Taj Mahal, 1780s, Museum für Asiatische Kunst, SMB, Berlin
REVIEW

## S. 21

“For either the metope between the coupled 
Columns must be wider than it is tall, or the 
moldings of the bases and their capitals must be 
melded together, or one falls victim to some other 
extravagance. We have, as an initial illustration, an 
example of this first irregularity in the Doric of the 
portal of the Church of Saint Gervais made by Mr. 
de Brosse, which despite this defect is one of the 
beautiful works of Architecture that we have.”
François Blondel, Cours d’architecture seconde et
troisième partie. Paris: Nicolas Langlois, 1683,
vol. 2, pt. 3, bk. 1, chap. 12, 238
“The coupling of columns in this order is more 
contrary to regularity than in any other … At the 
Portal of Saint Gervais, the Architect, having not 
made a square metope, did not want to be subject 
to the rule that constitutes the principal beauty of 
this order.”
Augustin-Charles d’Aviler, Cours d’architecture
qui comprend les ordres de Vignole, Paris: Nicolas
Langlois, 1691, vol. 1, 20-1
Salomon de Brosse, façade of Eglise de Saint Gervais Saint-

## S. 22

Claude Perrault, east façade of the Louvre, Paris, 1667
“[One of the major abuses of architecture is] 
the widening of the metopes of the Doric 
Order, so as to give the intercolumniations 
the width they need. If for example, we 
wish to pair two columns, we must 
necessarily space the triglyphs more widely 
and enlarge the metope, since the space 
between the middle of one triglyph and the 
middle of the next is much smaller than that 
between the idle of one column and the 
next, no matter how close the columns may 
be to one another. Now the Ancients would 
have been very hesitant about making such 
an enlargement… The Moderns are more 
audacious… The skillful architect of the 
façade of Saint-Gervais, which is one of the 
most beautiful works to have been built in 
the last one hundred years, was not so 
reluctant to enlarge the metopes of the first 
Doric Order so as to be able to pair his 
columns.”
Claude Perrault, Ordonnance for the Five 
Kinds of Columns After the Method of the 
Ancients, trans. I. K. McEwen, 1993, 170.
Salomon de Brosse, façade of Eglise de Saint Gervais Saint-
Protais, Paris, 1616-21 (18th century drawing of)
