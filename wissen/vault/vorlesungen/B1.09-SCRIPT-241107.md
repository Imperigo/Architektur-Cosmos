Civilized Wilderness and 
City Beautiful: 
The Park Movement of Olmsted 
and The Urban Plans of Burnham
In the late 19th century industrial development was at 
full pace in North America. The economy transitioned 
from manual labor and draft animals toward machinebased manufacturing, exemplified by the mechaniza­
tion of the textile industry, new iron-making techniques, 
and the use of refined coal for energy. This was paired 
with technological development, including the inven­
tion of the elevator, the bridge, the radiator, electric 
light bulb, the telephone, sewerage, central power, 
and new steel construction methods.
At the end of the 19th century the United States also 
saw an extreme rise in population (1790: 4 million/ 1920: 
106 million) and a surge in urbanization (1790: 5 % li­
ved in cities/ 1920: more than 50%). The physical and 
institutional infrastructure of cities was not prepared for 
this unprecedented urban growth. The result were la­
mentable living conditions, which included horrible air 
quality as a result of coal-fired factories, contaminated 
drinking water, as well as pestilence and disease from 
deficient sewerage and solid waste. Cities were often 
characterized by inefficient movement of goods and 
services, tenement housing with little fresh air or light, 
hazardous and unethical working conditions and extre­
me income disparities.
Social movements and reforms emerged from these 
conditions. Already in the last decades of the 19th cen­
tury many cities would develop strategies for the ame­
lioration of health and well-being of their inhabitants 
through improvements in the physical fabric of the city. 
It would take until 1908, however, before the Pittsburgh 
Survey offered the first time a detailed analysis of so­
cial welfare, addressing issues such as public health, 
labor conditions and housing. It was rapidly followed 
by the Plan of Chicago, which provided in 1909 the first 
comprehensive design for a clean, hospitable urban 
antidote to industrial squalor. 
The park movement should be situated within these 
wider attempts for physical and social reform. It was 
a reaction to the prevailing miasma theory of disea­
se and bad hygienic conditions in cities. It promoted 
the introduction of public parks and its ideology was 
predicated on the importance of open, public green 
space to the health and vitality of urban populations. 
This was also the viewpoint of Frederick Law Olmsted, 
who claimed that: ‘‘a park exercises a very different 
and much greater influence upon the progress of a city 
in its general structure than any other ordinary public 
work, and that after the design for a park has been fully 
digested, a long series of years must elapse before 
the ends of the design will begin to be fully realized.’’ 
Olmsted would illustrate his vision in the designs for 
Central Park in New York and the so-called Emerald 
Necklace in Boston.
Part of the progressive social reform in North America 
was also the City Beautiful Movement. It was initiated 
under the leadership of thinkers and architects of the 
upper-middle class –such as Daniel Burnham— con­
cerned with the social deprivation and potential social 
unrest of those living in poor and unhealthy conditions 
in all major cities, namely immigrants, in a period during 
which the total population grew drastically. At the same 
time, the City Beautiful Movement aimed to achieve a 
cultural parity with the cities of Europe. Inspired by the 
European Beaux-Arts, it postulated the necessity of or­
der, dignity and harmony in architecture and proposed 
monumental and classical forms to revitalize the city 
centre and increase property value. Its basic premise 
was that through reform of the urban landscape, toge­
ther with policies in other sectors, it would be possible 
to control the problems caused by the growing urban 
population.

Schoolmaster Hill: Named for Ralph Waldo Emerson 
who lived near this site in the 1820s when he was a

tables, century-old white pines, and offers a spectacular 
view across the park and to the Blue Hills beyond.
The Wilderness: A 65-acre native oak forest with 
meandering paths and huge Roxbury puddingstone 
outcroppings, the Wilderness is a picturesque landscape 
and a good example of urban woodlands.

Peters Hill: The highest point in the Emerald Necklace, 
Peters Hill (240 feet) offers spectacular views of Jamaica 
Plain, Roslindale, and the Boston skyline.
Explorers Garden: The area around Chinese Path 
has long been used by researchers to test the hardiness 
of new plants gathered from around the world by plant 
explorers. Don’t miss rare and unusual plants like the 
dove tree, paperbark maple, or Franklinia. 
Larz Anderson Bonsai Collection: See the oldest 
and smallest trees at the Arboretum. Open mid-April to 
early November.
Hunnewell Building: This building houses 
administrative ofﬁces, a library open to the public, and a 
visitor center with knowledgeable staff to help make the 
most of your visit. Maps, brochures, restrooms, as well as 
a small bookstore. Visitor center hours: Mon–Fri 9am–4pm, 
Sat 10am–4pm, Sun noon–4pm
Jamaica Pond Boathouse/Bandstand: Built in 
1912, these Tudor-style structures add a rustic element 
to the pond. Visitors can rent sailboats or rowboats to 
enjoy unique views of the park or simply drift on the 
water (www.courageoussailing.org). The Bandstand is home 
to numerous recreational, educational, and cultural 
activities. The Boathouse is open April 1 through Veteran’s Day. 
Parkman Memorial: Daniel Chester French, Sculptor

busy park, the promontory’s stunning views across the

Today, a granite outline marks the footprint of the last 
mansion that stood here. 
Ward’s Pond: This secluded pond is a glacial “kettlehole” formed at the end of the last ice age. A serene, 
heavily wooded area, the visitor ﬁnds a quiet wilderness, 
steps from the surrounding city. 
Wildﬂower meadow: Once the site of an indoor ice 
skating rink, the meadow now offers unique habitat for 
butterﬂies, bees, and other pollinators.
Daisy Field: Olmsted originally designed this as a large 
meadow surrounded by woods. Today, playing ﬁelds serve 
community groups for little league, softball, soccer and 
touch football. 
Allerton Overlook: This semi-circular walk descends

islands of Leverett Pond.
