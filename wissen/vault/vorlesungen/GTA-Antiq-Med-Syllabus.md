“And it happened [in Rome] as it happened elsewhere: as the empire declined so 
declined both architecture and architects. When the barbarous nations of Vandals, 
Goths, Longobards, Huns and others came, they brought along their own architects 
and built according to their customs.” 
- 
Antonio di Tuccio Manetti, Life of Brunelleschi, 1480s 
 
In the Renaissance, the practice of architecture transformed into the design-based 
discipline it is now largely assumed to be. This change was understood in opposition to 
“good” ancient and “bad” medieval models, an idea we (perhaps) no longer share. This 
course investigates Antiquity and the Middle Ages as fashioned in the mind of the 
architect and the architectural historian. How does our understanding of these periods 
inform our 
thinking 
about 
the use of history for the architect today? 
 
The course is a combination lecture and research class. Final assessment is either (1) a 
short research assignment or (2) in-person essay, both due during the August exam 
session. 
 
Assignments: 
- Occasional reading questions (3 readings over semester) (30% of the grade) 
- Paper proposal with annotated bibliography (~5 pages), or in-class essay, due during 
winter exam period (70%)

Components of the Course: 
Weekly Lectures: 
Lectures are held at 9:45am Thursdays. Some weeks I will be joined by a guest lecturer 
specialized in the time period under discussion. The lectures will be recorded although 
you are strongly encouraged to attend them in person. 
 
Primary text discussions (see schedule) are occasionally be planned for the second hour. 
Optional practical sessions (using the library, humanities research techniques, writing 
history versus criticism) will also be offered, pending demand. 
 
Reading texts and asking questions: (2-3 thoughtful questions per reading) 
We will occasionally read and discuss a short primary text. You are expected to read each 
week’s text carefully before the weekly lecture. PDFs of the readings are online on the 
course website. 
 
Assignment details: 
Reading questions (2-3 questions) are due before each class meeting. Please 
upload your questions to the Moodle folder and do not include your name (to 
enable anonymized grading). 
 
 A good discussion question is an art: imagine you are responsible for the 
discussion in class, and frame your questions in order to guide the conversation 
into deeper and richer territory. Rather than summarizing the week’s text, you 
should consider the critical framework of the article and elaborate on how it 
reflects the themes of the course. I may use your questions to guide the discussion 
in that week’s class. 
 
In order to receive a top score, questions must demonstrate considered 
engagement with the assigned texts. There is no length requirement, but more 
words are not necessarily better; a single sentence may drill clearly to the heart of 
the matter, or it may take a paragraph to set up the context for your point. Use your 
judgment. Late questions are only accepted due to illness or other 
exceptional circumstances; please inform me in advance. 
 
 
Final Assignment: Proposal with Annotated Bibliography (alt: In-Class Essay)

1. Final Assignment Option 1: Paper Proposal & annotated bibliography (300-
500 word proposal + 2-5 page bibliography, due during exam period) 
 
During the course of the term, please consider which aspects of the course are 
particularly interesting to you and which you would like to explore further. You 
should choose a single theme to develop into a more considered and organized 
short paper proposal. Please write a short proposal for the paper (300-500 
words) plus an annotated bibliography of relevant sources. You may find it 
easiest to begin with the “further reading” section for that topic as well as the 
essays in the “General Reference” texts, especially in their footnotes, and you may 
also wish to consult the lecture recordings of the course. However, an annotated 
bibliography should not be composed solely or primarily from these sources. Some 
topics will have extensive secondary literature, while others will have very little. 
If you find a great deal of bibliography, your task is to sift through the material and 
select the most important publications; if very little is available, you should 
identify relevant publications, either on the broader topic or comparable 
phenomena. Once you have compiled your bibliography, briefly annotate 
(maximum 2 sentences) each entry, indicating why that work will be 
relevant to your (imagined) paper. If there are any questions that you feel the 
works in your bibliography do not sufficiently answer, indicate them in a 
postscript. The length of your annotated bibliography will depend on how many 
publications you wish to include, but should not be less than two pages or ten 
sources. If you have questions about your topic, or merely wish to discuss it, please 
make an appointment in office hours. 
 
Your (proposed) paper can either be (a) a historical analysis, in which you make 
an original argument about something that occurred in the past, supported by 
secondary sources, or (b) an analytical criticism, in which you engage critically 
with a specific aspect of the text, making your point in comparison to other source 
literature. The paper proposal should already have a clear thesis claim based on 
the evidence you have identified during your research. REMINDER: There is not 
a full research paper included in this assignment! 
 
2. Final Assignment Option 2: (pick two questions, due during exam period, 
date tbd) 
 
This assignment is effectively a traditional written exam. On a date to be 
determined during the winter exams, you will write two in-class essays from a 
choice of three or four questions based on the material delivered during lectures 
and the course readings. The questions will be thematic rather than narrowly 
factual, but your answers should reflect specific knowledge of the materials 
discussed and considered critical analysis. In other words, it will not be possible 
to do well on the essays without having carefully studied the lectures and readings. 
 
Since this format is being offered as an alternative for students who prefer exams, 
and is arranged outside of the ETH exam scheduling office, it is mandatory that 
interested students sign up for Option 2 by the last class (4 Dec 2025). This 
will permit us to find a suitable date and location during the winter exam period.

General Assignment Guidelines: 
• You may write assignments in English, German, French, or Italian. 
However, please be aware that classes are conducted in English. Written 
feedback will be in English. 
• All work must be your own. Joint or work is not permitted and will result in a 
failing grade for the assignment in question. Oral discussion of ideas is 
permitted, as long as the final result is your own intellectual output. 
• Sources must be fully cited in a consistent format (I recommend the Chicago 
Manual of Style, available online via the library or in short form at 
https://www.chicagomanualofstyle.org/tools_citationguide.html). 
• Please write in full sentences and organized paragraphs according to the 
stylistic standards of your chosen language. You will not be penalized for minor 
errors or infelicities due to imperfect fluency, but please make sure you are 
clear. Anyone looking for a basic English style primer is recommended to 
consult Strunk and White’s The Elements of Style, 4th edition (online at 
archive.org). 
• All work must be turned in on time unless otherwise arranged in advance. 
Students in need of broader accommodation concerning deadlines or work 
output are strongly encouraged to get in touch with me as early as possible.

accepted with individual prior approval from the Studiensekretariat. For late 
submissions without prior approval, grades are deducted by .25-point 
amounts in two-day increments: i.e. a paper with an earned grade of 5.0 
submitted 12 hours late will receive a 4.75; the same paper submitted 2.5 days 
after the deadline will receive a 4.5, etc.

architectural history after 1450 as taught in the Bachelors curriculum. Anyone
