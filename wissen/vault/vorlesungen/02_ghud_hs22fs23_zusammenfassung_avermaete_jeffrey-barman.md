01 The History and Theory of the City as a Project 
A Manmade Material and Spatial Project 
Many fields have tried to understand what the city is and what it is about. Socialist 
Henri Lefebvre states the city is a social way of living together and doing things. 
Economist Henri Pirenne thinks the city is about trading and markets. For Tom 
Avermaete the city is not an organic result or an incident. It is the result of willful 
actions on the small (citizens) and large scale (urban planners). It is a manmade 
material and spatial project. 
Architects have long been trying to understand and describe the city. The first 
example of that is the map of Rome by Piranesi, as ordered by the pope. The map 
was very precise. Describing is a way to gain knowledge. Vitruvius also tried to 
explain cities in his 10 books. For him creating a city always needs fabrica, which is 
knowledge on how to build and construct, and ratiocinatio, which are big ideas and 
how the city should be laid out. Alberti also thinks about it and has 2 results. He 
says the city should be regarded as a project, one that can be planned. For him 
planning is creating a bridge between history and a possible future. Additionally he 
thinks that the house is a miniature city. Even in its smallest entity, we can think 
about how to live in a city. Alberti also mapped Rome (15th century) but on a 
circular shape, indicating walls, the Tiber and the monuments because they give 
character to a city. Alberti’s ideas would inspire many people. For example pope 
Sixtus V who started a big transformation project of Rome. In conclusion both 
Vitruvius and Alberti also think of the city as something that is planned. 
Urban Design as Engagement 
It is an engagement with the city. Urban design is an engagement with many 
different factors: 
Ground is an important aspect. Measuring is usually at the beginning of many cities. 
Topography is very important, for example in Istanbul. It is surrounded by water and 
the topography defines the layout of the city. Harbor and trade areas are lower, 
administration and religion is further up the hill. Topography is also important when

has to do with how an area can be most easily crossed. Las Vegas is a linear city

dominant. Earthquakes are another aspect. In Lisbon and Tokyo earthquakes led to 
destruction and fires. In Lisbon it resulted in a new grid with larger buildings, new 
facades and wider streets. In Tokyo houses don’t share walls anymore. 
Materials are also an important topic. Local materials are predominantly used and 
shape a city. In Bogota soil has lots of clay. Large parts of the city are made of 
brick, Avantgarde buildings and barrios. In Bath in the UK a big extension is 
conducted in the 18th century. Because lime stone is from the area both old and 
new buildings are made of the same material, creating continuity. 
Climate is another factor. Already Vitruvius wanted a city layout to be according to 
his 8 winds. In Bologna the urban element of the gallery is invented to give shade 
and protect from precipitation in summer and fall/spring respectively. Toronto is 
extremely cold in winter and as a response has an underground city with passages, 
squares, alleys… 
Culture is also a topic. In the case of the Paris’ passages urban design is an 
engagement with culture. They are designed for the developing Bourgeoise and 
allow to include the block’s interior in civic life. Another example is the parks in 
London, designed for the gentry. They seem public but are only accessible for 
residents around it. 
Philosophy or big ideas are another engagement. Platon thought the ideal city has 
12 sectors, some urban some rural. Every resident should have one place to live in 
each. Hippodamus was inspired by Platons ideas and applied some of them. 
Religion also influences cities, perhaps most in Islamic regions. An idea for 
Baghdad was a radial city with mosque and palace in the middle, then houses of 
the wealthy and in the outskirts the common people. In islamic cities the religious 
entity is usually in the centre. Even principles of the Koran are found in the city. 
Many houses have courtyards for private life without disturbing the collective. 
Politics are also important. An example is the Stalinallee, built as a socialist street. It 
is meant to be an alternative to the modern architecture in the west. Post colonial 
India is also an example. Neru wanted to go beyond the cast system and build cities 
representing the new society and attitudes of the country.

Social conditions can influence or be created by urban design. In 19th century 
Barcelona there were protests from workers for better working and living conditions. 
As a response the Cerda grid was developed. It reflects equal social conditions and 
gives quick access to the city centre with large roads. The French built many cities 
in their colonies, for example Meknes. Always with clearly separated areas for locals 
and Europeans. Urban design was also used to segregate, not only to connect. 
Economic reasons also influence cities. In London the royals decided to tear down 
houses of poorer residents and build the Regent Street instead. This way they could 
make a lot more money with their land. In post-war Paris urban design was used to 
create affordable housing for the masses in the form of the „villes nouvelles“. 
Technical influence is also important. In Chicago steel beams with concrete fillings 
were developed. Together with the elevator it enabled high-rise cities. Dessau 
Törten Estate is defined by technological construction with pre-fabricated elements. 
They were brought in with cranes, which needed tracks, which influenced layout. 
Art has an influence. Sometimes art has a critical role like in Jean-Luc Godard’s 
movie „2 ou 3 choses que je sais d’elle“. Through it designers realized the 
anonymity of the villes nouvelles and the presence of the bidonvilles. Sometimes it 
has an inspiring role. The painting „The Square“ directly influenced what the city 
Aprilia looks like. 
The Project of History and Theory 
The city also has a history, which people try to define with different approaches. 
Some historians define the city through experiences. Some see it as an organism 
where the built environment, people and animals work together in a series of 
functions. Others define the city’s history through a series of political decisions. 
We want to define the city through its architectural history. By doing so, we put 
ourselves in a long line. Camillo Sitte wrote „Der Städtebau“. He tells a history of 
different elements, like squares, and relations between them. He also compares 
them. The „American Vitruvius“ also looks at the elements that compose a city. They 
think the architectural practice has a knowledge-base made of elements that were 
built and those that were only planned. The most recent important reference is Aldo 
Rossi’s „L’Architettura della Citta“. He thinks buildings propel a city’s development 
and that they are urban artefacts. He also looks at their materiality and how they are 
constructed. An important aspect is his belief that buildings are important while 
they’re there but also after they’re gone. Destroyed amphi-theatres left an imprint in 
our perception of the city but also in the urban layout.

02 Of Rituals, Water and Mud: The Urban Revolution in 
Mesopotamia and the Indus 
The early settlements of Southwest Asia 
The global history of urban design starts in central and west Asia. Around 9000 BC 
an urban revolution takes place. New settlement forms appear in Mesoamerica, 
Andes, Egypt, Mesopotamia, Indus and China. They appear due to a neolithic 
revolution (=agricultural) that initiates the neolithic period (8550 - 5000 BC). Besides 
a technological advancement involving irrigation (=Bewässerung) and tools the most 
important change is a sedentary (=sesshaft) way of living, while domesticating 
plants and animals. With it comes a new cyclical concept of time. Plants have to be 
nurtured throughout a year … With the neolithic revolution also comes the urban 
revolution. People harvest more than they need, allowing them to store, trade or 
invest it. They also realize the societal advantage in security and ability to help one 
another when living together. The cultural surplus is being able to pass down 
knowledge and collectively improving their craft. 
One example of a new settlement is Göbekli Tepe (8000 BC). It 
sat on the border of Syria and Turkey and is a „prototype“ 
urban development. The site has many hills and rocks but soil 
is very fertile. The village has public buildings for rituals 
(temples) in the centre and housing surrounding it. The ritual 
buildings speak of the new concept of time. There were weekly 
rituals that reminded of cycling time and waiting for the 
harvest. Temples are large places for gatherings and rituals and 
have a distinct architecture. Embedded in the ground with large T-shaped columns 
that supported a roof. These are decorated with animals and agricultural elements. 
As are all the buildings, it is built of local materials. The ground played an important 
role in topography, soil and material. 
Catal Hüyük is another early settlement (7400 BC). It is built with 
mud bricks and reinforced with wood elements. Besides 
agriculture and animals the town traded with obsidian, which was 
used to build harvesting tools. Since it was close to a volcano 
they could easily find it. Topography is once again important. 
Society is very horizontal, no elevated leaders and high equality 
between genders. The entire city is a compact cluster of buildings 
without streets in between. All circulation took place on the rooftops. This is also 
advantageous for stability and defensive reasons. Courtyards were believed to be 
dumpsters. There were only domestic houses, as the rituals were happening inside

The Bronze Age Cities of Mesopotamia 
Mesopotamia sits between Tigris- and Euphrates river. Settlements in the location 
are called Sumerian cities with earliest ones around 5000 BC. They emerged in part 
because the soil is very rich and there was lots of water. Both good for agriculture. 
The area had lots of copper and tin, which was used to make bronze. Additionally 
important trade routes went through the area, which enabled trade in both goods 
and knowledge. The region was also known for agricultural inventions. For example 
a water system or the wheel. The first written descriptions of cities come from here, 
by Gilgamesh. Cities of the area have common characteristics: self governed and 
independent, geography and religion as main motives and each city belongs to a 
god. The temple is at the centre of every city and it fulfills a religious, economic and 
administrative role. 
Uruk is an example (3000 BC). It features defensive walls enclosing houses and 
religious buildings in the center. Such a structure is common. Also very important is 
its topographical position on a hill. The city is divided in neighbourhoods. The 
religious precinct is called Eanna district and has distinct architecture, differentiating 
it from housing. Buildings are made of sun-dried bricks with straw reinforcement. 
Walls are quite thick to counter the hot climate. The clayish material was also used 
to make statues and decorations, sometimes coloured. An entire city was built with 
one particular material. Its downside is being 
washed away, meaning cities had to be constantly 
renovated. Because of that, the city has different 
layers. This rebuilding becomes an urban figure 
called the ziggurat. On top of it are the temples. 
They are created by old material stacking up. 
Cities like Uruk had populations of over 50k. Norms and forms became necessary. 
They were not in the form of texts, but maps. The city of Nippur had maps showing 
streets, canals and fields belonging to the royal estates. 
Another example is the city of Ur (2100BC). It also features 
walls and a central religious district on top of a ziggurat. The 
ziggurat is for religion, trade and administration. We can also 
find canals and ports. In this town questions of regulation 
were adressed with behavioural codes. An example is the 
code of Ur-nammu, which forbids theft. Another form of 
regulation is architecture. Houses are very dense without 
windows and only a small opening to the street. They consist

The Cities of the Indus 
These cities were in nowadays Pakistan, close to India. Also 
very fertile with lots of water. They are called hydraulic 
civilizations and the earliest are 2800 BC. The city of 
Mohenjo-Daro (2600 BC) has 2 parts, both on an elevated 
site. The eastern one is the living area. The western one is 
the city’s centre, not religious but civic. It is generally more 
about society than religion. Everything is built of burned 
clay bricks, not mud ones, and thick walls. As a result constant renovation is not 
necessary. The urban fabric is very dense with smaller and larger streets and 
houses. The latter are once again 2-storey and with courtyards. 2-storey is easy 
with clay bricks and courtyards were sometimes covered, unlike previous examples. 
Houses have shared space and completely private areas. The upper floor is more 
exclusive and the lower for servants. Clay is also used for decorative elements. 
Public spaces have a hierarchy. There are primary, secondary and tertiary streets. 
Primary for everyone, secondary for some and tertiary for only a few. Along the 
primary streets there are more building openings with commercial functions. Along 
the secondary and tertiary street are housing units with few openings. 
Another important feature is an elaborate water infrastructure. It 
brings fresh water in and removes filthy water. Almost every 
house had a bathroom-like area with a fountain and a platform 
for washing. Filthy water goes through the wall into the canals, 
which all come together in larger ones. Always covered under the 
street, an entire sewage and fresh water system. Something very 
advanced that many European cities only had in the 20th 
century.

03 The idea of the Polis: Rome, Greece and beyond 
From Agora to Stoa 
The time of the greek empire is divided in the archaic period (800 - 500 BC), the 
classical period (500 - 336 BC) and the hellenistic period (336 - 27 BC). It is the start 
of interest for urban design, as predeceasing bronze age was more about building 
palaces than cities. Every now and then these palaces also lead to cities, for 
example Athens. During the greek era colonization starts to take place, which 
results in a need to plan cities. The beginning of the history of urban design goes 
hand in hand with colonization and slavery. 
The idea of the Polis (city) starts to emerge. Platon talks about how to govern 
society and thus the city. The centre of a Polis and the main public space is the 
Agora. In Athens it’s an open space on the pathway to the acropolis. The Agora is 
first introduced in 500 BC. It consists of the temple and a Bouleuterion. In the latter 
senators talk about governance and it consists of a stage and surrounding seats. 
The Agora develops and in the hellenistic period it looks quite different. There are 
new elements like the Metron (archive/office) and the Tholos, where senators were 
available for discussion. The most important innovation is the Stoa. Several of them 
form a covered walkway that connects alls buildings. Through time Stoas adopt a 
more commercial function. Another new element is the Odeion, a theatre, which is 
connected to the Agora with an underground path. In the end the agora is centre for 
politics, commerce, culture and religion, the city centre.

Hippodamus and the classical Greek city 
Hippodamus made a proposal for a new city plan of Miletus (5th 
century BC). He believed topography should influence urban 
design. His suggestion consists of a narrow grid at the narrow 
end of the peninsula, a wider one further back where the 
peninsula expands and a set of Agoras where both grids meet. 
Agoras consist of old and new elements like a market, 
bouleuterion, baths, gymnasium, sports … Hippodamus is 
considered the father of urban design. By studying a city’s 
functional problems and linking them to the state and 
administration system he divided population and city in 3 categories. Soldiers, 
artisans and husbandmen and sacred, public and private. His way of city planning 
with a grid spread quickly, as it is quite easy. 
Olynthus (4th century BC) is another example for a greek city planned with a grid. 
Each grid parcel features 10 houses of 7x7 metres. From the street one enters the 
house’s courtyard (oriented to the sun) and from there into the rooms. Besides the 
large streets there is also a small one along the long axis of a parcel. 
In Priene (4th century BC) the grid is elongated due to the steep terrain. Once again 
an agora in the middle with Stoa, Bouleuterion, theatre … House sizes vary with 
status, still feature a courtyard. 
Pergamon (2nd century BC) is a leap in development. The grid is now less important 
than planning according to topography. The Agora is in the south of the city and 
more monumentalized, less everyday aspects. The acropolis now features the 
cultural functions. There are thus two city centres. 
Dura-Europos (2nd century BC) is a rare occurrence of a clear grid in this time. 
There’s a defensive wall and once again an Agora, courtyard typologies … 
The Beginning of Urbanism in the West 
The greek empire didn’t only expand to the east but also 
westward (8th to 3rd century BC). This includes Italy and 
Sicily. Here grids remain more important. As usual for 
western greek cities Megara Hyblaea features two grids 
affected by the topography. Where they meet is the 
Agora. It also includes a wider and narrower set of 
streets. In Marzabotto the situation is similar. The grid is 
interrupted where topography requires it. Courtyard 
typologies inside the grid.

Town Planning in Roman Italy and its Empire 
The Roman Empire can be divided in Roman Republic 
(509 - 27 BC), Augustus reign (27 BC - 14 AD) and 
Roman Empire (14 - 476 AD). For the Romans slavery 
was common. They were used to build cities. The Forum 
is the centre of all Roman cities, the counterpart to the 
Agora. Forum Romanum was developed in the centre of 
Rome, where it sits in between Palatine- and Capitoline 
hill. Rome built in 8th century BC. To make the site less 
marshy and accessible the Cloaka Maxima, a sewage 
infrastructure, was created. The Forum grew gradually. Its most important element is 
the Curia, the senate of the elderly where city planning took place (=Bouleuterion). 
In the Commitium the city representation meets (=Tholos). The Rostra is a stage for 
public speeches. Evidently public discussion takes place in several locations. The 
Basilica is for legal and administrative purposes. Temples are also an important 
feature. The forum is for politics, administration, justice, religion and economy. 
As time progressed various emperors expanded the Forum 
Romanum, mainly with temples. Domus are a big housing typology 
for wealthy people and their slaves. They feature a large atrium. The 
common people lived in large blocks called Insulae. Insulaes 
created a dense urban fabric. Vitruvius really liked them, whereas 
others thought they were noisy, dense and dirty. 
At the base of planning new Roman cities is a combination urban design and 
military principles. It results in the Castrum, a type which became the basis for 
Roman settlements. First the sulcus, a plan in the sand, is created. Then the plan is 
subdivided. Headquarters and communal institutions in the middle 
and living outside. Tools like the Groma were important for 
creating precise measurement based on their unit, the Roman 
feet. The Decumanus (east-west axis) and Cardo (north-south 
axis) are the two main axes of Roman cities. Beginning with them 
they planned a city. Cosa (3rd century BC) is an example for such 
a city. It shows us that the Forum usually sits where Decumanus 
and Cardo cross. Of course topography was always important. 
The Romans also invented elaborate construction techniques for walls and longlasting roads. Their network of roads spanned across the entire empire. One Roman 
colony in Africa is Timgad. It is quite compact and has a city wall. Like in many 
Roman cities it features monumentalized Decumanus, Cardo and gate entrances. 
Pompeii is an example of a later time, were the grid typically is more loose. There 
are still many old features visible. A new one is a market at the edge of the forum.

Many of our cities nowadays still have visible influence from the Roman way of city 
planning. Verona features a grid with a Forum at a crossing. Luca is another 
example, where Decumanus and Cardo are still visible. 
Roman cities evolved. (1) It starts with a strict grid like in Marzabotto (400 BC). (2)

04 The long Middle Ages and their Counterparts: From the 
Towns of Tuscany to Delhi 
Religious patterns and practices 
From the 4th century onwards monasticism spreads in Europe, giving Christianity a 
permanent presence in the territory. In 7th - 9th century an Arab expansion takes 
place in the Near East, North Africa and Eastern Europe. In 11th and 12th century 
the first and third crusades took place to expand Christianity. It redefined the 
borders of countries and fueled the need to protect cities. 
Commercial Links and Urban Logics 
New commercial trade links are being established. The Hansa League (12th century) 
involves many cities in northern Europe like Hamburg, London, Riga … Most 
connections were across water. Such a transnational network of cities gives the city 
autonomy and power. They are not only dependent on a country anymore.

## S. 11

In Medieval times there was a pyramid of lord who owned the land, subtenants who 
used the lord’s land and paid for it and workers who worked on the land and were 
bound to it. The only exception to this were the cities. Here people had legal and 
economic rights. Cities are the exception to the dominant feudal system. Cities had 
a charter and a council to govern them. 
Medieval cities are usually a mixture of different ideals how 
cities should be designed. They’re often built on former 
settlements, for example Roman ones. Cities of the time 
are often considered organic but there is usually some logic 
behind it. For example Palermo lacks a clear grid but its 
fabric is based on the idea that every cell has access to at 
least one open space. 
The Monastery and religious motifs 
In the middle ages religion becomes institutionalized. The Bishop oversees an area 
and is representative of the pope. Two elements appear that materialize religion. The 
cathedral, where the Bishop resides. It succeeds temples or the basilica and is built 
quite similar to the latter. A central element of the city. Secondly the monastery, 
which was quite a large complex. They took care of the 
ill, orphans, travellers, mentally ill … The monastery was 
a place of religion but also an important social place. 
Many cities form around these two entities. One 
example is Arras (15th century), a binary town. Its left 
part is built on the old Roman settlement and around 
the cathedral. On the right the ville develops around the 
monastery and is the commercial area. 
In the monastery of St. Gallen a new plan is implemented in the 9th century. It 
allows for functional division. In the centre is a cathedral. Next to it are other areas 
for crafts, education, monks, guests … There is a very specific idea of where to 
locate and how to separate different zones. 
The City as Expression of Defensive Logics 
Burghal Hidage is a set of villages that used fortification as the main determinant for 
how to plan their city. This became in part necessary due to viking invasions in 8th - 
11th century. The type of settlement is called Burgh. They are located in England. 
Walls were simple with a few towers. Castles were another defensive element. In 
case of an attack the walls would be closed and if they were breached the people 
could retreat to the last fortification, the castle.

In Pembroke we see an example of a former burgh. It is 
elongated, with the castle in the far end, a market and one 
or several churches. A fishbone plan is common. Plots are 
elongated, called burgage plots, and feature a house facing 
the street and a garden for agriculture and animals behind. 
This way they could be self-sufficient. Even the castle has 
two different walls. Last defence inside the last defence. 
Bastides are the french version of defended cities. Usually 
rectangular with gates and towers at the wall entrances. The 
central plaza in the middle is called Carra. Adjacent to it are the 
town hall and military administration. The plots are called 
Moulon and are shaped by the streets. A good example is 
Aigues Mortes (13th century). 
In India the islamic wars also raised questions of defence. Tughlaqabad (14th 
century) is a good example for a fortress. Walls are slightly tilted. Palace of the ruler 
is again the second fortification of last resort. It would later initiate Delhi. 
Mercantile Urban Motifs in Europe 
Religion, defence, trade and commerce are important factors shaping the cities of 
the middle ages. The Hanseatic League consisted of some existing cities and some 
that were newly built. The latter usually have 3 characteristic elements. A good 
transport infrastructure with docks, gates … A storage infrastructure so that goods 
could be stored and shipped out. Lastly an exchange infrastructure where trade 
took place. Copenhagen illustrates this very well. It has many docks and dry docks 
to build ships (transport). There is an important marketplace and docks for 
exchange (trade). Warehouses are nearby to store goods (storage). It was part of the 
league but founded by a Bishop. There’s a city charter that determines citizens as 
free but also gives them duties like paying taxes. It also includes laws and rights. In 
materiality Hanseatic cities are also comparable. They have foundations of stone 
and wood and the main material is bricks. It’s because the league traded lots of 
brick. Other examples of cities with mercantile influence are Paris or Amsterdam. 
The Emergence of New Urban Institutions 
In medieval cities new urban institutions emerged. For example in Amsterdam big 
complexes emerged in many plots that the church owned. Examples would be an 
orphanage or a hospital. The complexes usually had a function in the city and were 
public spaces for the people. That was something completely new.

## S. 13

One example is the Binnengasthuis in Amsterdam. Ordered by the church as a 
hospital it soon developed a large set of gardens. The first hospital typology was a 
basilica filled up with rooms. Such complexes were for the citizens, not just 
patients. They provided civic open spaces. 
Markets and guild houses were also new public institutions. 
Another new institution is the University. The first one is founded 11th century in 
Bologna. Oxford is a good example of how universities shape a city. It originally 
grew from a monastery and universities were located outside the city. The collection 
of universities form a system of open spaces. Cohesion between them comes from 
public access and materiality as most are built in limestone. The different colleges, 
partly because they share a common material, start to build an urban entity. 
The Everyday Urban Fabric 
Medieval cities also had regular urban fabric. Most houses were made of stone, 
wood and brick. Initially houses were large halls made of wood. Because fires 
destroyed large city parts, they changed their construction techniques. They used 
more stone and built separating walls of brick to prevent fire from spreading. 
The City-State and the Zähringer Cities 
Some cities became states and entities of themselves. An example is Sienna. 
Governance became a civic matter, as the city governed itself. Initially there were 17 
smaller rural areas. Because of defensive reasons they worked together and formed 
a city. They created a new public center with public buildings. The square 
represents the 17 entities joining forces and working together. The buildings 
surrounding it have certain rules like height or materiality to make them monumental 
and suitable to celebrate the cooperation. Representatives of each part came 
together to decide how to govern the city. Amidst feudal states such a cooperation 
is an exception. Another example of a city-state is Florence. 
Bern is an example for a Zähringer City. They usually don’t have walls but 
watchtowers. There are 2 main axes with buildings close to the streets. Local 
materials are used.

05 Between Ideal and Laboratory: Of European Renaissance 
principles and Laws of the Indes 
Humanist Italy 
Between 14th and 16th century Europe is characterized by monarchies and 
independent city states. It is a time of explorers and colonization. The latter is 
mainly in coasts of Africa, South America and Asia. Explorations were also a way of 
spreading religion. It also influenced urban design. Trade routes were established. 
Goods were mainly shipped towards Europe, rarely away from it. 
A world of religion, exploration, colonization, trade … In this context new ideas for 
cities emerge, mainly in Italy. That’s because Italy is a hub for culture and exchange. 
In the 14th century the Renaissance emerges and spreads for 2 centuries. The term 
is based on the notion „Rinascimento“ from Giorgio Vasari in 16th century. 
Knowledge of the old Romans was accessed through libraries and reborn. The most 
important is the Imperial Library of Constantinople. It stored a lot of knowledge on 
Greeks and Romans. The new idea of the state emerges as well. It is an 
organization of society. Governed by reason, not an almighty king or pope. Niccolo 
Machiavelli thinks of the secular state. A form of politics, where power is not within 
one person. The printing press is also invented, most notably by Gutenberg. It is a 
new and faster way of spreading knowledge. In summary an interest in classical

The 19th century is a time of city expansions due to population increase. There were

11 The Long 20th Century and Its Challenges 
Urbanization really took flight in the 20th century. It brings a set of new challenges. 
The first challenge is urbanization as a spatial and social matter, along with new 
typologies. Cities’ footprints spread and people start living in an urban way. The 
massive population increase and new technologies lead to new typologies like the 
high-rise but also to displacement of people resulting in the typology of the slums. 
The second challenge is providing housing and public space in the extending urban 
areas. Especially the idea of affordable housing becomes important. Both can be 
helpful in bringing disconnected historic and new parts of cities together and 
improving living conditions. For example connecting parts of Vienna. 
The third challenge is dealing with the results of the second industrial revolution: 
pre-fabrication, automobiles and mass consumption. Construction is rationalized 
and efficient, resulting in pre-fabrication. In „das neue Frankfurt“ pre-fabrication is 
used to supply affordable housing. Cities also need to adapt to the car. 
The fourth challenge is wars and reconstructing cities. It brings up questions of how 
to reconstruct? In the old way or a new one? For example Rotterdam or Hiroshima. 
The fifth challenge is urban symbolism and the cold war. Urban design becomes 
entangled with and starts to present politics. For example Berlin. 
The sixth challenge is democratization and emancipation. Citizens democratically 
participate in design. Different projects and typologies emerge as a result. For 
example the Maison Dom-ino or museums and universities. 
The seventh challenge is the rust belt or more generally de-industrialization. Many 
cities have large vacant spaces, which can be turned into housing, leisure or nature. 
Examples are Detroit or the Ruhr Area. 
The eighth challenge is the limits to growth, the environment. In the 20th century 
there were small groups designing with it in mind but it was not mainstream. 
The ninth challenge is professionalization. New disciplines like hygiene, health or 
economics become involved. The focus of urban design also includes these. 
Urban design is between heteronomy and autonomy. Always related to a bigger 
picture but time and time again reflecting upon its own tools and history.

Housing and The Industrial City 
The context is the 20th century and the second industrial revolution. Machinery

Speculative Housing for Workers 
Resources were not only coal, gas or oil, but also agricultural. India exemplifies this. 
Farming shifted from subsistence to commercial. With the emergence of the railway 
goods could be brought to the harbours, which made it interesting to produce them 
for a living, not only survival. All over the world new industries created a new wave 
of urbanization. The emerging 1 million cities came with challenges for urban 
development. Perhaps the biggest is providing housing for the workers. Different 
parts of the world had different ways of solving the issue. 
One model is speculative housing. People develop housing to gain a financial profit. 
Mumbai’s Chawls are an example for this. The city was a center of urbanization and 
industry in India. It all started when the US became independent after their civil war

the people to live nearby the chawls were established. 88% of the population lived 
in them. Private owners who owned the mills also introduced the chawls. They are 
composed of two blocks around a central courtyard. 5 storeys high with commercial 
use in the road facing ground floor. Circulation is with a central 
stair and galleries in the courtyard. Sanitary rooms were 
collectivized. Entire families, sometimes several generations, 
lived in the 16 units per storey. As a result, the gallery was an 
extension of the private dwelling. A speculative model that 
allowed many units on a small area.

New Capitals for New Democracies - Fort Lamy 
The postwar period of the 50s and 60s was also the time of the second wave of 
decolonization. People protested against the oppression and many countries 
became independent. It led to them thinking about their own development and 
modernization. This often resulted in building symbolical new cities or capitals. 
Another characteristic of the time is further development of the old nations. An

The first example for a new city is Fort Lamy in Chad. Designed by

colonial city. Colonial cities usually have 3 characteristic parts: for 
local population, for Europeans and a zone sanitaire to separate

big continuous public space, from which new neighbourhood

created a new urban neighbourhood that combines high- and low-rise but above all 
connects local with colonial areas of the city. A very symbolic intervention that aims 
to erase the 3-parted structure of the colonial city.

New Institutions for Old Democracies - State Building 
In old democracies development and welfare become important

17 Rethinking Masterplanning 
Revised Typologies for the city. 
The first ones to become critical of modernist urban designs are the residents, who 
find them anonymous and uninteresting. An example of this critique is „2 ou 3 
choses que je sais d’elle“. Socialists like Lefebvre criticize the open planes as they 
lack the identity of the historic streets. Architects like Gordon Cullen also become 
critical. In an article he describes modernism as prairie planning, forgetting about 
the town with streets. CIAM was internally critiqued by the Team 10, which was 
founded in 1953. They looked at how people appropriate spaces and came to see 
the street as a social entity. There are 7 attitudes responding to this critique. 
The first attitude comes from Alison and Peter Smithson. Their first study is Golden 
Lane in 1950s. A proposal for a new housing environment in London. In it they 
suggest a new housing typology. It redefined the relation of housing and street 
(street = public space). It is composed of 3 layers of maisonettes and 3 „streets in 
the air“. The blocks could be combined to create a housing network with a new 
street network inside them. They call it the cluster city. It would create a new public 
space, superimposed on the existing network of streets. One feature is also a 
decrease of class differences. A new relation of housing and streets. Purely 
theoretical. 
In another project they wanted to show that a revised version of the modernist block 
can fit into the existing urban fabric. They place the Economist Building and 2 
others in the middle of London in 1960s. Together they shape a public space. The 
blocks relate to the existing city by keeping a certain scale and using the same 
Portland stone that the surroundings have. It was an attempt to relocate the modern 
tower from the open landscape back into the urban fabric. It is meant to be a test 
that can be repeated if successful. In conclusion, the first attitude aims to remodel 
the ideas of the urban towers and slabs, so that they can fit into the urban fabric. 
Towards a Configurative Urban Discipline 
Aldo van Eyck was part of Team 10. He deals with anonymity and decides to start 
planning in a different way. Not with the urban masterplan. He asks himself how 
social practices relate to the built environment. Classical architecture brings 
calmness and solidity. Vernacular architecture is something people relate to, that 
they have in their heart. Modern architecture stands for change and movement. He’s 
trying to show how a new urban environment could influence social aspects, 
keeping in mind the different influences of architecture types.

The orphanage is made of pre-fabricated concrete elements

The Public Space of Stem and Web 
Candilis, Josic and Woods, members of CIAM, developed another approach: Stem 
and Web. They were very critical of big housing units and their anonymous 
character. It was shown with collages. The concept of the stem rethinks urban 
development with the street. Their reference are the street villages, where the entire 
urban fabric and people are located along the street, making it a social entity. The 
stem tries to reintroduce this aspect. There’s a street with the collective functions 
and from it urban development is branching off. They go a step further and think of 
it not as a singular street but a network of stems that creates a web. The web 
reintroduces a new urban fabric. Their proposal is to go back to the idea of creating 
an urban fabric, away from the thought of a large block in an open landscape. 
They first implement this idea in the competition for the Frankfurt 
Römerberg in 1960s and see it as a web on superimposed 
layers. It consists of superimposed platforms, open spaces and a 
rigid street pattern. The latter is part of the platforms but still has 
all the conventional infrastructure like electricity etc. underneath. 
After providing this, they thought they could step back and it would develop by 
itself. Smaller and larger openings in the web would create different public spaces, 
allowing for a theatre or others. In conclusion a multi-layered urban ground between 
streets, open spaces and platforms. A reinterpretation of the urban fabric. 
A project that was actually realized is the Berlin Free University in 1960s. At the time 
Universities were often built in skyscrapers. CJW thought this creates vertical 
separation and suggest a „ground-scraper“. It would have more potential for 
interconnection. Building elements were pre-fabricated and assembled. All inner

Agency in Housing: Between Supports and Infill 
John Habraken criticizes the masterplan’s inability for change according to needs. 
He rethinks the agency in architecture and publishes the book „Supports - an 
alternative to mass housing“ in 1960s. He creates the concept of the Support and 
Infill. Supports are elements for a long time and infill are elements for 10-20 years. 
He argues that urban designers should think in temporalities, in the form of support 
and infill. For him the supports were generally the outlines of public space in the 
environment. The infill is basically everything else like partitions between units. 
SAR is a dutch group that starts to apply the concept. It was a research group that 
also designed new urban neighbourhoods. The support is the volume or outline of 
buildings, which define the public space. Within this layout the infill could take 
place. Molenvliet Housing Project by Frans van der Werf in 1970s. After designing 
the support, infill would be designed with the inhabitants, according to their needs. 
In a second round of consultation they would design the facades, which always 
corresponded to what happened inside the building. The agency of the support is 
the urban designer and the agency of the infill is a combination of people and urban 
designer. When new people moved in, the infill could be changed. 
Systems Thinking through Patterns and Clusters 
This fifth attitude argues that the urban environment must be changeable through 
time. The UN realized that masterplanning is not the way to go in the global south. 
An example is James Stirling’s Previ Lima project in 1960s. It is based on the idea 
that the city is able to grow and adapt on 2 levels: the city itself and the dwelling 
unit. He places an L shaped house as a basis on a square plot. Through time it 
could first expand horizontally and then vertically while being appropriated by the 
residents. The growing cell is combined with a constructive system of prefabricated concrete columns and beams. It allows for quick assembly and 
extension. Clusters made of 4 cells form a courtyard. These can be combined to 
form larger courtyards and so on. In the end it creates an urban fabric.

The metabolism approach was developed by Japanese architects who were 
interested in change and growth of a city. They thought of the city as an organism

water. It would be the backbone for future development. Externally there would be 
expansion and internally would be regeneration. He chose a linear system, because

Mega Structures 
The last attitude is that of the mega structures, which are similar to the metabolism. 
In some way a further elaboration of it. Moshe Safdie wanted to use the single cell

construction. Construction starts from a single house and the human relation to it. 
He wanted to combine the dense urban fabric and single housing with your private 
garden. Initially the project also had an A shaped permanent structure with

residents, an architecture office on site, equal numbers of houses demolished and 
created and no uniform treatment. The last one means every neighbourhood should 
have its own identity. The new neighbourhood was planned bit by bit, but always 
along the Byker Wall. The wall had a particular attitude. It is very closed to the loud

mega-structure. Identification also took place with the diverse housing typologies.

Freedom and Self-Determination - Babylon, Spatiale, Abierta and Segal 
In the 1960s mass consumption and production really takes off. Houses became 
places of accumulation and consumerism. It’s also the time of the cold war. There 
were many reaction to this consumerism, for example the „Limits to Growth“ (1972). 
It states that resources and people are not in harmony. Even regular people started 
to raise their voices. They show their critique on the current society with counter 
cultural movements. They aimed to improve ecology, having self-determination and 
finding new forms of authentic communal life. 
One of the proposals for new communal life is New Babylon by 
Constant Nieuwenhuys in 1950s. Self-determination sits at the 
core. It starts with the hypothesis that the industrialization will free 
man from labour. Therefore man can engage with the urban 
environment in a new way. It is influenced by a book called „Homo 
Ludens“ (The playing man). Nieuwenhuys’ man will engage with the 
environment in a playful way and is not connected to profit or material values. He / 
She is not static, but nomadic. Dwellings are constantly changed and reinvented by 
citizens. The city consists of ladders, stairs and platforms. Conventional elements 
combined into new formations, where life can be constantly reinvented. The 
environment is characterized by „extreme hospitality“. Everyone is welcome 
everywhere, which leads to encounters and starts the conversation of how and 
where one will live. New Babylon hovers over an open, collectively owned ground. 
The city is divided in sectors and invites for a dense, nomadic way of living. Sectors 
can be combined to form a bigger city. It could be superimposed on an existing city. 
Black nodes on maps indicate where it touches the ground. The city isn’t defined by 
the state but by inhabitants, who constantly reinvent how to live in it.  
Another important figure is Yona Friedman, who developed the 
Ville Spatiale in 1950s. It starts from the hypothesis that urban 
designers are unable to provide the situation people really 
need to live in it. Citizens should determine the urban 
environment, making them urban designers. He suggests a big 
frame superimposed on the city. He calls this rigid supporting grid an infrastructure. 
In it is housing and agriculture. Dwelling is not pre-determined but developed by the 
inhabitants. The ville will develop by itself. It can’t be planned, it can only happen. 
There’s infinite ways it could look like. The only rule is a respect for daylight. Light 
must still reach the ground floor. The grid consists of modules of 6x6 metres. In 
them can be components for housing, production, collective functions, circulation

… Components are mobile, designed by the citizen. Based on lightweight circular 
modules people could fashion their own living environment. They are cheap and 
easy to assemble and dissemble. In order to help people think about their layout 
and relations he supplies a Flatwriter. A set of suggestions how a flat can be 
designed. The resulting individuality of dwellings is celebrated. The city would 
become a theatre of everyday life. The idea can be superimposed on cities. Where 
new city touches the old one a node of multi-transportation is established. A wild 
idea that aims to give people more self-determination in their own city. 
Ciudad Abierta of 1970 is located close to the ocean in Chile. The idea is to create a 
new environment that focuses on ecology, self-determination and communal life. 
They started by creating spaces for encounter, collective spaces. These are not 
radically inserted in the environment but follow the topography. The future city 
should be respectful of the landscape. This includes not building any real streets. 
There are only connecting paths, which can be easily erased. They affected the 
landscape as little as possible. Another central topic is that people would build 
everything themselves, sometimes with invited guests. Construction was done with 
recycled materials. No one owns the buildings. Everyone is considered a temporary 
resident. The clear idea that at some point different people will live there nullifies 
private ownership. The entire city is a testing ground for a new community. Since 
the city is collectively owned they collectively decided who enters and lives there. 
A last experiment of self-determination and freedom is by Walter Segal. He 
developed a temporary house for his family in Highgate, built with materials from a 
regular do it yourself shop in 1960s. The entire construction is based on carpentry, 
making it very cheap and fast. It could be disassembled. His model was used on a 
larger scale in London. It became known as the Segal Method, which helps people 
with 0 experience design and build their own homes. It is composed of several 
steps, through which one is taken. How to design the plan, how to place elements 
on a grid, how to go to a structural grid … People could build houses with the help 
of their community. Many of them were built, for example in Lewisham. All houses 
have a recognizable grid but are individual as they were individually designed. 
Towards Urban Ecology - Drop City, Arcological City and Sea Ranch 
In Drop City in Colorado (1960s) there were two main ideas. 
Respecting the land and territory and a new communal life. 
They developed a new, creative urban environment. They built 
domes made of scrap or reused material. A laboratory project 
meant to house a few hundred, which turned out to be a few tens. Besides places 
to live there would also be communal space, animals … A lot of international 
attention was created because of the respect for the terrain, the use of recycled

material and the fact that people constructed the domes themselves. Communal 
life, ecology and cheap / sustainable materials were important aspects. They even 
had early forms of solar energy. Many people visited the community to understand 
how it works. In the end that caused it to close, as it turned into a tourist attraction. 
The Arcological City was designed by Paolo Soleri in Arizona. He 
thinks ecological urban design comes from building with the 
territory: earth. He experimented with dome-like buildings that 
were partly subterranean and built of earth and other cheap 
materials. In the 1950s he starts trying some bigger complexes. 
First one for himself with housing, office … Also partially 
subterranean. Using soil as a material and to make casts for concrete. The latter 
formed very expressive roofs. In the 1960s he starts to develop arcological cities, 
thinking about the relation of man and nature. He calls it the Mesa City. He designed 
large underground parts but also vertical elements. It’s more thoughts than actual 
proposals. One instance where he starts building such a city is Arcosanti in 1970. It 
has been growing since the 70s and is characterized by vaulted houses that provide 
coolness with shade and thick walls. Of course also built with local soil. 
The Sea Ranch is another example for urban ecology, 
started in 1960s by Lawrence Halprin in Sonoma County, 
California. They also sought a new form for urban 
development. It turned out to be a suburban 
development. An alternative to urban sprawl. One that 
starts with a respect for the environment. Urban elements are carefully situated in 
the environment’s climate, topography and character. There are a few principles. 
The first is that every settlement is set back from the coast by at least 30 meters. 
Secondly all settlements must relate to the local vegetation that is already there. 
Thirdly all urban development must be clustered, which facilitates respecting the 
environment. They realized that we as humans are only one of many agents shaping 
the environment. Thus they wanted to create a community where the whole is more 
than the parts. Not only some individual houses but a cluster that enhances the 
natural beauty. They planned the organic environment, not against or on top of it. In

Embracing Consumer Culture and Technology 
Other ideas radically embrace the consumer culture instead of 
countering it. One of them is the Instant City by Archigram in 
1960s. They embraced pop and mass culture, specifically the 
ephemeral. Elements and constructions come and go, they are 
disposable. You throw away what you don’t want anymore. It 
resulted in their idea of the Instant City, a traveling Metropolis, 
with architecture and structures that could be gone in a matter of days. They 
describe a set of steps how it works. First the regular city is sleeping. Then the 
Instant City arrives in a light-weight vehicle, introducing light weight architecture, of 
pop concerts with tents, spaces … It infiltrates the city and creates a set of events. 
It creates outdoor theatre, cinema, seminar rooms … At a certain moment the 
Instant City leaves. However now the city is understood in a completely different 
way. The minds of people have changed on what it can become or how it can 
function. After its departure suddenly an event center, info center or other activities 
will appear as a result. They also suggest concrete cities where it could take place. 
The Instant City is only there for a short term in an ephemeral way. But it affects the 
built environment in the long term. It tunes up an existing urban environment. 
Another idea comes from Superstudio in 1970s. They asked themselves how the 
generic character of the time develops into an urban context. They developed 
histograms, suggesting generic surfaces that express the built environment. They 
go from the smallest to the biggest scale. From furniture to an urban environment. 
Big generic supersurfaces on which a variety of things can happen. It can be 
inserted in the existing urban fabric. The city as a generic surface that can be 
appropriated. No streets or squares or hierarchy just an open condition. 
A third group is Archizoom. They had the idea that modern 
technology enabled us to inhabit the entire territory. The No-
Stop City (1970s), an endless city where a variety of things 
can happen. Infrastructure like water or electricity would be 
everywhere. The metropolis becomes an all-surrounding 
condition, not a place. A city is no longer a delineated place 
but can be all over the globe. Mass production is used to create an infinite urban 
situation. It is organized in a grid with punctual infrastructures and fields where 
people can live. The Residential Parkings are a more concrete suggestion. The 
entire territory would be covered with individual residential parkings. Multi-storey 
buildings with a park on top, services and circulation in the lower floors and living 
spaces in the higher floors. In between would be connecting infrastructure. 
Habitation would be a combination of lightweight elements, natural elements and 
commodities, in between which dwellings could be placed. It can take various 
forms like camping, vernacular, technological …

19 Finding Meaning in the Postmodern City 
Introduction 
Urban Design continuously becomes the focus of criticism. Criticizing the theories 
and effects on the historical city. Jane Jacobs reacted against modernist planning, 
especially against paternalistic attitudes, which means to planners imposing their 
visions. She was active from 50s to 70s and argued that projects like the Highway 
through downtown NY erase the qualities of the existing city. She calls for 
developing, not erasing old fabric. She delivers an alternative based on some 
principles. Sidewalks are an important part of the city to encounter and play. She 
also highlights neighbourhoods with their own school, community center … Her 4 
pillars are lively and interesting streets, the fabric of the streets becoming a 
continuous network, using parks squares and public buildings as part of the street 
and to have an identity of the neighbourhood. Gordon Cullen published 
„Townscape“. He argues for the qualities of historical cities and wants to reactivate 
the idea of the small town. For him a city is based on experiences. Oan Douglas 
Nairn published „Your England Revisited“. He sees towns as models that could be 
projected onto the future. All 3 of them look at the past and how it could affect 
future urban design. In Germany Wolf Jobst Siedler and Elisabeth Niggemeyer 
criticize the city’s anonymity and lack of quality. It lacks unexpected variety. 
 
Architecture and Territory 
The first result is an attitude that focuses on urban design 
related to the territory. In 1960s Vittorio Gregotti published „Il 
Territorio Dell’ Architettura“. He wants urban design to broaden 
the scope and be in the context of the territory, not the city. 
His idea of territory is a combination of manmade and natural 
elements. Agriculture, infrastructure, topography, natural 
conditions … The manmade factor is larger because of many interventions. Urban 
design should relate to the territory. He illustrates this with a new neighbourhood for 
the University of Calabria in Cosenza in 1970s. It is a 3km long linear structure 
spanning between two territorial elements: a road and a railway. Topography 
determines where for example student housing is located. Thus the nature- and 
manmade territory defines the project. At the end are the collective services, sports, 
administration … Along it are the faculty buildings. These are modular elements 
along a spine. They always react to the territory, especially in height. Outdoor rooms 
are crated with a strong connection to the territory. Circulation also takes place on 
the spine. Lower level pedestrian and upper level cars.

History and Topology - The Life and History of the Urban Tissue 
In 1950s Saverio Muratori published „Studi per una operante 
storia urbana di Venezia“, where he analyses Venice. While 
modern designers said forget about the historic city, Muratori 
urged to look back at it and learn from it. For him it isn’t 
static but develops over time. It can’t be part of the past as it 
adapts to contemporary ways of using and living in the city. 
It is an expression of the social, cultural and economic life. 
Urban designers should study and understand its structural logic. On the other hand 
the historic city also has a certain permanence through time. He shows this double 
principle with Venice. A comparison of the same space through time shows that the 
building’s outlines and their composition to form a square is permanent. What 
changes is the inside, the floor plan. It adapts to changes of social, economic and 
cultural patterns. He says permanence and change must be thought on 3 levels. 
The first and smallest level is type, that of the building. The second level is the 
urban tissue. Third is the organism, the entire city. All levels show permanence and 
change. 
Muratori also designs a new neighbourhood. First he uses the model of medieval 
Venice. A city composed of small islands, reached by boat, where you know your 
neighbour. Then he uses the model of 18th century Venice. Still reachable by land 
and water, larger communities, more anonymous, new typology with smaller 
housing blocks. Then he uses a land based model with high anonymity, larger 
blocks and economy on land. He shows that type and morphology are an 
expression of a certain way of life. It can be used to think the future city. 
History and Topology - Collective Memory and Urban Artefacts 
In 1960s Aldo Rossi publishes „L’Architettura della Citta“. He also looks at the 
historical city and says it is an artefact with the ability to adapt over time. He also 
talks of the city’s longevity and permanence in certain elements. For him the city is 
mainly composed of housing and artefacts. The city is an expression of how society 
has dealt with the question of housing. To exemplify this he looks at Berlin at 3 
different points in time. In the 19th century housing was very dense with small 
courtyards. Later in the 19th century light and air with a bigger courtyard system is 
important. In the 20th century the city is not only about private space but also about 
communal space, i.e. parks. He says the city is an artefact but also an expression of 
cultural, economic and social aspects, which is evident in housing. The big artefacts 
are the ones defining the city with a certain permanence through form and material, 
even when they materially disappear. The new housing blocks in the plot of a former 
colosseum will also take a circular shape. Artefacts give a certain identity to the

Learning from Popular Culture 
Another attitude is to embrace the popular city. Robert Venturi and Denise Scott-

From Manhattanism to Interior Urbanism 
Another approach was to embrace the big city, the 
Megalopolis, and turning it into an urban design approach. 
One of the main actors was Rem Koolhas, who published

grids and high-rise buildings, which can house a variety of things. Thus an interior 
urbanism. 
The theory was implemented by other architects. John Portman designed the 
Westin Bonaventura Hotel in LA in 1970s. The hotel is a model of a city, with all its 
typical functions incorporated. The city is interiorized.

Urban Design as Projet Urbain 
In the second half of the 20th century there is ongoing rapid urbanization. However 
it is paired with the phenomenon of imploding cities. Shrinking cities with 
decreasing population. This is usually the case where industries had formerly 
resided. In the shift from the production to the service industry many people lost 
their jobs and moved elsewhere. Usually not only sites but also their related 
infrastructures like harbours or railways become vacant. These vacant areas are 
often in the middle of the city and could be developed into new neighbourhoods. 
The first approach to deal with these situations is the Projet Urbain. They decided to 
move the food market in les Halles to the periphery. As a result the site became 
vacant and public discussions regarding its future emerged. Projet Urbain is a very 
specific way of urban design that starts in 1970s with the destruction of Les Halles. 
Not everything is accurately planned from the beginning. It works on an 
intermediate scale, between neighbourhood and building. Public space becomes 
the main attention. Lots of programs are combined like housing, infrastructure, 
commerce … The goal is not to deliver a finished project but to initiate a dialogue 
between designers, politicians, society and investors. Shifting away from 
masterplanning towards a process where many actors are involved. Economic, 
spatial and social matters are of equal importance. 
In 1960s the urbanism agency of Paris makes a proposal for 
les Halles. They speak of infrastructure, public space and how 
it connects. They are very vague in regard to buildings and 
program. On the basis of the proposal a first competition is 
organized in 1960s. Work was shown with models and then 
discussed. Projects weren’t used to imagine the final project but to initiate a 
dialogue with the public and politicians. Not a blueprint is expected but debatable 
options. Resulting from it is a second competition in 1970, which sets a program. Of 
the results the president liked Ricardo Bofill’s neo-classical design the most, much 
to the disliking of the Avantgarde. They launched a counter-competition in 1970s 
with more radical suggestions. The final project of 1970s was by Claude Vasconi 
and Georges Pencreac. It combines many of the previous elements. In 2000s they 
already held a new competition, as the building was considered outdated. 
Another example for projet urbain is the Eurolille competition for Lille in 1980s. The 
city was formerly industrial with many vacant sites and at a geographically 
interesting position, connecting to London, Paris, Amsterdam … The first

Infrastructural Urbanism 
Another approach is the infrastructural urbanism, focusing solely on infrastructure. 
In many cities there is a problem of disconnection from the water. In Barcelona there 
was a very broad road and a wharf in between the city and the sea. What was 
initially a small road expanded through time due to increasing importance of cars, 
which led to the disconnection. Manuel de Solà-Morales develops a project to 
change this. The essence is a belief that mobility infrastructure is a necessity, but 
can be better designed to allow reconnection. The project is called Moll de la Fusta. 
A 600m long and 150m wide intervention in 1980s. It is all about replanning 
infrastructure and the entire area between city and water through the section. Next

the promenade are small structures that can house a shop

the promenade. The big wharf received many trees to 
make it seem leisurely. By carefully redesigning the 
transport infrastructure he made urban design,

Landscape Urbanism 
Landscape urbanism doesn’t focus on the architecture but on the landscape. It’s 
not about the solids of a city but the open spaces. The city of the 20th century 
changes so much, that it can’t be precisely designed. Many realized that in these 
conditions it is smarter to look at open spaces. It allows to deal with the condition of 
the open-endedness. It was often used to design former industrial sites that are 
close to the city. The goal was to develop and allow future change. 
The Parc de la Villette is such a site. The former slaughterhouse area 
of Paris. They decided to turn it into a park and held a competition in 
1980s. The title was „Park for the 21st century“. They obviously 
wanted something special. Two entries caught attention. The first is 
by OMA. Instead of designing a precise park they proposed to divide 
it into strips with different programs like contemplation, production, 
sports … Once again a very open strategy. It is in essence the 
horizontal skyscraper or horizontal Downtown Athletic Club. The winning proposal 
was by Bernard Tschumi. His project didn’t define a finite form but structured the 
park based on 3 layers: points, lines and surfaces. He thought after defining those 
he could let the Park develop itself. First he designs the points structure. He calls 
the points follies. Follies were historically small pavilions in parks without any use, 
purely ornamental. He plans a set of them with varying sizes and functions. They 
can become a restaurant, sports club … The unifying characteristic is that they’re all 
red. Secondly he designs the lines, the pathways. Either straight or curved, covered 
or open or with follies along them. Thirdly the surfaces are either green or covered. 
He believed this system could be expanded upon the city and other public spaces. 
Design the 3-fold structure and let it to de the rest. 
IBA Emscher Park is a much larger scale than Parc de la Villette. 
The Ruhr area was formerly a center for the steel industry but also 
became vacant in the second half of the 20th century. The 
government launched an IBA (Internationale Bau-Ausstellung) to 
determine what to do, so citizens could profit from it. Took place 
in 1990s. The landscape park Duisburg north competition was 
won by Peter Latz. In a first step he wanted to understand the 
territory. He mapped it as a manmade landscape with railways, 
deposit hills and the canal. He implements a new set of walkways along the 
railways, the canal and to connect the site to the city. He thinks after doing this, his 
job is done and it will develop itself. He supplies the structural elements and inside 
the created landscape chambers others could develop a project. It led to very 
particular identities, for example in the Blast Furnace Park.
