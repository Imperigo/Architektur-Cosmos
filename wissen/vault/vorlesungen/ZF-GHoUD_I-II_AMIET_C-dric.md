1.01 The History and Theory of the City as a Project 
Context 
This course will talk about the history and theory of the city as a project. Just as designing a 
project also a history can be a design. It starts with a set of premises a set of concepts and 
these will be talked about in this lecture. 
1. The City? 
It is becoming increasingly challenging to delineate the boundaries of cities in the era of 
planetary urbanization (Brenner & Katsikis, 2014), which impacts the entire globe. With over 
50% of the world's population residing in urban areas, the question arises: what defines a city? 
Various perspectives have attempted to answer this question, reflecting the multifaceted nature 
of urban life. 
Lefebvre, in "La révolution urbaine" (1970), approached the definition of the city from a 
sociological standpoint, emphasizing the aggregation of masses and the anonymity inherent in 
urban environments. Pirenne, in "Les Villes et les institutions urbaines" (1939), viewed the city 
through an economic lens, focusing on commerce, exchange, and trade as defining features. 
Venturi, in "Learning from Las Vegas" (1972), questioned whether a place like Las Vegas, 
primarily known for gambling, could truly be considered a city. Meanwhile, Koolhaas, in 
"Delirious New York" (1978), characterized the city by hyper-density and the layering of diverse 
programs. 
Contrary to Venturi and Koolhaas, some argue that cities are self-organizing entities, evolving 
without overarching conceptual frameworks. They perceive the city as akin to an organism, 
continually adapting and developing autonomously. 
2. A Manmade Material and Spatial Project 
This course proposes that the city is not a self organizing entity that we can’t steer but that the 
city is a manmade material and spacial project. 
Hypothesis: 
The city is the result of a combination of willful acts that can be uncovered, mapped, 
understood and explained. Cities are no incidental occurrences, they are the material result of 
intentional decisions and processes. Along with other parties such as politicians, investors and 
citizens, architects and urban designers play a crucial role.

One of the earliest known descriptions of the city is the "Forma Urbis" map, created between 
203-211 AD, following a major fire in Rome. The pope decided to commission a large marble 
map of the city, which depicted buildings and open spaces. By examining these maps, we 
perceive the city as an artifact comprised of simple entities such as public and private spaces. 
In the map of Rome, we observe that the city already consisted of these two types, but we 
recognize their dynamic nature over time. The city can be likened to a palimpsest, with each era 
adding new elements. Some buildings endure for extended periods, while others are destroyed 
or rebuilt. However, even at the time of the "Forma Urbis," there was a keen interest in 
understanding the city. 
Describing the evolution of the city and comprehending its various components intrigued early 
architects. In his ten books on architecture (De Architectura Libri Decem, 25 BC), Vitruvius 
discusses materiality, building types, typologies, infrastructure, and more. According to 
Vitruvius, the city comprises "Fabrica," emerging from physical actions like construction, 
transformation, and rebuilding, as well as "Ratiocinatio," arising from thought and ideas. 
Another significant European thinker addressing the city in the 16th century was Leon Battista 
Alberti. Alberti expands on Vitruvius's ideas, considering the city as a project (De Re 
Aedificatoria, 1541). He views the city project not only at a large scale but starting at the 
individual house, where ideas of the larger city should be integrated. Alberti sees the city as a 
constant mediation between the past and the future, emphasizing the importance of 
understanding the present to envision the future. He provides a detailed description of Rome to 
facilitate a deeper understanding of the city. 
Alberti's ideas would influence subsequent thinkers, including Pope Sixtus V, who, in 1588, 
devised a plan for Rome focused not on everyday fabric but on monuments and their 
connections. He believed that reimagining Rome's monuments and their connections could 
reshape the city. Starting with existing monuments, he projected their significance into the 
future, envisioning a new configuration for the city. 
3. Urban Design as Engagement 
Understanding the city as a project of Fabrica and Ratiocinatio prompts the question: what is its 
essence? Urban design involves a reflective dialogue with the urban environment. Urban 
designers and architects immerse themselves in material culture, drawing inspiration primarily 
from tangible elements. Urban design serves as a reflection of specific circumstances, 
encompassing various dimensions.

The Ground: 
Urban design is fundamentally grounded in the physical terrain. Measuring and understanding 
the topography is the initial step in urban design. Many cities, like Constantinople/Istanbul, are 
shaped by their topographical features. For instance, the city's location surrounded by water 
dictated the placement of city walls and the arrangement of important buildings. Topography 
often influences defense strategies and city organization. The development and extension of 
cities are also influenced by topographical characteristics, such as Cairo's elongated form, 
determined by its valley location and connection to the Nile River. The quality of soil further 
impacts urban design decisions, as seen in cities like Amsterdam, built on swampy ground, and 
Berlin, characterized by sandy soil. 
Materials: 
Urban design responds to available materials. Despite socioeconomic disparities, cities like 
Bogota exhibit uniformity in building materials, such as local brick. Historical and contemporary 
extensions in cities like Bath maintain consistency through the use of locally sourced limestone. 
Climate: 
Climate significantly shapes urban design. Considerations like temperature extremes in 
Montreal prompted the construction of underground networks to navigate the city during harsh 
winters. Similarly, in Bologna, hot weather conditions led to the development of galleries 
providing protection from rain or sun. 
Culture: 
Urban design reflects cultural nuances. For instance, Parisian boulevards emerged to 
accommodate the bourgeois class for public representation. 
Philosophy: 
Philosophical ideologies, like Plato's concept of a just city, influence urban design principles. 
Plato's vision materialized in cities like Miletus, where access to both urban amenities and 
natural landscapes was prioritized. 
Religion: 
Religious beliefs often dictate urban layout, as seen in Baghdad, where central placement of 
religious and government buildings symbolizes cultural reverence for religion. 
Politics: 
Political ideologies shape urban infrastructure, exemplified by Berlin's Stalinallee, constructed 
as a socialist showcase and political platform.

Social: 
Urban design responds to societal needs and dynamics. Barcelona's urban redesign in the early

Economic: 
Economic factors drive urban development, impacting both positive and negative urban

4. The Project of History and Theory 
There are various perspectives through which the city can be examined: 
The City as Experience: 
Examining the city as an experience has a longstanding tradition. Descriptions help to 
comprehend and compare different facets of cities, focusing on how people live and the 
emotions they evoke. 
The City as Organism: 
From an organic standpoint, the city can be perceived as one large organism. Every actor within 
it plays a vital role, with interconnectedness between built and natural elements, as well as 
human and non-human factors. 
The City as a Political Archive: 
Another lens to view the city is as an archive of political decisions. Political choices shape the 
evolution of regions or the city as a whole, influencing aspects like the distribution of wealth, the 
development of neighborhoods, and overall social inequality. 
An Architectural History: 
The city also serves as an archive of architectural history. Its development can be traced 
through the lens of architectural evolution and interventions. The interrelation of architecture 
with specific time periods can be observed in the remaining architectural remnants, offering 
insights into the architectural trends and styles prevalent during those times.

1.02 Of Rituals, Water and Mud: The Urban Revolution 
in Mesopotamia and the Indus Valley 
Context 
According to the social geographer Gordon Childe, an "Urban Revolution" occurred from the 9th 
millennium BC onwards, independently in six parts of the world (Mesoamerica, Andes, Egypt, 
Mesopotamia, Indus & China). This revolution was characterized by vast changes, including the 
emergence of new types of settlements. Childe connects this urban revolution to the Neolithic 
revolution, which brought technological advancements such as new irrigation methods and 
tools, leading to faster harvesting. Additionally, it facilitated the domestication of wild plants and 
animals, marking the transition from hunting and gathering to agriculture. This shift enabled a 
more sedentary lifestyle and introduced a cyclical concept of time based on plant harvests and 
agricultural cycles. 
According to Childe, the Neolithic revolution created a resource surplus that could be traded, 
allowing for investment in settlements. Moreover, it generated not only an economic surplus but 
also a social surplus. This surplus fostered mutual care among people, facilitated knowledge 
sharing and learning, and promoted the development of crafts. Consequently, this cultural 
surplus led to the creation of new artifacts. 
1. The Early Settlements of Southwest Asia 
Sedentary Life and the New Concept of Time 
This section looks into Protourban settlements, which exhibit elements of cities but do not fully 
qualify as cities yet. 
Göbekli Tepe: 
Established around 9000 BC in present-day Turkey, Göbekli Tepe was significantly influenced 
by its topography, situated on a hill. The city featured large oval gathering spaces, used for 
rituals that marked the passage of time, such as celebrating harvests. These ritual areas were 
constructed using mud bricks made out of on-site materials and were adorned with columns 
made of natural stone to support roofs. Decorative elements reflecting the domestication of 
plants and animals adorned these spaces. The emphasis on public gatherings highlights the 
collective nature of the city, a characteristic that persists to this day. 
Catal Hüyük: 
Dating back to 7400 BC in present-day Turkey, Catal Hüyük was a city driven by trade,

2. The Bronze Age Cities of Mesopotamia 
Mesopotamia, situated between the rivers Euphrates and Tigris, witnessed the emergence of 
numerous cities from around 5000 BC onward. This region, also known as Sumeria, gave rise to 
Sumerian cities, no longer mere protourban settlements but bustling centers inhabited by a few 
thousand people (ranging from 5000 to 50,000 inhabitants). The fertility of the land for 
agriculture and the presence of metals like copper spurred technological advancements, 
facilitating trade routes that extended even to present-day Turkey. These factors contributed to 
the flourishing of the region. Notably, the development of canal systems for controlled irrigation 
of agricultural land marked a significant technological leap. The emergence of cities in 
Mesopotamia was intricately linked to agriculture and trade. Sumerian cities were deeply 
intertwined with religion; each city, while autonomous and self-governing, was dedicated to a 
particular god or goddess, with the temple serving as the central element rather than a space 
for communal gatherings. 
 
Uruk, a walled Sumerian city dating back to around 3000 BC, featured a prominent religious 
precinct at its core, dedicated to a goddess and serving as the seat of governance. Surrounding 
this central district were residential areas, constructed predominantly from locally sourced mud 
bricks reinforced with straw. These mud bricks were used not only for walls but also for floors, 
roofs, statues, and decorative elements, necessitating constant repair and reconstruction due to 
the material's fragility and susceptibility to rain damage. This cycle of rebuilding led to the

1.03 The Idea of the Polis: Rome, Greece and Beyond 
Context 
This lecture takes a significant leap in time, jumping from a few millennia before Christ to a few 
hundred years before Christ, exploring how both the Greek and Roman empires shaped the 
concept of the city with its distinct components. Urban design played a complicit role in empire 
and colonialism, not merely facilitating the exchange of ideas but often intertwined with colonial 
endeavors. It became a tool for imposing governance and values, including urban design 
principles, on other regions or peoples perceived as a mission by those in power. 
1. From Agora to Stoa 
To understand the Greek Empire, we must comprehend its distinct periods: the Archaic Period 
(7th to mid-5th century BC), the Classical Period (mid-5th century to the end of the 4th century 
BC), and the Hellenistic Period (end of the 4th century BC to the 1st century BC). 
During the Archaic Period, Greece was fragmented into smaller entities ruled by individual 
leaders. While these rulers established settlements, these cannot be classified as cities; rather, 
they predominantly consisted of palaces and reflected hierarchical structures. 
In the Classical Period, Greeks began colonizing regions around the Mediterranean Sea, 
prompting the establishment of new settlements and cities to assert dominance in their 
territories. Athens, initially a palace converted into a settlement, witnessed the emergence of a 
new urban feature known as the Agora. The Agora gradually developed in Athens from around 
500 BC to its final form by approximately 100 BC. Positioned along the Panatenaic way, the 
Agora featured temples and notable buildings, including the Bouleuterion, where the Senate 
convened in a U-shaped formation symbolizing democratic assembly and discuss. Next to the 
Bouleuterion, other structures like the Tholos, serving as a dining and sleeping area for 
senators, and the Metroon, functioning as both a religious space and an archive for city 
governance, began to emerge. The Agora served as a public space at the heart of the city, 
housing its political center, and was interconnected by the monumental and ornate Stoa, a 
colonnaded portico fostering cohesion among Agora buildings. The Stoa evolved into a 
significant social hub where people gathered for discussions and interactions. 
Later plans of the Agora incorporated additional elements, expanding beyond political to 
commercial functions. The southern square or Stoa was utilized for trade, with the nearby

Context 
The late 18th and early 19th centuries marked a period of significant technological and 
scientific progress, particularly with the advent of engines driving industrialization. Steampowered factories began to emerge across Europe, concentrated hubs of employment with a 
high demand for labor. This industrial boom also saw the rise of railroad lines, facilitating rapid 
transportation of people and goods and reshaping the European landscape, thereby impacting 
urban design. 
Many cities experienced explosive urban growth during this period, straining urban 
infrastructure and resulting in unsanitary living conditions. This situation garnered criticism, 
notably from Friedrich Engels in his 1845 work "Die Lage der arbeitenden Klasse in England," 
where he highlighted the dire living conditions and heightened mortality rates among the 
working class in cities. Similarly, Gustave Doré's illustrations depicted the tragic urban realities 
of the time. 
Amongst concerns about living conditions and disease, the Miasma theory emerged, attributing 
diseases to "bad air" in cities, influencing urban design practices. Engels and Doré's critiques 
spurred three distinct attitudes toward industrial society in urban design: 
1. Radical Utopianism: There is no way out of this crisis. Advocates for building human 
civilization anew, outside of industrial society, to achieve ideal social and environmental 
conditions. 
2. Reform: Believes in the potential improvement of social and environmental conditions 
through legislative measures and large-scale public works, initiating a tradition of urban 
transformation within existing cities. 
3. Continuity of Traditions: Embraces the continuity of tradition, prioritizing handicrafts and 
aesthetics over industrialization, and seeks to move away from the industrial civilization 
paradigm.

Context 
In the 19th century, the spread of industrialization emerged, starting in the UK and

1. A Collective Model of Urbanisation: Ebenezer Howard 
Ebenezer Howard (1850-1928) was an intellectual and writer who developed a new model of 
urbanization. In his 1898 publication "To-Morrow: A Peaceful Path to Real Reform," Howard 
suggested that as economies reached their limits, so too might our urbanization model, 
necessitating exploration of new methods for city expansion. During a visit to the Riverside 
neighborhood designed by Olmsted in Chicago, Howard drew inspiration, as well as from social 
concepts presented in publications like the socialist journal by William Morris. 
Howard introduced a new urbanization model, aiming to merge individualism and collectivism 
under the banner of "social individualism." This concept proposed that every family could have 
its own garden while fostering community through collective land ownership. 
Central to Howard's vision were the "three magnets": the Town, offering social opportunities but 
dense with little green space; the Country, abundant in nature but lacking in societal 
interactions; and the Town-Country, combining the best of both. Howard proposed a Town-City 
called the "Garden-City."

Context 
This lecture addresses the late 1950s and 1960s, a period marked by the realization of

criticism for the perceived lack of quality in housing, the growing anonymity within modernist 
structures, and their inability to adapt over time. This critique came from residents, cultural 
observers, and sociologists, and even emerged within the modernist movement itself,

1. Revised Typologies for the City 
One alternative approach to modernist master planning involves revising the main typologies 
used in modernist urban design. This shift was notably influenced by architects Alison and Peter 
Smithson, whose ideas had a significant impact on urban design during that period. Among 
their influential projects was the "Golden Lane Housing" project in 1953, situated on a bombed 
site in London. Here, the Smithsons proposed a reimagined version of the housing slab, 
incorporating a series of "streets in the air" on multiple levels within the building. These 
continuous corridor elements were designed to mimic streets in size and function. Rather than 
simple slabs, the blocks took on a branched format. The Smithsons envisioned extending this 
branching structure throughout the entire city, similar to a tree intersecting the existing street 
pattern. This concept remained theoretical.

Economist Building in central London in 1964. Here, they proposed integrating the modernist 
tower typology into the dense urban fabric of the historical 19th-century city, departing from the 
convention of locating such towers in green open spaces. This example illustrated that modern 
typologies need not be incompatible with the historical city but can instead be blended and 
incorporated into it.

4. Agency in Housing: Between Supports and Infill

1. Architecture ans Territory 
Of course, urban designers reacted to this criticism. In response, urban planners began to use 
the territory as a reference for urban design, shifting away from focusing solely on the existing 
city. Vittorio Gregotti, an Italian architect, played a significant role in this approach. He 
emphasized the need to expand the scope of architecture beyond the confines of the historical 
city to engage with the broader territory. According to him, the role of the urban designer is to 
situate urban planning within this wider context, considering factors such as topography, 
culture, agriculture, and infrastructure. 
Gregotti exemplified this approach in his design for the University of Calabria in 1972. The 
university town, stretching over 3km, was strategically positioned between two major 
infrastructural elements: a highway and a railroad. Leveraging the topographical conditions of 
the territory, Gregotti developed the project. A central spine-like bridge connected the various 
buildings on the site, serving as a constant element while the buildings were situated in relation 
to the natural contours of the land.

asserting that the historical center could serve as a foundation for new urban design 
approaches. Rather than viewing modern design as diametrically opposed to historical 
urbanism, Muratori proposed that modern approaches could draw lessons from historical cities. 
His studies revealed that while buildings evolved over time, certain parts or elements endured, 
indicating a continuity between the past and future. Thus, the past inherently encompasses the

Collective Memory and Urban Artifacts 
Similar to Muratori, the Italian architect Aldo Rossi was deeply interested in the history of cities. 
In 1966, he published his influential book "L'architettura della città," where he posited that the

instance, the Piazza Navona in Rome retains the shape of the Roman structure that once 
occupied the site. Rossi contends that the city itself is an artifact, expressing varied social, 
economic, and cultural realities throughout history. This artifact maintains a certain form and 
material stability, contributing to the city's identity and collective memory. Urban artifacts play a 
crucial role in shaping the collective memory of a city's inhabitants, becoming integral parts of 
their shared heritage.

Oswald Mattias Ungers also delved into typology, becoming highly influential as a German 
architect. He introduced the notion of Grossform into urban design in 1966 with his book 
"Grossformen im Wohnungsbau." His approach revolves around the idea that we can no longer 
develop the city at a large scale due to its rapid urbanization and dynamic nature. Instead, he 
advocates for intervention at an intermediate scale—the scale of the block. Grossform 
represents the scale of intervention between a single object and the entire city, embodying a 
distinct identity within the urban fabric.
