What was the ideal city for the twentieth century? 
The city that best expressed the power and beauty 
of modern technology and the enlightened ideas of 
social justice? Le Corbusier, Ludwig Hilberseimer, 
and Frank Lloyd Wright all tried to answer this question. Deeply fearful of the consequences for civilization if the old cities, with all the social conflict and 
miseries that they embodied, were allowed to persist, all three devised new, utopian plans for the city 
of the twentieth century.
The Green City
One of the most remarkable urban designers of the 
twentieth century city was the Swiss architect Le 
Corbusier, for whom envisioning a new city equaled 
imagining a new society. In response to the great 
devastation of the First World War, he conceived in 
1914 the basic dwelling cell for a new urban society: 
the Dom-Ino house. Named after a portmanteau of 
‘domus’ and ‘innovation’, this standardized two-story 
house was made of reinforced concrete slabs supported on columns and a staircase. The idea was that 
this would allow the local population to reconstruct 
their dwelling space according to their own means 
and needs. The name ‘Dom-Ino’ was also derived 
from the popular game, and suggested that single 
houses could be connected in a variety of ways, just 
like dominoes, to form new urban ensembles. 
Le Corbusier was convinced that the future dwelling 
cell—and, by extension, the future city—would be 
influenced by the principles of a mechanized society. The 1920 Citrohan house project announced a 
series of investigations into ‘machines à habiter’, for 
which Le Corbusier used his “five points for a new 
architecture”: columns, flat roofs, horizontal windows, free plan, free façade. The ‘Immeuble Villas’, 
which can be considered as a development of the 
‘Citrohan house’ and its integration into a collective 
context, was the closing element of Le Corbusier’s 
urban vision. In reaction to the French Loi Loucheur 
law, which promoted single family houses, Le Corbusier promoted a collective dwelling typology in 
which the privacy of the single family was celebrated. This new typology was based on Le Corbusier’s 
study of the Charterhouse of Galluzzo in Val d‘Ema, 
near Florence, where monks lived in two-story, L-
shaped cells with a private garden, while sharing 
collective utilities. In 1925, at the Exposition des Arts 
Décoratifs (Paris), Le Corbusier had the opportunity 
to create a full-scale example of one of the cells of 
his ‘Immeuble Villas’: The Pavillon de l‘Esprit Nouveau.
At the occasion of the Salon d’Automne (Paris, 
1922), Le Corbusier presented the project ‘Ville contemporaine de trois millions d‘habitants’, which demonstrated his main urban ambitions: decongestion 
of city centers, increase of density, enlargement of 
the means of circulation, and extension of the landscaped areas. As a reaction, he defined a set of 
theoretical urban principles: systematic zoning (distribution of activities in distinct zones), reflected in 
a diversity of urban forms, and separation of traffic. 
The Ville contemporaine was a radiocentric city, without a specific place or well-defined boundaries. Its 
center was made up of a business district, and surrounded by residential areas. These different areas 
were connected by a dense network of orthogonal 
and diagonal roads. The main public domain in this 
new city would no longer be the traditional ‘corridor 
street’, but rather a green urban landscape in which 
individuals, irrespective of their social standing, 
gender, or age, could explore the city and encounter other citizens.
In 1924, Le Corbusier demonstrated his urban concepts in the commission of a new workers’ neighborhood for the industrialist Henri Frugès: the Quartiers 
Modernes Henry-Frugès in Pessac, near Bordeaux. 
Though the 51 houses were based on a standard 
module of 5 by 5 meters, they resulted in different 
housing types called zig-zag, quincunx, skyscraper, arcade, detached house, and twin houses. With 
their terraced roofs, pure geometric forms, modular 
construction, and polychromic façades, the houses

The Vertical Metropolis
In 1924, after carefully studying Le Corbusier’s 
project, the German architect Ludwig Hilberseimer 
concluded that Le Corbusier’s ‘Ville Contemporaine’ 
was not radical enough. Although it proposed good 
spatial qualities, it did not solve the traffic problem, 
nor did it sufficiently plan for future growth and flexibility. As an alternative, he proposed a Highrise 
City for one million inhabitants which, instead of 
horizontal zoning, proposed vertical zoning. It was 
built up out of a series of identical block-slabs. The 
bottom five stories of these structures would contain mainly commercial programs, while the top 15 
stories were to offer mostly housing. In his Highrise 
City proposal, Hilberseimer took grade separation to 
the extreme, and proposed to place public transport 
underground, vehicular traffic on ground level, and 
pedestrian traffic on sidewalks on the sixth floor.
Urbanity without a City
Le Corbusier’s work also had a major influence on 
the American architect Frank Lloyd Wright, who responded to the former’s ‘Ville Contemporaine’ scheme with his very own ‘Broadacre City’ proposal. This 
proposal was inspired by a book that Wright published in 1932, entitled The Disappearing City, which 
was essentially a manifesto against centralized city 
development and which advocated for the total decentralization of cities. In the opening paragraph of 
this book, Wright stated that “the value of this earth, 
as man’s heritage, is pretty far gone from him now in 
the cities centralization has built. And centralization 
has over-built them all. Such urban happiness as the 
properly citified citizen knows consists in the warmth 
and pressure of the approbation of the crowd.” Acfunctioned as the basic elements of a life-size laboratory in which the architect tested his innovative urban ideas. Despite the ambitions of Frugès and Le 
Corbusier, however, the project was not a success. 
The bold modernist architecture was considered too 
avant-garde, and many workers refused to inhabit 
the apartments. Eventually, most of the structures to 
fell into disrepair and were abandoned. 
At the 1925 Exposition des Arts Décoratifs (Paris), 
Le Corbusier presented the so-called ‘Plan Voisin’, 
which was a study of his urban principles projected 
on the city center of Paris. Le Corbusier opposed 
the idea of building a new administrative city in the 
peripheral area of La Defense, and instead made an 
alternative proposal to locate the city center at the 
foot of Montmartre. All the existing buildings were 
to be demolished, with the exception of the churches and gates of Saint-Denis and Saint-Martin, and 
replaced by a 240-hectare business neighborhood 
with 18 cruciform skyscrapers as well as a residential neighborhood. The new city was structured by two 
new traffic arteries which cut across the city and its 
suburbs, and connected Paris to the major French 
and European cities. The intersection of these two 
avenues constituted the center of the city. 
In 1930, Le Corbusier further developed his urban 
ideas in his project for the ‘Ville Radieuse’ or Radiant 
City; a linear city as a blueprint for spatial and social 
reform. The design maintained the idea of high-rise housing blocks, free circulation, and abundant 
green spaces proposed in his earlier work. The 
functions are again clearly separated, but the clearly 
delineated radiocentric urban layout is replaced by 
a linear scheme—composed of a business district, 
housing neighborhoods, and industrial quarters—
that allows for gradual urban extension, as an effective response to both social and economic changes. 
The project was first presented at the 3rd Congres 
Internationaux d’Architecture Moderne (CIAM) in 
Brussels in 1930. In 1933, Le Corbusier defined the 
‘Athens Charter’, a manifesto which summarized 
the 4th CIAM, which took place in 1932, mostly aboard a passenger ship which sailed from Marseilles, 
France, to Athens, Greece, and back again. It was 
essentially a condensed version of the core ideas 
and principles of modern urban planning, which 
called for a total remaking of cities in the industrial 
world, in order to make them more efficient, rational, 
and hygienic. 
The Concentrated City
Le Corbusier’s urban design activities did not remain 
limited to the European continent, but also involved 
cities in Africa and South America. In his plans for 
Rio de Janeiro (1929), inspired by his flights over 
South America, Le Corbusier started to explore in 
greater detail the relation of the new city to existing 
landscape features, but also to existing patterns of 
dwelling and building. For Le Corbusier, the new city 
is a cultural construct with its own space and time. 
Most interesting in the sketches that he made for 
Rio (and other cities in Latin America) is a departure 
away from the geometric spatial distribution of his 
earlier planning work. In these designs, Cartesian 
spatiality was replaced by linear and sinuous urban 
development.
In 1931, he developed the linear megastructure of 
the Rio proposal as a plan for Algiers, suggesting 
that the new elevated coastal expressway could be 
infilled with housing below, leading to a new business center in a pair of towers adjacent to the old 
Arab Kasbah, which would be preserved. On the 
slopes above the old city would be tall interconnected curving housing slabs set in green areas. These housing units on the slopes above the old city 
(near the Fort l’Empereur) were to accommodate 
approximately a quarter of a million people, and

from all of Le Corbusier’s urban schemes—in fact, it

‘Broadacre City’ did not try to provide an ideal city 
model, nor was it intended as an alternative to existing cities. It was predicated on the belief that the 
modern city had become obsolete in the age of the 
telephone and the car, and that it would cease to 
exist. ‘Broadacre City’ was thus essentially anti-urban in its conception. Following his ‘Broadacre City’ 
model, Wright proposed to transform the United 
States into a nation of independent farmers and proprietors by giving each citizen a one-acre (0.4 hectare) plot of land from the US federal land reserves, 
on which they could grow their own food and become self-sufficient. This way, a new community would 
arise, which Wright called a ‘Usonian’ community. A 
new road system would allow for easy automobile 
access over a large and potentially almost infinitelyextendable area. At the same time, services and cultural institutions would not be clustered into a center, 
but would instead be spaced at half-mile (0.8-kilometer) intervals along the roadways.
Wright’s ‘Broadacre City’ was, evidently, never realized. However, a small experimental settlement was 
built in the 1940s, which sought to prove the validity of the concept. In 1945, a cooperative of young 
couples from New York City purchased a 100-acre 
rural tract of land in Pleasantville (NY), and enlisted 
Wright to plan the settlement. Wright decided where 
each house should be placed, and designed three of 
the homes himself. These homes, known as ‘Usonian homes’, clearly demonstrated Wright’s ideas that 
people should live in closer connection to nature—
all ‘Usonian homes’ have a strong visual connection 
between the interior and exterior spaces—and that 
the construction of a home should be simple and 
economical (many were set out on a grid), so that 
each family could, to some extent, construct their 
own housing.
