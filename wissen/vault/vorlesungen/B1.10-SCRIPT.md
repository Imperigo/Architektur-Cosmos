The Extension of the European 
City and Beyond: 
From the Viennese Ringstrasse to 
Amsterdam Zuid and New Delhi
The late 19th century was a period of great change in 
continental Europe. The Industrial Revolution not only 
transformed the continent’s mode of production and its 
patterns of work, it also greatly accelerated urbanization. Cities grew rapidly as large numbers of immigrants from rural areas were in search of industrial jobs, 
transforming a rural into an urban society.
Rapid population growth overwhelmed the capacity 
of early 19th century municipal governments to provide for the needs of their new citizens. Many European 
cities remained hemmed in by medieval fortifications, 
which were conceived to protect smaller populations 
from military attack. Urban development was also 
constrained by the almost complete absence of public transportation, which forced people to live close to 
their jobs so that they could be reached by foot. This 
led to increasingly dense patterns of residence. Additional floors were added to existing buildings, spacious 
apartments were subdivided into many smaller living 
units, inferior dwellings were erected in courtyards and 
other open spaces.
The resulting urban density produced serious health 
and social problems. In addition, the movement of 
people and goods became a problem in the streets 
of 19th century cities. Lacking sidewalks, pedestrians 
competed with horse-drawn vehicles for the opportunity to move through cramped streets wet with household waste water and soiled by horse droppings.
After 1850 European societies attempted to systematically engage with the problems of urban living. Cities 
like Barcelona, Vienna, Amsterdam and Helsinki would 
conceive major extension plans. These plans not only 
addressed the growth of the city but also articulated 
a new urbanity. They offered proposals for a radical 
transformation of city life and produced a modern pattern of urban living.
Several 19th century developments made these important transformation possible. The power of central 
governments grew everywhere, which offered the state 
not only the ability to command public resources in the 
form of taxes but also provided the personnel to undertake the first modern urban planning. The Industrial 
Revolution played also a major role in the urban transformation. It created new technologies and modes of 
transportation whose application would improve urban 
life.
The result was the emergence of new urban patterns 
across the European continent, which departed from 
the logics of the historical city and introduced the urbanity of the Großstadt and the metropole.
In India the British colonial government decided to 
move the capital to Delhi, which required a vast extension of the existing walled town. The British architect 
Edwin Lutyens was selected to advise on the conception of the new Indian capital at Delhi. His plan, with 
a central mall and diagonal avenues, may have owed 
something to the City Beautiful plan of Burnham, Mc 
Kim and Olmsted for Washington, but the total result 
was quite different: a monumental garden-city pattern, 
based on a series of hexagons separated by broad 
avenues with double lines of trees.
