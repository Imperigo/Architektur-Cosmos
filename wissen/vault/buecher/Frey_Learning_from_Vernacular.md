Dear Pierre,

Reading your text, there was not a single word to which I could not subscribe.
Tabulating forms of vernacular architecture took up a fair part of my architectural training and today the building site is at the heart of my daily practice.

Ilooked in the dictionary. It tells me that the term “vernacular” comes from
the Latin vernaculus, meaning indigenous, domestic, while verna refers to
“aslave born in the house”: Thus by recognising vernacular cultures, we would
first and foremost be restoring to peoples reduced to slavery their place in
the common abode of human history.

Then Ithought about the movement of knowledge:

how humans are nomadic and knowledge is mobile.

The first phase of globalisation was dangerous and undermined artistic
traditions. But seen from the perspective of nomadic knowledge, it can open
up new possibilities. Developing countries have been forced to work out pragmatic strategies; now, in an unexpected reversal, the Western world may come
to find in them models allowing it to overcome the social, environmental
and cultural crisis it is experiencing. It took time to find this route. It needed
scholarship and history before the West was able to perceive and acknowledge
forms of architecture without architects; this process is a political reversal
of colonisation.

Ihave always felt that the materialisation of architecture takes place not
in the drawing but in the construction. Otherwise, architecture is meaningless. Vernacular architecture, born in situ and fashioned by local constraints,
is more contextual by definition. The fact that it arises out of what is permanent rather than from a given event is precisely its strength and its fragility.

The lessons that you learn from the study of vernacular architecture
challenge our modes of producing the building and the built. For my part,
what I perceive in the current situation is a loss of knowledge and authority
in construction companies. In the past, the production ofthese enterprises
was like industrial production and depended on permanent staff. Today,
companies win markets without having the workforce or the competence
required. Subcontracting and temping have increased to the point where
the building workforce is constituted on the very day when the building-site
opens and those who belong to that workforce know neither the productionmode nor the philosophy ofthe company that recruited them. In my own
experience, at least halfthe time the tasks required of building workers
do not match their skills. A joiner puts up plasterboard, a locksmith installs
sanitation.

## S. 8

Faced with this reality, Ithought: since society can only supply me with
a dislocated build-force, why should Inot constitute my own? So on my construction sites, the first thing to be made is the site-house, made of reject
materials given by entrepreneurs. I call it “a shelter for democracy”, collectively
self-constructed by the entrepreneurs who bring the materials, the workers
who construct their own place and the architect who accompanies them. This
is the pioneering house on the basis of which we create the collective that
will produce the project. From that point on, the situation is very different:
there is no longer any plan, we remake the project together to match the constructive capacity available to us. This is when forms of knowledge appear,
the desire to build is reborn and we can finally associate the client with it.
Those who transform the project are those who will construct it.

When discussion takes place between architect and client, itisnot a debate
between equals: the architect attempts to impose his knowledge on someone
who does not understand. But when the client meets the constructor, a sort of
respect is established. Knowledge may have disappeared but the construction site remains that fertile moment when the desired object can at last be
materialised.

Your objection is this: on the ‘real’ building sites ofthe globalised world,
any possibility of reconstituting the buildforce has been strenuously and
meticulously eliminated. Today’s economy, you say, needs two kind of
worker: a small number of highly qualified technicians capable of exercising
power, and a vast number of interchangeable workers who have no notion
of what they are making. I believe that this globalised construction site, of
which Dubai is the most extreme consequence, will self-destruct. When it
does, my chosen method is auto-production building, which Iam currently
applying to social housing. On the five sites in progress, Ihave done no more
than accompany people who have spare time; their living conditions are too
modest for them to have access to ownership or subsidised housing but they
can invest their time, practical knowledge and labour power.

I believe in training on the job, by imitation and experiment. The building
worker is nowadays reduced to a kind of modern slavery by the global building site. Transforming our building sites into “vernacular” sites and restoring
the workers to the heart ofthe abode may deliver a renascence ofthe construction worker.

Patrick Bouchain

The Building Site Starved of Creative Work Observers would have us
believe that many traditional forms of construction were abandoned because
they required a large work force for both building and maintenance. They
are said to have been rejected as irremediably obsolescent - and are indeed
denounced as such. The hegemonic development discourse describes them
as unable to compete on cost with industrial production, which is presented
as the outcome of a rational system. On this interpretation, we face a ‘Darwinian’ process of natural selection that dooms the ancestral and traditional
modes of production of the built environment.

If we consider that, at the same time, in the same regions or countries,
capital to finance the products ofthe world construction industry is completely
lacking or available only at the price of international loans that come with
conditions set by the IMF or World Bank and if we add that unemployment is
endemic in these places, the picture before us is simply astonishing: it isthe
world turned upside down. The consequences are easy to predict. The native
workforce has nothing to do, falls away, loses its skills and finally joins the
lumpenproletariat flooding into the cities. Lacking all hope, obsessed by the
inaccessible civilisation ofthe commodity, it abandons traditional modes of
production along with the shelters and buildings that pertain to them though
it has nothing to put in their place.

## S. 12

From the niche market to a new vernacular space By spotlighting a
number of beautiful, impressive and very promising examples, I should like
to refute the far from disinterested notion that “there is no alternative” to
the dominant model. Many talented architects are now turning away from
the global scene of “architecture-as-spectacle”, restoring the dignity oftheir
art while creating works that renew its vocabulary and reintegrate the immense formal and constructive diversity inherent in the vernacular architecture ofthe past. A lovely fable by the Finnish author Arto Paasilinna
stimulated my thinking about these issues. In his novel, Canticle ofa Joyous
Apocalypse, he describes acommunity serving out its “penitence” for the
ravages caused by the social and political conflicts that pervaded Finland
during the twentieth century. That community is seeking to found a sort of
vernacular heterotopia; it builds a log-cabin cathedral and develops around
it asociety capable of resisting the moral and ecological prostration of
our globalised world. Paasilinna’s novel might be read as an oneiric and
anachronistic reaction to Viollet-le-Duc’s Histoire d’un hotel de ville et d’une
cathedrale [The Story of a Town Hall and a Cathedral]. It is also intermittently reminiscent of Fernand Pouillon’s Pierres sauvages [The Stones of
the Abbey]. Allthree narratives set out the historical and social conditions
from which a new architecture emerges. They are my evidence for the
idea that architecture very precisely reflects the condition and values of
a society.

The impact of economic logic on the production of “labour-saving”
building In the great building-sites of our time, an increasing proportion of
investment goes into the acquisition and installation of units of high-valueadded equipment. “Sanitary installation”, “kitchen”, “facade component”
and “heating-ventilation” units combine hi-tech, advanced materials and

| discriminating “industrial design”. These units and their installation require
a division oflabour of unprecedented complexity and exclusivity. They are
placed within the shell ofthe building under the supervision oftechnicians
trained in the supplier’s methodology who, by controlling the installation,
ensure that it enters service as it should and thus meets the warranty conditions. These assemblages are supplied in more or less identical fashion
throughout the world and account for a growing proportion ofinvestment
credits. Norms that are determined by specialist associations, which are
more or less directly under the control ofthe manufacturers or of state or
international organisations, drastically reduce freedom of choice.

This phenomenon has a number of undeniably beneficial effects: sophisticated services, reliability, security, maintenance and so on. But the disadvantages are serious too. The system brings in its wake a series of appalling

| side-effects affording abundant proof that for the most part, technological
progress, far from liberating its beneficiaries, at best enslaves them as the

long since suggested dividing the average speed obtained in a private car
by the working time required to finance it and showed that ifthese costs were
included, the driver went no faster than a man on foot.

object central to that industry is known as “design”. So it is worth taking
the time to consider the function of “design”, which Idescribed above as
discriminating, and to remind ourselves how it came to be imposed. The
idea of “design” refers back to the heroic historiography of twentieth-century
modernity, to the Dessau Bauhaus school and the first steps taken’ by architecture after its emancipation from the academic models ofthe nineteenth
century. That blazon of noble ancestry has been somewhat tarnished by
long exposure to every latitude and regime but remains a necessary relic of
the modern cult of “design”. This is a religion nourished by its own propaganda and it has lost nothing of its seductive power.

But that is not in itself enough to explain the hegemonic position that
“design” has carved out for itself over the last twenty years. Reduced to their
functions and the elementary forms that these imply, a chair or fork no
longer have the slightest value in the contemporary global market, whereas
a mere fifty years ago, even in rich countries and among the well-off, these
very objects were moved from house to house, handed down and considered
in the light of goods if not heirlooms. Today they can be sold, if at all, only
on the most impoverished markets. Even there they find themselves in competition with goods paid for by “humanitarian aid”. Forks, chairs, bathrooms,
jacuzzis, kitchens and lifts can no longer be profitably sold on the basis of
their functionality and proven qualities. It is no longer sufficient to demonstrate their “use value”. To avoid an inexorable decline in profit margins,
something more is needed, this supposedly added value that whets the
appetite ofthe consumer, transforming her into a “a member ofthe same
family”, bestowing on her the distinction of “good form” and setting free
in her person the membership labels that will, through the simple fact of
mimetic contamination, make of her a zealous proselyte. And so there comes
about a universal, an almost viral version of Tupperware parties.

The conditions governing the production ofthe built environments are
thus doubly determined. First point: the construction industry, by rationalising and optimising its processes, at one and the same time renders them
uniform and causes a massive displacement oftthe centres of decision-making.
By the end ofthis process, the building site can no longer be described as
the place where building is produced. It is merely the place where components designed and built elsewhere are assembled. Obviously (the point
is widely admitted) this process has a profound impact on the life ofthe
building site. This is the second point. The accentuated division of labour
is reflected in society. On the one hand, we have the highly qualified technicians who are familiar with managerial methodology; on the other, the
literally interchangeable manual workers whose employment is consequently precarious and who are assigned to repetitive and subaltern tasks.

"S" for Sustainable Inzones of prosperity and development, this
“design”, this “good form” has recently acquired a further title to distinction: “sustainability”. Whatever the true environmental impact (properly
researched) of products labelled “sustainable” or “green”, whenever a highvalue-added industrial product is assigned to this category, its chances of
winning a market and generating high profit margins are much improved.
One widely received line of thought has it that the lightness ofa building’s
environmental footprint consists primarily in the sum ofits physicaland
chemical characteristics and the sum of the direct or indirect forms of energy
that its construction consumed. These data can indeed be measured precisely and therefore inscribed in norms in the name of which certification can
be awarded. But this dogmatic vision is at odds with the infinite complexity
ofthe real world, the cumulative character ofthe various parameters and their
invisible dimensions. Certain evaluation charts comprise hundreds of criteria without on that account affording a clear and unarguable vision ofthings.
Consequently this is a field of knowledge wide open to all kinds of slippage
and abuse.

The prosperity enjoyed by “sustainability” has generated a parasitical
dynamic, in which environmental certification has become a source of autonomous profit. The most flagrant example is the American architect William
McDonough, who appropriated the expression “cradle-to-cradle”, launched
during the 1970s by Walter Stahel, and transformed it into a certification
brand whose profits he is seeking to monopolise.*

The subject ofthe ecological footprint ofthe constructed environment
leads us into considerable difficulties. Here the road to Hell is paved with
good intentions. Decryption is needed. The environmental arguments brandished by political ecologists are crudely Malthusian. The power sought
by those who exploit these arguments is based on fear and the illusion of
“supplementary moral dignity”. The genuine and almost certainly reliable
scientific work done by a host of highly specialised researchers is then synthesised by politicians and civil servants. In 1969, this argument was highlighted by Buckminster Fuller who pointed out the risks involved in excessive
scientific specialisation and emphasised the role of data-processing which
would, he said, “make man obsolete as a physical production and control
specialist.”® He concluded with a warning that humankind would have to
re-establish its innate function of comprehension, which he describes as
humanity’s function: that of making judgements based on ethics and a sense
ofresponsibility.

Building eco-systems on a human scale In contrast with this smokescreen, vernacular practices can furnish a marvellous stock of ingenious
devices that bear witness to the spectacular effects available from techniques
that are highly economical in terms of both materials and energy. Some of
these are for use in buildings themselves, conferring, for example, considerable resistance to earthquakes or allowing them to accommodate extreme
temperatures. Others relate to entire portions of a national territory, whose
seemingly inhospitable zones can be rendered habitable. Thus the foggaras
or khettaras of south Algeria and Morocco have given rise to ecosystems
created by humanity. They show that a simple process founded on observation of nature and the environment can bring water to an arid habitat perceived as irremediably hostile and thus develop and maintain the hospitality
ofthe oasis.

These techniques comprise profound, broad-based complementary
knowledge. Think ofthe fact that the date palm is a cultivar selected so asto
thrive and provide fruit in this ‘invented’ environment. The number and
diversity ofthese kinds of knowledge, and still more their quality and efliciency, is related to the fact that they have been built up over the centuries,
transmitted from generation to generation and are available without cost in
the societies that developed them. They remind us that empirical observation, the collection, processing and correlation of large quantities of data are
by no means the appanage of modern scientists and their laboratories. On
the contrary, they underpin all authentic forms of knowledge, including those
of societies organised around oral tradition. They show that such knowledge
can be the basis for human action on the environment, accurately correlate
such action to what the environment can support, and thus permit the rise
of cultures of great vitality and originality.

ecological and social result. The global trend might be to copy the Western
lifestyle making self-sustainable cultures depending on external markets.
I believe in focusing on existing potentials and advantages. To me this seems
to be the most successful development strategy.”

Bangladesh is one of the poorest countries in the world but is nevertheless aland of architecture. In Dhaka, at the instigation ofthe architect
Musrahul Islam, Louis Kahn built some of the biggest (and final) masterpieces ofhis life. The Dhaka Parliament Building sets a scene of water and
sunlight, thus interpreting two elements of which Bangladesh has something of an excess while the Eye Hospital pits materials (brick) and mass
against the oppressive heat.

Anna Hehringer began her first construction in the north ofthe country, lacking everything that she had been taught was essential to twentiethcentury architecture. Instead, she found 400 tons of earth along with
straw, buffalo, bamboo, the future users and the local population. Her work
rediscovered, extended and interpreted traditional techniques. She thus
contrived a safe, comfortable home for that basis of every culture: the primary school.
