PREFACE

Nearly all developing countries in the world
today are facing a serious crisis in housing. About
a quarter of the world's people —- 1,250 million
human beings-do not have adequate shelter and
100 million people have no housing whatsoever.
They dwell on the pavements, the doorways,
under bridges and in the recesses of the public
buildings. The majority of these people live in the
developing countries. The World Bank also
estimated that a quarter of the urban population
in most African and Asian cities cannot afford even
minimal housing. This housing crisis in the Third
World is due to many reasons, one of which
is the adoption of modern conventional housing
approaches.

Such approaches which are based on the
Western context and experience, often fail
although attempts are made to adapt it to local
conditions. They are ill-suited to the local climate
and create social settings and living environments
which are alienating and do not fit the local
culture.

The most devastating effect of such housing
approaches is that they are expensive and deny
the majority of the population, who are poor,
access to housing by concentrating the housing
resources in the hands of a few. This was made

possible firstly by the erosion of confidence in the
traditional housing solutions. Through education
and the media, traditional solutions are portrayed
as primitive and inferior. On the other hand, the
Western housing approaches were applied
internationally as a universal housing solution and
were promoted as superior and modern. The huge
pool of traditional housing knowledge is thus being
gradually discarded in the name of progress.

This book is an attempt to rediscover one of
the indigenous housing forms in the Third World
— the traditional Malay house.

The traditional Malay house is one of the
richest components of Malaysia’s cultural heritage.
Designed and built by ordinary villagers
themselves, it manifests the creative and aesthetic
skills of the Malays.

It created near-perfect solutions to the control
of climate, multifunctional use of space, flexibility
in design and a sophisticated prefabricated system
which can extend the house with the growing
needs of the family.

The traditional Malay house was created in
response to the actual housing needs and
aspirations of the Malays. This explains the perfect
fit of the house to meet their socio-economic,
cultural and environmental requirements.

PREFACE

This book documents the architecture of the
traditional Malay house, giving details of the
various housetypes, the construction system, the
climatic design and the adaptations to the social
and cultural requirements of the Malays. It also
focuses on the self-reliant and autonomous
housing approaches of the traditional Malay house
which are especially appropriate for housing the
poor. The book also examines the forces which are
threatening the existence of the traditional Malay
house, and the changes which are taking place in
the design of the Malay house itself.

Finally the book draws ideas from the
traditional Malay house for the development of a
Malaysian identity in architecture. More
importantly the book points out the vital and
essential role of the Malay house in fulfilling the
present and future housing needs of ordinary
Malaysians.

The study of the traditional Malay house was
initiated when I was a student at the School of
Housing, Building and Planning in Universiti Sains
Malaysia. This continuing interest was intensified
when I did research on it for the ‘Sharing of
Traditional Technology’’ project of the United
Nations University. The project revealed to me the
importance of traditional technologies in the

framework of self-reliant and appropriate
development for developing countries.

INTRODUCTION

The traditional Malay house
which is well adapted to
the environment, climate
and the needs of its users
is today faced with a
serious threat of possible
extinction in the future.

HIS book first aims to create a better

understanding of and to renew confi-

dence in the traditional Malay house — a
house that owes little or nothing to self-conscious
design. A house designed and built by the users
themselves — the Malays in Malaysia — according
to their own needs and with a good understanding
of nature and environment, incorporating and
reflecting their way of life and culture.

The house is undergoing many changes. It is
being vulgarised by modern houseforms and
materials in the attempt by users to adapt itto the
modern situation. More serious is the threat of
extinction caused by the loss of confidence in the
traditional Malay house due to the overglorification of modern westernised houseforms in
Malaysia. The traditional Malay house has lost its
status in the village, replaced by new but not
necessarily better houseforms. Villagers look to
these new houseforms as models and status
symbols without looking again at their traditional
houses for inspiration. At this level the book aims
to stop this trend through showing the sophistication and design superiority of the traditional Malay
house by documenting scientifically its design
principles and its housing processes.

At another level, this book is directed at the
policy-makers, designers, academics, researchers
and the professionals. There has been a continuing search for the Malaysian architectural identity
during the past years but this has been hampered
by many factors, one of which is the lack of documentation and research on traditional Malaysian
houses. It is hoped that this book will help to fill
the vacuum of indigenous architecture studies in
Malaysia, but more importantly to create an
awareness of the quality and relevance of the
traditional Malay house amongst our policymakers, professionals and academics. It is also
hoped that this book will provide an important
source in the search for the Malaysian identity,
not only for the large modern institutional and
commercial buildings, but also for the Malaysian
homes.

THE MALAY HOUSE

Although the traditional Malay house is set
against a rural and Malay ethnic background, it
does not mean that there is nothing to learn from
it for the urban situation and for the other ethnic
groups. On the other hand, the traditional Malay
house is not presented here as a cure-all for our
housing needs and problems.

Certain elements of the study are culturally
and ethnically specific to the Malays, but other
elements relating to the principles of climatic
design, building systems, spatial design, environmental design, community development and
design flexibility can be adapted and applied to
the urban area or to other ethnic situations.

The self-help and mutual-help approaches and
the autonomy of the traditional Malay house,
although set in the rural context, are approaches
which can be used to help house the urban and
rural poor as they are low-cost and self-reliant
approaches of housing development.

Certain basic differences between the urban
and rural contexts need to be identified in order
to provide a clear picture of the setting of the book.
The urban situation has market forces playing a
dominant role in the urban housing processes.
Profit, which means the pushing up of land and
property prices, is the primary motive for modern
private housing development. Housing for the
poor, who have no effective market demand,
becomes a serious social problem.

The dilemma of low-cost public housing is that
it faces the same market forces at work — high
land and material costs and speculation. These
market forces tend to displace the housing needs
of the poor. This means that low-cost public
housing will have to be highly subsidised. High
land cost which is used to justify the far-off
location of low-cost housing also makes low-cost
housing fall into the hands of the rich. The poor,
due to the localised nature of their jobs and their
limited mobility, need housing near their
workplace. Besides, most of the poor also cannot
even afford conventional 'low-cost' houses.

In contrast, the housing process in the rural

areas is more use-oriented rather than marketoriented. Housing is more for shelter rather than
for profit and investment. Housing is more
autonomous here, whereas housing in the urban
areas is more heteronomous, meaning that housing in the rural areas is more self-governed while
housing in the urban areas is controlled and
governed by others. Access to land is easier and
cheaper because of the lower demand and
lesser speculation.

In the urban areas, there is hardly any userinvolvement in the housing process while in the
rural areas, the user-involvement and decisionmaking in housing are important. It starts
from the design stagestothe final stages of house
construction. The middlemen of the urban housing process (the housing developer and the
professionals) are, in most cases, removed in the
housing processes in the rural areas. The user
himself becomes the housing developer and the
professional's role is often aptly taken over by the
traditional carpenters and craftsmen. This not only
creates a better match of the user's needs and
priorities to the housing process, it also removes
the role of the intermediaries, thus also removing
the extraction of profits by them in the process.
The translation of the user's needs in housing
becomes more accurate, direct, and varied according to the needs of each user. The user also feels
a sense of belonging and pride through his
involvement in the housing process and is
willing to take responsibility for an environment
created by himself and not by others for him.

Besides differences in the housing processes
in the urban and rural areas, there are the different
physical characteristics, needs and constraints in
the rural and urban areas. Land scarcity (often
artificially created for speculative purposes) is the
main problem in the urban areas. This means that
design considerations in terms of spatial standards
will be different in the urban and rural areas.
Urban and rural lifestyles also have different
requirements for privacy and security besides
having different livelihood and activity patterns.

It is hoped that this study will also provide
some ideas on alternative housing development.
The traditional Malay house which is an autonomous housing process using self-help and
mutual-help approaches, can throw some light
into the development of a modern autonomous
housing model which is based on the needs and
aspirations of the users, rather than being imposed by others onto the users. Autonomous
housing solutions continue to serve the housing
needs of the majority in the world through
traditional and new settlements in the rural areas
and through spontaneous and self-help housing in
the urban areas.
The book also challenges the conventional
norms and values in many ways. The philosophical base of the traditional Malay house is
basically very different from that of the conventional westernised modern houses: the environmentally-respectful against the nature-conqueror;
the conserver culture against the consumer
culture; use-values against exchange-values;
decentralisation against centralisation; and basic
needs against luxury needs.
The book is also set in a wider context and
background which recognises the realities and

THE HOUSING SITUATION

Malaysia, like most developing countries, is
faced with massive housing problems. Poor
housing conditions, acute housing shortages,
spiralling house prices, speculation, bad housing
designs, unrealistic housing standards, and
malpractices in the housing industry plague the
Malaysian housing scene.

To understand the nature of the housing
situation in Malaysia, we will briefly discuss the
characteristics of housing in Malaysia, the major
problems of housing shortage and housing prices,
and the reasons for the failure of conventional
housing solutions in Malaysia. The importance of
the autonomous traditional Malay house, as an
alternative housing solution which is already
existent but which is forgotten and unrecognised,
will be evident when we analyse the housing
crisis in Malaysia.

Housing characteristics

In 1980, there were 2.63 million private living
quarters, of which 2.21 million were in Peninsular Malaysia. Most Malaysians live in the rural
areas making up 66% of the households in
Malaysia. This was however a decrease of 7%,
from 73% in 1970, clearly showing the rapid
urbanisation trends in Malaysia.

Rapid urban in-migration during the decade
resulted in the growth of squatter areas in major
towns which were characterised by overcrowding, poor living conditions and inadequate
amenities. The Kuala Lumpur Draft Structure Plan
estimated that the squatter population in the
Federal Territory was nearly a quarter million,

making up 26% of the total population of the
Federal Territory. The squatters occupy 1,771
hectares of the total area of the Federal Territory
and live in 177 squatter settlements. It was also
estimated that 40% of the population in the Federal
Territory lived in slums and squatter settlements.
This means that at least 40% of the population in
Federal Territory live in generally unhealthy and
inadequated housing conditions, reflecting the
acute housing problems in our urban areas.

The 1980 Population Census reveals that
houses and bungalows were the most common
living quarters in Malaysia, housing 92% of the
total households with the remaining 8%
staying in apartments and rooms. Within the
houses and bungalows category, the kampong
houses (houses in Malay villages) constituted the
majority of units. This implies that kampong
houses, which are largely made out of traditional
Malay houses, are the most important housetype
in the country.

Housing tenure patterns in Malaysia reveal a
very high percentage of owner-occupiers in the
rural areas and conversely a high percentage of
tenants in the urban areas. In 1980, some 64% of
the total households in Malaysia were owneroccupiers, while 23% were tenants and 13% had
other arrangements for occupancy of the living
quarters. In the rural areas, the owner-occupiers
were a much larger group making up 73% of the
households. Only 13% were tenants. This contrasts with the urban areas where there were
nearly equal proportions of owner-occupiers and
tenants, making up 47% and 44% of the
households, respectively.

While the majority of Malaysian households
are nuclear families, the extended family is still
an important household type in Malaysia. In 1980,
55% of the total households comprised of nuclear
families while 28% were extended families.
Among the various ethnic groups in Peninsular
Malaysia, the Chinese and Indians have more
extended family households (32% and 31%,
respectively) when compared to the Malays (25%).

Housing shortage

The acute housing shortage in Malaysia is clearly
reflected by the large slum and squatter population in our urban centres.

According to the Fourth Malaysia Plan, nearly a million houses (923,300) are required to be
built during the 1981-85 period in order to fulfill
the housing needs of Malaysians.

The ambitious target set by the Fourth
Malaysia Plan means that 185,000 housing units
must be built yearly for the 1981-85 period.
However, the completion rate for that period was
only about 406,070 units. The shortfall was about
56%. In the case of low-cost housing programes
the shortfall was even larger. It was 66%.

During the 1971-80 decade, 744,000 housing
units were built, 28% of which were built by the
public sector and 72% by the private sector. But
in the period of 1981-85, the involvement of the
public sector increased. The public and private

sectors built nearly equal numbers of housing
units and contributed 201,900 and 204,170 units
respectively.

The housing developer is the largest private
producer of houses in Malaysia. But the houses
built by them cater mainly for the upper and
upper-middle class Malaysians. In 1981, only 23%
of the housing units approved for construction by housing developers was below $25,000
despite the recent quota requirement imposed on
them to produce more low-cost houses. Houses
costing $25,000 - $80,000 made up 32% of the
approved houses while almost half of the houses
were priced at $80,000 and above. During the
1981-85 period, of the total 104,800 units built by
private developers, about 82% of them were high
and medium cost housing. Only 18% of the
housing units built by private developers were
low-cost housing. This was 78.7% short of the lowcost housing target for private housing developers
set in the Fourth Malaysia Plan.

The above statistics on housetypes built by
the private developers show that the responsibility to develop low-cost houses is still much left
to the government despite the expectation of
80,000 low-cost housing units to be built annually
by the private developers in the 1986-88 period
of ihe Fifth Malaysia Plan.

Government performance in low-cost housing
however is still dismal despite the push to build
low-cost houses for the poor. In the Third Malaysia
Plan period, the achievement of public expenditure for public low-cost housing and other lowcost housing schemes were only 69% and 45%
respectively. In the Fourth Malaysia Plan period
of 1981-85, 176,500 low-cost housing units were
planned to be built by the public sector. ‚But

Closely related to housing shortage is the problem
of spiralling house prices. They have shot up so
high that even an average middle-class family is

Over the 1970-80 decade, house prices in
the urban areas have risen by 200%-400%. This

The housing boom, at its peak during the

The sharp price increases, which benefit the

HOUSING ALTERNATIVES

It can be seen from the above analysis of the
housing crisis that the use of conventional housing approaches have largely failed to solve the

acute housing problems in Malaysia. Houses
today are beyond the affordability of most
Malaysians and the situation has become so acute
that even low-cost houses are beyond the reach
of the majority.

A radical review must be done in light of the
failure of conventional high-cost housing approaches. Conventional housing solutions are
capital-intensive and expensive. They do not
reach the poor because they are based on middle
class high-cost standards imposed on the poor
without regard to their needs, means and culture.
The use of such housing approaches also create
very crowded and low-quality living environments when the attempt is made to lower its high
costs to suit the poor.

Conventional housing processes which are
institutionalised also tend to remove access of the
poor to housing resources and concentrate control and power over housing resources in the
hands of the rich and powerful businessmen, the
professionals, and the state. They also do not
recognise the ability of the poor to house themselves and the informal sector to provide housing
for the poor. This is unfair, since people have for
generations been building houses for themselves
in both traditional and spontaneous settlements.
It nas been estimated that traditional houses constitute about two-thirds of the total housing stock
in the world. (Oliver: 1969)

their houses and improving them. The lack of
amenities and services in these areas also exacerbate the poor housing conditions.

Proportion and Scale

Proportion is the relation of the elements of the
building to one another and to the building as a
whole. The degree to which there is a correct
scheme of proportion in mass and detail determines the aesthetic unity or otherwise of a
building.

reflects the importance of the different parts of

based on dimensions of the human body. The
length of the forearm [hasta), the length of the
outstretched palm (jengkal), and the length of 2
arms outstretched /depa), are all used as measurements. These traditional linear dimensions are
seldom used today. The traditional Malay house
based on the human scale thus developed sensitive
dimensions for the use of space and traditional
furniture.

Good scale is the proper relation of the
elements to one another and to the whole in
terms of size, related and regulated by accepted
standard measurements. The traditional Malay
house is an example of good scale, because like
most vernacular architecture, it is based on
human scale and needs, developed through the
generations by the builders who are often the
users themselves.

MALAYSIAN CLIMATE AND
ENVIRONMENT

The climate of Malaysia can be classified as
warm-humid equatorial, characterised by high
temperatures and humidity. Air temperature
averages between 22°C and 32°C with small
annual and diurnal ranges. It is continually near
but seldom exceeds normal skin temperature.

Humidity is high throughout the year, averaging about 75% or more. With heavy cloud
cover and high water vapour content in the air,
direct solar radiation is filtered. Although reduced, solar radiation is strong and can cause
painful sky glare. The high humidity also accelerates rotting, rusting and the growth of algae
and mould.

Winds are generally of low-variable speed.
Strong winds can occur with the rains. Winds
come in two dominant directions, from the
northeast and southwest. Rainfall is also high
throughout the year averaging 250 to 300 cm
annually. Rains become more intense with the
monsoons.

Vegetation growth is prolific and is sometimes difficult to control under the favourable
conditions of moist air, moderate heat and high
rainfall. Thick vegetation growth creates both

On one hand, the thick plant cover reduces

Air flowing across any suface is subject to

increasing height and are lower near the ground
where there are many obstructions. Uneven
ground covers like bushes and thick undergrowth
decrease wind speeds rapidly, thus causing steep
wind gradients.

Locations near the sea may get a continuous
supply of land and sea breezes throughout the day
and night, but exposure to the large areas of sea
and sky here may cause problems of glare. The
relatively high percentages of reflected solar
radiation from the sea, sky and beach make glare
controls in houses necessary. Occasional strong
winds which occur during monsoon seasons may
pose serious hazards to houses and settlers.

THERMAL COMFORT REQUIREMENTS
The main causes of climatic stress in Malaysia are
high temperatures, solar radiation, humidity and
glare. To achieve climatic comfort in the Malaysian home, these factors must be controlled
besides the control of rain, floods and occasional
strong winds.

For thermal comfort, heat produced by the
human metabolic process must be dissipated
from the body to the environment in order to
maintain a balanced and constant body temperature of around 37°C. Metabolism involves the
conversion of food to energy for muscular work
and tissue-building, emitting heat in the process.
Heat gain by the body from the environment
through solar radiation or warm air must also be
minimised.

Heat is dissipated from the body to the
environment by convection, radiation or eva-

poration, and to a lesser extent, by conduction.
However, heat loss through conduction, radiation
and convection is negligible in the Malaysian
climate because the air temperatures are continually near the skin temperature.

Similarly, because of high humidity, evaporative cooling and perspiration are greatly
reduced and even inhibited. Evaporation of moisture from the body in humid climate quickly
forms a saturated air envelope around the body.
The saturated air envelope prevents any further
evaporation from the body and undermines the
last means of heat dissipation.

Thus to achieve some degree of thermal
comfort the saturated air envelope around the
body must be removed. Air flowing across the
body can remove the saturated air envelope and
accelerate evaporation. However, this is insufficient because without ventilation (air exchange)
both the temperature and humidity in a room will
build up to very high levels leading to very
uncomfortable conditions. This temperature and
humidity build-up is caused by the heat and
moisture output of human bodies within an
enclosed space.

Though natural ventilation is often accompanied by air movement, the reverse is not
necessarily true. Air movement can often occur
without ventilation. This is illustrated in the
familiar situation of the use of fans in badly
ventilated rooms. The circulation of hot and
humid air within a confined space does little to
relieve climatic stress. Thus, adequate ventilation
is the critical factor in dissipating body heat.

Direct and indirect solar radiation, hot air,
together with conduction and radiation from the
building fabric are the main sources of heat gain
to the body.

There are numerous features in the traditional Malay house that are geared towards
providing effective ventilation. The house is
raised on stilts to catch winds of a higher velocity.
The elongated structure of the traditional Malay

allow easy passage of air and cross ventilation.

Windows are plentiful in the Malay house

roof space. Ventilation joints in the roof called

used to ventilate the roof space.

also encouraged to flow through the houses. The
random arrangement of the kampong houses and
the careful planting and selection of trees ensure
that winds are not blocked for the houses in the
latter path of the wind.

The light-weight construction of the Malay

low thermal capacity and high insulation mater-

our climate. Wood, bamboo and attap used have
good insulating properties and they retain or
conduct little heat into the building.

the large thatched Malay house roof with large
overhangs. The walls of the house are low, thus
effectively reducing the vertical areas of the house
exposed to solar radiation. The low walls also
make the tasks of shading easier.

The large overhangs which provide good
shading also provide good protection against
driving rain. They also allow the windows to be
left open most of the time for ventilation, even
during the rain.

The Malay house is also designed to control
direct exposure to heat from direct sunlight. Traditionally, many Malay houses are oriented to face
Mecca for religious reasons. This East-West

the house to direct solar radiation. The compound

trees and covered with vegetation. This sets the
house in a cooler environment, by the trees and
vegetation not absorbing and storing heat from
solar radiation and reradiating it into the environment.

excluding open skies and bright areas from the

CONCLUSION

From the comparison above, it can be seen that
modern housing estate houses are not only badly
designed climatically, but they usually go against
the basic requirements for thermal comfort.

Materials of high thermal capacity and conductivity like bricks, cement and zinc are used.
Wind is blocked out and exposure to solar
radiation is uncontrolled. Such are the characteristics found in many modern houses making them
very uncomfortable to live in.

The use of inappropriate building materials,
the lack of vegetation, air and noise pollution
have caused adverse microclimates in the housing
estates and in the urban areas. Studies by Sham
Sani (1981) reveal that new housing estates with
limited mature trees have constantly high temperatures between 25.5 and 27.3°C, which are
considerably higher than the temperature in the
comfort range of between 20.6 and 22.8°C. In the
urban areas, temperatures are considerably
higher than temperatures in the more natural
surrounding areas, resulting in the 'heat island’
effect. In 1980, the temperature in the commercial centre of Kuala Lumpur was 7.8°C higher than
that outside the city.

Besides the use of unsuitable building materials in our environment, social and economic
pressures have also contributed to our adverse
urban climate. With high density living, more
areas are paved and less left for trees and greenery.
Higher building costs and profit motivation have
also cut the necessary large roof eaves short, made
open shady verandahs disappear and lowered
ceiling heights.

The need for greater security and privacy in
the urban areas have led to house designs which
are more closed, thus reducing ventilation. Modern bulky furniture and finishes also make the
house warmer as they store up heat and make the
house stuffy and crowded. The way modern
housing estates are developed makes the housing
estate barren of vegetation. Residents have to

suffer the intense heat absorbed and stored in the
barren environment.

Clearly lessons can be drawn from the climatic
design of the traditional Malay house for housing
in the modern context. Although the use of
appropriate building materials like wood and
thatch for housebuilding may not be suitable for
high density living in the urban areas, wooden
houses and lightweight construction can be promoted in the surburban areas in the housing
estates where densities are not so high.

Like the kampong environment, more greens
should be grown and pavements should be
reduced in the modern housing schemes. Roads
and pavements are known to take up to 40% of
the total site area in modern housing estate. Trees
should be kept and not indiscriminately chopped
down in housing development as they are the most
important element for the creation of a comfortable thermal environment. They provide shade,
help to reduce glare, prevent the storing up of heat
in the environment, act as a filter against noise and
dust and provide oxygen and fresh air.

The maximisation of ventilation exhibited in
the traditional Malay house should be emulated
in modern house design. However, windows and
other openings for ventilation must be protected
from solar radiation, driving rain and glare. The
use of open verandahs and large overhangs should
be encouraged to overcome this problem.

The good solar radiation and glare controls in
the Malay house are other examples of good
climatic design which are applicable to modern
housing. The orientation of modern houses must
be carefully designed so as to minimise solar heat
gain in the building. Roofs, being the most
important element in the control of solar radiation,
should be made from low-thermal capacity
materials. Glare should also be controlled as
shown in the traditional Malay house, by the use
of trees and protection of visual field from open
skies.

The influence of Islam is felt when we look at

While it is in accord with adat (customary law)

we see also that one of the Five Pillars of Islam
exhorts the good Muslim to perform acts of

Malay society also require that elders and adat are

In traditional Malay society there seems to

close social relationships. The strong community

competition.

The preference for community intimacy is
reflected in the lack of territorial markings and
physical barriers in the traditional Malay house

interaction and a sense of belonging to these

quiet, lonesome, or in the absence of human

reflected in the very open interiors in the house.

life of the Malays. Five prayers are performed
daily at definite times, namely, at dawn, noon,
afternoon, sunset and nightfall. Congregational
prayers are held in the mosque at noon on
Fridays. Prayers at home are usually performed at

the serambi or the rumah ibu of the house. A
special area where the floor is made with gaps is
sometimes found in the serambi. This area is used
for bathing the dead. This is an example of how
a cultural and ritual necessity is integrated into the
very structure of the house.

Cooking, washing and dining are usually
carried outin the kitchen. Food is served on mats
with people sitting either on the mats or on the
floor around the dishes. It is not unusual to find
that men, women and children eat in separate
domains within a house, at separate times, usually
men first.

Bathing and toilet activities are usually
carried out outside the house. Islam stresses the
importance of modesty for both men and women.
Thus Malay women who bathe at a family well
orin the stream wear a sarong fastened above the
breast, reaching below their knees. Malay women
usually bathe at different times from the men and
it is bad manners to look at bathers at a well or
stream. Similarly, Malay men must wrap a cloth
called kain basahan around the lower half of their
bodies before bathing in the wells or streams.
Modesty is also strictly applied to other toilet
activities.

CONCEPTION AND USE OF SPACE

The interior spaces in the traditional Malay
houses are moulded by the culture, values and
lifestyle of the Malays. As seen earlier, the low
priority for privacy but a preference for community intimacy; the seasonal needs of feasting and
other cultural activities; the lack of regard for
ostentatious living; and the deep understanding
of thermal comfort requirements have all led to
open flexible interior spaces in the traditional
Malay houses.

The interior spaces of the Malay houses are
generally not defined by the use of the space but
rather by the houseform itself. The interior spaces
are instead named by zones created by the
houseform. The only exception where the spaces
are named by its use are the kitchen and the toilet.

There are no area names like living-room,
bed-room, dining-room, hall, drawing-room,
study-room, which are directly named after specific activities, as in the Western and modern
houses. This reflects the multi-functional and
non-specialised use of space in the Malay house.
This definition of space leads to a more flexible
and optimal use of space and is consistent with
the inclination towards simple living as described
earlier.

Non-specialisation of the use of space means
that the same space is used for many different
purposes at different times of the day and year. A
living area can be used for sleeping at night with a
mat laid out, which in the morning could be rolled
up and stored. This same place can be used during
the day for sitting, prayers, entertaining friends,
sewing or other activities. During a marriage or
other ceremonies, the same place may even be
used for feasting.

Interior spaces in the Malay house are not
defined by interior partitions or walls, but rather
by the less obstructing minor change in levels. The
change in levels of up to seven inches (one
step up) is used to define certain areas. This
allows the defined spaces to be merged and
provides a larger expanse when the occasion calls
for it. With walls and partitions, the spaces
cannot be merged unless the partition is removed. Sometimes curtains and sliding panels
are used as partitions to define areas, and these
are more flexible than fixed partitions.

The use of minimal furniture in the Malay
house also helps to create a more flexible interior
space. Bulky furniture not only clutters and breaks
up interior spaces, it also defines and confines
the space to certain uses only.

The Malay house is, thus, an example of the
economical, flexible and optimal use of space.
Openness and emptiness are the essence of the
interior spaces in the traditional Malay house.
Such qualities also facilitate good ventilation,
lighting and views in the house.

THE KAMPONG LAYOUT

The kampong on first encounter may look haphazard to many observers. It has few clear visual
landmarks or focal points which may help a
person to locate his orientation. The kampong is
randomly distributed with Malay houses, trees,
compounds and paths. The houses look similar
and blend harmoniously with the environment.
There are usually not many main roads in the
kampong except occasionally for the access road
leading into the kampong. Instead, paths link the
village, leading from one house to another,
winding through the houses and leading to other
parts of the village. Paths are unclear as many of
them merge into sandy open compounds of
houses. The mosque, shops and clinic are possibly
the only landmarks for the visitor. Orientation is,
however, not problematic in the village because
it is relatively small and is a linear settlement in
many cases.

There is no clear geometric order in the
layout of the kampong. Instead the layout is
determined by the social relationships and the
culture and lifestyle of the villagers. House sites
are traditionally selected by observation and
religious rituals. Houses are spaced far apart for
future expansion, tree-planting and privacy.
Adequate privacy is provided by the dark
interiors and the distance between the houses in
most cases. However, the distance between houses
can be drastically reduced as in the case of fishing
villages and urban kampongs where housing
density is high.

important role in determining the layout of the
houses in the kampong unlike the case in modern
housing. In the kampong, electricity and water

INFRASTRUCTURES

Generally, amenities and infrastructures are adequate in the kampongs. However, there is much
room for improvement in these areas. According
to the Fourth Malaysia Plan, in 1980, only 47.2%
of the rural population in Peninsular Malaysia
was served with piped water supply, compared
to 90.7% in the urban areas. In 1980, 790,000 rural
households were supplied with electricity. This
means that only 48% of the rural households in
Malaysia have electricity supply. Like the other

services, sewerage facilities are also still largely
concentrated in the urban areas. Electricity and
piped water where they exist usually serve the
individual households but wells and toilets are
sometimes shared by households.

Water Supply

In places where piped water is not available the
common means of water supply in the kampong
are the wells, streams, rivers and spring water in
the foothills. Despite about half of the Malaysian
rural population being served with piped water,
natural sources of water supplies are still very
important to the rural people. Water pumps are
also used increasingly.

Drinking water is usually taken from the
pipes, wells and the springs while river water is
mostly used for washing and bathing purposes.
However, during the dry seasons, wells often
run dry and villagers resort to the river for even
their drinking water. This has caused frequent
outbreaks of water-borne diseases like cholera in
the states of Kedah and Perlis. The important
natural water resources for the rural people are
also frequently polluted by factories, pesticides
and human waste. Silting, muddy waters and
floods are also becoming common problems
caused by the clearing of land for development.

Wells and rivers are not only used for their
water resources, but also act as a place where
social interaction among the women and children
take place. Children love to play in the shallow
streams. The wells are usually sheltered by walls
made of temporary and non-durable materials
like the coconut palm fronds, or are covered by
bushes. These are more of a suggestive privacy
barrier. The strong social control in the kampong
prevents people from nearing these private areas.

Community water standpipes are commonly
found in the kampong. They are usually found
along the main road which passes through the
village. Locating the standpipe along the road,
however, does not provide adequate privacy for
the villagers.

Sanitation

Most sanitation facilities in the kampong are
decentralised with wastes frequently disposed by
dispersal. This is sufficiently clean for most of the
less dense rural kampongs but often become a
major health hazard in the denser kampongs in
the fishing and urban areas.

The common toilets used are the pit jjamban
korek) and pour-flush toilets (jjamban curah ,
Jamban jitra). Many villages in the remote rural
areas often do not have any proper toilet facilities.
In such cases, the drains, rivers and the jungle are
used for their toilet functions. The women and
others who require more privacy in these cases
perform their toilet functions after dark.

Toilet facilities are usually separated from
the dwellings in the Malay kampong. Toilets are
kept a safe distance away from the wells and
house. It is usually sheltered by a separate wood
and zinc structure. Modern sewerage systems are
seldom found in Malay kampong.

Open pits which were more common in the
past are seldom used today. The jamban jitra
which is a pour-flush system with a sealed pit is
more commonly used. It is a very hygienic and
efficient decentralised toilet system for rural areas.

A large pit, about six feet deep, is first dug out,
sometimes unlined but often lined with large
concrete rings on the walls of the pit. A shelter
with a platform is made to house the toilet bowl
in front of the pit. A modern toilet bowl is fixed
on the concrete platform with a chute leading the
waste into the pit now behind the shelter. Modern
porcelain toilet bowls are often used but
sometimes locally made concrete versions are
used. Like a modern toilet, the waste is washed
into the pit and the water-seal in the toilet bowl
prevents the odour from surfacing.

In fishing villages, one often finds unhealthy
toilets. Here the human waste is often simply
released from a shelter built across the water
into the sea to be washed away, but the tides sometimes carry back the wastes onto the shore.

Another sanitation problem in the kampong is the drainage of waste water from the
kitchens. The waste water is usually drained out
of the house into a pit, or even worse, the waste
water is simply dripped into a pit at the bottom
of the house. The pit collects sediment and water,
breeds mosquitoes and houseflies and often
stinks.

THE KAMPONG AS A COMMUNITY SPACE
Although the study of the kampong setting in this
chapter is mainly a physical one, certain lessons
can be learnt from these physical characteristics
which act as bases for socio-cultural and economic
activities. The physical aspect of the kampong, like
the house, may not be the major determinant of
socio-cultural activities but, as a space, it is
an important modulator which can serve as a
facilitator or constraint for an activity or development. In this sense the physical setting is important for community building and socio-cultural
activities.

The kampong with the houses randomly

and the additive quality of the house well.
The random arrangement allows for the spontaneous use and growth of socio-cultural spaces

actual needs of the users without the users having
to forcibly orientate themselves to conform to
rigid geometrical patterns drawn up by the
professionals.

The random layout, the natural setting, the
use of local building materials and the lack of
physical barriers, gives the kampong an informal
and open atmosphere which is conducive to
intimate social relations. The kampong environment is an expression of the culture and needs of
the users, unlike modern settlements which are
expressions of a larger socio-economic world order
which has imposed its physical, social and
economic structures on us.

In traditional Malay society, the construction of

The building process begins with the site

prefabricated housing system where the building
components are first made on the ground and

later assembled together on the site to form the
house.

RITUAL SITING OF THE HOUSE
The siting of the house in Malay society is
very important, for the Malays believe that the
spirit of the site must be appeased in order that
the house will be peaceful and that the well-being
of the occupants will be taken care of.
Preselection of the house site is done by
observation of the site. Small mounts or slightly
raised sites are avoided as it is believed that these
are places where ghosts reside. These places also
often contain nests of ants and termites. Lowlying areas which flood are also avoided. The site
is usually near the place of work with easy
accessibility to water, transport and other services.

LABOUR ARRANGEMENTS IN
CONSTRUCTING HOUSE

In the past, with skills handed down from
generation to generation and a tradition of
building strongly established, most families built
their own houses. However, for building larger
houses of the wealthier families, carpenters were
engaged.

Cooperative labour was commonly practised
in building the traditional Malay house. It was
mainly practised in the erection of the house
where such tasks needed much manpower. Besides cooperative and self-help labour, mutual
assistance was also commonly practised in the
construction of the traditional Malay house.

Today such cooperative self-help and mutualhelp labour arrangements are being eroded. The
use of local carpenters is more common although
self-help and mutual-help approaches are still
practised among the poor for building basic
houses. Cooperative labour for the erection of the
house is still quite common in the rural areas but
this practice is quickly disappearing in more

urbanised areas.

Although the participation of the family in
the traditional housing process is greatly reduced
by the employment of carpenters, user-participation is still dominant here when compared to userparticipation in the modern housing process.
The user decides on the scale, use of materials, use
of space, embellishments and other design aspects
of the house according to his needs, affordability
and taste.

The payment to carpenters for their housebuilding services in the villages is generally
cheaper than in the urban areas. This can be
attributed to the limited demand for carpenters in
the rural areas. Moreover, since most of the
carpenters come from the same locality, it is
important for the carpenters to maintain their
good reputation by providing good services and
charging reasonable rates.

With the guide of a building tradition and the
village carpenter, the user designs the house to
match his socio-economic and cultural needs
efficiently. Intermediaries of the modern housing
processes like the developers, architects and other
professionals are not required in this process. This
not only creates a more direct and good match of
the house to the user-needs, it also removes
the extraction of profits and control by the
intermediaries in the process.

Various labour arrangements using local
carpenters for building the house are observed in
the different parts of Malaysia. In some areas of
Kedah, wages are paid to the carpenter based on
the amount of materials purchased for the house.
The wages paid to the carpenter are equivalent to
half the cost of materials for the house. Lump sum
wages based on the size and the type of the
house are also practised. In other cases, single
contractual sums are paid to the carpenter based
on the wages and the materials for the house. Daily
wages paid to the carpenter are also practised. The

THE CONSTRUCTION SYSTEM

The traditional Malay house is basically a post and
lintel timber structure with a thatched gable roof.
The house is raised on stilts and rests on concrete
or stone footings. No foundations are required
since the house is a light-weight structure.

The structural frame of the house consists of
columns braced by floor joists and roof girders.
The main roof rests on the ridge supported by king
posts and girts on the sides. Lean-to roofs are
used to cover the smaller areas in the house like
the serambi and entrances.

The traditional Malay house is a prefabricated housing system where most components
are first made on the ground and later assembled
on the site.

Various mortise and tenon, lap and dovetail
joints are used in the traditional Malay house

construction. In the past when nails were not
used in the house, wooden dowels were used
instead. Pin joints with wooden dowels were
commonly used then. Wooden corbels are used
for house additions and extensions.

The use of wedges [baji) to tighten mortise and
tenon joints is an important feature in the Malay
house. This allows the joints to be easily taken
apart and reassembled without damaging the
fabric of the building. In the past, it was quite
common for buildings to be taken apart and
reassembled elsewhere when there was a need to
shift the houses to other locations.

A major drawback of the construction system
is the lack of diagonal bracing in the structure
of the house. This causes the structure to sway in
instances of uneven loading. This happens when
the joints are improperly made and wedged.

THE ADDITION SYSTEM

IKE the design flexibility found in other
user-designed traditions, the traditional

Malay house caters well to the varied
needs of the users. This design flexibility is
clearly expressed in the addition system of the
traditional Malay house. This is basically a system
in which new extensions are added on to the basic
core house. The new parts may be built as
extensions at various stages and times as and when
the need arises, for instance when the family
grows in size. The system grew out of the needs,
means, constraints and socio-economic contexts
of the users. It is a very well developed and
sophisticated system which is based on addition
principles which are sound in design, construction
and aesthetics, and causes minimal disruption to
the original house.

The traditional Malay house is set in the rural
setting where the main economic activities of the
people are farming and fishing. The seasonal
patterns of work leave much spare time to the
villagers during the off-seasons for housebuilding,
mending nets and boats, making household
implements and doing other part-time economic
activities. The addition system of the Malay house
is well suited for this seasonal pattern of
work by facilitating housebuilding during the offseasons, and thus allows the house to be built up
gradually at a pace controlled by the users.

The addition system also fits well with the
economic means and the needs of the users. As
a family accumulates savings over time, or asthe
family needs grow larger, or due to the desire for
a more comprehensive dwelling place, additions
to the house are made. The addition system which
allows the house to grow slowly also does not
create heavy financial burdens on the user by
allowing him to build according to his financial
resources over time.

house is not an ad hoc system of extensions like
those made to modern houses and other nontraditional houses such as the spontaneous squatter houses. The addition system is a highly EM

ADDITION METHODS
The use of the tupai (Malay word for squirrel), is
one of the main construction features of the
addition system. The fupai is a piece of timber
corbel nailed or bolted to the columns of the core

rasuk (floor joists) of the extension. When used,

or beams, or it can be quite elaborately carved.
Basically there are two types of tupai. One is
the shorter version which does not reach to the
footing for support. It is generally used to support
the roof beams and floor joists of the minor extensions which bear less loads. The second type of
tupai is the longer version which goes right to the
bottom and touches the concrete footing. This is
generally used for supporting the floor joists which
support heavier loads. The tupai is sometimes
used to support two joists or beams.

CONCLUSION

The concept of incremental housing shown in the
addition system of the Malay house is a flexible
approach which grows with the needs and means
of the user. This housing concept is most appropriate for housing the poor as the house
requires only a small initial capital investment
and grows when the family has the means to
expand the house. This lessens the financial

burden on the poor for housing.

Incremental housing seen in the traditional
Malay house can also be found in other vernacular
houses and other autonomous houses such as those
built by squatters. But incremental housing for the
poor is today obstructed by rigid bylaws which
require many bureaucratic procedures of housing
approval, use middle-class and Western standards
and require expensive housing materials.

The incremental housing concept is also
hindered by the modern concept of housing as a
highly finished final product rather than as an
activity which changes and grows. Housing as a
highly finished final product for the poor is
oppressive in a sense that the heavy financial
burden placed on the buyer or user removes the
opportunity for the use of financial resources for
other basic needs and social mobility. Special
allowances for the flexibility in the use of building
materials and building design standards must be
made to cater for the special needs of the poor.

A major cause of this problem is the erosion of

THE BIAS AGAINST WOODEN HOUSES
Besides the problem of the low status of wooden
houses, various other reasons can be attributed to
the decline in popularity of wooden houses in
Malaysia. Since the traditional Malay house is
largely made of wood, the reasons described below
for wooden houses will be largely applicable to
traditional Malay houses.

First, the Malaysian timber industry is
heavily export-oriented. This has affected the
quality, quantity and cost of timber available for

the local market. In 1984, 31.1 million cubic
metres of sawlogs were produced and 53.4% of
it was exported. The export-oriented timber
industry has pushed up local timber prices
and since most of the high quality timbers are
exported, the local market was deprived of high
quality "hardwoods.

Secondly, restrictive archaic and stringent
building by-laws to control fires have deterred the
building of timber houses in the urban areas. The
uniform building by-laws which were based on
stringent British Standards demand a high degree
of scientific treatment of the timber. Unless the
timber used meets these requirements, the house
would be classified as a temporary dwelling.

While timber houses are considered a fire risk,
it has been proven that large timber beams actually
perform better than steel or concrete in a strong
fire. Under intense heat, steel buckles and concrete crumbles, causing a total collapse of a
building. Timber, on the other hand, after initial
ignition smoulders at a rate of around 0.6 mm per
minute.

Fire-fighting regulations relating to timber
buildings are not clearly spelt out in the building
by-laws and architects and designers have no
definite idea of what can or cannot be done. The
by-laws not only restrict the use of timber
materials but also curb new timber products like
woodwool which are approved by SIRIM. A
marketing director of the Malaysian Timber
Industry Board was reported to have said ''What
the by-laws amount to would be to phase out the
use of timber buildings altogether'' (Sunday Mail:
16 July 1978).

Thirdly, the discrimination against wooden
structures in housing finance and the demand of
higher premiums for insurance have deterred
many from building timber houses. Insurance
agents have been reported to charge 50% to 400%
higher than normal rates on insurance premiums
for protection of timber buildings against fire.

Lastly, the majority of architects in Malaysia
have relatively little timber technology know-

how. This gap is not likely to be filled with the
present difficulties resulting from restrictive
building by-laws and the general prejudices against
timber houses. Timber technological research in
Malaysia for buildings is also insufficient. Intensive research should be made to provide guides
for the use of local timbers in construction.

DIMINISHING RESOURCES AND SKILLS
Diminishing resources for building the Malay
house is another big problem faced by the traditional Malay house. Building materials, which
were once freely available, have to be bought at
a very high cost today. Many materials which in
the past could be gathered free from the environment are no longer easily accessible because the
natural surroundings have been increasingly
cleared for development projects and agriculture.
Attap which was once the main roofing material
for Malay houses is today becoming scarce. So too
are bamboo, the nibong tree and other trees which
supply free timber for building the houses. Unless
there are positive steps to rehabilitate the resource
base for appropriate local building materials, one
can foresee the increasing dependence on modern
materials in the building of Malay houses.

The general lack of appreciation of the traditional Malay house by the younger generation and
the lack of rural labour are additional factors
causing the erosion of the traditional Malay house.
The carpenters, wood-carvers, attap-weavers and
other artisans involved in the building of the
traditional Malay houses are a dying breed. This
trend is caused by the diminishing popularity of
the houses, inappropriate legislation and the lack
of interest by the young people to continue in the
trade.

THE THREAT OF BUILDING BY-LAWS
Another threat looming over the traditional Malay
house is the imposition of inappropriate building
by-laws in the rural areas. In 1978, the Penang
State Government drafted laws to include the rural
areas under the jurisdiction of building by-laws
which were formerly applicable only to the urban
areas.

The traditional Malay house is designed,
managed and financed by the user. It is built by
users and village carpenters. But the building bylaws extended to the rural areas require that
formal building plans be drawn up and approved
by the local authorities before a house can be built

or extended. The building by-laws are made in
relation to modern buildings in the urban areas
and are wholly unsuitable for the rural areas
where traditional Malay houses predominate.

The enforcement of such by-laws mean that
villagers wishing to build or extend a Malay house
would have to get building plans drawn up and
approved. Such plans will have to be prepared
by registered architects, very few of whom
in Malaysia have any deep knowledge of traditional Malay houses. In rural areas where the
rate of poverty is high, most villagers cannot
afford the expensive services of architects. Besides,
traditional Malay house users and carpenters do
not build according to plans but by experience.
They are unable to read or follow formal house
plans. Subsequently commercial contractors from
the urban areas have to be employed to construct
the houses according to plans. This completely
goes against the principle of the traditional Malay
house where the user controls the design and
construction of the house.

Thus the extension of the by-laws poses a
serious threat not only to the continued existence
of the house in the future, but also the existence
of traditional carpenters, craftsmen, attapweavers and others involved in the traditional
Malay house construction. The by-laws in effect
will displace and replace them with contractors,
developers and professionals.

PROMOTION OF THE HOUSE

We now examine the steps needed to promote
an appreciation of and the use of the Malay
house. Firstly, the image of the Malay house
needs to be built up so that the users themselves
can re-build their confidence in the houses they
construct and live in. Then, obstacles which
hinder the development of the traditional Malay
house should be removed. This requires concrete
and practical government policies, and most of all
a political will and active commitment to promote
the traditional Malay house.

Many existing building regulations which
were drawn up on colonial lines should be reviewed. Instead, building standards should reflect
the people's socio-economic needs and local cultural and ecological conditions. Building research
should also incorporate the non-physical aspects
related to buildings such as the Malaysian sociocultural use of space and buildings as a source and
reflection of Malaysian identity.

Probably the most important condition for the
promotion of traditional Malay houses is the
creation of accessibility to resources like finance,
land and building materials for the poor to build
their own houses themselves. Availability of land
and land tenure security are key factors required
to improve housing quality of the poor and to
increase their housing stock. Without land tenure
security, the poor will only build low-quality
houses and will not inıprove them because of the
temporary and illegal status accorded to the
houses.

Finance is another constraint to better housing quality for the poor. Since finance for housing the poor is impossible to obtain from the
private sector, government intervention in this
area would be most needed.

Instead of imposing by-laws, rigid middleclass standards and bureaucratic procedures which
hinder and restrict the building of houses by the
poor using self-help, mutual-help and informal
housing approaches, the government should take

positive steps to encourage and develop the
potential resources of the poor and informal
sector to create housing for themselves. The
importance of their contribution cannot be denied
since most of the housing stock in the world
today, especially in the Third World, are not
created by architects, governments, and
technocrats but by the people themselves.

The government can encourage self-help,
mutual-help and site-and-services approaches by
providing land, resources and finance. This will
also help reduce the strain on scarce capital
resources in developing countries as these approaches require much less capital input compared
to conventional mass housing. The traditional
Malay house which is a highly sophisticated
building system could be developed as a prototype
for such approaches. The incremental housing
properties of the traditional Malay house which
allows the house to grow with the increasing
means and needs of the users are most appropriate
to housing the poor who do not have the resources
to build their houses all at once.

The depletion of indigenous building materials
caused by the destruction of natural resources
through modern agricultural and development
activities must be checked. Measures must be
taken to renew natural resources by the setting up
of forest reserves for building materials all over
the country. The indigenous building materials
industry should be revived to produce the climatically superior attap thatches, bamboo wall
matting, nibong flooring and various wooden
panels. However, research should be made to
improve the durability and quality of these
building materials. New indigenous materials and
new designs should be also developed to supplement and stimulate the indigenous building
materials industry.

In this vein of development, the government
should promote timber houses and timber as a
building material. This will greatly enhance the
status of traditional Malay houses which are
synonymous with timber houses in the rural

areas. Good timber should be made cheaply
available for building purposes. Export of timber
should be controlled so that quality timber will
be available for local use and timber prices
will be kept low in our timber-rich country. The
availability of good quality timber at cheaper
prices will become an encouragement for
Malaysians to build high quality timber houses.
Other obstacles to the building of timber houses
(described in Chapter 11) especially in the
urban areas should be removed. The promotion
and building of high quality timber homes in the
urban areas will help remove the present low
status attached to timber houses. The building of
timber houses in the urban areas is vital for
uplifting the status of timber houses in the rural
areas, since values and trends in the rural areas
are greatly influenced by what happens in the
cities.

the creation of a Malaysian
