NATURE AND ARCHITECTURE

1. The house is among the first things man needs to
maintain his existence in nature: /nitium vitae hominis aqua et
banis et vestimentum et domus protegens turpitudinem (Sirach
XAIX 28). Unlike other living beings, we are not provided
by nature with food, clothes and a house, but thrown back
on our own resources; it is our intellect, which
distinguishes us from these other creatures, that enables us
to choose the most suitable form for each of these additions.

The ground being too hard for our bare feet we make
ourselves sandals of softer material than the ground, but
tougher than our feet. Were they as hard as the ground or
as soft as our feet they would give us no advantage, but being just hard enough to stand up to wear and yet just soft
enough to be comfortable, they bring about a harmony between our tender feet and the rough ground.

With the house it is a matter not just of the contact between our feet and the ground, but of the meeting of our
whole being with the total natural environment. The
means by which the harmony between the two comes about
is no longer ‘a little bit of soft ground’ that we carry about
on our feet, but a ‘piece of habitable space’ that we
separate from the natural environment by means of walls.

Whereas the sandal is fastened on the foot to complete
the body and enable it to bear the hard ground, the house
must be seen as an addition to nature, by which natural
space is completed and made habitable for us.

2. The elements of the house can be derived only from
nature: the primary datum of the wall-separated space is
the unlimited mass of the earth with the limitless space
above it; so the limited mass of the walls must also be
drawn from the earth in order to withdraw a limited piece
of space from the space of nature.

However clearly the wall that encloses the space cannot
be got from the earth in one piece. The compact, rounded
form of the material we extract from the earth—be it a
block of stone, a piece of wood or a lump of clay—cannot
produce the enclosed form of interior space directly; for
this at least a few pieces of material need to be joined
together. Dolmens and other megalithic monuments are
examples of such primitive space-formation by means of a
minimal number of pieces (fig. a).

Before man can inhabit his house two events must follow
one another: first the materials needed for the house must
be dislodged from their natural context, and later they
must be put together elsewhere in a new, technical context
to form the artificial whole that is the house.

Between the extreme terms of the housing proces—man
and nature—two intermediate terms are therefore present:
first the building materials extracted from the earth, and
secondly the house, the technical ensemble into which these
materials are integrated.

In the complete housing process there is always a functional relation between four terms: nature, material, house
and man. The materials are drawn from nature, the house
is put together out of materials and man inhabits the house.

Dwelling, building technique and preparation of
materials are thus the three functions by which the four
terms—man, house, material and nature—are related.

3. Analysis of the housing process into its four terms and
three functions shows clearly the limits within which the
architect exercises his profession; this is confined to the
middle function—the technical assemblage of materials to
form the house.

In a primitive society it is possible for someone to build
his own house and himself extract the materials for it from
nature; the profession of architect has not yet come into being. But in a more advanced stage of society the figure of
the architect appears on the scene: the man who builds
houses that will be lived in by others, with materials that
have likewise been extracted by others from nature. His
special function in the housing process consists in bringing
about the relation between the two middle terms of the process, material and house: in short, in the technique of
building.

To prevent this it is not enough for him to be aware of

To gain an insight into this general form the architect
must not base himself solely on specific living requirements, but set out from the overall need for housing,
which arises from the physical and psychic nature of man;
he must not only take account of the practical uses of this or
that material, but penetrate to the overall space-dividing
and space-ordering properties of solid matter; and lastly he
must not only have an eye to the technical processes
employed in the working of materials to form walls and
roofs, but rather concern himself with the overall necessity
of putting together a number of solid elements in order to
enclose spaces. For what is of primary importance for the
general form of the house is not what purpose it serves, but
that it serves; not what it is made of, but that it is made;
and not how it is put together, but that it is put together.

5. Ifhuman existence were purely material like that of a
piece of wood or stone, it could be protected by a material
form that enclosed it tightly, as a precious gem is kept in a
padded box. But an animate existence that shows itself in
spontaneous movement needs a shelter that leaves over
enough space for moving about in. And movement is
guided by senses, which also impose certain demands; lastly the intellect must freely direct towards their goal both the
movement of the body and the working of the senses.

At each of these levels of our existence we come in contact with the spatial datum of nature. In addition to commanding the space we need for movement, we also form a
clear image of it, and realize that a piece of natural space is
involved in our existence. We call this our experiencespace.

6. Our experience-space is necessarily in conflict with
the space of nature. The space that nature offers us rises
above the ground and is oriented entirely towards the
earth’s surface. The contrast between the mass of the earth
below and the space of the air above, which meet at the surface of the earth, is the primary datum of this space. On
account of their weight all material beings are drawn into
this spatial order, and live as it were against the earth.

Through his intellect and his upright stance man can
detach himself from this order and relate to himself the
piece of space that he needs for action and movement. He
is conscious of a horizontal orientation centred upon
himself in the midst of the vertical orientation centred upon
the earth— of a space around him in the midst of the space
above the earth.

7. Architecture is born of this original discrepancy between the two spaces—the horizontally oriented space of
Our experience and the vertically oriented space of nature;
it begins when we add vertical walls to the horizontal surface of the earth.

Through architecture a piece of natural space is as it
were set on its side so as to correspond to our experiencespace. In this new space we live not so much against the
earth as against the walls; our space lies not upon the earth
but between walls.

This space brings a completion to natural space that
allows it to be brought into relation with our experience-
Space; at the same time it allows our specifically human

space to be assimilated into the homogeneous order of
nature.

8. The contrast between the mass of the wall and the
space oriented in relation to it is simply a special form of
the major contrast between the mass of the earth and the
open air above it that we encounter in nature.

The only aspect of this natural datum we perceive is the
earth’s horizontal surface, for in this primordial condition
there is no question of any spatial or solid form. The earth
and the air above it could only have a form for us if they
had a number of mutually corresponding bounding surfaces as terms or limits of their volume. But there is no
other surface corresponding to the interface between air
and earth; seeing only one term we look upon it as a term
of the mass, and so speak of the ‘earth’s surface’.

Folds in that surface may give rise here and there to convex and concave forms—mountains and valleys—but we
must make a clear distinction between these and genuine
forms, which are based not on the contrast between convex
and concave but on that between solid and void. Mountains and valleys affect the surface of the earth but not its
volume. For us the earth’s surface is horizontal and
without any other corresponding horizontal surface; we
know that the earth is round, but so long as we live upon it
we cannot visualize its form.

9. Our experience of the earth’s surface is comparable
to that ofthe ‘now’ in time, for the ‘now’ is likewise the only phenomenon we experience of the passage of time. It
presents itself as a separation between a past without beginning and a future without end—once again a beginning
and an end that both elude us.

Our now can stretch into a today or a this year, it then has a
certain duration just as mountains and valleys give the
earth’s surface a sort of form. But the now is in principle
without duration, without any definite extension in time.
In order to experience a length of time, two moments in
time must be picked out and related together in the
memory; the two moments then correspond to each other

One might ask why, instead of using the material
elsewhere to divide spaces off from natural space and leaving the resulting pits unused, we do not discard the solid
materials and live in man-made caves, just as moles live in
their runs and leave behind the excavated earth as molehills. And in fact, out of need or asceticsm men have
sometimes contented themselves with cave- or pitdwellings of this kind.

However such a dwelling-form in no sense brings about
the reconciliation of man and nature; by retreating into a
cave man flees from nature instead of adapting it to his existence. But to serve that existence we must ourselves complete our environment, precisely by supplementing the
natural contrast of unlimited solid and void between earth
and air by the contrast of limited solid and void in the form
of spaces bounded by walls, which we call houses.

12. Thus the true human habitat arises, not from a
hollowing-out of the earth’s mass, but through the separation of delimited spaces from the great space of nature by
means of the solid form of walls. We must from the start
make a clear distinction between these two ways of forming
spaces.

The first kind of space is bounded by surfaces, just like a
solid volume in space; and as with the solid volume, the
correspondence between these surfaces gives the hollowedout space a form of its own. But it is quite otherwise with a
space separated from the great unlimited space by the
limited mass of walls: here the form belongs not to the
separated space but to the mass of the wall; space is now
separated from space by a solid form, whereas before, with
the hollowed-out space, mass and space met in a surface.

Hence to avoid giving this separated space the appearance of a hollowed-out mass one must ensure that the
interface between the delimited space and the mass of the
wall remains attached to the mass, so that the form does not
transfer from the mass to the space, as happens with the
non-architectonic cave.

13. A single solid element in the form of a block taken
from the earth is not enough to create a separated space.
For once such a block is set down somewhere on the earth it
loses its self-contained form. The underside vanishes,
together with the part of the earth’s surface that has been
covered up, which is now replaced by the upper surface of
the block. The block raises a portion of the earth’s surface,
but brings about no separation of the space above it. If
more blocks are laid next to the first a flatter form is produced which is still less self-contained. Its upper surface is
still larger in relation to its height above the earth’s surface
and is therefore yet more closely identified with it.

The opposite happens if one piles further blocks of stone
on top of the first and so builds it up into a bar-shaped pier.
With each successive piled-up block the upper surface
steadily diminishes in size in relation to the sides until it
gradually becomes so small that it loses its relation to the
earth’s surface. The mass thus regains its self-contained
form and likewise its value as a space-divider.

Such an upright bar-form must therefore be regarded as
the first architectonic datum.

14. GOlearly we cannot divide space—still less divide off a
space—by building up a single bar-shaped mass, any more
than we can divide a blank sheet of paper into two parts by
marking a point on it: for what a point is in relation to a
surface, an upright bar is in relation to space. But once the
Point develops into a line—even a dotted line—the surface
is divided into two parts on either side of it.

By analogy, in order to subdivide space into two parts
the bar-form must be broadened into a slab. From this
horizontal extension of the upright bar is born the wall,
which must be regarded as the second architectonic datum.

Architectonic space involves not only an artificial

The major space does not in this case make way for two

remains, a new space comes into being. Against the
background of natural space there appears, by a superposition, the architectonic space that is the subject of this study.

1. Architectonic space must be seen as an addition to
natural space whereby the conflict between it and our
experience-space is overcome, just as the conflict between
rough ground and tender feet was overcome by means of
sandals. Sandals, and clothing in general, complete the
human body; the house completes the space of nature.

Out of this enrichment of natural space by architectonic
space there arises a natural space-image that can be harmonized with the space-image of our experience. The two spaceimages can be so attuned that each becomes the perfect
complement of the other, and they form as it were one
whole. Our experience-space is thereby wholly assimilated
into that of nature.

The space-images correspond, in that in both cases it is a
matter of a limited piece of space within the vast space of
nature that stretches out limitlessliy around us over the
earth’s surface. However the limitation of that space comes
about quite differently in each case.

Architectonic space owes its definition to the mass of the

wall, which bounds the space from without. By contrast the
space that we experience and relate to ourselves gets its
definition from the activity of our various faculties, which
determine its boundaries from within.

walls, whereas the second space presents itself as a ‘core-
