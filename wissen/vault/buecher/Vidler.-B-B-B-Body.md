## S. 4

The b-b-b-Body: Block, Blob, Blur

Anthony Vidler

Formulations of body and space are of course just that - intellectual formulations conceived in
order to give character and form to that which has no inherent character and form. They are also
essentially modern formulations, as the ‘Renaissance’ is conceived in the late nineteenth century
as an imaginary haven of stability and human values in the face of their evident disappearance;
as the modern is conceived as a battleground of ambiguities and displacements, for better (Le
Corbusier) or for worse (Walter Benjamin), according to the criteria developed by art historians
for interpreting what they called ‘late’ whether Roman or Renaissance, criteria of form that once
identified were (Rowe) directly ascribable to modernism itself; as the instrument of psychology
and then psychoanalysis, which, at once resisting spatial explanations of spatial phobias, nevertheless sought to situate neuroses in space, and, later as unconscious extensions of inner space; as
the inevitable result ofthe digitalization of culture, of the disassembling of every cultural object
and process into bits and bytes, pixels.

Each of these modern fictions of the body was constructed in and for its own special historical
context and each has produced, and is still producing architecture ofa particular type.

I begin with the idea of the body in Renaissance space - that body surrounded by a space imagined by nineteenth-century historians of the Renaissance as centralized, conceived for the body
and as a play space/space of play for the body - a spielraum, as Walter Benjamin called it after
Heinrich Wölfflin, within which the body would literally have elbow room to move, situate itself,
and stage its own performance as body. Such a space was conceived ofas an enabling, humanistic
space, that at once confirmed the supreme and ideal form of the body as microcosm for and of
architecture, and was confirmed by an architecture constructed as analogous to this body in its
proportions and centrality. This so-called ‘renaissance’ body was a result of deep historical nostalgia, of the sense of loss attending on the incursions of industrial work, of what Giedion has
called the machining of the body, construed by a society of nineteenth-century privilege, academic
and social, building around itself a mythical wall of comfort - comfort in the sense of recuperation
of a lost past never lived. From Burckhardt to Berenson, from Walter Pater to Geoffrey Scott,
from Wölflin to Worringer, the myth of the ideal Renaissance body was carefully fabricated to fit
with and confirm those other myths fabricated by the late nineteenth century, the 'renaissance’,
“"humanism’, and of course ‘space’ itself - that flux which eventually was to undermine the very
body image it was invented to confirm and situate. The rote repetition of Vitruvian lore, indulged
in by writers from Alberti to Palladio, together with the attempt to illustrate Vitruvius by successive artists from Francesco di Giorgio to Cesariano and thence to Leonardo, was taken to signal
the existence of a similar lore in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries. So compelling was this
construction, that it was, and still is taken as fact by historians - Wittkower is the prime example.

Similarly, when it became evident that modern life just simply couldn’t live up
to this ideal, and could in no way be inscribed within the circle or the square,
another myth, that of the modern body was gradually built up, finding its comforting opposite in unity, stability, centrality, and its expressive counter-aesthetic
in fragmentation, mobility, peripherality. From Marey to Duchamp to Dvorak
the modern body was analogous to that body which conveniently had, in this
new interpretation, taken over and destroyed the Renaissance body, what was
now called the Mannerist body; the body of El Greco now supplanted the body
of Raphael.

The body in this mannerist and modernist space, was dismembered and dislocated,
ambiguous in front ofa foreshortened and increasingly flattened (back)ground,
like those ethereal dancers in the Late Roman bas-reliefs examined by Alois
Riegl, feet hardly touching the ground, their position in space unmarked by
coordinates of breadth and depth, no longer representing stability and centrality,
but mobility and peripherality.

But Mannerism had its opponents fortified by the apparent triumph of the
spiritual body in what was called the Baroque; and while Wölflin had feared this
baroque as the very instrument of the dissemination and decentralization he
understood marked the end of renaissance harmony and the beginning of
modern disharmony, a second generation of thinkers - Schmarsow to Sedimayr
— proposed that it was not the material renaissance, nor its necessary disaggregation in the tormented mannerist period, but its final sublimation and ecstatic
fulfillment of a truly spiritual humanism in the baroque. Geoffrey Scott was the
most successful propagator of this myth in modern times, but it was entirely
bought by those modernists who found the lack of unity in the mannerist model
disturbing - thus Giedion on Le Corbusier.

Peripheral or central, this body was however essentially a version of the ‘human-

ist’ body invented by the late nineteenth century, and whether disseminated as

in the modernism of Le Corbusier, or much later, fragmented in the modernism
of so-called deconstruction, ‘humanism’ remains the dominant paradigm.
Countering this body, whether envisaged as whole and healthy, or in parts, and
broken, was a second type of modernist body, perhaps the true body of modernism,
that invented by late nineteenth-century psychology and psychoanalysis. This
body was entirely non-physical, or rather its physical attributes were construed
through the mental - through mental disturbance - neuroses and phobias, or
through the unconscious, as a scene of trauma and loss, nostalgia and desire.

This is the body in psychoanalytical space, tied to a projection of introjections,

no longer positioned with reference to an ideal universal spatial grid, but distorting space itself
through the drives, wish-fulfillments, and phobias of the mental unconscious. A body whose
unity and form was itself constituted by and through a projection of its perceived image - the
mirror image of the whole body - reduced in scale, reversed, and distant from the actual body; a
body constituted as an Imago of a body, one never to be fully internalized, but always out of
reach and never quite conforming to its image. From Freud and Bergson to Lacan.

It was the Viennese Frederick Kiesler, first in Weimar Berlin, then in New York, who comprehensively embraced the architectural implications of this ‘other’ body. From the ‘City in Space’
installation in Paris (1925) to the ‘Space House’ (1933) pavilion in New York; the Art of this Century installation for the Surrealist Gallery in New York to the House of Superstitions for the
International Surrealism exhibition in Paris of 1947; Kiesler experimented with the problem, as
he wrote, of accommodating the ‘latent’ functions of mental and corporeal physicality.

Thus he studied the nature of psychological perception, toward the construction of a ‘vision
machine’ some time around 1938 and 1939. Here, he tries to intersect the physical conditions of
perception with their psychological blockages and interruption. He wrote ‘the vision machine
demonstrates first the flow of sight. It also portrays the origin and flow of visionary images. It
consists of [x] the object [2] the eye [3] the dividing partition between the outside and the inside
[4] a cycle system of man’s physiology and [5] a base which contains the built-in talking apparatus’, which was the apparatus that told the subject what the subject was looking at and what its
responses were. Kiesler invents this apparatus to prove his theory, common to psychologists and
surrealists alike in the 1920s, that vision is not a separate faculty but deformed and determined
by total experience which, in turn, sees not a real object but rather an invented or symbolic image.

It is, perhaps, not accidental that the vision machine takes on a form not unlike the diagrams
developed by a contemporary of Kiesler, Jacques Lacan, in order to explain the interference of
desire with normal vision. Lacan, in 1954, had elaborated his theory of the symbolic imaginary of
the subject by delineating the famous diagram of the upturned bunch of flowers, a diagram that,
for Lacan, signified the relations between our body, the objects of our desire, and the eye, situated
in the world of the symbolic. Kiesler’s vision machine then, translated into Lacanian terms,
would be a mechanism to reveal these pulsations of desire and their place in the symbolic world
of the modern subject. The subsequent development of the space of the Endless House would
then be an attempt to house this subject in a container where outside and inside were endlessly
ambiguous, where the insides of the inside were also so many outsides, and so on, and where the
topology of the house space was of parallel construction to the subject’s own experiential vision.

Here, Kiesler takes direct issue with Le Corbusier’s notion ofthe house asa _

machine for living, one that functionally, like a tool, and physiologically, like a
sensation machine, works to exploit all the emotions and biological needs ofthe
modern subject. Rather, for Kiesler, as he states in the Manifeste du Corr&alisme: “T'he house is neither a machine nor a work of art. The house is a living
organism ... the skin of the human body.’ And later: ‘Form does not follow
Function. Function follows Vision. Vision follows Reality.’

The second thematic that was common to Kiesler and the avant-garde of the
19205 and 19305 would be the nature of the subject’s space itself. Here the key is
the word ‘inside’, repeated insistently by Kiesler, as he works away at the concept,
both virtual and literal. He has pictured an inside, and every model of the outside’s shell-like exterior poses a hypothetical inside, itself revealed by sectional
model after sectional model. The space of the Endless House, correalist or not,
is certainly in line with those imagined by surrealists themselves. Remember the
globular houses of Dali, or, more importantly, the imagined cave houses of’Tristan Tzara, described in Minotaur, where, following Andre Breton, he speaks of
the interior of the house as a kind of womb, a kind of prosthetic umbilical space
to the uncanny origins of the self. Such a project might be, as represented in his
Endless House experiments after 1950, characterized as towards the ‘bio-blob’.

As Kiesler stated:
Architecture as a single block is finished. Closed in on all four sides, top to

bottom. Cubical or rectangular, it does not matter ... the air is locked up. To
breathe, you need window holes, high, low, rectangular, cubical, or in the
latest fashion, horizontal glass strip-tease. Stone or glass block, nothing is
anymore in the way of abolishing the low or high solidity of a house, a
government building, a palace, a skyscraper tickling the clouds.

With endlessness, with continuity, with verticality, the vertical erection of
buildings is gone. The verticality is now bent down in parabolic curves,
twisted turns, spheroid curvatures - no resistance can prevent this outreaching into space, into neighboring areas, like fire tongues leaping ahead until
they have devoured themselves. Let's live in space, rather than look at it.
(Continuity, the New Principle of Architecture’, 1965, in Selected Writings,

P-130)

Recent interest in Kiesler attempts to connect his experiments in spatial flow,
and especially the projects for the Endless House, with the present interest in
computer generated spaces, themselves often taking the form of the informe, the
organic, and the biologically inspired. And certainly, there seems to be an immediate similarity between the morphologies of the Endless House and its endless
variants, as sketched by Kiesler between 1947 and his death in 1965, and the com-

puter generated iterations of morphed form produced, say, by Greg Lynn in the past few years.
Smooth undulating surfaces, topological complexity, ambiguity between outside and inside, a
rejection of the rationalist geometries of the modern movement in favor of a biologism or literal
organicism closer to the surrealists than to the modernists, and a general sense of the endless
potentials of spatial form if unconstrained by Cartesian limits. Even the serial nature of Kiesler’s
sketches, like so many storyboards for a movie, seem to anticipate the more direct activity ofthe
avatars set in motion by contemporary animation programs.

Certainly, in the knowing search for precedence, the obligatory authorization of historical origins,
those who, in the last few years, sought to counter the apparent fading of energy of traditional
late-modernism or the corrupt play of historical motifs characteristic of post-modernism, have
found solace not only in Georges Bataille’s concept of the informe or Gilles Deleuze’s ideas of
folding and streaming, but also in counter-modernist projects, like those of Kiesler.

This said, however, there are what I believe to be essential differences between the project of
Kiesler and those of his apparent heirs in computer design. Differences that go to the heart of
the real divide that separates us in the early twenty-first century from the second generation of
modernists. First in line would be the fundamental difference between what Kiesler and his peers
imagined to be their human subject, what they thought the generic human was made of and in
what way it would respond to space. For Kiesler, from the outset, this subject was conceived as
still modernist, and by this I mean a subject with all the enlightenment faculties of opticality and
reason but also one with the subsequent disturbances to conscious reason interjected by the dis-

covery, ifI can call it that, ofthe Freudian unconscious.

For in the domain of the subject and the domain of the subject’s space, contemporary architecture
finds itselfin a different, if not radically different terrain. First, if the modernist subject, Freudian
or Lacanian, surrealist or correalist, was endowed with all the articulated structures of what
Freud called the architecture of the unconscious, one that, despite its mysteries, was fundamentally open to the inquiries of analysis, our late-modern subject is nothing but ambiguous in its
self-definition and its psychological structure. Where Lacan and the surrealists found their
material and instruments in opticality, as imaged in the reflective qualities of the mirror and
constructed according to all the laws of perspectivity, contemporary subject identity, if it is optical at all, finds its subject in screens, in clouded surfaces, in the indeterminacy of non-perspectival structure. Secondly and spatially, if modernists and counter-modernists debated the merits
of transparency and darkness, glass houses and womb houses, late-modernists have long rejected
the myth oftransparency and refused its opposite in favor of opacity, of translucency, ofthe

Thus the necessary conditions proposed for the inhabitation of an Endless House
are sharply different for those, in the present, that pertain to the Klein bottle
curves of Ben van Berkel and Caroline Bos’ house or to the warped spaces of
Greg Lynn. So in the end, we’re left with two distinet conditions that throw
Kiesler’s own achievement into even sharper relief and that leave us to our own
devices when it comes to inventing our own Endless Houses in an age when
infinity has been decidedly domesticated and where the simple space-time
equations of Einsteinian modernism have been irrevocably complicated, if not

discarded.

The body now finds its identity in digital space, now flattened even more through

“the framing of the screen - a screen no longer a mirror, but opaque and resistant

to anything but a projective spatial entry. The space of the hacker, moving
through projection and introjection as if through an infinite spatial realm,
“jacked in’ as Gibson would have it into a universal matrix. A space that is not
spatial, constructed out of an endless string of os and ıs, wound round a screen

for convenience, but actually in no space at all.

Perhaps the most interesting experiment in articulating the conditions for interpreting this spaceless, placeless, body was that conducted by Diller+Scofidio at
Yverdon-les-Bains between 1998 and this last summer, what they have termed
the BLUR building. You all know its character - a building constructed out of
water droplets, a cloud in effect. In Diller+Scofidio’s words it ‘is not a building’
but ‘pure atmosphere, water particles suspended in mid-air. The fog is a dynamic,
phantom mass, which changes form constantly.’ (Blur, 325) Not a real mass, but a
phantom mass; not form or informe, but constantly changing form. If for Kiesler,
the psychology of vision would allow for a body to be positioned within space,
then in BLUR ‘vision is put out of focus’, and no longer can be relied on for its
traditional role - orientation, information, spectacle - but rather serves disorientation, erasure of information; BLUR is, they write, ‘a spectacle with nothing
to see’. (ibid.)

This is the key: sight is blocked, or rather whited out in this atmosphere - the
keywords of the experience of entering, as Diller+Scofidio listed them were:
Atmosphere

Scotoma

Nothing
Disorientation
Artificial Nature
Formless

White Noise
White Out
Sublime

It is the last of these words, however, that gives the keys to all the others, and perhaps to the
project as a whole in its historical context. For the sublime, a category long used in classical rhetoric had been given a specific connotation in the eighteenth century by the British philosopher
Edmund Burke. For him it was an aesthetic category, responding directly to experiences that
could not on any traditional register be considered beautiful, in the classical sense of harmony,
proportion, balance, etc. Experiences like great natural disasters, floods, earthquakes, fires; like
the aspect of terrible storms over the ocean and forests; experiences in other words that inspired
pure terror in the viewer. The sublime came to be understood as the most forceful of aesthetic
sensations, to be sought in painting, literature, and of course architecture - it was Ledoux who
tried in fact to apply such sensations to his ideal designs. Gradually, however, throughout the
nineteenth century, the sublime became a cheapened category - the social ‘sublime’ of art gallery
viewers. In modernism, once more, with the re-reading of philosophers like Immanuel Kant,
who had understood the sublime to be something attached to the idea of striving for representation of something that could not be represented, in modernist abstraction, the sublime took on
new meaning as a way of understanding abstraction - the form of the unknown, the very form of
the inability to express. In the context of our BLUR, then, the revival of the idea of the sublime
attaches itself to the enlightenment idea of nature, of natural control, but of nature so to speak
always out of control — the weather is the one factor that cannot be controlled here; fog, despite
all measures, despite the installation of fog police at the lake edge, cannot be contained. It
spreads, its makes obscure, it causes danger, it conceals and reveals on its own terms. It is the
atmospheric agent of scotoma, of white noise, of white out; it is the ultimate formless; artificial

nature become nature again, and thus sublime in its effects and experiences.

The body thus constructed is haptically rather than optically identified, rather like those body
images conjured by the phenomenologist and psychoanalyst Eugene Minkowski, who explored
the projections of those patients that seemed to live in ‘dark’ space - no matter how objectively
‘light’ the space was, they lived in an inner darkness. The BLUR atmosphere produced something of the same effect, but in light space.
