Introduction 
Kabutarnama, a book on the art of pigeon-keeping written and 
illustrated by Valih Musavi in 1788, includes several depictions 
of contemporaneous pigeon dwellings that were incorporated 
into human habitats in the Mughal towns of North India. Cotes, 
niches, cages, ledges, feeders, and small water tanks are seen 
integrated into terraces, external walls, parapets, and archways. 
These images speak of the existing and effortless culture of 
accommodating multispecies habitats within the creation of architecture. In one of the paintings by Musavi, A Courtyard Filled 
with Pigeons (1788), pigeons occupy more canvas area than 
humans Fig 0.1. In another, A Lady Smoking a Huqqa and Watching Pigeons (unknown artist, c.1760s), pigeons occupy the center 
of the frame Fig 0-2. In the background is an elaborate rectilinear 
three-storied dovecote, a portable structure with tiny arched 
openings for pigeons to perch in. The painting suggests that the 
birds and the humans are sharing a restful evening on the terrace 
of a royal house. Sharing moments of leisure indicates a departure from utilitarian purposes toward the social, or inter-species 
epitome of cohabitation. The presence and representation of pigeons within human-made architecture historically and culturally 
prompts us to trace the actions that led to a loss of such environments of cohabitation. With an awareness that pigeons are a 
highly adaptable species and extremely invasive, Living Together 
foregrounds these examples to extend the cohabitative environments of humans and more-than-humans to include other urban 
species and speculate what that architecture would be like. 
17 
Modern architecture has prioritized human exclusivity to such an 
extent that ecological interrelationships have become obscured. 
The construction of an architectural project is initiated with the 
clearing and cleansing of the construction site, interfering with the 
existing more-than-human relationships on the site and disrupting 
them at local and ecosystemic scales. Foundations are dug in this 
tabula rasa, overturning organic matter that hosts myriad visible 
and invisible species. Other life-forms and habitats are obliterated. 
We argue that the development of such modern architecture has 
been instrumental in creating the threat of mass extinction in cities 
that we face today. Modernity has taught us that exclusively living apart from other species and exploiting natural resources has 
only been detrimental to human habitation. Hence, a renewed urgency is emerging that calls on us to reconsider this distancing 
and imagine human and nonhuman worlds as coinciding. Positioning itself in this conversation, Living Together centers architecture 
as an agent for cohabitation and multispecies proliferation.

It doesn't require big gestures to create cohabitative architecture; simple, matter-of-fact, everyday gestures that can be made 
by one and all are sufficient. Housing solitary bees is one such 
opportunity. With urbanization, natural permeable surfaces are 
replaced by impermeable materials, reducing nesting sites for 
solitary bees.6 To host solitary bees, all that is required are hollow wooden or bamboo sticks tied together and hung for the 
cavity-nesting bees, and some soil left free in unpaved gardens 
for the ground-nesting species (Kumar-Rao 2021). This act to 
build biodiversity can be achieved by any ordinary city resident. 
To proliferate bees in an effortless way, ATREE (Ashoka Trust for 
Research in Ecology and the Environment, Bangalore) designed 
a two-foot-high porous wooden structure called the Bee Hotel, 
which can be easily installed in private gardens and on balconies 
and terraces.7 Similarly, Space10 created the open-source Bee 
Home.8 These digitally fabricated blocks with holes can be assembled by users, encouraging the integration of bee habitats 
into urban areas. Likewise, Faye Clifton has designed the Bee 
Brick-a 
block with cavities that replaces a brick in the wall, providing a nesting place for several species of solitary bees.9 
These projects have stemmed from a growing awareness that 
human dominance and exploitation of other species have been 
detrimental to humanity's own well-being and survival. Their various approaches take new theoretical standpoints, integrate 
technocentric strategies, and implement civic-action programs. 
The projects have focused on a deep reading of bird habitats, 
bee habitats, and biological invasion in abandoned areas. 
The intention is to increase our knowledge of how morethan-human species live and thrive, which can be integrated into our built environment. Consequently, there 
is an emerging awareness that coexistence with 
other species is crucial at this point.

What does it mean for architecture when plants and other 
species exercise their own agency? Architecture would become truly receptive-both 
physically, premised on creating 
spaces for accommodating other-than-humans, and intellectually, premised on becoming aware of the needs of species 
other-than-human. Architecture would have to draw the line 
between providing for other-than-human species and allowing them to exercise their agency to construct their own habitat. It is time to stop architecting and let other species do 
their bit and make architecture a truly collaborative venture. 
However, when approached from a solutionist perspective 
for a particular species, such initiatives risk being patchy and 
hyper-specific, whereas when integrated with contemporary 
technologies, they risk being technocratic. The concern for 
cohabitative environments cannot be addressed by architectural design alone. However, a largely theoretical approach 
might be difficult to translate into design parameters. Designbased approaches, whether technocratic or demonstrating a 
theoretical standpoint, demand a holistic rather than a fragmented approach.

When participants of an assemblage interact with 
other entities, they change and sometimes fuse 
together to form hybrids-that 
is, new assemblages. A hybrid may be a fusing of biological and 
technological components, both organic and inorganic. Hybridity, which involves the merging of 
different elements, ensures richness and diversity 
within an assemblage. Hybridity makes the assemblage more resilient and capable of responding to 
dynamic environments. Haraway's cyborg, which 
disrupts established power structures, is a hybrid. 
In an assemblage, a hybrid acknowledges the 
agency of each participant, promoting equitable 
transactions. For the work Portal (2022) by the 
Australian artist Jamie North, two cylindrical columns were made out of cast concrete and industrial aggregates. These carefully crafted columns 
serve as an unexpected habitat for several plant 
species native to Brisbane. Nourished by water, 
indigenous plants established themselves in the 
crevices of the columns, forming a hybrid of plant 
and concrete-a 
resilient assemblage. 
For Deleuze and Guattari, a rhizome is that which "becomes" 
using transversal communications and does not reproduce 
genealogically (Deleuze and Guattari 1985, 239). Foregrounding contagion, they argue that it is through "interkingdoms" and 
"unnatural participations" that "nature operates-against 
itself" 
(Deleuze and Guattari 1985, 242). Dismissing the conventional 
emphasis on "filiative production or hereditary reproduction, in 
which the only differences retained are a simple duality between 
sexes within the same species, and small modifications across 
generations," they emphasize the need to consider "many beings 
[coming] from different worlds [which] form rhizomes" (Deleuze 
and Guattari 1985, 242). The rhizome represents a multiplicity of interconnected elements cofunctioning by contagion and 
participating in assemblages. Furthermore, a rhizome is decentered-that 
is, it doesn't have a single point of origin or a privileged center (Deleuze and Guattari 1985, 8)-which 
challenges 
traditional notions of authority and control. A rhizome operates 
along lines passing "between things, between points," and hence 
encourages multiplicity by forming connections that expand an 
assemblage (Deleuze and Guattari 1985, 505).

Prosthetics 
A prosthesis, derived from the Greek pro-
(instead of) and thesis (placing), is defined 
as the replacement of all or part of a damaged or diseased anatomical organ by an artificial device. A prosthesis is applied externally to support a fragile body. It is temporary in nature. Prosthetics is a branch of surgery concerned with the making and fitting of artificial body parts; hence, it is the act of 
mediation between an artificial device and the living body. Prostheses become relevant 
when the body has had an accident, undergone an amputation, or has weakened parts. 
In the wake of the ongoing destruction of species and relationships, Haraway argues 
that a terrapolis can only occur when the pluriverse recuperates. If that living body is a 
terrapolis or assemblage, an insert that helps in recuperating the pluriverse becomes 
a prosthesis. The prosthesis would ensure that the various individual environments of 
organisms occupying the lower limit of the 
terrapolis synchronize with one another 
and calibrate the necessary balance. Prosthetics are different from hybrids in their resistance to mutation. Medically speaking, 
the inserted prosthetic does not change as 
it supports the fragile body. Once the body 
has lived its life, the inserted prosthetics 
may appear as the remnant of a support 
system and become available to support 
something else. Prostheses such as bionic 
limbs become part of the body and complete otherwise broken or frail connections Fig 2-3_ Prostheses such as cosmetic 
gloves, to cover myoelectric arms, can be 
removed or worn as and when needed. 
Prosthetics such as metal implants are recycled-titanium 
hips and cobalt-chrome

construction of cars and planes, and sometimes sold for reuse in the medical industry.

Vacancy 
The components of an assemblage play a material and expressive role. The vibrancy of entities such as tree roots and the vacancy of a fractured rock collaborate and collude to stabilize the 
assemblage of the ruin-the 
hybrid that resists erosion. This is 
an example of territorialization in the ruin. The materiality of the 
tree roots helps it follow the available space in the cracked rock. 
Simultaneously, the materiality of the rock ensures the vacancy 
can hold moisture and soil as the tree root finds its way through it. 
The tree root is expressive in going in directions that are suitable 
to it; the vacancy is expressive in being accommodative. In the 
intermingling of the two entities, each reacting to the other, the 
assemblage of the ruin emerges. 
At the top of a fractured basalt hill, the soil erodes further with each monsoon shower. 
The slopes of the hill are most susceptible to processes of deterritorialization. However, 
the eroding soil caught up in the fissures on the hill face keeps the hill stabilized, becoming part of the territorialization process. Here, the tree and the vacancy act as a rhizome-they 
are in a lateral relationship. The lines of the rhizome are what Deleuze and 
Guattari call arborescence (Deleuze and Guattari 1985, 34). When matter moves from 
one assemblage and into another, it deterritorializes the former and reterritorializes the 
latter. The example of the eroding soil is useful once again: The abandoned quarry is an 
assemblage consisting of a basalt hill, quarry pits, loose rocks, debris, denuded desire 
paths, caves, streams, forests, and heath. The basalt hill is an assemblage of several assemblages. The soil on the hilltop is part of the assemblage of the grassy terrain there, 
until it interacts with the rainwater that forcefully hits it, giving in to the gravity and weight 
of water. It leaves the assemblage of the terrain and enters the assemblage of flowing 
water. It remains part of that assemblage until it encounters a cavity in the rock and enacts its ability to cause friction to the moving water. The soil then enters the assemblage 
of the cavity and gets stabilized. It remains in that assemblage until other forms of matter start to collect there and change the composition and density of the accumulation.

more-than-humans. The architect's obsession with planning 
misses opportunities to notice precarious situations. Thinking 
through precarity can help architecture revisit its obsession 
with deterministic forms. Architecture can limit itself to creating 
conditions for habitations that can be fully realized by those who 
inhabit them; thus, architecture would be open to adaptation. 
Precarity challenges architecture's obsession with controlling 
form, which is driven by modernist ideals of defined aesthetics 
and monumentality. It can help architects share control for the 
creation of habitats with other-than-humans; it also helps architects notice what and whom is available to work with. Such unpredictable encounters are transformative, letting an architect 
lose control and become vulnerable. This vulnerability allows for 
continuous interaction, awareness of the environment, and an 
ability to continuously transform and adapt our structures.
