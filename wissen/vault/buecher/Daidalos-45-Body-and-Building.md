Although the educated clas: ses only became aware of the korai of the
Erechtheum on the Acropolis in Athens as a result of 18th century
literature on Greece, the notion of the anthropomorphic character of the
column had never been completely forgotten. When, from the twenties of
the quattrocento on, Filippo Brunelleschi once again used the swollen piers
with their more or less fixed proportions - instead of sharp-edged or
cluster-like, arbitrarily compact or extended piers, which had been
accorded preferential treatment for centuries - he was thus not only
establishing a link with antiquity (which was considered to be a classical
model) but also gave expression to the idea of grasping the pier as an
architectural allegory of the human being over and above its material
function of bearing and applying load.

The beginnings of the “intrinsic significance of the pier as an
anthropomorphically conceived being extend to realms of animist
religiosity, just as the stone and the tree were revered as divine beings per
se’!. Asan idol ora being presented with life, the column was ori iginally as
little linked to the structure as a sign or a national ensign. It was developed
as the tectonic-plastic embodiment of elevation and applying load by the
Greeks, who emphasised its anthropomorphic character as a load-bearing
architectural element by replacing it with support figures such as korai,
caryatides or atlantes.

Alongside the possibility of replacing the column by human bodies
and besides their subdivision into base, swelling shaft and capital which
were to be understood anthropomorphically, their most important
architectural allusion to human beings lay in the derivation of their
proportions from those of the human body. According to Vitruvius the
concentrated proportions of the Doric column correspond to the ‘strength
and grace of the male body’, those of the Ionic column to those of “feminine
slenderness’ and, finally, those of the Corinthian to those of * vir: gin
tenderness’.? The basis in each case is the respective relationship of the si
of the feet to the height of the body, which corresponds to the relationship
of the diameter of the column to its height (including the capital).
Depending on the column order taken - the three classical orders, viz the
Doric, Ionic and Corinthian were later supplemented by that of the
composite, the Italian, as well as the Tuscan orders - the column diameter of
6 to 10 times is given by the column height. The column orders were first
brought together in a graphic scheme, and these later went through
countless variations and interpretations by Serlio* and Vignola as wellas in
French treatisess and Nordic literature on columns? and reached a larger
audience in the Vitruvius edition published in Como in 1521 by a pupil of
Bramante, Cesare Cesariano.® Whilst these column orders show the
various capitals familiar to us, they are distinguished, by their proportions
only, in the architectural tracts which were written carlier, yet not
published, by the Florentine Antonio Averlino - called Il Filarete (1464)? -
as well as those by the Sienese Francesco di Giorgio Martini (c. 1480/90)!0.

high.

Talking about bodies, especially talking cogently and coherently
about my own body, is notoriously awkward. Ludwig Wittgenstein
devoted much of his last book, curiously misnamed On Certainty, to
questioning how anyone could maintain that he ‘knows that this is my
hand’, as he raises it or points to it. Knowledge of my hand is only
acceptable, it would seem (extrapolating from Wittgenstein), when I speak
of my body as a mental construct.

Although I know my body in such indirect ways, it is constantly inmy
speech - on my tongue, as it were. All of us trip forever over bodyreferences, body-metaphors. Daily speech is peppered and larded with
mention of parts of the body, of its inner and outer movements. And yet
such seemingly direct allusions are historically, even pre- historically
rooted, as Wittgenstein also clearly saw. ‘Our speech is an incarnation of
ancient myths; and the rituals of ancient myth were a language’, he wrote —
meaning verbal speech as well as the body-language of gesture, which is
indissolubly connected to and dependent on it.

Language has always meant articulated sound — eked out by facial
grimace and manual gesture — to convey intention and proposition most
exactly and rapidly. Yet grimace and gesture and expressive — even
articulated - sound is something that man shares with many other animals.
They make their signs by inherited, inborn code. However complex its
organisation (as in worker-bee ‘dances’ outside a hive or the courtship
“rituals’ of peacocks) a non-human sign system always works in the
expectation of the one-to-one reading, while human sound and gesture are
made to (or at) some interpreting ‘other’ in a constant hope - and fear — of
the polyvalence of the sign: that also is implied in Wittgenstein’s insistence
on the historicity of language. Man shares with other animals not only
expressive, articulated sound but also play as well: yet the difference
between animal games and human ones is marked by the preposition we
add to the verb ‘to play’: animals play ‘with’, men play ‘at’. And indeed the
play of human beings presupposes a ground and a rule-a reference, and an
agreement or compact.

The interpretative compact may be the primary -and ultimate - social
bond of mankind. It is made in signs for which the body
and its movements are both source and medium. The
body’s metaphoric value is therefore your fundamental human inheritance and mine, which had to be conquered by a violent, catastrophic separation of the
human body from the undifferentiated flux of nature.
I sense this my body (and by extension and reflection also recognise that of my reader and all his like)

We may consider the anthropomorphic foundations for the creation
of spatial concepts to be the reason why parallel conceptions continued to

of man as a microcosm bearing the universe within himself and being
involved in a reciprocal relationship with the universe conceived as his
equivalent is to be given priority here.

A particularly interesting version and extension of this body of ideas
is to be found in the works of the Avignon clerie Opieinus de Canistris
(1296-1352). He drew a number of portolano cards which exactly reproduced the outlines of the European continent and individual country
areas. Into some of these line networks he then projected human bodies,
faces and forms which interpreted the accidental, geographical contours in
accordance with fears that the end of the world was nigh. 'Thus, for
example, one sheet representing the Mediterranean shows the “Africa
Woman’ whispering words of evil into the receptive car of Europe Man’.
The head of Europe Man is presented from the front, whilst Africa Woman
is depicted as a profile. Both contours are formed from the outlines of the
Moroccan coast and the Iberian Peninsula, whereby the concrete form of
the world appears in this case as a sinful couple who are inexorably
predestined to damnation.

This almost recalls the bizarre works of the I6th and 17th centuries
which projected phantastic human physiognomies into all conceivable

formed to represent deformed, stretched-out giants and, conversely, his
humans arc composed of elementary geometric rooms and chambers.
Driven on by this obsession of wanting to rediscover the human body and
countenance in the objects of the natural and artificial worlds, innumerable
tendencies swim against the current of the main directions in art right up
to the present, tendencies which link the Surrealism of our age to the
phantastic art of former epoches. Salvador Dali’s Visage Paranoiaque,
which is intended to present a portrait of the Marquis de Sade in a ‘style
picassien’, arose in 1934/35 through giving prominence to certain features
on a photograph, turned 90°, of an African village. A similar technique of
‘doubling the picture” had already been employed by Giuseppe
Arcimboldo in the invertible pictures he painted around 1590, which show
a still-life of vegetables that becomes the portrait of a vegetable gardener
when turned upside down.

When seen in relationship to everyday housing, however, this eloquent

Latent anthropomorphism, however, which suggests without
asserting, creates the possibility of identifying with architecture in a very
literal sense by permitting one to rediscover oneself in a specific space and
to feel at home there.

Being at home in the world is also the basic theme of the picture
puzzles by Giuseppe Arcimboldo. Consequently it is possible to see in
them a theory of anthropomorphism in general. On the surface, his bizarre
forms would appear to be nothing but grotesque inspirations, distorted
beings with root noses and beards of bushes of the type the amazed people
visiting the fairs loved to see and which incorporated the nobility in effigy
in art collections and curio galleries. However, these are the pictures of the
nobility such as the Habsburgs, and their significance as a political allegory
of imperial omnipotence was revealed by Fonteo’s poems, which arose at
the same time (1563). The attributes of the seasons, of which the Kaiser
portraits of Maximilian II and Rudolf II are composced, are intended to
represent the rule of this house as universal and divine law, timeless,
depersonalised and eternally renewing themselves in cynical succession.

In the case of Arcimboldo, however, i.c. in his portraits of the rulers,
this theme was finally developed to create a picture of man, which is no
longer intended to signify the realm or the Kaiser or any individual
whatsoever but purely and simply man as a species: man who bears the
wealth of things within himself, in full accord with Paracelsus, who stated
that “all hat a man encloses can be seen from the outside, and thus, by
observing the exterior, we can get to know the inside ...”?.
